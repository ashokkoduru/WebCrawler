<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Sensible heat</b> is <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> exchanged by a body or <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">thermodynamic system</a> in which the exchange of heat changes the temperature of the body or system, and some macroscopic variables of the body or system, but leaves unchanged certain other macroscopic variables of the body or system, such as volume or pressure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span></h2>

<p>The term is used in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>, which is the amount of heat exchanged that is hidden, meaning it occurs without change of temperature. For example, during a phase change such as the melting of ice, the temperature of the system containing the ice and the liquid is constant until all ice has melted. The terms latent and sensible are correlative. That means that they are defined as a pair, depending on which other macroscopic variables are held constant during the process.</p>

<p><i>Sensible heat</i> and <i>latent heat</i> are not special forms of energy. Rather, they describe exchanges of heat under conditions specified in terms of their effect on a material or a thermodynamic system.</p>
<p>In the writings of the early scientists who provided the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, sensible heat had a clear meaning in <a href="/wiki/Calorimetry" title="Calorimetry">calorimetry</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> characterized it in 1847 as an energy that was indicated by the thermometer.</p>
<p>Both sensible and latent heats are observed in many processes while transporting energy in nature. Latent heat is associated with changes of state, measured at constant temperature, especially the <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase changes</a> of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Vaporization" title="Vaporization">vaporization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>, whereas sensible heat directly affects the temperature of the atmosphere.</p>
<p>In meteorology, the term 'sensible heat flux' means the conductive heat <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> from the Earth's surface to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. It is an important component of Earth's surface energy budget. Sensible heat flux is commonly measured with the <a href="/wiki/Eddy_covariance" title="Eddy covariance">eddy covariance</a> method.</p>
