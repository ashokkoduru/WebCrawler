<p><b>Cooking</b> or <b>cookery</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a> of preparing <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> for consumption with the use of heat. Cooking techniques and ingredients vary widely across the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">world</a>, from grilling food over an open fire to using electric stoves, to baking in various types of ovens, reflecting unique environmental, economic, and cultural traditions and trends. The ways or types of cooking also depend on the skill and type of training an individual cook has. Cooking is done both by people in their own dwellings and by professional cooks and chefs in restaurants and other food establishments. Cooking can also occur through chemical reactions without the presence of heat, most notably with <a href="/wiki/Ceviche" title="Ceviche">ceviche</a>, a traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_American" title="South American">South American</a> dish where <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> is cooked with the acids in <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> or lime juice.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis#Cooking" title="Pyrolysis">Preparing food with heat or fire</a> is an activity unique to humans. Some anthropologists think that cooking fires first developed around 250,000 years ago, although there is evidence for the controlled use of fire by <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> beginning 400,000 years ago.</p>
<p>The expansion of agriculture, commerce, trade and transportation between civilizations in different regions offered cooks many new ingredients. New inventions and technologies, such as the invention of <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> for holding and boiling water, expanded cooking techniques. Some modern cooks apply advanced scientific techniques to food preparation to further enhance the flavor of the dish served.</p>

<p>Archaeology, by its nature, can only identify the <i>current</i> earliest evidence of cooking. The possibility always remains of finding new evidence which indicates an even earlier date. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" title="Phylogenetic">Phylogenetic</a> analysis suggests that human ancestors may have invented cooking as far back as 1.8 million to 2.3 million years ago. Re-analysis of burnt bone fragments and plant ashes from the <a href="/wiki/Wonderwerk_Cave" title="Wonderwerk Cave">Wonderwerk Cave</a>, South Africa, has provided evidence supporting human control of fire there by 1 mya. There is evidence that <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> was cooking their food as early as 500,000 years ago. Evidence for the controlled use of fire by <i>Homo erectus</i> beginning some 400,000 years ago has wide scholarly support. Archeological evidence, from 300,000 years ago, in the form of ancient hearths, <a href="/wiki/Earth_oven" title="Earth oven">earth ovens</a>, burnt animal bones, and <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a>, are found across Europe and the Middle East. <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">Anthropologists</a> think that widespread cooking fires began about 250,000 years ago, when <a href="/wiki/Hearth" title="Hearth">hearths</a> started appearing. More recently, the earliest hearths have been reported to be at least 790,000 years old.</p>
<p>In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, food was a classic marker of identity in Europe. In the nineteenth-century "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Nationalism" title="Age of Nationalism">Age of Nationalism</a>" cuisine became a defining symbol of national identity.</p>
<p>Communication between the Old World and the New World influenced the history of cooking. The movement of foods across the Atlantic, from the New World, such as <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yam_(vegetable)" title="Yam (vegetable)">yams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">bell pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pecan" title="Pecan">pecan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cashew" title="Cashew">cashew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">blueberry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">sunflower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gourds" title="Gourds">gourds</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" title="Squash (plant)">squash</a>, had a profound effect on Old World cooking. The movement of foods across the Atlantic, from the Old World, such as <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oats" title="Oats">oats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chick_pea" title="Chick pea">chick peas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_bean" title="Green bean">green beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustard_plant" title="Mustard plant">mustard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrots</a>, had a profound effect on New World cooking.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> brought mass-production, mass-marketing and standardization of food. Factories processed, preserved, canned, and packaged a wide variety of foods, and processed cereals quickly became a defining feature of the American breakfast. In the 1920s, <a href="/wiki/Frozen_food" title="Frozen food">freezing methods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria" title="Cafeteria">cafeterias</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast-food_restaurant" title="Fast-food restaurant">fast-food establishments</a> emerged.</p>
<p>Along with changes in food, starting early in the 20th century, governments have issued nutrition guidelines, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Food_pyramid_(nutrition)" title="Food pyramid (nutrition)">food pyramid</a> (introduced in Sweden in 1974). The 1916 "Food For Young Children" became the first USDA guide to give specific dietary guidelines. Updated in the 1920s, these guides gave shopping suggestions for different-sized families along with a Depression Era revision which included four cost levels. In 1943, the USDA created the "Basic Seven" chart to make sure that people got the recommended nutrients. It included the first-ever Recommended Daily Allowances from the National Academy of Sciences. In 1956, the "Essentials of an Adequate Diet" brought recommendations which cut the number of groups that American school children would learn about down to four. In 1979, a guide called "Food" addressed the link between too much of certain foods and chronic diseases, but added "fats, oils, and sweets" to the four basic food groups.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients">Ingredients</span></h2>

<p>Most ingredients in cooking are derived from <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">living organisms</a>. Vegetables, fruits, grains and nuts as well as herbs and spices come from plants, while meat, eggs, and dairy products come from animals. Mushrooms and the yeast used in baking are kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>. Cooks also use <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>. Cooks can also use <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">spirits</a>.</p>
<p>Naturally occurring ingredients contain various amounts of molecules called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a></i>. They also contain water and minerals. Cooking involves a manipulation of the chemical properties of these molecules.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbohydrates">Carbohydrates</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">Carbohydrate</a></div>
<p>Carbohydrates include the common sugar, <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> (table sugar), a disaccharide, and such simple sugars as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> (from the digestion of table sugar) and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> (from fruit), and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a> from sources such as cereal flour, rice, <a href="/wiki/Arrowroot" title="Arrowroot">arrowroot</a> and potato. The interaction of heat and carbohydrate is complex.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">Long-chain sugars</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> tend to break down into <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">simpler sugars</a> when cooked, while simple sugars can form <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrups</a>. If sugars are heated so that all water of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystallisation" title="Crystallisation">crystallisation</a> is driven off, then <a href="/wiki/Caramelization" title="Caramelization">caramelization</a> starts, with the sugar undergoing thermal decomposition with the formation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, and other breakdown products producing <a href="/wiki/Caramel" title="Caramel">caramel</a>. Similarly, the heating of sugars and proteins elicits the <a href="/wiki/Maillard_reaction" title="Maillard reaction">Maillard reaction</a>, a basic flavor-enhancing technique.</p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a> of starch with fat or water can, when gently heated, provide thickening to the dish being cooked. In <a href="/wiki/European_cuisine" title="European cuisine">European</a> cooking, a mixture of butter and flour called a <a href="/wiki/Roux" title="Roux">roux</a> is used to thicken liquids to make stews or sauces. In Asian cooking, a similar effect is obtained from a mixture of rice or <a href="/wiki/Corn_starch" title="Corn starch">corn starch</a> and water. These techniques rely on the properties of starches to create simpler mucilaginous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saccharide" title="Saccharide">saccharides</a> during cooking, which causes the familiar thickening of <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauces</a>. This thickening will break down, however, under additional heat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fats">Fats</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">Fat</a></div>

<p>Types of fat include <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oils</a>, animal products such as butter and <a href="/wiki/Lard" title="Lard">lard</a>, as well as fats from grains, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> oils. Fats are used in a number of ways in cooking and baking. To prepare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stir_fry" title="Stir fry">stir fries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grilled_cheese" title="Grilled cheese">grilled cheese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">pancakes</a>, the pan or griddle is often coated with fat or oil. Fats are also used as an ingredient in baked goods such as cookies, cakes and pies. Fats can reach temperatures higher than the boiling point of water, and are often used to conduct high heat to other ingredients, such as in frying, deep frying or sautéing. Fats are used to add flavor to food (e.g., butter or bacon fat), prevent food from sticking to pans and create a desirable texture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proteins">Proteins</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">Protein (nutrient)</a></div>
<p>Edible animal material, including <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>, milk, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egg_white" title="Egg white">egg whites</a>, contains substantial amounts of protein. Almost all vegetable matter (in particular <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>) also includes proteins, although generally in smaller amounts. Mushrooms have high protein content. Any of these may be sources of <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acids</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> are heated they become denatured (unfolded) and change texture. In many cases, this causes the structure of the material to become softer or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friable" title="Friable">friable</a> – meat becomes <i>cooked</i> and is more friable and less flexible. In some cases, proteins can form more rigid structures, such as the coagulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albumen" title="Albumen">albumen</a> in egg whites. The formation of a relatively rigid but flexible matrix from egg white provides an important component in baking cakes, and also underpins many desserts based on <a href="/wiki/Meringue" title="Meringue">meringue</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a></div>

<p>Cooking often involves water, frequently present in other liquids, which is both added in order to immerse the substances being cooked (typically water, <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">stock</a> or wine), and released from the foods themselves. A favorite method of adding flavor to dishes is to save the liquid for use in other recipes. Liquids are so important to cooking that the name of the cooking method used is often based on how the liquid is combined with the food, as in <a href="/wiki/Steaming" title="Steaming">steaming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simmer" title="Simmer">simmering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boiling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braising" title="Braising">braising</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blanching_(cooking)" title="Blanching (cooking)">blanching</a>. Heating liquid in an open container results in rapidly increased <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Concentrate" title="Concentrate">concentrates</a> the remaining <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a> and ingredients – this is a critical component of both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stewing" title="Stewing">stewing</a> and sauce making.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vitamins_and_minerals">Vitamins and minerals</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">Vitamin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrient)" title="Mineral (nutrient)">Mineral (nutrient)</a></div>
<p>Vitamins are materials required for normal <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> but which the body cannot manufacture itself and which must therefore come from external sources. Vitamins come from several sources including fresh fruit and vegetables (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">Vitamin C</a>), carrots, <a href="/wiki/Liver_(food)" title="Liver (food)">liver</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">Vitamin A</a>), cereal bran, bread, liver (B vitamins), fish liver oil (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">Vitamin D</a>) and fresh green vegetables (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">Vitamin K</a>). Many minerals are also essential in small quantities including iron, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>; and in very small quantities copper, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>. The micronutrients, minerals, and vitamins in fruit and vegetables may be destroyed or eluted by cooking. Vitamin C is especially prone to oxidation during cooking and may be completely destroyed by protracted cooking. The bioavailability of some vitamins such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thiamin" title="Thiamin">thiamin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B6" title="Vitamin B6">vitamin B6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niacin" title="Niacin">niacin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a> are increased with cooking by being freed from the food microstructure. Blanching or steaming vegetables is a way of minimizing vitamin and mineral loss in cooking.</p>

<p>There are very many methods of cooking, most of which have been known since antiquity. These include baking, roasting, frying, grilling, barbecuing, smoking, boiling, steaming and braising. A more recent innovation is microwaving. Various methods use differing levels of heat and moisture and vary in cooking time. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety">Health and safety</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_safety">Food safety</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">Food safety</a></div>
<p>Cooking can prevent many <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne illnesses</a> that would otherwise occur if the food is eaten raw. When heat is used in the preparation of food, it can kill or inactivate harmful organisms, such as bacteria and viruses, as well as various parasites such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapeworm" title="Tapeworm">tapeworms</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Toxoplasma_gondii" title="Toxoplasma gondii">Toxoplasma gondii</a></i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">Food poisoning</a> and other illness from uncooked or poorly prepared food may be caused by bacteria such as <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli_O157:H7" title="Escherichia coli O157:H7">pathogenic strains</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salmonella_typhimurium" title="Salmonella typhimurium">Salmonella typhimurium</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Campylobacter" title="Campylobacter">Campylobacter</a></i>, viruses such as <a href="/wiki/Norovirus" title="Norovirus">noroviruses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Entamoeba_histolytica" title="Entamoeba histolytica">Entamoeba histolytica</a></i>. Parasites may be introduced through salad, meat that is uncooked or done <a href="/wiki/Doneness" title="Doneness">rare</a>, and unboiled water.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilizing</a> effect of cooking depends on temperature, cooking time, and technique used. However, some bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_botulinum" title="Clostridium botulinum">Clostridium botulinum</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_cereus" title="Bacillus cereus">Bacillus cereus</a></i> can form spores that survive boiling, which then germinate and regrow after the food has cooled. It is therefore recommended that cooked food should not be reheated more than once to avoid repeated growths that allow the bacteria to proliferate to dangerous level.</p>
<p>Cooking also increases the digestibility of some foods because many foods, such as grains, are inedible when raw, and some are poisonous. For example, <a href="/wiki/Kidney_bean" title="Kidney bean">kidney beans</a> are toxic when raw or improperly cooked due to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Phytohaemagglutinin" title="Phytohaemagglutinin">phytohaemagglutinin</a>, which can be inactivated after cooking for at least ten minutes at 100 °C (212 °F). A <a href="/wiki/Slow_cooker" title="Slow cooker">slow cooker</a> however may not reach the desired temperature and cases of poisoning from red beans cooked in a slow cooker have been reported.</p>
<p>Other considerations for food safety in cooking include the preparation, handling, and storage of food. According to the USDA, the temperature range from 40 to 140 °F (4 to 60 °C) is the "<a href="/wiki/Danger_zone_(food_safety)" title="Danger zone (food safety)">Danger zone</a>" where bacteria is likely to proliferate, and food therefore should not be stored in this temperature range. Washing of hands and surfaces, especially when handling different meats, and keeping raw food separate from cooked food to avoidance of cross-contamination are good practices in food safety. Food prepared on plastic cutting boards may be less likely to harbor bacteria than wooden ones, other research however suggested otherwise. Washing and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfection" title="Disinfection">sanitizing</a> cutting boards is highly recommended, especially after use with raw meat, poultry, or seafood. Hot water and soap followed by a rinse with a diluted antibacterial cleaner, or a trip through a dishwasher with a "sanitize" cycle, are effective methods for reducing the risk of illness due to contaminated cooking implements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_nutritional_content_of_food">Effects on nutritional content of food</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Raw_foodism" title="Raw foodism">Raw foodism</a></div>

<p>Proponents of <a href="/wiki/Raw_foodism" title="Raw foodism">raw foodism</a> argue that cooking food increases the risk of some of the detrimental effects on food or health. They point out that during cooking of vegetables and fruit containing <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>, the vitamin elutes into the cooking water and becomes degraded through oxidation. Peeling vegetables can also substantially reduce the vitamin C content, especially in the case of potatoes where most vitamin C is in the skin. However, research has shown that in the specific case of <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a> a greater proportion is absorbed from cooked vegetables than from raw vegetables.</p>
<p>German research in 2003 showed significant benefits in reducing breast cancer risk when large amounts of raw vegetable matter are included in the diet. The authors attribute some of this effect to heat-labile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytonutrient" title="Phytonutrient">phytonutrients</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sulforaphane" title="Sulforaphane">Sulforaphane</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Glucosinolate" title="Glucosinolate">glucosinolate</a> breakdown product, which may be found in vegetables such as <a href="/wiki/Broccoli" title="Broccoli">broccoli</a>, has been shown to be protective against <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a>, however, much of it is destroyed when the vegetable is boiled.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carcinogens">Carcinogens</span></h3>

<p>In a human epidemiological analysis by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Doll" title="Richard Doll">Richard Doll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Peto" title="Richard Peto">Richard Peto</a> in 1981, diet was estimated to cause a large percentage of cancers. Studies suggest that around 32% of cancer deaths may be avoidable by changes to the diet. Some of these cancers may be caused by carcinogens in food generated during the cooking process, although it is often difficult to identify the specific components in diet that serve to increase cancer risk. Many foods, such as beef steak and broccoli, contain low concentrations of both <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anticarcinogen" title="Anticarcinogen">anticarcinogens</a>.</p>
<p>Several studies published since 1990 indicate that cooking meat at high temperature creates <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic_amine" title="Heterocyclic amine">heterocyclic amines</a> (HCAs), which are thought to increase cancer risk in humans. Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/National_Cancer_Institute" title="National Cancer Institute">National Cancer Institute</a> found that human subjects who ate beef rare or medium-rare had less than one third the risk of stomach cancer than those who ate beef medium-well or well-done. While avoiding meat or eating meat raw may be the only ways to avoid HCAs in meat fully, the National Cancer Institute states that cooking meat below 212 °F (100 °C) creates "negligible amounts" of HCAs. Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwaving" title="Microwaving">microwaving</a> meat before cooking may reduce HCAs by 90% by reducing the time needed for the meat to be cooked at high heat. <a href="/wiki/Nitrosamine" title="Nitrosamine">Nitrosamines</a> are found in some food, and may be produced by some cooking processes from proteins or from nitrites used as food preservatives; cured meat such as bacon has been found to be carcinogenic, with links to colon cancer. <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">Ascorbate</a>, which is added to cured meat, however, reduces nitrosamine formation.</p>
<p>Research has shown that grilling, barbecuing and smoking meat and fish increases levels of carcinogenic <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAH). In Europe, grilled meat and smoked fish generally only contribute a small proportion of dietary PAH intake since they are a minor component of diet – most intake comes from cereals, oils and fats. However, in the US, grilled/barbecued meat is the second highest contributor of the mean daily intake of a known PAH carcinogen <a href="/wiki/Benzo(a)pyrene" title="Benzo(a)pyrene">benzo[a]pyrene</a> at 21% after ‘bread, cereal and grain’ at 29%.</p>
<p>Baking, grilling or broiling food, especially starchy foods, until a toasted crust is formed generates significant concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">acrylamide</a>, a possible <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_health_issues">Other health issues</span></h3>
<p>Cooking dairy products may reduce a protective effect against colon cancer. Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> suggest that ingesting uncooked or unpasteurized <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Raw_milk" title="Raw milk">Raw milk</a>) may reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer" title="Colorectal cancer">colorectal cancer</a>. Mice and rats fed uncooked sucrose, casein, and beef tallow had one-third to one-fifth the incidence of <a href="/wiki/Adenoma" title="Adenoma">microadenomas</a> as the mice and rats fed the same ingredients cooked. This claim, however, is contentious. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">Food and Drug Administration of the United States</a>, health benefits claimed by raw milk advocates do not exist. "The small quantities of antibodies in milk are not absorbed in the human intestinal tract," says Barbara Ingham, PhD, associate professor and extension food scientist at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. "There is no scientific evidence that raw milk contains an anti-arthritis factor or that it enhances resistance to other diseases."</p>
<p>Heating sugars with proteins or fats can produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_glycation_end_product" title="Advanced glycation end product">advanced glycation end products</a> ("glycotoxins"). These have been linked to ageing and health conditions such as diabetes and obesity.</p>
<p>Deep fried food in restaurants may contain high level of <a href="/wiki/Trans_fat" title="Trans fat">trans fat</a>, which is known to increase levels of <a href="/wiki/Low-density_lipoprotein" title="Low-density lipoprotein">low-density lipoprotein</a> that in turn may increase risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_disease" title="Coronary disease">heart diseases</a> and other conditions. However, many fast food chains have now switched to trans-fat-free alternatives for deep-frying.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_aspects">Scientific aspects</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Molecular_gastronomy" title="Molecular gastronomy">molecular gastronomy</a></div>
<p>The application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_knowledge" title="Scientific knowledge">scientific knowledge</a> to cooking and <a href="/wiki/Gastronomy" title="Gastronomy">gastronomy</a> has become known as <a href="/wiki/Molecular_gastronomy" title="Molecular gastronomy">molecular gastronomy</a>. This is a subdiscipline of <a href="/wiki/Food_science" title="Food science">food science</a>. Important contributions have been made by scientists, chefs and authors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herve_This" title="Herve This">Herve This</a> (chemist), <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Kurti" title="Nicholas Kurti">Nicholas Kurti</a> (physicist), <a href="/wiki/Peter_Barham" title="Peter Barham">Peter Barham</a> (physicist), <a href="/wiki/Harold_McGee" title="Harold McGee">Harold McGee</a> (author), <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Corriher" title="Shirley Corriher">Shirley Corriher</a> (biochemist, author), <a href="/wiki/Heston_Blumenthal" title="Heston Blumenthal">Heston Blumenthal</a> (chef), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferran_Adria" title="Ferran Adria">Ferran Adria</a> (chef), <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wolke" title="Robert Wolke">Robert Wolke</a> (chemist, author) and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gagnaire" title="Pierre Gagnaire">Pierre Gagnaire</a> (chef).</p>
<p>Chemical processes central to cooking include the <a href="/wiki/Maillard_reaction" title="Maillard reaction">Maillard reaction</a> – a form of non-enzymatic browning involving an amino acid, a reducing sugar and heat.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home-cooking_and_commercial_cooking">Home-cooking and commercial cooking</span></h2>

<p>Home cooking has traditionally been a process carried out informally in a home or around a <a href="/wiki/Communal_oven" title="Communal oven">communal fire</a>, and can be enjoyed by all members of the family, although in many cultures women bear primary responsibility. Cooking is also often carried out outside of personal quarters, for example at restaurants, or schools. <a href="/wiki/Bakery" title="Bakery">Bakeries</a> were one of the earliest forms of cooking outside the home, and bakeries in the past often offered the cooking of pots of food provided by their customers as an additional service. In the present day, factory food preparation has become common, with many "ready-to-eat" foods being prepared and cooked in factories and home cooks using a mixture of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/from_scratch" title="wikt:from scratch">scratch made</a>, and factory made foods together to make a <a href="/wiki/Meal" title="Meal">meal</a>. The nutritional value of including more commercially prepared foods has been found to be inferior to home-made foods. Home-cooked meals tend to be healthier with fewer calories, and less <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated fat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol_embolism" title="Cholesterol embolism">cholesterol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> on a per calorie basis while providing more <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">fiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. The ingredients are also directly sourced, so there is control over authenticity, taste, and nutritional value. The superior nutritional quality of home-cooking could therefore play a role in preventing <a href="/wiki/Chronic_condition" title="Chronic condition">chronic disease</a>. Cohort studies following the elderly over 10 years show that adults who cook their own meals have significantly lower mortality, even when controlling for confounding variables.</p>
<p>"Home-cooking" may be associated with <a href="/wiki/Comfort_food" title="Comfort food">comfort food</a>, and some commercially produced foods are presented through advertising or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging" title="Packaging">packaging</a> as having been "home-cooked", regardless of their actual origin.</p>
<p>Commercial cooking methods have evolved to a point where many of the ingredients and techniques used at home are being used in commercial cooking to great success and acceptance by patrons.</p>
