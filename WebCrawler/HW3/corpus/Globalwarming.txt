"<a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a>" can also refer to climate trends at any point in Earth's history. <p><b>Global warming</b> and <b>climate change</b> are terms for the observed century-scale rise in the average temperature of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate system</a> and its related effects. Multiple lines of scientific evidence show that the climate system is warming. Although the increase of near-surface atmospheric temperature is the measure of global warming often reported in the popular press, most of the additional energy stored in the climate system since 1970 has gone into the oceans. The rest has melted ice and warmed the continents and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Many of the observed changes since the 1950s are unprecedented over tens to thousands of years.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">Scientific understanding of global warming</a> is increasing. The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">reported in 2014</a> that scientists were more than 95% certain that global warming is mostly being caused by <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">human (anthropogenic) activities</a>, mainly increasing concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>). Human-made <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">continues to increase</a> above levels not seen in hundreds of thousands of years. Methane and other, often much more potent, greenhouse gasses are also rising along with CO2. Currently, about half of the carbon dioxide released from the burning of fossil fuels remains in the atmosphere. The rest is absorbed by vegetation and the oceans. <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">Climate model</a> projections summarized in the report indicated that during the 21st century the global surface temperature is likely to rise a further 0.3 to 1.7 °C (0.5 to 3.1 °F) for their lowest <a href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">emissions scenario</a> and 2.6 to 4.8 °C (4.7 to 8.6 °F) for the highest emissions scenario. These findings have been recognized by the national science academies of the major industrialized nations and are not disputed by any scientific body of national or international standing.</p>
<p>Future climate change and associated impacts will differ from <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">region to region</a> around the globe. Anticipated <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects</a> include warming global temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">rising sea levels</a>, changing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, and expansion of <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>. Warming is expected to be greater over land than over the oceans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">greatest in the Arctic</a>, with the continuing <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">retreat of glaciers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>. Other likely changes include more frequent <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events including <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a>, heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">floods</a> and heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowfall" title="Snowfall">snowfall</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_climate_change" title="Extinction risk from climate change">species extinctions</a> due to shifting temperature regimes. Effects significant to humans include the threat to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">food security</a> from decreasing crop yields and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_refugee" title="Climate refugee">abandonment of populated areas</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">rising sea levels</a>. Because the climate system has a large "<a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>" and greenhouse gasses will stay in the atmosphere for a long time, many of these effects will not only exist for decades or centuries, but will persist for tens of thousands of years.</p>
<p>Possible societal responses to global warming include <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigation</a> by emissions reduction, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation</a> to its effects, building systems <a href="/wiki/Climate_resilience" title="Climate resilience">resilient</a> to its effects, and possible future <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a>. Most countries are parties to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC), whose ultimate objective is to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">prevent dangerous anthropogenic climate change</a>. Parties to the UNFCCC have agreed that deep cuts in emissions are required and that global warming should be limited to well below 2.0 °C (3.6 °F) relative to pre-industrial levels, with efforts made to limit warming to 1.5 °C (2.7 °F).</p>
<p>Public reactions to global warming and concern about its effects are also increasing. A global 2015 <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> report showed a <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> of 54% consider it "a very serious problem". There are significant regional differences, with <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a> (whose economies are responsible for <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">the greatest annual CO2 emissions</a>) among the least concerned.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_temperature_changes">Observed temperature changes</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">Instrumental temperature record</a></div>




<p>The global average (land and ocean) surface temperature shows a warming of 0.85 [0.65 to 1.06] °C in the period 1880 to 2012, based on multiple independently produced datasets. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_average_surface_temperature" title="Global average surface temperature">Earth's average surface temperature</a> rose by <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273890000000000♠</span>0.74<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.18 °C</span> over the period 1906–2005. The rate of warming almost doubled for the last half of that period (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273279999999999♠</span>0.13<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.03 °C per decade</span>, versus <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273219999999999♠</span>0.07<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.02 °C per decade</span>).</p>
<p>The average temperature of the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> has increased between 0.13 and 0.22 °C (0.23 and 0.40 °F) per decade since 1979, according to <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">satellite temperature measurements</a>. <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">Climate proxies</a> show the temperature to have been relatively stable over the <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record_of_the_past_1000_years" title="Temperature record of the past 1000 years">one or two thousand years</a> before 1850, with regionally varying fluctuations such as the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>.</p>
<p>The warming that is evident in the instrumental temperature record is consistent with a wide range of observations, as documented by many independent scientific groups. Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, widespread melting of snow and land ice, increased <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">heat content of the oceans</a>, increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_humidity" title="Specific humidity">humidity</a>, and the earlier <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">timing</a> of spring events, e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering" title="Flowering">flowering</a> of plants. The <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> that these changes could have occurred by chance is virtually zero.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span></h3>
<p>Temperature changes vary over the globe. Since 1979, land temperatures have increased about twice as fast as ocean temperatures (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273399999999999♠</span>0.25 °C per decade</span> against <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273279999999999♠</span>0.13 °C per decade</span>). Ocean temperatures increase more slowly than land temperatures because of the larger effective <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> of the oceans and because the ocean loses more heat by evaporation. Since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">beginning of industrialisation</a> the temperature difference between the <a href="/wiki/Hemispheres_of_the_Earth" title="Hemispheres of the Earth">hemispheres</a> has increased due to melting of sea ice and snow in the North. Average arctic temperatures have been increasing at almost twice the rate of the rest of the world in the past 100 years; however arctic temperatures are also highly variable. Although more greenhouse gases are emitted in the Northern than Southern Hemisphere this does not contribute to the difference in warming because the major greenhouse gases persist long enough to mix between hemispheres.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate_inertia#Thermal_inertia" title="Climate inertia">thermal inertia</a> of the oceans and slow responses of other indirect effects mean that climate can take centuries or longer to adjust to changes in forcing. One <a href="/wiki/Climate_commitment" title="Climate commitment">climate commitment</a> study concluded that if greenhouse gases were stabilized at year 2000 levels, surface temperatures would still increase by about one-half degree Celsius, and another found that if they were stabilized at 2005 levels surface warming could exceed a whole degree Celsius. Some of this surface warming will be driven by past natural forcings which are still seeking <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget#Climate_forcings_and_global_warming" title="Earth's energy budget">equilibrium in the climate system</a>. One study using a highly simplified climate model indicates these past natural forcings may account for as much as 64% of the committed 2050 surface warming and their influence will fade with time compared to the human contribution.</p>
<p>Global temperature is subject to short-term fluctuations that overlay long-term trends and can temporarily mask them. The relative stability in surface temperature from 2002 to 2009, which has been dubbed the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_hiatus" title="Global warming hiatus">global warming hiatus</a> by the media and some scientists, is consistent with such an episode. 2015 updates to account for differing methods of measuring ocean surface temperature measurements show a positive trend over the recent decade.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warmest_years">Warmest years</span></h3>
<p>Fifteen of the top 16 warmest years have occurred since 2000. While record-breaking years can attract considerable public interest, individual years are less significant than the overall trend. Some climatologists have criticized the attention that the popular press gives to "warmest year" statistics; for example, <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Schmidt" title="Gavin Schmidt">Gavin Schmidt</a> stated "the long-term trends or the expected sequence of records are far more important than whether any single year is a record or not." Ocean oscillations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) can affect global average temperatures, causing temperatures of a given year to be abnormally warm or cold for reasons not directly related to the overall trend of climate change. For example, 1998 and 2015 temperatures were significantly enhanced by strong El Niño conditions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_causes_of_temperature_changes_.28external_forcings.29">Initial causes of temperature changes (external forcings)</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change</a></div>

<p>The climate system can warm or cool in response to changes in <i>external forcings.</i> These are "external" to the climate system but not necessarily external to Earth. Examples of external forcings include changes in atmospheric composition (e.g., increased concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>), <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> eruptions, and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">variations in Earth's orbit</a> around the Sun.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">Greenhouse effect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">Radiative forcing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Earth's energy budget</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a></div>
<p>The greenhouse effect is the process by which <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">emission</a> of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation by gases in a planet's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> warm its lower atmosphere and surface. It was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> in 1824, discovered in 1860 by <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a>, was first investigated quantitatively by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> in 1896, and was developed in the 1930s through 1960s by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Stewart_Callendar" title="Guy Stewart Callendar">Guy Stewart Callendar</a>.</p>

<p>On Earth, naturally occurring amounts of greenhouse gases cause air temperature near the surface to be about 33 °C (59 °F) warmer than it would be in their absence. Without the Earth's atmosphere, the Earth's average temperature would be well below the freezing temperature of water. The major greenhouse gases are <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapour</a>, which causes about 36–70% of the greenhouse effect; <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), which causes 9–26%; <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), which causes 4–9%; and <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (O<sub>3</sub>), which causes 3–7%. Clouds also affect the radiation balance through <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forcing" title="Cloud forcing">cloud forcings</a> similar to greenhouse gases.</p>
<p>Human activity since the Industrial Revolution has increased the amount of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, leading to increased radiative forcing from CO<sub>2</sub>, methane, <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">tropospheric ozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFCs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>. According to work published in 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Anthropogenic_greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gas">concentrations</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> and methane have increased by 36% and 148% respectively since 1750. These levels are much higher than at any time during the last 800,000 years, the period for which reliable data has been extracted from <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>. Less direct geological evidence indicates that CO<sub>2</sub> values higher than this were last seen about 20 million years ago.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">Fossil fuel</a> burning has produced about three-quarters of the increase in CO<sub>2</sub> from human activity over the past 20 years. The rest of this increase is caused mostly by changes in land-use, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_and_climate_change" title="Deforestation and climate change">particularly deforestation</a>. Another significant non-fuel source of anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions is the <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> for <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> production, a chemical process which releases CO<sub>2</sub>. Estimates of global CO<sub>2</sub> emissions in 2011 from fossil fuel combustion, including cement production and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_flaring" title="Gas flaring">gas flaring</a>, was 34.8 billion tonnes (9.5 ± 0.5 PgC), an increase of 54% above emissions in 1990. Coal burning was responsible for 43% of the total emissions, oil 34%, gas 18%, cement 4.9% and gas flaring 0.7%</p>

<p>In May 2013, it was reported that readings for CO<sub>2</sub> taken at the world's primary benchmark site in <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa</a> surpassed 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>. According to professor <a href="/wiki/Brian_Hoskins" title="Brian Hoskins">Brian Hoskins</a>, this is likely the first time CO<sub>2</sub> levels have been this high for about 4.5 million years. Monthly global CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations exceeded 400 ppm in March 2015, probably for the first time in several million years. On 12 November 2015, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA scientists</a> reported that human-made carbon dioxide <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">continues to increase</a> above levels not seen in hundreds of thousands of years: currently, about half of the carbon dioxide released from the burning of fossil fuels is not absorbed by vegetation and the oceans and remains in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Over the last three decades of the twentieth century, <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> per capita and <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> were the main drivers of increases in greenhouse gas emissions. CO<sub>2</sub> emissions are continuing to rise due to the burning of fossil fuels and land-use change. Emissions can be <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Regional_and_national_attribution_of_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">attributed to different regions</a>. Attributions of emissions due to land-use change are subject to considerable uncertainty.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario#Emissions_scenarios" title="Climate change scenario">Emissions scenarios</a>, estimates of changes in future emission levels of greenhouse gases, have been projected that depend upon uncertain economic, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technological</a>, and natural developments. In most scenarios, emissions continue to rise over the century, while in a few, emissions are reduced. Fossil fuel reserves are abundant, and will not limit carbon emissions in the 21st century. Emission scenarios, combined with modelling of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, have been used to produce estimates of how atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases might change in the future. Using the six IPCC <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a> "marker" scenarios, models suggest that by the year 2100, the atmospheric concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> could range between 541 and 970 ppm. This is 90–250% above the concentration in the year 1750.</p>
<p>The popular media and the public often confuse global warming with <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, i.e., the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">stratospheric ozone (e.g., the ozone layer)</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a>. Although there are a few <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion#Ozone_depletion_and_global_warming" title="Ozone depletion">areas of linkage</a>, the relationship between the two is not strong. Reduced stratospheric ozone has had a slight cooling influence on surface temperatures, while increased tropospheric ozone has had a somewhat larger warming effect.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerosols_and_soot">Aerosols and soot</span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">Global dimming</a>, a gradual reduction in the amount of global direct <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> at the Earth's surface, was observed from 1961 until at least 1990. <a href="/wiki/Particulates#Climate_effects" title="Particulates">Solid and liquid particles</a> known as <i>aerosols</i>, produced by volcanoes and human-made <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a>, are thought to be the main cause of this dimming. They exert a cooling effect by increasing the reflection of incoming sunlight. The effects of the products of fossil fuel combustion – CO<sub>2</sub> and aerosols – have partially offset one another in recent decades, so that net warming has been due to the increase in non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases such as methane. Radiative forcing due to aerosols is temporally limited due to the processes that remove aerosols from the atmosphere. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_deposition" title="Wet deposition">Removal by clouds and precipitation</a> gives tropospheric aerosols an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_lifetime" title="Atmospheric lifetime">atmospheric lifetime</a> of only about a week, while <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> aerosols can remain for a few years. Carbon dioxide has a lifetime of a century or more, and as such, changes in aerosols will only delay climate changes due to carbon dioxide. <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">Black carbon</a> is second only to carbon dioxide for its contribution to global warming.</p>
<p>In addition to their direct effect by scattering and absorbing solar radiation, aerosols have indirect effects on the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Earth's radiation budget</a>. Sulfate aerosols act as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a> and thus lead to clouds that have more and smaller cloud droplets. These clouds reflect solar radiation more efficiently than clouds with fewer and larger droplets, a phenomenon known as the <a href="/wiki/Twomey_effect" title="Twomey effect">Twomey effect</a>. This effect also causes droplets to be of more uniform size, which reduces <a href="/wiki/Cloud_physics#Collision-coalescence" title="Cloud physics">growth of raindrops</a> and makes the cloud more reflective to incoming sunlight, known as the <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_effect" title="Albrecht effect">Albrecht effect</a>. Indirect effects are most noticeable in marine stratiform clouds, and have very little radiative effect on convective clouds. Indirect effects of aerosols represent the largest uncertainty in radiative forcing.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">Soot</a> may either cool or warm Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate system</a>, depending on whether it is airborne or deposited. Atmospheric soot directly absorbs solar radiation, which heats the atmosphere and cools the surface. In isolated areas with high soot production, such as rural India, as much as 50% of surface warming due to greenhouse gases may be masked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_brown_cloud" title="Atmospheric brown cloud">atmospheric brown clouds</a>. When deposited, especially on glaciers or on ice in arctic regions, the lower surface <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> can also directly heat the surface. The influences of atmospheric particles, including black carbon, are most pronounced in the tropics and sub-tropics, particularly in Asia, while the effects of greenhouse gases are dominant in the extratropics and southern hemisphere.</p>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_activity">Solar activity</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_activity_and_climate" title="Solar activity and climate">Solar activity and climate</a></div>
<p>Since 1978, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar irradiance</a> has been measured by <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>. These measurements indicate that the Sun's radiative output has not increased since 1978, so the warming during the past 30 years cannot be attributed to an increase in solar energy reaching the Earth.</p>
<p>Climate models have been used to examine the role of the Sun in recent climate change. Models are unable to reproduce the rapid warming observed in recent decades when they only take into account variations in solar output and volcanic activity. Models are, however, able to simulate the observed 20th century changes in temperature when they include all of the most important external forcings, including human influences and natural forcings.</p>
<p>Another line of evidence against solar variations having caused recent climate change comes from looking at how temperatures at different levels in the Earth's atmosphere have changed. Models and observations show that greenhouse warming results in warming of the lower atmosphere (the troposphere) but cooling of the upper atmosphere (the stratosphere). Depletion of the ozone layer by chemical <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a> has also resulted in a strong cooling effect in the stratosphere. If solar variations were responsible for observed warming, warming of both the troposphere and stratosphere would be expected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_in_Earth.27s_orbit">Variations in Earth's orbit</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a></div>
<p>The tilt of the Earth’s axis and the shape of its orbit around the Sun vary slowly over tens of thousands of years and are a natural source of climate change, by changing the seasonal and latitudinal distribution of solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>.</p>
<p>During the last few thousand years, this phenomenon contributed to a slow cooling trend at high latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere during summer, a trend that was reversed by greenhouse-gas-induced warming during the 20th century.</p>
<p>Variations in orbital cycles may initiate a new <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial period</a> in the future, though the timing of this depends on greenhouse gas concentrations as well as the orbital forcing. A new glacial period is not expected within the next 50,000 years if atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration remains above 300 ppm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedback">Feedback</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">Climate change feedback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">Climate sensitivity</a></div>

<p>The climate system includes a range of <i>feedbacks</i>, which alter the response of the system to changes in external forcings. Positive feedbacks increase the response of the climate system to an initial forcing, while negative feedbacks reduce it.</p>
<p>There are a range of feedbacks in the climate system, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback" title="Water vapor feedback">water vapour</a>, changes in <a href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo</a> (snow and ice cover affect how much the Earth's surface absorbs or reflects incoming sunlight), clouds, and changes in the Earth's carbon cycle (e.g., the release of carbon from <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>). The main negative feedback is the energy the Earth's surface <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">radiates</a> into space as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a>. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a>, if the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">absolute temperature</a> (as measured in <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>) doubles, radiated energy increases by a factor of 16 (2 to the 4th power).</p>
<p>Feedbacks are an important factor in determining the sensitivity of the climate system to increased atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations. Other factors being equal, a higher <i><a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a></i> means that more warming will occur for a given increase in greenhouse gas forcing. Uncertainty over the effect of feedbacks is a major reason why different climate models project different magnitudes of warming for a given forcing scenario. More research is needed to understand the role of clouds and carbon cycle feedbacks in climate projections.</p>
<p>The IPCC projections previously mentioned span the "likely" range (greater than 66% probability, based on expert judgement) for the selected emissions scenarios. However, the IPCC's projections do not reflect the full range of uncertainty. The lower end of the "likely" range appears to be better constrained than the upper end.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_models">Climate models</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">Global climate model</a></div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate model</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_models" title="Mathematical models">representation</a> of the physical, chemical and biological processes that affect the climate system. Such models are based on scientific disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> as well as physical processes such as <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer</a>. The models may be used to predict a range of variables such as local air movement, temperature, clouds, and other atmospheric properties; ocean temperature, <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salt content</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" title="Ocean circulation">circulation</a>; ice cover on land and sea; the transfer of heat and moisture from soil and vegetation to the atmosphere; and chemical and biological processes, among others.</p>
<p>Although researchers attempt to include as many processes as possible, simplifications of the actual climate system are inevitable because of the constraints of available computer power and limitations in knowledge of the climate system. Results from models can also vary due to different greenhouse gas inputs and the model's climate sensitivity. For example, the uncertainty in IPCC's 2007 projections is caused by (1) the use of multiple models with differing sensitivity to greenhouse gas concentrations, (2) the use of differing estimates of humanity's future greenhouse gas emissions, (3) any additional emissions from climate feedbacks that were not included in the models IPCC used to prepare its report, i.e., greenhouse gas releases from permafrost.</p>
<p>The models do not assume the climate will warm due to increasing levels of greenhouse gases. Instead the models predict how greenhouse gases will interact with radiative transfer and other physical processes. Warming or cooling is thus a result, not an assumption, of the models.</p>
<p>Clouds and their effects are especially difficult to predict. Improving the models' representation of clouds is therefore an important topic in current research. Another prominent research topic is expanding and improving representations of the carbon cycle.</p>
<p>Models are also used to help investigate the <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">causes of recent climate change</a> by comparing the observed changes to those that the models project from various natural and human causes. Although these models do not unambiguously attribute the warming that occurred from approximately 1910 to 1945 to either natural variation or human effects, they do indicate that the warming since 1970 is dominated by anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions.</p>
<p>The physical realism of models is tested by examining their ability to simulate contemporary or past climates. Climate models produce a good match to observations of global temperature changes over the last century, but do not simulate all aspects of climate. Not all <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> are accurately predicted by the climate models used by the IPCC. Observed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">Arctic shrinkage</a> has been faster than that predicted. Precipitation increased proportionally to atmospheric humidity, and hence significantly faster than global climate models predict. Since 1990, sea level has also risen considerably faster than models predicted it would.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_and_expected_environmental_effects">Observed and expected environmental effects</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a></div>

<p>Anthropogenic forcing has likely contributed to some of the observed changes, including sea level rise, changes in climate <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extremes</a> (such as the number of warm and cold days), <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">declines in Arctic sea ice</a> extent, <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glacier retreat</a>, and greening of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>.</p>
<p>During the 21st century, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and snow cover are projected to continue their widespread retreat. Projections of declines in Arctic sea ice vary. Recent projections suggest that Arctic summers could be ice-free (defined as ice extent less than 1 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_km" title="Square km">square km</a>) as early as 2025-2030.</p>
<p>"Detection" is the process of demonstrating that climate has changed in some defined <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical</a> sense, without providing a reason for that change. Detection does not imply attribution of the detected change to a particular cause. "Attribution" of causes of climate change is the process of establishing the most likely causes for the detected change with some defined level of confidence. Detection and attribution may also be applied to observed changes in physical, ecological and social systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather">Extreme weather</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_impacts_of_climate_change#Extreme_events" title="Physical impacts of climate change">Physical impacts of climate change § Extreme events</a></div>
<p>Changes in regional climate are expected to include greater warming over land, with most warming at high northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>, and least warming over the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and parts of the North Atlantic Ocean.</p>
<p>Future changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are expected to follow existing trends, with reduced precipitation over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> land areas, and increased precipitation at subpolar latitudes and some <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> regions. Projections suggest a probable increase in the frequency and severity of some extreme weather events, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>.</p>
<p>A 2015 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Climate_Change" title="Nature Climate Change">Nature Climate Change</a></i>, states:</p>

<p>Data analysis of extreme events from 1960 till 2010 suggests that droughts and heat waves appear simultaneously with increased frequency. Extremely wet or dry events within the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> period have increased since 1980.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_rise">Sea level rise</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">Sea level rise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a></div>

<p>The sea level rise since 1993 has been estimated to have been on average 2.6 mm and 2.9 mm per year ± 0.4 mm. Additionally, sea level rise has accelerated from 1995 to 2015. Over the 21st century, the IPCC projects for a high emissions scenario, that global mean <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level</a> could rise by 52–98 cm. The IPCC's projections are conservative, and may underestimate future sea level rise. Other estimates suggest that for the same period, global mean sea level could rise by 0.2 to 2.0 m (0.7–6.6 ft), relative to mean sea level in 1992.</p>
<p>Widespread <a href="/wiki/Coastal_flood" title="Coastal flood">coastal flooding</a> would be expected if several degrees of warming is sustained for millennia. For example, sustained global warming of more than 2 °C (relative to pre-industrial levels) could lead to eventual sea level rise of around 1 to 4 m due to thermal expansion of sea water and the melting of glaciers and small <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>. Melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a> could contribute an additional 4 to 7.5 m over many thousands of years. It has been estimated that we are already committed to a sea-level rise of approximately 2.3 metres for each degree of temperature rise within the next 2,000 years.</p>
<p>Warming beyond the 2 °C target would potentially lead to rates of sea-level rise dominated by ice loss from <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Continued CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from fossil sources could cause additional tens of metres of sea level rise, over the next millennia and eventually ultimately eliminate the entire Antarctic ice sheet, causing about 58 metres of sea level rise.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_systems">Ecological systems</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems" title="Climate change and ecosystems">Climate change and ecosystems</a></div>
<p>In terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, the earlier timing of spring events, as well as poleward and upward shifts in plant and animal ranges, have been linked with high confidence to recent warming. Future climate change is expected to affect particular ecosystems, including <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>. It is expected that most ecosystems will be affected by higher atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> levels, combined with higher global temperatures. Overall, it is expected that climate change will result in the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of many species and reduced diversity of ecosystems.</p>
<p>Increases in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations have led to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidity</a>. Dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> increases ocean acidity, measured by lower <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> values. Between 1750 and 2000, surface-ocean pH has decreased by ≈0.1, from ≈8.2 to ≈8.1. Surface-ocean pH has probably not been below ≈8.1 during the past 2 million years. Projections suggest that surface-ocean pH could decrease by an additional 0.3–0.4 units by 2100. Future ocean acidification could threaten <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_with_coral_reefs" title="Environmental issues with coral reefs">coral reefs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_species" title="Protected species">protected species</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> of value to society.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_deoxygenation" title="Ocean deoxygenation">Ocean deoxygenation</a> is projected to increase <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxia</a> by 10%, and triple suboxic waters (oxygen concentrations 98% less than the mean surface concentrations), for each 1 °C of upper ocean warming.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_effects">Long-term effects</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_global_warming" title="Long-term effects of global warming">Long-term effects of global warming</a></div>
<p>On the timescale of centuries to millennia, the magnitude of global warming will be determined primarily by anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions. This is due to carbon dioxide's very long lifetime in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Stabilizing the global average temperature would require large reductions in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, as well as reductions in emissions of other greenhouse gases such as methane and nitrous oxide. Emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> would need to be reduced by more than 80% relative to their peak level. Even if this were achieved, global average temperatures would remain close to their highest level for many centuries.</p>
<p>Long-term effects also include a response from the Earth's crust, due to ice melting and deglaciation, in a process called <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a>, when land masses are no longer depressed by the weight of ice. This could lead to <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> and increased seismic and volcanic activities. Tsunamis could be generated by submarine landslides caused by warmer ocean water thawing ocean-floor permafrost or releasing <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">gas hydrates</a>. Some world regions, such as the French Alps, already show signs of an increase in landslide frequency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Large-scale_and_abrupt_impacts">Large-scale and abrupt impacts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">Abrupt climate change</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cold_blob_(North_Atlantic)" title="Cold blob (North Atlantic)">Cold blob (North Atlantic)</a></div>
<p>Climate change could result in global, large-scale changes in <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social systems</a>. Examples include the possibility for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation" title="Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> to slow- or shutdown, which in the instance of a shutdown would change weather in Europe and North America considerably, ocean acidification caused by increased atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, and the long-term melting of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, which contributes to sea level rise.</p>
<p>Some large-scale changes could occur <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abruptly</a>, i.e., over a short time period, and might also be <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Irreversibilities" title="Effects of global warming">irreversible</a>. Examples of abrupt climate change are the rapid release of methane and carbon dioxide from <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, which would lead to amplified global warming, or the <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a>. Scientific understanding of abrupt climate change is generally poor. The probability of abrupt change for some climate related feedbacks may be low. Factors that may increase the probability of abrupt climate change include higher magnitudes of global warming, warming that occurs more rapidly, and warming that is sustained over longer time periods.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_and_expected_effects_on_social_systems">Observed and expected effects on social systems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Social_systems" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming § Social systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming#Regional_impacts" title="Regional effects of global warming">Regional effects of global warming § Regional impacts</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_national_security" title="Climate change and national security">Climate change and national security</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans" title="Effects of climate change on humans">effects of climate change on human systems</a>, mostly due to warming or shifts in <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> patterns, or both, have been detected worldwide. Production of wheat and maize globally has been impacted by climate change. While crop production has increased in some mid-latitude regions such as the UK and Northeast China, economic losses due to extreme weather events have increased globally. There has been a shift from cold- to heat-related mortality in some regions as a result of warming. Livelihoods of <a href="/wiki/Circumpolar_peoples" title="Circumpolar peoples">indigenous peoples of the Arctic</a> have been altered by climate change, and there is emerging evidence of climate change impacts on livelihoods of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> in other regions. Regional impacts of climate change are now observable at more locations than before, on all continents and across ocean regions.</p>
<p>The future social impacts of climate change will be uneven. Many risks are expected to increase with higher magnitudes of global warming. All regions are at risk of experiencing negative impacts. Low-latitude, less developed areas face the greatest risk. A study from 2015 concluded that economic growth (gross domestic product) of poorer countries is much more impaired with projected future climate warming, than previously thought.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">meta-analysis</a> of 56 studies concluded in 2014 that each degree of temperature rise will increase violence by up to 20%, which includes fist fights, violent crimes, civil unrest or wars.</p>
<p>Examples of impacts include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Food</a></i>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_productivity" title="Crop productivity">Crop production</a> will probably be negatively affected in low latitude countries, while effects at northern latitudes may be positive or negative. Global warming of around 4.6 °C relative to pre-industrial levels could pose a large risk to global and regional food security.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">Health</a></i>: Generally impacts will be more negative than positive. Impacts include: the effects of extreme weather, leading to injury and loss of life; and indirect effects, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undernutrition" title="Undernutrition">undernutrition</a> brought on by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_failure" title="Crop failure">crop failures</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_inundation">Habitat inundation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans#Displacement.2Fmigration" title="Effects of climate change on humans">Effects of climate change on humans § Displacement/migration</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_refugee" title="Climate refugee">Climate refugee</a></div>
<p>In small islands and <a href="/wiki/Mega_delta" title="Mega delta">mega deltas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">inundation</a> as a result of sea level rise is expected to threaten vital infrastructure and human settlements. This could lead to issues of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_refugee" title="Climate refugee">homelessness</a> in countries with low-lying areas such as <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Statelessness" title="Statelessness">statelessness</a> for populations in countries such as the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming" title="Economics of global warming">Economics of global warming</a></div>
<p>Estimates based on the IPCC A1B emission scenario from additional CO<sub>2</sub> and CH<sub>4</sub> greenhouse gases released from permafrost, estimate associated impact damages by US$43 trillion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span></h3>
<p>Continued permafrost degradation will likely result in unstable infrastructure in Arctic regions, or Alaska before 2100. Thus, impacting roads, pipelines and buildings, as well as water distribution, and cause <a href="/wiki/Slope_stability" title="Slope stability">slope failures</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_responses_to_global_warming">Possible responses to global warming</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_action" title="Climate action">Climate action</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">Climate change mitigation</a></div>

<p>Mitigation of climate change are actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, or enhance the capacity of carbon sinks to absorb GHGs from the atmosphere. There is a large potential for future reductions in emissions by a combination of activities, including: <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> and increased <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>; the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-carbon_energy" title="Low-carbon energy">low-carbon energy</a> technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>; and enhancing carbon sinks through, for example, <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a> and preventing <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. A 2015 report by <a href="/wiki/Citibank" title="Citibank">Citibank</a> concluded that transitioning to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon_economy" title="Low carbon economy">low carbon economy</a> would yield positive return on investments.</p>
<p>Near- and long-term trends in the global energy system are inconsistent with limiting global warming at below 1.5 or 2 °C, relative to pre-industrial levels. Pledges made as part of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">Cancún agreements</a> are broadly consistent with having a likely chance (66 to 100% probability) of limiting global warming (in the 21st century) at below 3 °C, relative to pre-industrial levels.</p>
<p>In limiting warming at below 2 °C, more stringent emission reductions in the near-term would allow for less rapid reductions after 2030. Many integrated models are unable to meet the 2 °C target if pessimistic assumptions are made about the availability of mitigation technologies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">Adaptation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">Adaptation to global warming</a></div>
<p>Other policy responses include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation</a> to climate change. Adaptation to climate change may be planned, either in reaction to or anticipation of climate change, or spontaneous, i.e., without government intervention. Planned adaptation is already occurring on a limited basis. The barriers, limits, and costs of future adaptation are not fully understood.</p>
<p>A concept related to adaptation is <i><a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a></i>, which is the ability of a system (human, natural or managed) to adjust to climate change (including climate variability and extremes) to moderate potential damages, to take advantage of opportunities, or to cope with consequences. Unmitigated climate change (i.e., future climate change without efforts to limit greenhouse gas emissions) would, in the long term, be likely to exceed the capacity of natural, managed and human systems to adapt.</p>
<p>Environmental organizations and public figures have emphasized changes in the climate and the risks they entail, while promoting adaptation to changes in infrastructural needs and emissions reductions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_engineering">Climate engineering</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Climate engineering</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Climate engineering</a> (sometimes called <i>geoengineering</i> or <i>climate intervention</i>) is the deliberate modification of the climate. It has been investigated as a possible response to global warming, e.g. by NASA and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>. Techniques under research fall generally into the categories <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">solar radiation management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a>, although various other schemes have been suggested. A study from 2014 investigated the most common climate engineering methods and concluded they are either ineffective or have potentially severe side effects and cannot be stopped without causing rapid climate change.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discourse_about_global_warming">Discourse about global warming</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_discussion">Political discussion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">Politics of global warming</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2011</a>, <a href="/wiki/2012_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2012 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2012</a>, <a href="/wiki/2013_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2013 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2013</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015</a> sessions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="United Nations Climate Change Conference">United Nations Climate Change Conference</a></div>

<p>Most countries in the world are parties to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC). The ultimate objective of the Convention is to prevent dangerous human interference of the climate system. As stated in the Convention, this requires that GHG concentrations are stabilized in the atmosphere at a level where <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems" title="Climate change and ecosystems">ecosystems</a> can adapt naturally to climate change, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">food production</a> is not threatened, and <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming" title="Economics of global warming">economic development</a> can proceed in a sustainable fashion. The Framework Convention was agreed in 1992, but since then, global emissions have risen.</p>
<p>During negotiations, the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_77" title="Group of 77">G77</a> (a lobbying group in the United Nations representing 133 developing nations) pushed for a mandate requiring developed countries to "[take] the lead" in reducing their emissions. This was justified on the basis that: the developed world's emissions had contributed most to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Cumulative_and_historical_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">cumulation</a> of GHGs in the atmosphere; <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Annual_and_per_capita_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">per-capita emissions</a> (i.e., emissions per head of population) were still relatively low in developing countries; and the emissions of developing countries would grow to meet their development needs.</p>
<p>This mandate was sustained in the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> to the Framework Convention, which entered into legal effect in 2005. In ratifying the Kyoto Protocol, most developed countries accepted legally binding commitments to limit their emissions. These first-round commitments expired in 2012. United States President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> rejected the treaty on the basis that "it exempts 80% of the world, including major population centres such as China and India, from compliance, and would cause serious harm to the US economy."</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2009_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2009 United Nations Climate Change Conference">15th UNFCCC Conference of the Parties</a>, held in 2009 at <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, several UNFCCC Parties produced the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Accord" title="Copenhagen Accord">Copenhagen Accord</a>. Parties associated with the Accord (140 countries, as of November 2010) aim to limit the future increase in global mean temperature to below <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002275149999999999♠</span>2 °C</span>. The 16th Conference of the Parties (COP16) was held at <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a> in 2010. It produced an agreement, not a binding treaty, that the Parties should take urgent action to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to meet a goal of limiting global warming to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002275149999999999♠</span>2 °C</span> above pre-industrial temperatures. It also recognized the need to consider strengthening the goal to a global average rise of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002274649999999999♠</span>1.5 °C</span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_discussion">Scientific discussion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">Scientific opinion on climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surveys_of_scientists%27_views_on_climate_change" title="Surveys of scientists' views on climate change">Surveys of scientists' views on climate change</a></div>
<p>There is continuing discussion through published peer-reviewed scientific papers, which are assessed by scientists working in the relevant fields taking part in the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> as of 2013 stated in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> is that it "is extremely likely that human influence has been the dominant cause of the observed warming since the mid-20th century". A 2008 report by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> stated that most scientists by then agreed that observed warming in recent decades was primarily caused by human activities increasing the amount of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> in the atmosphere. In 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> stated that while the overwhelming majority of scientists were in agreement on the main points, some individuals and organizations opposed to the consensus on urgent action needed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions have tried to undermine the science and work of the IPCC. National science academies have called on world leaders for policies to cut global emissions.</p>
<p>In the scientific literature, there is a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">strong consensus that global surface temperatures have increased</a> in recent decades and that the trend is caused mainly by human-induced emissions of greenhouse gases. No scientific body of national or international standing <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change#Opposing" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">disagrees with this view</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discussion_by_the_public_and_in_popular_media">Discussion by the public and in popular media</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">Climate change denial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">Global warming controversy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Media_coverage_of_climate_change" title="Media coverage of climate change">Media coverage of climate change</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">global warming controversy</a> refers to a variety of disputes, substantially more pronounced in the <a href="/wiki/Media_coverage_of_climate_change" title="Media coverage of climate change">popular media</a> than in the scientific literature, regarding the nature, causes, and consequences of global warming. The disputed issues include the causes of increased <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">global average air temperature</a>, especially since the mid-20th century, whether this warming trend is unprecedented or within normal climatic variations, whether humankind has contributed significantly to it, and whether the increase is completely or partially an artefact of poor measurements. Additional disputes concern estimates of climate sensitivity, predictions of additional warming, and what the consequences of global warming will be.</p>
<p>From 1990 to 1997, <a href="/wiki/Left%E2%80%93right_politics#Contemporary_usage_in_the_United_States" title="Left–right politics">right-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> think tanks in the United States mobilized to challenge the legitimacy of global warming as a social problem. They <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">challenged the scientific evidence</a>, argued that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benefits_of_global_warming" title="Benefits of global warming">global warming will have benefits</a>, and asserted that proposed solutions would do more harm than good. Some people dispute aspects of climate change science. Organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Competitive_Enterprise_Institute" title="Competitive Enterprise Institute">Competitive Enterprise Institute</a>, conservative commentators, and some companies such as <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> have challenged IPCC climate change scenarios, funded scientists who disagree with the scientific consensus, and provided their own projections of the economic cost of stricter controls. On the other hand, some fossil fuel companies have scaled back their efforts in recent years, or even called for policies to reduce global warming. Global oil companies have begun to acknowledge climate change exists and is caused by human activities and the burning of fossil fuels.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveys_of_public_opinion">Surveys of public opinion</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Public opinion on climate change">Public opinion on climate change</a></div>
<p>The world public, or at least people in economically advanced regions, became broadly aware of the global warming problem in the late 1980s. Polling groups began to track opinions on the subject, at first mainly in the United States. The longest consistent polling, by <a href="/wiki/Gallup_(company)" title="Gallup (company)">Gallup</a> in the US, found relatively small deviations of 10% or so from 1998 to 2015 in opinion on the seriousness of global warming, but with increasing polarization between those concerned and those unconcerned.</p>
<p>The first major worldwide poll, conducted by Gallup in 2008-2009 in 127 countries, found that some 62% of people worldwide said they knew about global warming. In the advanced countries of North America, Europe and Japan, 90% or more knew about it (97% in the U.S., 99% in Japan); in less developed countries, especially in Africa, fewer than a quarter knew about it, although many had noticed local weather changes. Among those who knew about global warming, there was a wide variation between nations in belief that the warming was a result of human activities.</p>
<p>By 2010, with 111 countries surveyed, Gallup determined that there was a substantial decrease since 2007–08 in the number of Americans and Europeans who viewed global warming as a serious threat. In the US, just a little over half the population (53%) now viewed it as a serious concern for either themselves or their families; this was 10 points below the 2008 poll (63%). Latin America had the biggest rise in concern: 73% said global warming is a serious threat to their families. This global poll also found that people are more likely to attribute global warming to human activities than to natural causes, except in the US where nearly half (47%) of the population attributed global warming to natural causes.</p>
<p>A March–May 2013 survey by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center_for_the_People_%26_the_Press" title="Pew Research Center for the People &amp; the Press">Pew Research Center for the People &amp; the Press</a> polled 39 countries about global threats. According to 54% of those questioned, global warming featured top of the perceived global threats. In a January 2013 survey, Pew found that 69% of Americans say there is solid evidence that the Earth's average temperature has got warmer over the past few decades, up six points since November 2011 and 12 points since 2009.</p>
<p>A 2010 survey of 14 industrialized countries found that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_skeptics" title="Climate change skeptics">skepticism</a> about the danger of global warming was highest in Australia, Norway, New Zealand and the United States, in that order, correlating positively with <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">per capita emissions of carbon dioxide</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>In the 1950s, research suggested increasing temperatures, and a 1952 newspaper reported "climate change". This phrase next appeared in a November 1957 report in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_of_Northwest_Indiana" title="The Times of Northwest Indiana">The Hammond Times</a></i> which described <a href="/wiki/Roger_Revelle" title="Roger Revelle">Roger Revelle</a>'s research into the effects of increasing human-caused CO<sub>2</sub> emissions on the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>, "a large scale global warming, with radical climate changes may result". Both phrases were only used occasionally until 1975, when <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Smith_Broecker" title="Wallace Smith Broecker">Wallace Smith Broecker</a> published a scientific paper on the topic; "Climatic Change: Are We on the Brink of a Pronounced Global Warming?" The phrase began to come into common use, and in 1976 <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Budyko" title="Mikhail Budyko">Mikhail Budyko</a>'s statement that "a global warming up has started" was widely reported. Other studies, such as a 1971 <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> report, referred to the human impact as "inadvertent climate modification", but an influential 1979 <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> study headed by <a href="/wiki/Jule_Gregory_Charney" title="Jule Gregory Charney">Jule Charney</a> followed Broecker in using <i>global warming</i> for rising surface temperatures, while describing the wider effects of increased CO<sub>2</sub> as <i>climate change</i>.</p>
<p>In 1986 and November 1987, NASA climate scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> gave testimony to Congress on global warming. There were increasing heatwaves and drought problems in the summer of 1988, and when Hansen testified in the Senate on 23 June he sparked worldwide interest. He said: "global warming has reached a level such that we can ascribe with a high degree of confidence a cause and effect relationship between the greenhouse effect and the observed warming." Public attention increased over the summer, and <i>global warming</i> became the dominant popular term, commonly used both by the press and in public discourse.</p>
<p>In a 2008 NASA article on usage, <a href="/wiki/Erik_M._Conway" title="Erik M. Conway">Erik M. Conway</a> defined <i>Global warming</i> as "the increase in Earth’s average surface temperature due to rising levels of greenhouse gases", while <i>Climate change</i> was "a long-term change in the Earth’s climate, or of a region on Earth." As effects such as changing patterns of rainfall and rising sea levels would probably have more impact than temperatures alone, he considered <i>global climate change</i> a more scientifically accurate term, and like the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, the NASA website would emphasize this wider context.</p>
