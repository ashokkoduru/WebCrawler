<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Economy_(disambiguation)" title="Economy (disambiguation)">Economy (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p>An <b>economy</b> (From Greek οίκος – "household" and νęμoμαι – "manage") is an area of the <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goods_(economics)" title="Goods (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> by different agents in a given geographical location. Understood in its broadest sense, 'The economic is defined as a social domain that emphasizes the practices, discourses, and material expressions associated with the production, use and management of resources'. Economic agents can be individuals, businesses, organizations, or governments. Economic transactions occur when two parties agree to the value or price of the transacted good or service, commonly expressed in a certain <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>, but monetary transactions are only a small part of the economic domain.</p>
<p>Economic activity is spurred by production which uses natural resources, labor, and capital. It has changed over time due to <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> (automation, accelerator of process, reduction of cost functions), <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> (new products, services, processes, new markets, expands markets, diversification of markets, niche markets, increases revenue functions) such as that which produces intellectual property and changes in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_relations" title="Industrial relations">industrial relations</a> (for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">child labor</a> being replaced in some parts of the world with <a href="/wiki/Universal_access_to_education" title="Universal access to education">universal access to education</a>).</p>
<p>A given economy is the result of a set of processes that involves its culture, values, education, technological evolution, history, social organization, political structure and legal systems, as well as its geography, natural resource endowment, and ecology, as main factors. These factors give context, content, and set the conditions and parameters in which an economy functions. In other words, the economic domain is a social domain of human practices and transactions. It does not stand alone.</p>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market-based_economy" title="Market-based economy">market-based economy</a> is where <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> are produced and exchanged according to <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supply_(economics)" title="Supply (economics)">supply</a> between participants (economic agents) by <a href="/wiki/Barter" title="Barter">barter</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debit" title="Debit">debit</a> <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> accepted within the network, such as a unit of currency.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">command-based economy</a> is where political agents directly control what is produced and how it is sold and distributed.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-carbon" title="Low-carbon">low-carbon</a>, resource efficient, and socially inclusive. In a green economy, growth in income and employment are driven by public and private investments that reduce carbon emissions and pollution, enhance energy and resource efficiency, and prevent the loss of biodiversity and ecosystem services.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span></h2>
<p>Today the range of fields of study examining the economy revolve around the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, but may include <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Economic_sociology" title="Economic sociology">economic sociology</a>), <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> (<a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">economic history</a>), <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Economic_anthropology" title="Economic anthropology">economic anthropology</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> (<a href="/wiki/Economic_geography" title="Economic geography">economic geography</a>). Practical fields directly related to the human activities involving <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">exchange</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> as a whole, are <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>.</p>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">professions</a>, occupations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_agent" title="Economic agent">economic agents</a> or economic activities, contribute to the economy. <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">Consumption</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saving_(money)" title="Saving (money)">saving</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> are variable components in the economy that determine macroeconomic equilibrium. There are three main sectors of economic activity: <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary</a>.</p>
<p>Due to the growing importance of the financial sector in modern times, the term <i>real economy</i> is used by analysts as well as politicians to denote the part of the economy that is concerned with the actual production of goods and services, as ostensibly contrasted with the paper economy, or the financial side of the economy, which is concerned with buying and selling on the financial markets. Alternate and long-standing terminology distinguishes measures of an economy expressed in <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">real</a> values (adjusted for <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>), such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_GDP" title="Real GDP">real GDP</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">nominal</a> values (unadjusted for inflation).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> words "economy" and "<a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>" can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">οἰκονόμος</span> (i.e. "household management"), a composite word derived from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">οἶκος</span> ("house;household;home") and νέμω ("manage; distribute;to deal out;dispense") by way of <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">οἰκονομία</span> ("household management").</p>
<p>The first recorded sense of the word "economy" is in the phrase "the management of œconomic affairs", found in a work possibly composed in a monastery in 1440. "Economy" is later recorded in more general senses, including "thrift" and "administration".</p>
<p>The most frequently used current sense, denoting "the economic system of a country or an area", seems not to have developed until the 19th or 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_times">Ancient times</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Palace_economy" title="Palace economy">Palace economy</a></div>
<p>As long as someone has been making, supplying and distributing goods or services, there has been some sort of economy; economies grew larger as societies grew and became more complex. <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> developed a large-scale economy based on <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a>, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">Babylonians</a> and their neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_states" title="City states">city states</a> later developed the earliest system of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> as we think of, in terms of rules/laws on <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>, legal contracts and law codes relating to business practices, and private property.</p>
<p>The Babylonians and their city state neighbors developed forms of economics comparable to currently used civil society (law) concepts. They developed the first known codified legal and administrative systems, complete with courts, jails, and government records.</p>
<p>The ancient economy was mainly based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence_farming" title="Subsistence farming">subsistence farming</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Shekel" title="Shekel">Shekel</a> referred to an ancient unit of weight and currency. The first usage of the term came from <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> circa 3000 BC., and referred to a specific mass of <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> which related other values in a <a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a> such as silver, bronze, copper etc. A barley/shekel was originally both a unit of <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> and a unit of weight, just as the British Pound was originally a unit denominating a one-pound mass of silver.</p>
<p>For most people the exchange of goods occurred through social relationships. There were also traders who bartered in the marketplaces. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>, where the present English word 'economy' originated, many people were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_slaves" title="Bond slaves">bond slaves</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeholder_(government)" title="Freeholder (government)">freeholders</a>. Economic discussion was driven by <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">scarcity</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_ages">Middle ages</span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> times, what we now call economy was not far from the subsistence level. Most exchange occurred within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_groups" title="Social groups">social groups</a>. On top of this, the great conquerors raised <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> (from <i>ventura</i>, ital.; <i>risk</i>) to finance their captures. The <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> should be refunded by the goods they would bring up in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. Merchants such as <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Fugger" title="Jakob Fugger">Jakob Fugger</a> (1459–1525) and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Bicci_de%27_Medici" title="Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici">Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici</a> (1360–1428) founded the first <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a>. The discoveries of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> (1254–1324), <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> (1451–1506) and <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> (1469–1524) led to a first <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">global</a> economy. The first <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">enterprises</a> were trading establishments. In 1513, the first <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a> was founded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antwerpen" title="Antwerpen">Antwerpen</a>. Economy at the time meant primarily <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_times">Early modern times</span></h3>
<p>The European captures became branches of the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> states, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonies" title="Colonies">colonies</a>. The rising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nation-state" title="Nation-state">nation-states</a> <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> tried to control the trade through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Custom_duties" title="Custom duties">custom duties</a> and ]] (from <i>mercator</i>, lat.: <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a>) was a first approach to intermediate between private wealth and <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a> in Europe allowed states to use the immense property of the church for the development of towns. The influence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobles" title="Nobles">nobles</a> decreased. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretaries_of_State" title="Secretaries of State">Secretaries of State</a> for economy started their work. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankers" title="Bankers">Bankers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Amschel_Mayer_Rothschild" title="Amschel Mayer Rothschild">Amschel Mayer Rothschild</a> (1773–1855) started to finance national projects such as wars and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. Economy from then on meant national economy as a topic for the economic activities of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens" title="Citizens">citizens</a> of a state.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Industrial_Revolution">The Industrial Revolution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a></div>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economist</a> in the true modern meaning of the word was the Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> (1723–1790) who was inspired partly by the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Physiocracy" title="Physiocracy">physiocracy</a>, a reaction to mercantilism and also later Economics student, Adam Mari. He defined the elements of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_economy" title="National economy">national economy</a>: <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> are offered at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_price" title="Natural price">natural price</a> generated by the use of <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competition</a> - <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">supply and demand</a> - and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_of_labor" title="Division of labor">division of labor</a>. He maintained that the basic motive for <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> is human self-interest. The so-called self-interest hypothesis became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropological" title="Anthropological">anthropological</a> basis for economics. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> (1766–1834) transferred the idea of supply and demand to the problem of <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> was a period from the 18th to the 19th century where major changes in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> had a profound effect on the socioeconomic and cultural conditions starting in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, then subsequently spreading throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, and eventually the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a>. The onset of the Industrial Revolution marked a major turning point in human history; almost every aspect of daily life was eventually influenced in some way. In Europe wild <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> started to replace the system of <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a> (today: <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>) and led to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>. The period today is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> because the system of <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">Production</a>, production and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_of_labor" title="Division of labor">division of labor</a> enabled the <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a> of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_World_War_II">After World War II</span></h3>
<p>After the chaos of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War" title="World War">World Wars</a> and the devastating <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, policymakers searched for new ways of controlling the course of the economy. This was explored and discussed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_von_Hayek" title="Friedrich August von Hayek">Friedrich August von Hayek</a> (1899–1992) and <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a> (1912–2006) who pleaded for a global <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> and are supposed to be the fathers of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>. However, the prevailing view was that held by <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> (1883–1946), who argued for a stronger control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markets" title="Markets">markets</a> by the state. The theory that the state can alleviate economic problems and instigate economic growth through state manipulation of aggregate demand is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keynesianism" title="Keynesianism">Keynesianism</a> in his honor. In the late 1950s, the economic growth in America and Europe—often called <a href="/wiki/Wirtschaftswunder" title="Wirtschaftswunder">Wirtschaftswunder</a> (ger: <i>economic miracle</i>) —brought up a new form of economy: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_consumption_economy" title="Mass consumption economy">mass consumption economy</a>. In 1958, <a href="/wiki/John_Kenneth_Galbraith" title="John Kenneth Galbraith">John Kenneth Galbraith</a> (1908–2006) was the first to speak of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affluent_society" title="Affluent society">affluent society</a>. In most of the countries the economic system is called a <a href="/wiki/Social_market_economy" title="Social market economy">social market economy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th_.E2.80.93_beginning_of_21st_century">Late 20th – beginning of 21st century</span></h3>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the fall of the Iron Curtain</a> and the transition of the countries of the Eastern Block towards democratic government and market economies, the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a> is brought into importance as its role is to mark together the significance that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_sector" title="Service sector">service sector</a> receives at the place of the industrialization, as well the first usage of this term, some relate it to Daniel Bell's 1973 book, <i>The Coming of Post-Industrial Society</i>, while other - to social philosopher Ivan Illich's book, <i>Tools for Conviviality</i>. The term is also applied in philosophy to designate the fading of <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a> in the late 90s and especially in the beginning of the 21st century. But, the term came to common usage to describe the growth of economies like the Chinese at the period (the term is specifically used by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in a speech about Republic of China in 1998).</p>
<p>With the spread of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> as a mass media and communication medium especially after 2000-2001, the idea for the Internet and information economy is given place because of the growing importance of ecommerce and electronic businesses, also the term for a global information society as understanding of a new type of "all-connected" society is created. In the late 00s, the new type of economies and economic expansions of countries like China, Brazil, and India bring attention and interest to different from the usually dominating Western type economies and economic models.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_phases_of_precedence">Economic phases of precedence</span></h2>
<p>The economy may be considered as having developed through the following Phases or Degrees of Precedence.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient economy</a> was mainly based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence">subsistence</a> farming.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> phase lessened the role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence">subsistence</a> farming, converting it to more <a href="/wiki/Extensive_farming" title="Extensive farming">extensive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">mono-cultural</a> forms of agriculture in the last three centuries. The economic growth took place mostly in mining, construction and manufacturing industries. <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">Commerce</a> became more significant due to the need for improved exchange and distribution of produce throughout the community.</li>
<li>In the economies of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_society" title="Consumer society">consumer societies</a> phase there is a growing part played by <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">knowledge economy</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In modern economies, these phase precedences are somewhat differently expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Three-sector_theory" title="Three-sector theory">three-sector theory</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">Primary stage/degree of the economy</a>:</b> Involves the extraction and production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_materials" title="Raw materials">raw materials</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. (A coal miner and a fisherman would be workers in the primary degree.)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">Secondary stage/degree of the economy</a>:</b> Involves the transformation of raw or intermediate materials into goods e.g. manufacturing steel into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cars" title="Cars">cars</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> into clothing. (A builder and a dressmaker would be workers in the secondary degree.) At this stage the associated industrial economy is also sub-divided into several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_sectors" title="Economic sectors">economic sectors</a> (also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industries" title="Industries">industries</a>). Their separate evolution during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> phase is dealt with elsewhere.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">Tertiary stage/degree of the economy</a>:</b> Involves the provision of services to consumers and businesses, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby-sitting" title="Baby-sitting">baby-sitting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">cinema</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banking" title="Banking">banking</a>. (A shopkeeper and an accountant would be workers in the tertiary degree.)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Quaternary sector of the economy">Quaternary stage/degree of the economy</a>:</b> Involves the research and development needed to produce products from natural resources and their subsequent by-products. (A logging company might <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> ways to use partially burnt wood to be processed so that the undamaged portions of it can be made into pulp for paper.) Note that <b>education</b> is sometimes included in this sector.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other sectors of the developed community include :</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Sector" title="Public Sector">Public Sector</a> or state sector (which usually includes: parliament, law-courts and government centers, various emergency services, public health, shelters for impoverished and threatened people, transport facilities, air/sea ports, post-natal care, hospitals, schools, libraries, museums, preserved historical buildings, parks/gardens, nature-reserves, some universities, national sports grounds/stadiums, national arts/concert-halls or theaters and centers for various religions).</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_Sector" title="Private Sector">Private Sector</a> or privately run businesses.</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sector" title="Social sector">Social sector</a> or <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_sector" title="Voluntary sector">Voluntary sector</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_measures">Economic measures</span></h2>
<p>There are a number of ways to measure economic activity of a nation. These methods of measuring economic activity include:</p>
<ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GDP">GDP</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> - Gross domestic product of a country is a measure of the size of its economy. The most conventional economic analysis of a country relies heavily on economic indicators like the GDP and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a>. While often useful, it should be noted that GDP only includes economic activity for which money is exchanged.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Informal_economy">Informal economy</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informal_economy" title="Informal economy">Informal economy</a></div>
<p>An informal economy is economic activity that is neither taxed nor monitored by a government, contrasted with a formal economy. The informal economy is thus not included in that government's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_National_Product" title="Gross National Product">Gross National Product</a> (GNP). Although the informal economy is often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>, all economic systems contain an informal economy in some proportion.</p>
<p>Informal economic activity is a dynamic process which includes many aspects of economic and social theory including exchange, regulation, and enforcement. By its nature, it is necessarily difficult to observe, study, define, and measure. No single source readily or authoritatively defines informal economy as a unit of study.</p>
<p>The terms "under the table" and "off the books" typically refer to this type of economy. The term <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> refers to a specific subset of the informal economy. The term "informal sector" was used in many earlier studies, and has been mostly replaced in more recent studies which use the newer term.</p>
