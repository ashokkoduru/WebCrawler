<p><b>Energy conversion efficiency</b> (<i><b>η</b></i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> between the useful output of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_machine" title="Energy conversion machine">energy conversion machine</a> and the input, in <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> terms. The input, as well as the useful output may be <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> (radiation), or <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<p>Energy conversion efficiency is not defined uniquely, but instead depends on the usefulness of the output. All or part of the heat produced from burning a fuel may become rejected <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> if, for example, work is the desired output from a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycle</a>. Energy converter is an example of an energy transformation. Goal or mission oriented terms include <a href="/wiki/Effectiveness" title="Effectiveness">effectiveness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">efficacy</a>.</p>
<p>Generally, energy conversion efficiency is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless" title="Dimensionless">dimensionless</a> number between 0 and 1.0, or 0% to 100%. Efficiencies may not exceed 100%, e.g., for a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">perpetual motion</a> machine. However, other <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">effectiveness measures</a> that can exceed 1.0 are used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" title="Heat pumps">heat pumps</a> and other devices that move heat rather than convert it.</p>
<p>When talking about the efficiency of heat engines and power stations the convention should be stated, i.e., HHV (aka Gross Heating Value etc.) or LCV (aka Net Heating value), and whether gross output (at the generator terminals) or net output (at the power station fence) are being considered. The two are separate but both must be stated. Failure to do so causes endless confusion.</p>
<p>Related, more specific terms include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electrical_efficiency" title="Electrical efficiency">Electrical efficiency</a>, useful power output per electrical power consumed;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mechanical_efficiency" title="Mechanical efficiency">Mechanical efficiency</a>, where one form of mechanical energy (e.g. potential energy of water) is converted to mechanical energy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">work</a>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">Thermal efficiency</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">Fuel efficiency</a>, useful <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a> <a href="/wiki/Work_output" title="Work output">output</a> per input energy such as the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> consumed;</li>
<li>'Total efficiency', e.g., for <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, useful <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> and heat output per fuel energy consumed. Same as the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_efficiency" title="Luminous efficiency">Luminous efficiency</a>, that portion of the emitted electromagnetic radiation is usable for human vision.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_heating_values_and_efficiency">Fuel heating values and efficiency</span></h2>
<p>In Europe the usable energy content of fuel is typically calculated using the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">lower heating value</a></b> (LHV) of that fuel, the definition of which assumes that the <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> produced during fuel <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> (oxidation), remains gaseous, and is not <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensed</a> to liquid water so the latent heat of vaporization of that water is not usable. Using the LHV, a <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">condensing boiler</a> can achieve a "heating efficiency" in excess of 100% (this does not violate the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a> as long as the LHV convention is understood, but does cause confusion). This is because the apparatus recovers part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a>, which is not included in the definition of the lower heating value of fuel. In the U.S. and elsewhere, the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value">higher heating value</a></b> (HHV) is used, which includes the latent heat for condensing the water vapor, and thus the thermodynamic maximum of 100% efficiency cannot be exceeded with HHV's use.</p>
