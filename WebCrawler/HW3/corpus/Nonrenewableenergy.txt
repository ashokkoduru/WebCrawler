<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p>A <b>non-renewable resource</b> (also called a finite resource) is a resource that does not renew itself at a sufficient rate for sustainable economic extraction in meaningful human time-frames. An example is carbon-based, organically-derived fuel. The original organic material, with the aid of heat and pressure, becomes a fuel such as oil or gas. <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> (<a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> in certain <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> are all considered non-renewable resources, though individual <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> are almost always conserved.</p>
<p>In contrast, resources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> (when <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_forest_management" title="Sustainable forest management">harvested sustainably</a>) and wind (used to power energy conversion systems) are considered <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a>, largely because their localized replenishment can occur within time frames meaningful to humans.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_minerals_and_metal_ores">Earth minerals and metal ores</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">Mineral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">Ore</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> minerals and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> ores are examples of non-renewable resources. The metals themselves are present in vast amounts in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, and their extraction by humans only occurs where they are concentrated by <a href="/wiki/Ore_genesis" title="Ore genesis">natural geological processes</a> (such as heat, pressure, organic activity, weathering and other processes) enough to become economically viable to extract. These processes generally take from tens of thousands to millions of years, through <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_subsidence" title="Tectonic subsidence">tectonic subsidence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustal_recycling" title="Crustal recycling">crustal recycling</a>.</p>
<p>The localized deposits of metal ores near the surface which can be extracted economically by humans are non-renewable in human time-frames. There are certain <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_mineral" title="Rare earth mineral">rare earth minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">elements</a> that are more scarce and exhaustible than others. These are in high demand in <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, particularly for the <a href="/wiki/Electronics_industry" title="Electronics industry">electronics industry</a>.</p>
<p>Most metal ores are considered vastly greater in supply to fossil fuels, because metal ores are formed by crustal-scale processes which make up a much larger portion of the Earth's near-surface environment, than those that form fossil fuels which are limited to areas where carbon-based life forms flourish, die, and are quickly buried.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuels">Fossil fuels</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">Fossil fuel</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Oil_depletion" title="Oil depletion">Oil depletion</a></div>
<p>Natural resources such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> (crude oil) and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> take thousands of years to form naturally and cannot be replaced as fast as they are being consumed. Eventually it is considered that fossil-based resources will become too costly to harvest and humanity will need to shift its reliance to other sources of energy such as solar or wind power, see <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>.</p>
<p>An alternative hypothesis is that carbon based fuel is virtually inexhaustible in human terms, if one includes all sources of carbon-based energy such as methane hydrates on the sea floor, which are vastly greater than all other carbon based fossil fuel resources combined. These sources of carbon are also considered non-renewable, although their rate of formation/replenishment on the sea floor is not known. However their extraction at economically viable costs and rates has yet to be determined.</p>
<p>At present, the main energy source used by humans is non-renewable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. Since the dawn of <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> technologies in the 17th century, petroleum and other fossil fuels have remained in continual demand. As a result, conventional <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> systems, which are fitted to combustion engines, remain prominent throughout the globe. The continual use of fossil fuels at the current rate is believed to increase <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and cause more severe <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fuels">Nuclear fuels</span></h2>


<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">Nuclear fuel</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_proposed_as_renewable_energy" title="Nuclear power proposed as renewable energy">Nuclear power proposed as renewable energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peak_uranium" title="Peak uranium">Peak uranium</a></div>
<p>In 1987, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Environment_and_Development" title="World Commission on Environment and Development">World Commission on Environment and Development</a> (WCED) an organization set up by but independent from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> classified fission reactors that produce more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> nuclear fuel than they consume -i.e. <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactors</a>, and when it is developed, <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">fusion power</a>, among conventional renewable energy sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">falling water</a>. The <a href="/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute" title="American Petroleum Institute">American Petroleum Institute</a> likewise does not consider conventional nuclear fission as renewable, but that <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactor</a> nuclear power fuel is considered renewable and sustainable, before explaining that radioactive waste from used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spent_fuel" title="Spent fuel">spent fuel</a> rods remains dangerous, and so has to be very carefully stored for up to a thousand years. With the careful monitoring of radioactive waste products also being required upon the use of other renewable energy sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>.</p>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_technology" title="Nuclear technology">nuclear technology</a> relying on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">fission</a> requires <a href="/wiki/Naturally_occurring_radioactive_material" title="Naturally occurring radioactive material">Naturally occurring radioactive material</a> as fuel. <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">Uranium</a>, the most common fission fuel, and is present in the ground at relatively low concentrations and <a href="/wiki/Uranium_mining" title="Uranium mining">mined</a> in 19 countries. This mined uranium is used to fuel energy-generating nuclear reactors with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissionable</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a> which generates heat that is ultimately used to power <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbines</a> to generate electricity.</p>
<p>Nuclear power provides about 6% of the world's energy and 13â€“14% of the world's electricity. Nuclear energy production is associated with potentially dangerous <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a> as it relies upon unstable elements. In particular, nuclear power facilities produce about 200,000 metric tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_and_intermediate_level_waste" title="Low and intermediate level waste">low and intermediate level waste</a> (LILW) and 10,000 metric tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_level_waste" title="High level waste">high level waste</a> (HLW) (including spent fuel designated as waste) each year worldwide.</p>
<p>Issues entirely separate from the question of the sustainability of nuclear fuel, relate to the use of nuclear fuel and the high-level radioactive waste the nuclear industry generates that if not properly contained, is <a href="/wiki/Acute_radiation_syndrome" title="Acute radiation syndrome">highly hazardous</a> to people and wildlife. Radiocontaminants in the environment can enter the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> and become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioaccumulative" title="Bioaccumulative">bioaccumulated</a>. Internal or external exposure can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutagenic" title="Mutagenic">mutagenic</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> breakage producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogenic" title="Teratogenic">teratogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">generational</a> <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">birth defects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancers</a> and other damage. The United Nations (<a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Scientific_Committee_on_the_Effects_of_Atomic_Radiation" title="United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation">UNSCEAR</a>) estimated in 2008 that average annual human radiation exposure includes 0.01 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millisievert" title="Millisievert">millisievert</a> (mSv) from the legacy of past atmospheric nuclear testing plus the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a> and the nuclear fuel cycle, along with 2.0 mSv from natural radioisotopes and 0.4 mSv from <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>; all exposures <a href="/wiki/Background_radiation" title="Background radiation">vary by location</a>. natural uranium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotopes" title="Radioisotopes">radioisotopes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">nuclear waste</a> and naturally in the ground emits radiation for the prolonged period of 4.5 billion years or more, and storage has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_and_radiation_accidents" title="Nuclear and radiation accidents">risks of containment</a>. The storage of waste, health implications and dangers of radioactive fuel continue to be a topic of debate, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_debate" title="Nuclear power debate">controversial and unresolved industry</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_resources">Renewable resources</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">Renewable resource</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">Recycling</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">Natural resources</a>, known as renewable resources, are replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_phenomenon" title="Natural phenomenon">natural processes and forces</a> persistent in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a>. There are <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">intermittent</a> and reoccurring renewables, and <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recyclable materials</a>, which are utilized during a <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">cycle</a> across a certain amount of time, and can be harnessed for any number of cycles.</p>
<p>The production of goods and services by <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> in <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic systems</a> creates many <a href="/wiki/List_of_waste_types" title="List of waste types">types of waste</a> during production and after the <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumer</a> has made use of it. The material is then either <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incinerated</a>, buried in a <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycled" title="Recycled">recycled</a> for reuse. Recycling turns materials of value that would otherwise become waste into valuable resources again.</p>

<p>The natural environment, with <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are all renewable resources, as long as they are adequately <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">monitored, protected and conserved</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</a> is the cultivation of plant and animal materials in a manner that preserves plant and animal <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> over the long term. The <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> of the oceans is one example of where an industry practice or method can threaten an ecosystem, endanger <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">species</a> and possibly even determine whether or not a <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> is sustainable for use by humans. An unregulated industry practice or method can lead to a complete <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>.</p>
<p>The renewable energy from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_wave" title="Surface wave">wave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal</a> energies are based on renewable resources. Renewable resources such as the movement of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">radiant energy</a> from geothermal heat (used for <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a>) and solar energy (used for <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar power</a>) are practically infinite and cannot be depleted, unlike their non-renewable counterparts, which are likely to run out if not used sparingly.</p>
<p>The potential wave energy on coastlines can provide 1/5 of world demand. Hydroelectric power can supply 1/3 of our total energy global needs. Geothermal energy can provide 1.5 more times the energy we need. There is enough wind to power the planet 30 times over, wind power could power all of humanity's needs alone. Solar currently supplies only 0.1% of our world energy needs, but there is enough out there to power humanity's needs 4,000 times over, the entire global projected energy demand by 2050.</p>
<p>Renewable energy and <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy efficiency</a> are no longer niche <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">sectors</a> that are promoted only by governments and environmentalists. The increasing levels of investment and that more of the capital is from conventional financial actors, both suggest that sustainable energy has become mainstream and the future of energy production, as non-renewable resources decline. This is reinforced by <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> concerns, nuclear dangers and accumulating radioactive waste, <a href="/wiki/2000s_energy_crisis" title="2000s energy crisis">high oil prices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> and increasing government support for renewable energy. These factors are <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_commercialization" title="Renewable energy commercialization">commercializing renewable energy</a>, enlarging the market and growing demand, the adoption of new products to replace obsolete technology and the conversion of existing infrastructure to a renewable standard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_models">Economic models</span></h2>
<p>In economics, a non-renewable resource is defined as <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a>, where greater consumption today implies less consumption tomorrow. <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a> in his early works analysed the pricing of exhaustible resources, where he argued that the price of a mineral resource should increase over time. He argued that the spot price is always determined by the mine with the highest cost of extraction, and mine owners with lower extraction costs benefit from a differential rent. The first model is defined by <a href="/wiki/Hotelling%27s_rule" title="Hotelling's rule">Hotelling's rule</a>, which is a 1931 economic model of non-renewable <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">resource management</a> by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Hotelling" title="Harold Hotelling">Harold Hotelling</a>. It shows that efficient exploitation of a nonrenewable and nonaugmentable resource would, under otherwise stable conditions, lead to a <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depletion</a> of the resource. The rule states that this would lead to a net price or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotelling_rent" title="Hotelling rent">Hotelling rent</a>" for it that rose annually at a rate equal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rate_of_interest" title="Rate of interest">rate of interest</a>, reflecting the increasing scarcity of the resources. The <a href="/wiki/Hartwick%27s_rule" title="Hartwick's rule">Hartwick's rule</a> provides an important result about the <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> of welfare in an economy that uses non-renewable source.</p>
<p>However, nearly all metal prices have been declining over time in inflation adjusted terms, because of a number of false assumptions in the above. Firstly, metal resources are non-renewable, but on a world scale, largely inexhaustible. This is because they are present throughout the earth's crust on a vast scale, far exceeding human demand on all time scales. Metal ores however, are only extracted in those areas where nature has concentrated the metal in the crust to a level whereby it is locally economic to extract. This also depends on the available technology for both finding the metal ores as well as extracting them, which is constantly changing. If the technology or demand changes, vast amounts of metal previously ignored can become economically extractable. This is why Ricardo's simplistic notion that the price of a mineral resource should increase over time has in fact turned out to be the opposite, nearly all metal ores have decreased in inflation adjusted prices since well before the early 20th century. The main reason he was wrong is that he assumed that metals are exhaustible on a world scale, and he also misunderstood the effect of globally competing markets; in human terms the amount of metal in the earth's crust is essentially limitless. It is only in localized areas that metal ores can become depleted, as these local areas compete with extraction costs of resources elsewhere, which does have ramifications for the sustainability of local economies.</p>
