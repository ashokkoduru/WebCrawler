<p><b>Hydrogen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_symbol" title="Chemical symbol">chemical symbol</a> <b>H</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 1. With an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000100794000000000♠</span>1.007<span style="margin-left:.25em;">94</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a></span>, hydrogen is the lightest element on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic">monatomic</a> form (H) is the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">most abundant</a> chemical substance in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>, constituting roughly 75% of all <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryonic</a> mass. Non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_remnant" title="Stellar remnant">remnant</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> are mainly composed of hydrogen in the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> state. The most common <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of hydrogen, termed <i>protium</i> (name rarely used, symbol H), has one <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>.</p>
<p>The universal emergence of atomic hydrogen first occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology)" title="Recombination (cosmology)">recombination epoch</a>. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, hydrogen is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">colorless</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odorless" title="Odorless">odorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">tasteless</a>, non-toxic, <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal" title="Nonmetal">nonmetallic</a>, highly <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustible</a> <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> H<sub>2</sub>. Since hydrogen readily forms <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent</a> compounds with most nonmetallic elements, most of the hydrogen on Earth exists in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">molecular forms</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>. Hydrogen plays a particularly important role in <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reactions</a> because most acid-base reactions involve the exchange of protons between soluble molecules. In <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a>, hydrogen can take the form of a negative charge (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>) when it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydride</a>, or as a positively charged (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>) <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">species</a> denoted by the symbol H. The hydrogen cation is written as though composed of a bare proton, but in reality, hydrogen cations in ionic compounds are always more complex. As the only neutral atom for which the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger equation</a> can be solved analytically, study of the energetics and bonding of the hydrogen atom has played a key role in the development of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.</p>
<p>Hydrogen gas was first artificially produced in the early 16th century by the reaction of acids on metals. In 1766–81, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> was the first to recognize that hydrogen gas was a discrete substance, and that it produces water when burned, the property for which it was later named: in Greek, hydrogen means "water-former".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">Industrial production</a> is mainly from steam reforming natural gas, and less often from more energy-intensive methods such as the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis of water</a>. Most hydrogen is used near the site of its production site, the two largest uses being <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> processing (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocracking" title="Hydrocracking">hydrocracking</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> production, mostly for the fertilizer market. Hydrogen is a concern in <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> as it can <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">embrittle</a> many metals, complicating the design of pipelines and storage tanks.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion">Combustion</span></h3>


<p>Hydrogen gas (dihydrogen or molecular hydrogen) is highly flammable and will burn in air at a very wide range of concentrations between 4% and 75% by volume. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_combustion" title="Enthalpy of combustion">enthalpy of combustion</a> is −286 kJ/mol:</p>

<p>Hydrogen gas forms explosive mixtures with air in concentrations from 4–74% and with chlorine at 5–95%. The explosive reactions may be triggered by spark, heat, or sunlight. The hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Autoignition_temperature" title="Autoignition temperature">autoignition temperature</a>, the temperature of spontaneous ignition in air, is 500 °C (932 °F). Pure <a href="/wiki/Oxyhydrogen" title="Oxyhydrogen">hydrogen-oxygen</a> flames emit <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light and with high oxygen mix are nearly invisible to the naked eye, as illustrated by the faint plume of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Main_Engine" title="Space Shuttle Main Engine">Space Shuttle Main Engine</a>, compared to the highly visible plume of a <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Solid_Rocket_Booster" title="Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster">Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster</a>, which uses an <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_perchlorate_composite_propellant" title="Ammonium perchlorate composite propellant">ammonium perchlorate composite</a>. The detection of a burning hydrogen leak may require a <a href="/wiki/Flame_detector" title="Flame detector">flame detector</a>; such leaks can be very dangerous. Hydrogen flames in other conditions are blue, resembling blue natural gas flames.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">destruction of the Hindenburg airship</a> was a notorious example of hydrogen combustion and the cause is still debated. The visible orange flames in that incident were the result of a rich mixture of hydrogen to oxygen combined with carbon compounds from the airship skin.</p>
<p>H<sub>2</sub> reacts with every oxidizing element. Hydrogen can react spontaneously and violently at room temperature with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> to form the corresponding hydrogen halides, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, which are also potentially dangerous <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electron_energy_levels">Electron energy levels</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">Hydrogen atom</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy level</a> of the electron in a hydrogen atom is −13.6 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a>, which is equivalent to an ultraviolet <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> of roughly 91 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">nm</a> wavelength.</p>
<p>The energy levels of hydrogen can be calculated fairly accurately using the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">Bohr model</a> of the atom, which conceptualizes the electron as "orbiting" the proton in analogy to the Earth's orbit of the Sun. However, the atomic electron and proton are held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" title="Electromagnetic force">electromagnetic force</a>, while planets and celestial objects are held by <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. Because of the discretization of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> postulated in early <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> by Bohr, the electron in the Bohr model can only occupy certain allowed distances from the proton, and therefore only certain allowed energies.</p>
<p>A more accurate description of the hydrogen atom comes from a purely quantum mechanical treatment that uses the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger equation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a> or even the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Feynman</a> <a href="/wiki/Path_integral_formulation" title="Path integral formulation">path integral formulation</a> to calculate the <a href="/wiki/Probability_amplitude" title="Probability amplitude">probability density</a> of the electron around the proton. The most complicated treatments allow for the small effects of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_polarization" title="Vacuum polarization">vacuum polarization</a>. In the quantum mechanical treatment, the electron in a ground state hydrogen atom has no angular momentum at all—illustrating how the "planetary orbit" differs from electron motion.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elemental_molecular_forms">Elemental molecular forms</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spin_isomers_of_hydrogen" title="Spin isomers of hydrogen">Spin isomers of hydrogen</a></div>

<p>There exist two different <a href="/wiki/Spin_isomers_of_hydrogen" title="Spin isomers of hydrogen">spin isomers of hydrogen</a> diatomic molecules that differ by the relative <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> of their nuclei. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthohydrogen" title="Orthohydrogen">orthohydrogen</a> form, the spins of the two protons are parallel and form a triplet state with a molecular spin quantum number of 1 (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">⁄<sub>2</sub></span>+<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">⁄<sub>2</sub></span>); in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parahydrogen" title="Parahydrogen">parahydrogen</a> form the spins are antiparallel and form a singlet with a molecular spin quantum number of 0 (<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">⁄<sub>2</sub></span>–<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">⁄<sub>2</sub></span>). At standard temperature and pressure, hydrogen gas contains about 25% of the para form and 75% of the ortho form, also known as the "normal form". The equilibrium ratio of orthohydrogen to parahydrogen depends on temperature, but because the ortho form is an <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited state</a> and has a higher energy than the para form, it is unstable and cannot be purified. At very low temperatures, the equilibrium state is composed almost exclusively of the para form. The liquid and gas phase thermal properties of pure parahydrogen differ significantly from those of the normal form because of differences in rotational heat capacities, as discussed more fully in <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_isomers_of_hydrogen" title="Spin isomers of hydrogen">spin isomers of hydrogen</a></i>. The ortho/para distinction also occurs in other hydrogen-containing molecules or functional groups, such as water and <a href="/wiki/Methylene_group" title="Methylene group">methylene</a>, but is of little significance for their thermal properties.</p>
<p>The uncatalyzed interconversion between para and ortho H<sub>2</sub> increases with increasing temperature; thus rapidly condensed H<sub>2</sub> contains large quantities of the high-energy ortho form that converts to the para form very slowly. The ortho/para ratio in condensed H<sub>2</sub> is an important consideration in the preparation and storage of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>: the conversion from ortho to para is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> and produces enough heat to evaporate some of the hydrogen liquid, leading to loss of liquefied material. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">Catalysts</a> for the ortho-para interconversion, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferric_oxide" title="Ferric oxide">ferric oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>, platinized asbestos, rare earth metals, uranium compounds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromic_oxide" title="Chromic oxide">chromic oxide</a>, or some nickel compounds, are used during hydrogen cooling.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Category:Hydrogen_compounds" title="Category:Hydrogen compounds">Category:Hydrogen compounds</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Covalent_and_organic_compounds">Covalent and organic compounds</span></h4>
<p>While H<sub>2</sub> is not very reactive under standard conditions, it does form compounds with most elements. Hydrogen can form compounds with elements that are more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronegative" title="Electronegative">electronegative</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> (e.g., F, Cl, Br, I), or <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>; in these compounds hydrogen takes on a partial positive charge. When bonded to <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, hydrogen can participate in a form of medium-strength noncovalent bonding with the hydrogen of other similar molecules, a phenomenon called <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonding</a> that is critical to the stability of many biological molecules. Hydrogen also forms compounds with less electronegative elements, such as <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloids</a>, where it takes on a partial negative charge. These compounds are often known as <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydrides</a>.</p>
<p>Hydrogen forms a vast array of compounds with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, and an even vaster array with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteroatoms" title="Heteroatoms">heteroatoms</a> that, because of their general association with living things, are called <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>. The study of their properties is known as <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> and their study in the context of living <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> is known as <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>. By some definitions, "organic" compounds are only required to contain carbon. However, most of them also contain hydrogen, and because it is the carbon-hydrogen bond which gives this class of compounds most of its particular chemical characteristics, carbon-hydrogen bonds are required in some definitions of the word "organic" in chemistry. Millions of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> are known, and they are usually formed by complicated synthetic pathways that seldom involve elementary hydrogen.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrides">Hydrides</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">Hydride</a></div>
<p>Compounds of hydrogen are often called <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydrides</a>, a term that is used fairly loosely. The term "hydride" suggests that the H atom has acquired a negative or anionic character, denoted H, and is used when hydrogen forms a compound with a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a> element. The existence of the <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydride anion</a>, suggested by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a> in 1916 for group 1 and 2 salt-like hydrides, was demonstrated by Moers in 1920 by the electrolysis of molten <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydride" title="Lithium hydride">lithium hydride</a> (LiH), producing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometry</a> quantity of hydrogen at the anode. For hydrides other than group 1 and 2 metals, the term is quite misleading, considering the low electronegativity of hydrogen. An exception in group 2 hydrides is <span class="chemf nowrap">BeH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>, which is polymeric. In <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">lithium aluminium hydride</a>, the <span class="chemf nowrap">AlH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
4</span></span> anion carries hydridic centers firmly attached to the Al(III).</p>
<p>Although hydrides can be formed with almost all main-group elements, the number and combination of possible compounds varies widely; for example, more than 100 binary borane hydrides are known, but only one binary aluminium hydride. Binary <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a> hydride has not yet been identified, although larger complexes exist.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic chemistry</a>, hydrides can also serve as <a href="/wiki/Bridging_ligand" title="Bridging ligand">bridging ligands</a> that link two metal centers in a <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complex</a>. This function is particularly common in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_13_element" title="Group 13 element">group 13 elements</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Borane" title="Borane">boranes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> hydrides) and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> complexes, as well as in clustered <a href="/wiki/Carborane" title="Carborane">carboranes</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protons_and_acids">Protons and acids</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">Acid–base reaction</a></div>
<p>Oxidation of hydrogen removes its electron and gives <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">H</a>, which contains no electrons and a <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> which is usually composed of one proton. That is why <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span> is often called a proton. This species is central to discussion of <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronsted-Lowry_theory" title="Bronsted-Lowry theory">Bronsted-Lowry theory</a>, acids are proton donors, while bases are proton acceptors.</p>
<p>A bare proton, <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span>, cannot exist in solution or in ionic crystals because of its unstoppable attraction to other atoms or molecules with electrons. Except at the high temperatures associated with plasmas, such protons cannot be removed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">electron clouds</a> of atoms and molecules, and will remain attached to them. However, the term 'proton' is sometimes used loosely and metaphorically to refer to positively charged or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cationic</a> hydrogen attached to other species in this fashion, and as such is denoted "<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span>" without any implication that any single protons exist freely as a species.</p>
<p>To avoid the implication of the naked "solvated proton" in solution, acidic aqueous solutions are sometimes considered to contain a less unlikely fictitious species, termed the "<a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion" (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span>). However, even in this case, such solvated hydrogen cations are more realistically conceived as being organized into clusters that form species closer to H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
9</span><span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span>. Other <a href="/wiki/Oxonium_ion" title="Oxonium ion">oxonium ions</a> are found when water is in acidic solution with other solvents.</p>
<p>Although exotic on Earth, one of the most common ions in the universe is the <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
3</span></span> ion, known as <a href="/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation" title="Trihydrogen cation">protonated molecular hydrogen</a> or the trihydrogen cation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_hydrogen" title="Isotopes of hydrogen">Isotopes of hydrogen</a></div>



<p>Hydrogen has three naturally occurring isotopes, denoted <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">1<br/></span>H</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2<br/></span>H</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3<br/></span>H</span>. Other, highly unstable nuclei (<span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">4<br/></span>H</span> to <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">7<br/></span>H</span>) have been synthesized in the laboratory but not observed in nature.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">1<br/></span>H</span></b> is the most common hydrogen isotope with an abundance of more than 99.98%. Because the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> of this isotope consists of only a single proton, it is given the descriptive but rarely used formal name <i>protium</i>.</li>
<li><b><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2<br/></span>H</span></b>, the other stable hydrogen isotope, is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a></i> and contains one proton and one <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> in the nucleus. All deuterium in the universe is thought to have been produced at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, and has endured since that time. Deuterium is not radioactive, and does not represent a significant toxicity hazard. Water enriched in molecules that include deuterium instead of normal hydrogen is called <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">heavy water</a>. Deuterium and its compounds are used as a non-radioactive label in chemical experiments and in solvents for <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">1<br/></span>H</span>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>. Heavy water is used as a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderator</a> and coolant for nuclear reactors. Deuterium is also a potential fuel for commercial <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>.</li>
<li><b><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3<br/></span>H</span></b> is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a></i> and contains one proton and two neutrons in its nucleus. It is radioactive, decaying into <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">helium-3</a> through <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 12.32 years. It is so radioactive that it can be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioluminescent_paint" title="Radioluminescent paint">luminous paint</a>, making it useful in such things as watches. The glass prevents the small amount of radiation from getting out. Small amounts of tritium are produced naturally by the interaction of cosmic rays with atmospheric gases; tritium has also been released during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" title="Nuclear testing">nuclear weapons tests</a>. It is used in nuclear fusion reactions, as a tracer in <a href="/wiki/Isotope_geochemistry" title="Isotope geochemistry">isotope geochemistry</a>, and in specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-powered_lighting" title="Self-powered lighting">self-powered lighting</a> devices. Tritium has also been used in chemical and biological labeling experiments as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiolabel" title="Radiolabel">radiolabel</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Hydrogen is the only element that has different names for its isotopes in common use today. During the early study of radioactivity, various heavy radioactive isotopes were given their own names, but such names are no longer used, except for deuterium and tritium. The symbols D and T (instead of <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2<br/></span>H</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3<br/></span>H</span>) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium, but the corresponding symbol for protium, P, is already in use for <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> and thus is not available for protium. In its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature" title="IUPAC nomenclature">nomenclatural</a> guidelines, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> allows any of D, T, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2<br/></span>H</span>, and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3<br/></span>H</span> to be used, although <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2<br/></span>H</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3<br/></span>H</span> are preferred.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_use">Discovery and use</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_hydrogen_technologies" title="Timeline of hydrogen technologies">Timeline of hydrogen technologies</a></div>
<p>In 1671, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a> discovered and described the reaction between <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> filings and dilute <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, which results in the production of hydrogen gas. In 1766, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> was the first to recognize hydrogen gas as a discrete substance, by naming the gas from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-acid_reaction" title="Metal-acid reaction">metal-acid reaction</a> "inflammable air". He speculated that "inflammable air" was in fact identical to the hypothetical substance called "<a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">phlogiston</a>" and further finding in 1781 that the gas produces water when burned. He is usually given credit for the discovery of hydrogen as an element. In 1783, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> gave the element the name hydrogen (from the Greek ὑδρο- <i>hydro</i> meaning "water" and -γενής <i>genes</i> meaning "creator") when he and <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">Laplace</a> reproduced Cavendish's finding that water is produced when hydrogen is burned.</p>

<p>Lavoisier produced hydrogen for his experiments on mass conservation by reacting a flux of steam with metallic <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> through an incandescent iron tube heated in a fire. Anaerobic oxidation of iron by the protons of water at high temperature can be schematically represented by the set of following reactions:</p>



<p>Many metals such as <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> undergo a similar reaction with water leading to the production of hydrogen.</p>
<p>Hydrogen was <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquefied</a> for the first time by <a href="/wiki/James_Dewar" title="James Dewar">James Dewar</a> in 1898 by using <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_cooling" title="Regenerative cooling">regenerative cooling</a> and his invention, the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_flask" title="Vacuum flask">vacuum flask</a>. He produced <a href="/wiki/Solid_hydrogen" title="Solid hydrogen">solid hydrogen</a> the next year. <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">Deuterium</a> was discovered in December 1931 by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Urey" title="Harold Urey">Harold Urey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> was prepared in 1934 by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Oliphant" title="Mark Oliphant">Mark Oliphant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Harteck" title="Paul Harteck">Paul Harteck</a>. <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">Heavy water</a>, which consists of deuterium in the place of regular hydrogen, was discovered by Urey's group in 1932. <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Isaac_de_Rivaz" title="François Isaac de Rivaz">François Isaac de Rivaz</a> built the first <a href="/wiki/De_Rivaz_engine" title="De Rivaz engine">de Rivaz engine</a>, an internal combustion engine powered by a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen in 1806. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Daniel_Clarke" title="Edward Daniel Clarke">Edward Daniel Clarke</a> invented the hydrogen gas blowpipe in 1819. The <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6bereiner%27s_lamp" title="Döbereiner's lamp">Döbereiner's lamp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limelight" title="Limelight">limelight</a> were invented in 1823.</p>
<p>The first hydrogen-filled <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloon</a> was invented by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Charles" title="Jacques Charles">Jacques Charles</a> in 1783. Hydrogen provided the lift for the first reliable form of air-travel following the 1852 invention of the first hydrogen-lifted airship by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Giffard" title="Henri Giffard">Henri Giffard</a>. German count <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Zeppelin" title="Ferdinand von Zeppelin">Ferdinand von Zeppelin</a> promoted the idea of rigid airships lifted by hydrogen that later were called <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelins</a>; the first of which had its maiden flight in 1900. Regularly scheduled flights started in 1910 and by the outbreak of World War I in August 1914, they had carried 35,000 passengers without a serious incident. Hydrogen-lifted airships were used as observation platforms and bombers during the war.</p>
<p>The first non-stop transatlantic crossing was made by the British airship <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R34_(airship)" title="R34 (airship)">R34</a></i> in 1919. Regular passenger service resumed in the 1920s and the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> reserves in the United States promised increased safety, but the U.S. government refused to sell the gas for this purpose. Therefore, H<sub>2</sub> was used in the <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg"><i>Hindenburg</i></a> airship, which was destroyed in a midair fire over <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> on 6 May 1937. The incident was broadcast live on radio and filmed. Ignition of leaking hydrogen is widely assumed to be the cause, but later investigations pointed to the ignition of the <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminized</a> fabric coating by <a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">static electricity</a>. But the damage to hydrogen's reputation as a <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lifting gas</a> was already done.</p>
<p>In the same year the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-cooled_turbogenerator" title="Hydrogen-cooled turbogenerator">hydrogen-cooled turbogenerator</a> went into service with gaseous hydrogen as a <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> in the rotor and the stator in 1937 at <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton</a>, Ohio, by the Dayton Power &amp; Light Co.; because of the thermal conductivity of hydrogen gas, this is the most common type in its field today.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_hydrogen_battery" title="Nickel hydrogen battery">nickel hydrogen battery</a> was used for the first time in 1977 aboard the U.S. Navy's Navigation technology satellite-2 (NTS-2). For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">ISS</a>, <a href="/wiki/2001_Mars_Odyssey" title="2001 Mars Odyssey">Mars Odyssey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">Mars Global Surveyor</a> are equipped with nickel-hydrogen batteries. In the dark part of its orbit, the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> is also powered by nickel-hydrogen batteries, which were finally replaced in May 2009, more than 19 years after launch and 13 years beyond their design life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_quantum_theory">Role in quantum theory</span></h3>

<p>Because of its simple atomic structure, consisting only of a proton and an electron, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a>, together with the spectrum of light produced from it or absorbed by it, has been central to the development of the theory of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> structure. Furthermore, study of the corresponding simplicity of the hydrogen molecule and the corresponding cation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H2%2B" title="H2+"><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
2</span></span></a> brought understanding of the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a>, which followed shortly after the quantum mechanical treatment of the hydrogen atom had been developed in the mid-1920s.</p>
<p>One of the first quantum effects to be explicitly noticed (but not understood at the time) was a Maxwell observation involving hydrogen, half a century before full <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical theory</a> arrived. Maxwell observed that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> of H<sub>2</sub> unaccountably departs from that of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> gas below room temperature and begins to increasingly resemble that of a monatomic gas at cryogenic temperatures. According to quantum theory, this behavior arises from the spacing of the (quantized) rotational energy levels, which are particularly wide-spaced in H<sub>2</sub> because of its low mass. These widely spaced levels inhibit equal partition of heat energy into rotational motion in hydrogen at low temperatures. Diatomic gases composed of heavier atoms do not have such widely spaced levels and do not exhibit the same effect.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antihydrogen" title="Antihydrogen">Antihydrogen</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">H</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> counterpart to hydrogen. It consists of an <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiproton</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a>. Antihydrogen is the only type of antimatter atom to have been produced as of 2015.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span></h2>

<p>Hydrogen, as atomic H, is the most <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">abundant</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> in the universe, making up 75% of <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">normal matter</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and more than 90% by number of atoms. (Most of the mass of the universe, however, is not in the form of chemical-element type matter, but rather is postulated to occur as yet-undetected forms of mass such as <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>.) This element is found in great abundance in stars and <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a> planets. <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">Molecular clouds</a> of H<sub>2</sub> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>. Hydrogen plays a vital role in powering <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton-proton_reaction" title="Proton-proton reaction">proton-proton reaction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout the universe, hydrogen is mostly found in the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> states, with properties quite different from those of molecular hydrogen. As a plasma, hydrogen's electron and proton are not bound together, resulting in very high electrical conductivity and high emissivity (producing the light from the Sun and other stars). The charged particles are highly influenced by magnetic and electric fields. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> they interact with the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> giving rise to <a href="/wiki/Birkeland_current" title="Birkeland current">Birkeland currents</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(phenomenon)" title="Aurora (phenomenon)">aurora</a>. Hydrogen is found in the neutral atomic state in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. The large amount of neutral hydrogen found in the damped Lyman-alpha systems is thought to dominate the cosmological baryonic density of the Universe up to <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> <i>z</i>=4.</p>
<p>Under ordinary conditions on Earth, elemental hydrogen exists as the diatomic gas, H<sub>2</sub>. However, hydrogen gas is very rare in the Earth's atmosphere (1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Part_per_million" title="Part per million">ppm</a> by volume) because of its light weight, which enables it to <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_escape" title="Atmospheric escape">escape from Earth's gravity</a> more easily than heavier gases. However, hydrogen is the third most abundant element on the Earth's surface, mostly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> and water. Hydrogen gas is produced by some bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and is a natural component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatus" title="Flatus">flatus</a>, as is <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, itself a hydrogen source of increasing importance.</p>
<p>A molecular form called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protonated_molecular_hydrogen" title="Protonated molecular hydrogen">protonated molecular hydrogen</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
3</span></span>) is found in the interstellar medium, where it is generated by ionization of molecular hydrogen from <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. This charged ion has also been observed in the upper atmosphere of the planet <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>. The ion is relatively stable in the environment of outer space due to the low temperature and density. <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
3</span></span> is one of the most abundant ions in the Universe, and it plays a notable role in the chemistry of the interstellar medium. Neutral <a href="/wiki/Triatomic_hydrogen" title="Triatomic hydrogen">triatomic hydrogen</a> H<sub>3</sub> can exist only in an excited form and is unstable. By contrast, the positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_molecular_ion" title="Hydrogen molecular ion">hydrogen molecular ion</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
2</span></span>) is a rare molecule in the universe.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">Hydrogen production</a></div>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is produced in chemistry and biology laboratories, often as a by-product of other reactions; in industry for the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">unsaturated</a> substrates; and in nature as a means of expelling <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> equivalents in biochemical reactions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_reforming">Steam reforming</span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen can be prepared in several different ways, but economically the most important processes involve removal of hydrogen from hydrocarbons, as about 95% of hydrogen production came from steam reforming around year 2000. Commercial bulk hydrogen is usually produced by the <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. At high temperatures (1000–1400 K, 700–1100 °C or 1300–2000 °F), steam (water vapor) reacts with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> to yield <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal-acid">Metal-acid</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is usually prepared by the reaction of dilute <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_acid" title="Oxidizing acid">non-oxidizing acids</a> on some reactive metals such as <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> with <a href="/wiki/Kipp%27s_apparatus" title="Kipp's apparatus">Kipp's apparatus</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Aluminium</a> can also produce <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> upon treatment with bases:</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis of water</a> is a simple method of producing hydrogen. A low voltage current is run through the water, and gaseous oxygen forms at the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> while gaseous hydrogen forms at the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>. Typically the cathode is made from platinum or another inert metal when producing hydrogen for storage. If, however, the gas is to be burnt on site, oxygen is desirable to assist the combustion, and so both electrodes would be made from inert metals. (Iron, for instance, would oxidize, and thus decrease the amount of oxygen given off.) The theoretical maximum efficiency (electricity used vs. energetic value of hydrogen produced) is in the range 80–94%.</p>

<p>An alloy of aluminium and <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a> in pellet form added to water can be used to generate hydrogen. The process also produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>, but the expensive gallium, which prevents the formation of an oxide skin on the pellets, can be re-used. This has important potential implications for a hydrogen economy, as hydrogen can be produced on-site and does not need to be transported.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermochemical">Thermochemical</span></h3>
<p>There are more than 200 thermochemical cycles which can be used for <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">water splitting</a>, around a dozen of these cycles such as the <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide_cycle" title="Iron oxide cycle">iron oxide cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide%E2%80%93cerium(III)_oxide_cycle" title="Cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle">cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_zinc-oxide_cycle" title="Zinc zinc-oxide cycle">zinc zinc-oxide cycle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfur-iodine_cycle" title="Sulfur-iodine cycle">sulfur-iodine cycle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper-chlorine_cycle" title="Copper-chlorine cycle">copper-chlorine cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_sulfur_cycle" title="Hybrid sulfur cycle">hybrid sulfur cycle</a> are under research and in testing phase to produce hydrogen and oxygen from water and heat without using electricity. A number of laboratories (including in France, Germany, Greece, Japan, and the USA) are developing thermochemical methods to produce hydrogen from solar energy and water.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_in_transformers">Formation in transformers</span></h3>
<p>From all the fault gases formed in power <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>, hydrogen is the most common and is generated under most fault conditions; thus, formation of hydrogen is an early indication of serious problems in the transformer's life cycle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_in_processes">Consumption in processes</span></h3>
<p>Large quantities of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> are needed in the petroleum and chemical industries. The largest application of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is for the processing ("upgrading") of fossil fuels, and in the production of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. The key consumers of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> in the petrochemical plant include <a href="/wiki/Hydrodealkylation" title="Hydrodealkylation">hydrodealkylation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">hydrodesulfurization</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocracking" title="Hydrocracking">hydrocracking</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> has several other important uses. <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is used as a hydrogenating agent, particularly in increasing the level of saturation of unsaturated fats and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">oils</a> (found in items such as margarine), and in the production of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. It is similarly the source of hydrogen in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a> of metallic <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>.</p>
<p>Hydrogen is highly soluble in many <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> and is soluble in both nanocrystalline and <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_metal" title="Amorphous metal">amorphous metals</a>. Hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in metals is influenced by local distortions or impurities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">crystal lattice</a>. These properties may be useful when hydrogen is purified by passage through hot <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> disks, but the gas's high solubility is a metallurgical problem, contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">embrittlement</a> of many metals, complicating the design of pipelines and storage tanks.</p>
<p>Apart from its use as a reactant, <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> has wide applications in physics and engineering. It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Shielding_gas" title="Shielding gas">shielding gas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> methods such as <a href="/wiki/Atomic_hydrogen_welding" title="Atomic hydrogen welding">atomic hydrogen welding</a>. H<sub>2</sub> is used as the rotor coolant in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generators</a> at <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>, because it has the highest <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of any gas. Liquid H<sub>2</sub> is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> research, including <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> studies. Because <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is lighter than air, having a little more than <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">⁄<sub>14</sub></span> of the density of air, it was once widely used as a <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lifting gas</a> in balloons and <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>.</p>
<p>In more recent applications, hydrogen is used pure or mixed with nitrogen (sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Forming_gas" title="Forming gas">forming gas</a>) as a tracer gas for minute leak detection. Applications can be found in the automotive, chemical, power generation, aerospace, and telecommunications industries. Hydrogen is an authorized food additive (E 949) that allows food package leak testing among other anti-oxidizing properties.</p>
<p>Hydrogen's rarer isotopes also each have specific applications. <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">Deuterium</a> (hydrogen-2) is used in <a href="/wiki/CANDU_reactor" title="CANDU reactor">nuclear fission applications</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">moderator</a> to slow <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> reactions. Deuterium compounds have applications in chemistry and biology in studies of reaction <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_isotope_effect" title="Kinetic isotope effect">isotope effects</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">Tritium</a> (hydrogen-3), produced in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>, is used in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bombs</a>, as an isotopic label in the biosciences, and as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_radiation" title="Beta radiation">radiation</a> source in luminous paints.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> temperature of equilibrium hydrogen is a defining fixed point on the <a href="/wiki/International_Temperature_Scale_of_1990" title="International Temperature Scale of 1990">ITS-90</a> temperature scale at 13.8033 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coolant">Coolant</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen-cooled_turbo_generator" title="Hydrogen-cooled turbo generator">Hydrogen-cooled turbo generator</a></div>
<p>Hydrogen is commonly used in power stations as a coolant in generators due to a number of favorable properties that are a direct result of its light diatomic molecules. These include low <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, low <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, and the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of all gases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_carrier">Energy carrier</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">Hydrogen economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_infrastructure" title="Hydrogen infrastructure">Hydrogen infrastructure</a></div>
<p>Hydrogen is not an energy resource, except in the hypothetical context of commercial <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> power plants using <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a>, a technology presently far from development. The Sun's energy comes from <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">nuclear fusion</a> of hydrogen, but this process is difficult to achieve controllably on Earth. Elemental hydrogen from solar, biological, or electrical sources require more energy to make it than is obtained by burning it, so in these cases hydrogen functions as an energy carrier, like a battery. Hydrogen may be obtained from fossil sources (such as methane), but these sources are unsustainable.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> per unit <i>volume</i> of both <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compressed_hydrogen" title="Compressed hydrogen">compressed hydrogen</a> gas at any practicable pressure is significantly less than that of traditional fuel sources, although the energy density per unit fuel <i>mass</i> is higher. Nevertheless, elemental hydrogen has been widely discussed in the context of energy, as a possible future <i>carrier</i> of energy on an economy-wide scale. For example, <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_sequestration" title="CO2 sequestration">sequestration</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> could be conducted at the point of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> production from fossil fuels. Hydrogen used in transportation would burn relatively cleanly, with some <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a> emissions, but without carbon emissions. However, the infrastructure costs associated with full conversion to a hydrogen economy would be substantial. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a> can convert hydrogen and oxygen directly to electricity more efficiently than internal combustion engines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductor_industry">Semiconductor industry</span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen is employed to saturate broken ("dangling") bonds of <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_silicon" title="Amorphous silicon">amorphous silicon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_carbon" title="Amorphous carbon">amorphous carbon</a> that helps stabilizing material properties. It is also a potential <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donor</a> in various oxide materials, including <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">ZnO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" title="Tin dioxide">SnO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_oxide" title="Cadmium oxide">CdO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">MgO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_dioxide" title="Zirconium dioxide">ZrO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Hafnium(IV)_oxide" title="Hafnium(IV) oxide">HfO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanum(III)_oxide" title="Lanthanum(III) oxide">La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium(III)_oxide" title="Yttrium(III) oxide">Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">TiO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Strontium_titanate" title="Strontium titanate">SrTiO<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_aluminate" title="Lanthanum aluminate">LaAlO<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">ZrSiO<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Hafnon" title="Hafnon">HfSiO<sub>4</sub></a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium_zirconate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Strontium zirconate (page does not exist)">SrZrO<sub>3</sub></a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_reactions">Biological reactions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Biohydrogen" title="Biohydrogen">Biohydrogen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_hydrogen_production_(Algae)" title="Biological hydrogen production (Algae)">Biological hydrogen production (Algae)</a></div>
<p>H<sub>2</sub> is a product of some types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">anaerobic metabolism</a> and is produced by several <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, usually via reactions <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> called <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenase" title="Hydrogenase">hydrogenases</a>. These enzymes catalyze the reversible <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reaction between H<sub>2</sub> and its component two protons and two electrons. Creation of hydrogen gas occurs in the transfer of reducing equivalents produced during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> to water. The natural cycle of hydrogen production and consumption by organisms is called the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cycle" title="Hydrogen cycle">hydrogen cycle</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">Water splitting</a>, in which water is decomposed into its component protons, electrons, and oxygen, occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Light-dependent_reactions" title="Light-dependent reactions">light reactions</a> in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a> organisms. Some such organisms, including the alga <i><a href="/wiki/Chlamydomonas_reinhardtii" title="Chlamydomonas reinhardtii">Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>, have evolved a second step in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_reaction" title="Dark reaction">dark reactions</a> in which protons and electrons are reduced to form H<sub>2</sub> gas by specialized hydrogenases in the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a>. Efforts have been undertaken to genetically modify cyanobacterial hydrogenases to efficiently synthesize H<sub>2</sub> gas even in the presence of oxygen. Efforts have also been undertaken with genetically modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_hydrogen_production_(Algae)" title="Biological hydrogen production (Algae)">alga in a bioreactor</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_precautions">Safety and precautions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_safety" title="Hydrogen safety">Hydrogen safety</a></div>
<p>Hydrogen poses a number of hazards to human safety, from potential <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonations</a> and fires when mixed with air to being an <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiant</a> in its pure, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>-free form. In addition, liquid hydrogen is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogen" title="Cryogen">cryogen</a> and presents dangers (such as <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>) associated with very cold liquids. Hydrogen dissolves in many metals, and, in addition to leaking out, may have adverse effects on them, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">hydrogen embrittlement</a>, leading to cracks and explosions. Hydrogen gas leaking into external air may spontaneously ignite. Moreover, hydrogen fire, while being extremely hot, is almost invisible, and thus can lead to accidental burns.</p>
<p>Even interpreting the hydrogen data (including safety data) is confounded by a number of phenomena. Many physical and chemical properties of hydrogen depend on the <a href="/wiki/Spin_isomers_of_hydrogen" title="Spin isomers of hydrogen">parahydrogen/orthohydrogen</a> ratio (it often takes days or weeks at a given temperature to reach the equilibrium ratio, for which the data is usually given). Hydrogen detonation parameters, such as critical detonation pressure and temperature, strongly depend on the container geometry.</p>
