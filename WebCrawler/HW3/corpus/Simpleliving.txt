<p><b>Simple living</b> encompasses a number of different voluntary practices to <a href="/wiki/Simplicity" title="Simplicity">simplify</a> one's <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyle</a>. These may include reducing one's <a href="/wiki/Personal_property" title="Personal property">possessions</a>, generally referred to as Minimalism, or increasing <a href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" title="Self-sufficiency">self-sufficiency</a>, for example. Simple living may be characterized by individuals being satisfied with what they have rather than <a href="/wiki/Want" title="Want">want</a>. Although <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">asceticism</a> generally promotes living simply and refraining from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxury_good" title="Luxury good">luxury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indulgence" title="Indulgence">indulgence</a>, not all proponents of simple living are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascetics" title="Ascetics">ascetics</a>. Simple living is distinct from those living in forced <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, as it is a voluntary lifestyle choice.</p>
<p>Adherents may choose simple living for a variety of personal reasons, such as <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, increase in <a href="/wiki/Quality_time" title="Quality time">quality time</a> for family and friends, <a href="/wiki/Work%E2%80%93life_balance" title="Work–life balance">work–life balance</a>, personal taste, <a href="/wiki/Frugality" title="Frugality">frugality</a>, or reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)">stress</a>. Simple living can also be a reaction to <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">conspicuous consumption</a>. Some cite socio-political goals aligned with the <a href="/wiki/Anti-consumerism" title="Anti-consumerism">anti-consumerist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anti-war_movement" title="Anti-war movement">anti-war movements</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" title="Conservation ethic">conservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tax_resistance" title="Tax resistance">tax resistance</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_and_spiritual">Religious and spiritual</span></h3>


<p>A number of religious and spiritual traditions encourage simple living. Early examples include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shramana" title="Shramana">Shramana</a> traditions of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_India" title="Iron Age India">Iron Age India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>, and biblical <a href="/wiki/Nazirite" title="Nazirite">Nazirites</a> (notably <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>). <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> himself is said to have lived a simple life. He is said to have encouraged his disciples "to take nothing for their journey except a staff—no bread, no bag, no money in their belts—but to wear sandals and not put on two tunics." Various notable individuals have claimed that spiritual inspiration led them to a simple living lifestyle, such as <a href="/wiki/Benedict_of_Nursia" title="Benedict of Nursia">Benedict of Nursia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">Francis of Assisi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammon_Hennacy" title="Ammon Hennacy">Ammon Hennacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Schweitzer" title="Albert Schweitzer">Albert Schweitzer</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Gandhi">Mohandas Gandhi</a>.</p>
<p>Simple living has traditions that stretch back to the Orient, resonating with leaders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zarathustra" title="Zarathustra">Zarathustra</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laozi" title="Laozi">Laozi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a> and was heavily stressed in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> culture and <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> ethics. <a href="/wiki/Diogenes_of_Sinope" title="Diogenes of Sinope">Diogenes of Sinope</a>, a major figure in the ancient Greek philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">Cynicism</a>, claimed that a simple life was necessary for virtue, and was said to have lived in a wine jar.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plain_people" title="Plain people">Plain people</a> are Christian groups who have for centuries practiced lifestyles in which some forms of <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> are excluded for religious or philosophical reasons. Groups include the <a href="/wiki/Shakers" title="Shakers">Shakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Order_Mennonite" title="Old Order Mennonite">Mennonites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hutterites" title="Hutterites">Hutterites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amana_Colonies" title="Amana Colonies">Amana Colonies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruderhof_Communities" title="Bruderhof Communities">Bruderhof</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_German_Baptist_Brethren" title="Old German Baptist Brethren">Old German Baptist Brethren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harmony_Society" title="Harmony Society">Harmony Society</a>, and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quakers</a>. There is a Quaker belief called <i><a href="/wiki/Testimony_of_Simplicity" title="Testimony of Simplicity">Testimony of Simplicity</a></i> that a person ought to live her or his life simply.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> strongly praised the simple life in many of his writings, especially in his <i>Discourse on the Arts and Sciences</i> (1750) and <i>Discourse on Inequality</i> (1754).</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sufism" title="Sufism">Sufism</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secular">Secular</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureanism</a>, based on the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a>, flourished from about the fourth century BC to the third century AD. Epicureanism upheld the untroubled life as the paradigm of happiness, made possible by carefully considered choices. Specifically, Epicurus pointed out that troubles entailed by maintaining an extravagant lifestyle tend to outweigh the pleasure of partaking in it. He therefore concluded that what is necessary for happiness, bodily comfort, and life itself should be maintained at minimal cost, while all things beyond what is necessary for these should either be tempered by moderation or completely avoided.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a>, an American <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> and author, is often considered to have made the classic <a href="/wiki/Secularity" title="Secularity">secular</a> statement advocating a life of simple and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">sustainable living</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i> (1854). Thoreau conducted a two-year experiment living a plain and simple life on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Walden_Pond" title="Walden Pond">Walden Pond</a>.</p>
<p>In Victorian Britain, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stephens_Salt" title="Henry Stephens Salt">Henry Stephens Salt</a>, an admirer of Thoreau, popularised the idea of "Simplification, the saner method of living". Other British advocates of the simple life included <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carpenter" title="Edward Carpenter">Edward Carpenter</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>, and the members of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_New_Life" title="The Fellowship of the New Life">The Fellowship of the New Life</a>". <a href="/wiki/Charles_Robert_Ashbee" title="Charles Robert Ashbee">C.R. Ashbee</a> and his followers also practiced some of these ideas, thus linking simplicity with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_Movement" title="Arts and Crafts Movement">Arts and Crafts Movement</a>. British novelist <a href="/wiki/John_Cowper_Powys" title="John Cowper Powys">John Cowper Powys</a> advocated the simple life in his 1933 book <i>A Philosophy of Solitude</i>. <a href="/wiki/John_Middleton_Murry" title="John Middleton Murry">John Middleton Murry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Plowman" title="Max Plowman">Max Plowman</a> practised a simple lifestyle at their Adelphi Centre in Essex in the 1930s. Irish poet <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Kavanagh" title="Patrick Kavanagh">Patrick Kavanagh</a> championed a "right simplicity" philosophy based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruralism" title="Ruralism">ruralism</a> in some of his work.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Lorenzo_Noyes" title="George Lorenzo Noyes">George Lorenzo Noyes</a>, a naturalist, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineralogist" title="Mineralogist">mineralogist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Development_criticism" title="Development criticism">development critic</a>, writer, and artist, is known as the Thoreau of Maine. He lived a wilderness lifestyle, advocating through his creative work a simple life and reverence for nature. During the 1920s and 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Agrarians" title="Southern Agrarians">Vanderbilt Agrarians</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> advocated a lifestyle and culture centered upon traditional and sustainable <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian values</a> as opposed to the progressive urban <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialism" title="Industrialism">industrialism</a> which dominated the Western world at that time.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen" title="Thorstein Veblen">Thorstein Veblen</a> warned against the <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">conspicuous consumption</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialistic</a> society with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Theory_of_the_Leisure_Class" title="The Theory of the Leisure Class">The Theory of the Leisure Class</a></i> (1899); <a href="/wiki/Richard_Gregg_(social_philosopher)" title="Richard Gregg (social philosopher)">Richard Gregg</a> coined the term "voluntary simplicity" in <i>The Value of Voluntary Simplicity</i> (1936). From the 1920s, a number of modern authors articulated both the theory and practice of living simply, among them <a href="/wiki/Gandhism" title="Gandhism">Gandhian</a> Richard Gregg, economists <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Borsodi" title="Ralph Borsodi">Ralph Borsodi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Nearing" title="Scott Nearing">Scott Nearing</a>, anthropologist-poet <a href="/wiki/Gary_Snyder" title="Gary Snyder">Gary Snyder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopian</a> fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Callenbach" title="Ernest Callenbach">Ernest Callenbach</a>. <a href="/wiki/E._F._Schumacher" title="E. F. Schumacher">E. F. Schumacher</a> argued against the notion that "bigger is better" in <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a></i> (1973); and <a href="/wiki/Duane_Elgin" title="Duane Elgin">Duane Elgin</a> continued the promotion of the simple life in <i>Voluntary Simplicity</i> (1981). The Australian academic <a href="/wiki/Ted_Trainer" title="Ted Trainer">Ted Trainer</a> practices and writes about simplicity, and established The Simplicity Institute. at Pigface Point, some 20 km from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales" title="University of New South Wales">University of New South Wales</a> to which it is attached A secular set of nine values was developed with the <a class="external text" href="http://ethify.org/en/content/values" rel="nofollow"><i>Ethify Yourself</i></a> project in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, having a simplified life style in mind and accompanied by an online book (2011).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practices">Practices</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_consumption.2C_work_time.2C_and_possessions">Reducing consumption, work time, and possessions</span></h3>

<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:22%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Simplicity boils down to two steps: Identify the essential. Eliminate the rest.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_Babauta" title="Leo Babauta">Leo Babauta</a></cite></div>
<p>Some people practice simple living by reducing <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>. By lowering expenditure on goods or services, the time spent earning <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> can be reduced. The time saved may be used to pursue other interests, or help others through <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">volunteering</a>. Some may use the extra free time to improve their <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>, for example pursuing creative activities such as art and crafts. Developing a <a href="/wiki/Detachment_(philosophy)" title="Detachment (philosophy)">detachment</a> from money has led some individuals, such as <a href="/wiki/Suelo" title="Suelo">Suelo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Boyle_(Moneyless_Man)" title="Mark Boyle (Moneyless Man)">Mark Boyle</a>, to live with no money at all. Reducing expenses may also lead to increasing savings, which can lead to <a href="/wiki/Financial_independence" title="Financial independence">financial independence</a> and the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Retirement#Early_retirement" title="Retirement">early retirement</a>.</p>

<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>You have succeeded in life when all you really want is only what you really need.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Vernon_Howard" title="Vernon Howard">Vernon Howard</a></cite></div>
<p>The 100 Thing Challenge is a grassroots movement to whittle down personal possessions to one hundred items, with the aim of decluttering and simplifying life. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_house_movement" title="Small house movement">small house movement</a> includes individuals who chose to live in small, mortgage-free, low-impact dwellings, such as <a href="/wiki/Log_cabin" title="Log cabin">log cabins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beach_hut" title="Beach hut">beach huts</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increasing_self-sufficiency">Increasing self-sufficiency</span></h3>

<p>One way to simplify life is to get <a href="/wiki/Back-to-the-land_movement" title="Back-to-the-land movement">back-to-the-land</a> and grow your own food, as increased <a href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" title="Self-sufficiency">self-sufficiency</a> reduces dependency on money and the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hodgkinson" title="Tom Hodgkinson">Tom Hodgkinson</a> believes the key to a free and simple life is to stop consuming and start producing. This is a sentiment shared by an increasing number of people, including those belonging to the millennial generation such as writer and eco blogger Jennifer Nini, who left the city to live off-grid, grow food and "be a part of the solution not part of the problem."</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">Forest gardening</a>, developed by simple living adherent <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a>, is a low-maintenance plant-based food production system based on woodland ecosystems, incorporating fruit and nut trees, shrubs, herbs, vines and perennial vegetables. Hart created a model forest garden from a 0.12 acre orchard on his farm at <a href="/wiki/Wenlock_Edge" title="Wenlock Edge">Wenlock Edge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>.</p>
<p>The idea of <a href="/wiki/Food_miles" title="Food miles">food miles</a>, the number of miles a given item of food or its ingredients has travelled between the farm and the table, is used by simple living advocates to argue for locally grown food. This is now gaining mainstream acceptance, as shown by the popularity of books such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_100-Mile_Diet" title="The 100-Mile Diet">The 100-Mile Diet</a>,</i> and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Kingsolver" title="Barbara Kingsolver">Barbara Kingsolver</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal,_Vegetable,_Miracle:_A_Year_of_Food_Life" title="Animal, Vegetable, Miracle: A Year of Food Life">Animal, Vegetable, Miracle: A Year of Food Life</a>.</i> In each of these cases, the authors devoted a year to reducing their carbon footprint by eating locally.</p>
<p>City dwellers can also produce fresh home grown fruit and vegetables in <a href="/wiki/Container_garden" title="Container garden">pot gardens</a> or miniature indoor greenhouses. Tomatoes, lettuce, spinach, Swiss chard, peas, strawberries, and several types of herbs can all thrive in pots. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Merkel" title="Jim Merkel">Jim Merkel</a> says that a person "could sprout seeds. They are tasty, incredibly nutritious, and easy to grow... We grow them in wide mouthed mason jars with a square of nylon window screen screwed under a metal ring". Farmer Matt Moore spoke on this issue: "How does it affect the consumer to know that broccoli takes 105 days to grow a head? [...] The supermarket mode is one of plenty — it's always stocked. And that changes our sense of time. How long it takes to grow food — that's removed in the marketplace. They don't want you to think about how long it takes to grow, because they want you to buy right now". One way to change this viewpoint is also suggested by Mr. Moore. He placed a video installation in the produce section of a grocery store that documented the length of time it took to grow certain vegetables. This aimed to raise awareness in people of the length of time actually needed for gardens.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/DIY_ethic" title="DIY ethic">do it yourself ethic</a> refers to the principle of undertaking necessary tasks oneself rather than having others, who are more skilled or experienced, complete them for you.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconsidering_technology">Reconsidering technology</span></h3>
<p>People who practice simple living have diverse views on the role of technology. The American political activist <a href="/wiki/Scott_Nearing" title="Scott Nearing">Scott Nearing</a> was skeptical about how humanity would use new technology, citing destructive inventions such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>. Those who eschew all technology are often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Luddite" title="Luddite">Luddites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neo-Luddism" title="Neo-Luddism">neo-Luddites</a>. Although simple living is often a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular">secular</a> pursuit, it may still involve reconsidering personal definitions of <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a>, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabaptist" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptist</a> groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonites" title="Mennonites">Mennonites</a> have done.</p>
<p>Technological proponents see cutting-edge technologies as a way to make a simple lifestyle within mainstream culture easier and more sustainable. They argue that the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> can reduce an individual's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> through <a href="/wiki/Telecommuting" title="Telecommuting">telecommuting</a> and paper usage. Some have also calculated their energy consumption and have shown that one can live simply and in an emotionally satisfying way by using much less energy than is used in Western countries. Technologies they may embrace include computers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_array" title="Photovoltaic array">photovoltaic arrays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a>.</p>
<p>Technological interventions that appear to simplify living may actually induce side effects elsewhere or at a future point in time. <a href="/wiki/Evgeny_Morozov" title="Evgeny Morozov">Evgeny Morozov</a> warns that tools like the internet can facilitate <a href="/wiki/Mass_surveillance" title="Mass surveillance">mass surveillance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">political repression</a>. The book <i>Green Illusions</i> identifies how wind and solar energy technologies have hidden side effects and can actually increase energy consumption and entrench environmental harms over time. Authors of the book <i>Techno-Fix</i> criticize technological optimists for overlooking the limitations of technology in solving agricultural problems.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">Advertising</a> is criticised for encouraging a consumerist mentality. Many advocates of simple living tend to agree that cutting out, or cutting down on, <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> viewing is a key ingredient in simple living. Some see the Internet, <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_radio" title="Community radio">community radio</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio" title="Pirate radio">pirate radio</a> as viable alternatives.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplifying_diet">Simplifying diet</span></h3>
<p>Another practice is the adoption of a simplified <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a>. Diets that may simplify domestic food production and consumption include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegan_diet" title="Vegan diet">vegan diets</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gandhi_diet" title="Gandhi diet">Gandhi diet</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_Compassionate_Living" title="Movement for Compassionate Living">Movement for Compassionate Living</a> was formed by Kathleen and Jack Jannaway in 1984 to spread the vegan message and promote simple living and self-reliance as a remedy against the <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_natural_resources" title="Exploitation of natural resources">exploitation</a> of humans, animals, and the Earth.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_activism">Politics and activism</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmentalism">Environmentalism</span></h3>
<p>Simple living may be undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">Green parties</a> often advocate simple living as a consequence of their "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Pillars_of_the_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Four Pillars of the Green Party (page does not exist)">four pillars</a>" or the "Ten Key Values" of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. This includes, in policy terms, their rejection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_modification" title="Genetic modification">genetic modification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and other technologies they consider to be hazardous. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_movement" title="Green movement">Greens</a>' support for simplicity is based on the reduction in natural resource usage and environmental impact. This concept is expressed in <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Callenbach" title="Ernest Callenbach">Ernest Callenbach</a>'s "green triangle" of ecology, <a href="/wiki/Frugality" title="Frugality">frugality</a> and health.</p>

<p>Many with similar views avoid involvement even with <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a> as compromising simplicity, however, and advocate forms of <a href="/wiki/Green_anarchism" title="Green anarchism">green anarchism</a> that attempt to implement these principles at a smaller scale, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">ecovillage</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology</a>, a belief that the world does not exist as a resource to be freely exploited by humans, proposes <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">wilderness preservation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_control" title="Human population control">human population control</a> and simple living.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-war">Anti-war</span></h3>
<p>The alleged relationship between <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, when fought for control and exploitation of natural and human resources, is considered a good reason for promoting a simple living lifestyle. Avoiding the perpetuation of the <a href="/wiki/Resource_curse" title="Resource curse">resource curse</a> is a similar objective of many simple living adherents.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anti-war_movement" title="Anti-war movement">Opposition to war</a> has led peace <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activists</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ammon_Hennacy" title="Ammon Hennacy">Ammon Hennacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Thomas" title="Ellen Thomas">Ellen Thomas</a>, to a form of <a href="/wiki/Tax_resistance" title="Tax resistance">tax resistance</a> in which they reduce their income below the <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_threshold" title="Income tax threshold">tax threshold</a> by taking up a simple living lifestyle. These individuals believe that their government is engaged in immoral, unethical or destructive activities such as war, and paying taxes inevitably funds these activities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span></h3>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">Bohemianism</a> has been used to describe a long tradition of both voluntary and involuntary poverty by <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">artists</a> who devote their time to artistic endeavors rather than paid labor.</p>
<p>In May 2014, a story on <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> suggested that positive attitudes towards living in poverty for the sake of art are becoming less common among <a href="/wiki/List_of_American_artists_1900_and_after" title="List of American artists 1900 and after">young American artists</a>, and quoted one recent graduate of the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_School_of_Design" title="Rhode Island School of Design">Rhode Island School of Design</a> as saying "her classmates showed little interest in living in <a href="/wiki/Garret" title="Garret">garrets</a> and eating <a href="/wiki/Ramen" title="Ramen">ramen noodles</a>." </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span></h2>
<p>A new economics movement has been building since the UN conference on the environment in 1972, and the publication that year of <i>Only One Earth</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>, and <i>Blueprint For Survival</i>, followed in 1973 by <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a>: Economics As If People Mattered.</i></p>
<p>Recently, David Wann has introduced the idea of “simple prosperity” as it applies to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> lifestyle. From his point of view, and as a point of departure for what he calls real <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, “it is important to ask ourselves three fundamental questions: what is the point of all our commuting and consuming? What is the economy for? And, finally, why do we seem to be unhappier now than when we began our initial pursuit for rich abundance?” In this context, simple living is the opposite of our modern quest for affluence and, as a result, it becomes less preoccupied with quantity and more concerned about the preservation of cities, traditions and nature.</p>
<p>A reference point for this new economics can be found in <a href="/wiki/James_Robertson_(activist)" title="James Robertson (activist)">James Robertson</a>'s <i>A New Economics of Sustainable Development,</i> and the work of thinkers and activists, who participate in his <i>Working for a Sane Alternative</i> network and program. According to Robertson, the shift to sustainability is likely to require a widespread shift of emphasis from raising incomes to reducing costs.</p>
<p>The principles of the new economics, as set out by Robertson, are the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>systematic <a href="/wiki/Empowerment" title="Empowerment">empowerment</a> of people (as opposed to making and keeping them dependent), as the basis for people-centred development</li>
<li>systematic conservation of resources and <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">the environment</a>, as the basis for environmentally sustainable development</li>
<li>evolution from a “wealth of nations” model of economic life to a one-world model, and from today's inter-national economy to an ecologically sustainable, decentralising, multi-level one-world economic system</li>
<li>restoration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political" title="Political">political</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethical" title="Ethical">ethical</a> factors to a central place in economic life and thought</li>
<li>respect for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qualitative_data" title="Qualitative data">qualitative</a> values, not just quantitative values.</li>
</ul>
