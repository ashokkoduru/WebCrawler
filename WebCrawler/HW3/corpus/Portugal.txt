<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the country. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Portugal_(disambiguation)" title="Portugal (disambiguation)">Portugal (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Portugal</b> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[puɾtuˈɣaɫ]</a></span>), officially the <b>Portuguese Republic</b> (Portuguese: <span lang="pt"><i>República Portuguesa</i></span>), is a country on the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Southwestern Europe</a>. It is the westernmost country of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainland_Europe" title="Mainland Europe">mainland Europe</a>. To the west and south it is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and to the east and north by <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Portugal%E2%80%93Spain_border" title="Portugal–Spain border">Portugal–Spain border</a> is 1,214 km (754 mi) long and considered the longest uninterrupted border within the European Union. The republic also includes the Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_Portugal" title="Autonomous regions of Portugal">autonomous regions</a> with their own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_government" title="Regional government">regional governments</a>.</p>
<p>The territory of modern Portugal has been continuously settled, invaded and fought over since <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Iberia" title="Prehistoric Iberia">prehistoric times</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pre-Celtic" title="Pre-Celtic">Pre-Celts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Carthaginians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Romans</a> were followed by the invasions of the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigothic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a> <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>. In 711 the Iberian Peninsula was invaded by the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a> and for the following centuries Portugal would be part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Andalus" title="Al Andalus">Muslim Al Andalus</a>. Portugal was born as a result of the Christian <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i>, and in 1139, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afonso_Henriques" title="Afonso Henriques">Afonso Henriques</a> was proclaimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Portugal" title="King of Portugal">King of Portugal</a>, thus firmly establishing Portuguese independence.</p>
<p>In the 15th and 16th centuries, Portugal established the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">first global empire</a>, becoming one of the world's major economic, political and military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_superpower" title="Education superpower">powers</a>. During this time, Portuguese explorers pioneered maritime exploration in the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>, notably under royal patronage of Prince <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Navigator" title="Henry the Navigator">Henry the Navigator</a> and King <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">João II</a>, with such notable discoveries as <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>'s sea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_India_Armadas" title="Portuguese India Armadas">route to India</a> (1497–98), the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> (1500), and the reaching of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>. Portugal monopolized the <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a> during this time, and the Portuguese Empire expanded with military campaigns led in Asia. But the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisbon_Earthquake" title="Lisbon Earthquake">1755 earthquake</a>, the country's occupation during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Independence_of_Brazil" title="Independence of Brazil">independence of Brazil</a> (1822), and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Wars" title="Liberal Wars">Liberal Wars</a> (1828–34), all left Portugal crippled from war and diminished in its world power.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/5_October_1910_revolution" title="5 October 1910 revolution">1910 revolution</a> deposed the monarchy, the democratic but unstable <a href="/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic" title="First Portuguese Republic">Portuguese First Republic</a> was established, later being superseded by the <i><a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo</a></i> right-wing authoritarian regime. Democracy was restored after the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> in 1974. Shortly after, independence was granted to almost all <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_African_countries" title="Portuguese-speaking African countries">its overseas territories</a>, marking the end of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">longest-lived European colonial empire</a>. Portugal has left a profound cultural and architectural influence <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_the_Portuguese_Empire" title="Evolution of the Portuguese Empire">across the globe</a> and a legacy of over 250 million <a href="/wiki/Lusophone" title="Lusophone">Portuguese speakers today</a>.</p>
<p>Portugal is a <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed country</a> with a high-income <a href="/wiki/Developed_country#IMF_advanced_economies" title="Developed country">advanced economy</a> and a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_standard" title="Living standard">living standard</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Global_Peace_Index" title="Global Peace Index">the 5th most peaceful country in the world</a>, maintaining a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a> <a href="/wiki/Semi-presidential_system" title="Semi-presidential system">semi-presidential</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republican</a> form of government. It has the 18th highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Progress_Index" title="Social Progress Index">Social Progress</a> in the world, putting it ahead of other Western European countries like <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. A founding member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries" title="Community of Portuguese Language Countries">Community of Portuguese Language Countries</a>, it is also a member of numerous other international organizations, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal" title="History of Portugal">History of Portugal</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history:_Pre-Celts_and_Celts">Early history: Pre-Celts and Celts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Suebi" title="Kingdom of the Suebi">Kingdom of the Suebi</a></div>

<p>The early history of Portugal is shared with the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> located in South Western Europe. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_name_of_Portugal" title="The name of Portugal">The name of Portugal</a> derives from the joined Romano-Celtic name <a href="/wiki/Portus_Cale" title="Portus Cale">Portus Cale</a>. The region was <a href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_peoples_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula" title="Pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula">settled</a> by Pre-<a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> and Celts, giving origin to peoples like the <a href="/wiki/Gallaeci" title="Gallaeci">Gallaeci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lusitanians" title="Lusitanians">Lusitanians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celtici" title="Celtici">Celtici</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cynetes" title="Cynetes">Cynetes</a>, visited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthaginians" title="Carthaginians">Carthaginians</a>, incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> dominions as <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, after 45 BC until 298 AD, settled again by <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buri_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Buri (Germanic tribe)">Buri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>, and conquered by <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>. Other influences include some 5th-century vestiges of <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alan</a> settlements, which were found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alenquer_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Alenquer Municipality, Portugal">Alenquer (old Germanic <i>Alankerk</i>, from Alan+kerk; meaning <i>temple of the Alans</i>)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.</p>
<p>The region of present-day Portugal was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> and then by <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a>, who roamed the border-less region of the northern Iberian peninsula. These were subsistence societies that, although they did not establish prosperous settlements, did establish organized societies. Neolithic Portugal experimented with domestication of herding animals, the raising of some cereal crops and fluvial or marine fishing.</p>
<p>It is believed by some scholars that early in the first millennium BC, several waves of <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> invaded Portugal from <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and inter-married with the local populations, forming <a href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_peoples_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula" title="Pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula">different ethnic groups</a>, with many tribes.</p>
<p>Chief among these tribes were the <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Calaicians or Gallaeci of Northern Portugal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lusitanians" title="Lusitanians">Lusitanians of central Portugal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Celtici" title="Celtici">Celtici of Alentejo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Cynetes" title="Cynetes">Cynetes or Conii of the Algarve</a>. Among the lesser tribes or sub-divisions were the <a href="/wiki/Bracari" title="Bracari">Bracari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coelerni" title="Coelerni">Coelerni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equaesi" title="Equaesi">Equaesi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grovii" title="Grovii">Grovii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interamici" title="Interamici">Interamici</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leuni" title="Leuni">Leuni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luanqui" title="Luanqui">Luanqui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limici" title="Limici">Limici</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narbasi" title="Narbasi">Narbasi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nemetati" title="Nemetati">Nemetati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paesuri" title="Paesuri">Paesuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quaquerni" title="Quaquerni">Quaquerni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seurbi" title="Seurbi">Seurbi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamagani" title="Tamagani">Tamagani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tapoli" title="Tapoli">Tapoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turduli" title="Turduli">Turduli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turduli_Veteres" title="Turduli Veteres">Turduli Veteres</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turdulorum_Oppida" title="Turdulorum Oppida">Turdulorum Oppida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turodi" title="Turodi">Turodi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zoelae" title="Zoelae">Zoelae</a>. A few small, semi-permanent, commercial coastal settlements (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tavira_Municipality" title="Tavira Municipality">Tavira</a>) were also founded in the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> region by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthaginians" title="Carthaginians">Carthaginians</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Lusitania_and_Gallaecia">Roman Lusitania and Gallaecia</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a></div>

<p>Romans first invaded the Iberian Peninsula in 219 BC. During the last days of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, almost the entire peninsula had been annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a>. The Carthaginians, Rome's adversary in the <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a>, were expelled from their coastal colonies.</p>
<p>The Roman conquest of what is now part of modern-day Portugal took almost two hundred years and took many lives of young soldiers and the lives of those who were sentenced to a certain death in the slavery mines when not sold as slaves to other parts of the empire. It suffered a severe setback in 150 BC, when a rebellion began in the north. The Lusitanians and other native tribes, under the leadership of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viriathus" title="Viriathus">Viriathus</a>, wrested control of all of western Iberia.</p>
<p>Rome sent numerous legions and its best generals to Lusitania to quell the rebellion, but to no avail—the Lusitanians kept conquering territory. The Roman leaders decided to change their strategy. They bribed Viriathus's allies to kill him. In 139 BC, Viriathus was assassinated, and Tautalus became leader.</p>
<p>Rome installed a colonial regime. The complete Romanization of Lusitania only took place in the Visigothic era.</p>
<p>In 27 BC, Lusitania gained the status of <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a>. Later, a northern province of Lusitania was formed, known as <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, with capital in Bracara Augusta, today's <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>. There are still many ruins of castros (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_forts" title="Hill forts">hill forts</a>) all over modern Portugal and remains of <a href="/wiki/Castro_culture" title="Castro culture">Castro culture</a>. Numerous Roman sites are scattered around present-day Portugal, some urban remains are quite large, like <a href="/wiki/Con%C3%ADmbriga" title="Conímbriga">Conímbriga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mir%C3%B3briga" title="Miróbriga">Mirobriga</a>. The former, beyond being one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> settlements in Portugal, is also classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monuments_of_Portugal" title="Monuments of Portugal">National Monument</a>. Conímbriga lies 16 km from <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a> which by its turn was the ancient <i><a href="/wiki/Aeminium" title="Aeminium">Aeminium</a></i>). The site also has a museum that displays objects found by archaeologists during their excavations.</p>
<p>Several works of engineering, such as baths, temples, bridges, roads, circus, theatres and layman's homes are preserved throughout the country. Coins, some of which coined in Lusitanian land, as well as numerous pieces of ceramics were also found. Contemporary historians include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" title="Paulus Orosius">Paulus Orosius</a> (c. 375–418) and <a href="/wiki/Hydatius" title="Hydatius">Hydatius</a> (c. 400–469), bishop of <a href="/wiki/Aquae_Flaviae" title="Aquae Flaviae">Aquae Flaviae</a>, who reported on the final years of the Roman rule and arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic tribes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_kingdoms:_Suebi_and_Visigoths">Germanic kingdoms: Suebi and Visigoths</span></h3>

<p>In the early 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic tribes</a>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a> (<a href="/wiki/Silingi" title="Silingi">Silingi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hasdingi" title="Hasdingi">Hasdingi</a>) together with their allies, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatian" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a> invaded the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> where they would form their kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Suebi" title="Kingdom of the Suebi">Kingdom of the Suebi</a> was the Germanic post-Roman kingdom, established in the former Roman provinces of <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>-<a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a>.</p>
<p>About 410 and during the 6th century it became a formally declared kingdom, where king <a href="/wiki/Hermeric" title="Hermeric">Hermeric</a> made a peace treaty with the Gallaecians before passing his domains to <a href="/wiki/Rechila" title="Rechila">Rechila</a>, his son. In 448 Réchila died, leaving the state in expansion to <a href="/wiki/Rechiar" title="Rechiar">Rechiar</a>.</p>
<p>In the year 500, the <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic Kingdom</a> was installed in Iberia, centred on <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>. The Visigoths eventually conquered the Suebi and its capital city Bracara (modern day Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>) in 584–585, following the consecutive defeats of the two last Suebi kings <a href="/wiki/Audeca" title="Audeca">Audeca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaric" title="Malaric">Malaric</a>. The former Kingdom of the Suebi then became the sixth province of the Visigothic Kingdom of Hispania.</p>
<p>For the next 300 years and by the year 700, the entire Iberian Peninsula was ruled by the Visigoths. This period lasted until 711, when King <a href="/wiki/Roderic" title="Roderic">Roderic</a> (Rodrigo) was killed while opposing a Moorish invasion from the south. From the various Germanic groups who settled in Western Iberia, the Suebi left the strongest lasting cultural legacy in what is today Portugal, Galicia and Asturias.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_period_and_the_Reconquista">Islamic period and the Reconquista</span></h3>

<p>Today's modern day continental Portugal, along with most of modern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, was part of the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a>. This occupation lasted one century in the North (effectively for some decades and later only as a mainly military and administrative claimed region) to approximately four and five centuries in most of the Center and in the South, respectively (711 AD - 1249 AD), following the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a> conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> in 711 AD.</p>
<p>After defeating the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> in only a few months, the Umayyad Caliphate started expanding rapidly in the peninsula. Beginning in 711, the land that is now Portugal became part of the vast Umayyad Caliphate's empire of <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, which stretched from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> river in the Indian sub-continent (now <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>) up to the South of France, until its collapse in 750. That year the west of the empire gained its independence under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abd-ar-Rahman_I" title="Abd-ar-Rahman I">Abd-ar-Rahman I</a> with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Emirate of Córdoba">Emirate of Córdoba</a>. After almost two centuries, the Emirate became the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Caliphate of Córdoba">Caliphate of Córdoba</a> in 929, until its dissolution a century later in 1031 into no less than 23 small kingdoms, called <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">Taifa</a> kingdoms.</p>
<p>The governors of the taifas each proclaimed themselves <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">Emir</a> of their provinces and established diplomatic relations with the Christian kingdoms of the north. Most of Portugal fell into the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Badajoz" title="Taifa of Badajoz">Taifa of Badajoz</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aftasid_Dynasty" title="Aftasid Dynasty">Aftasid Dynasty</a>, and after a short spell of an ephemeral <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Lisbon" title="Taifa of Lisbon">Taifa of Lisbon</a> in 1022, fell under the dominion of the <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Seville" title="Taifa of Seville">Taifa of Seville</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbadids" title="Abbadids">Abbadids</a> poets. The Taifa period ended with the conquest of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravids" title="Almoravids">Almoravids</a> who came from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> in 1086 winning a decisive victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sagrajas" title="Battle of Sagrajas">Battle of Sagrajas</a>, followed a century later in 1147, after the second period of Taifa, by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohads" title="Almohads">Almohads</a>, also from <a href="/wiki/Marrakesh" title="Marrakesh">Marrakesh</a>.</p>
<p>Al-Andalus was divided into different districts called <i>Kura</i>. Gharb Al-Andalus at its largest was constituted of ten kuras, each with a distinct capital and governor. The main cities of the period in Portugal were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beja_(Portugal)" title="Beja (Portugal)">Beja</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silves_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Silves Municipality, Portugal">Silves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1cer_do_Sal" title="Alcácer do Sal">Alcácer do Sal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Portugal" title="Santarém, Portugal">Santarém</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.</p>
<p>The Muslim population of the region consisted mainly of native Iberian converts to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> (the so-called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muwallad" title="Muwallad">Muwallad</a></i> or <i>Muladi</i>) and berbers. The Arabs were principally noblemen from <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>; and though few in numbers, they constituted the elite of the population. The Berbers were originally from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_mountains" title="Atlas mountains">Atlas mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">Rif</a> mountains of North Africa and were essentially nomads. The territory which is now Portugal was part of various Muslim states, including the Emirate of Cordoba, the Taifa of Badajoz and the Almohade and Almoravid empires.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="County_of_Portucale">County of Portucale</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_of_Portugal" title="County of Portugal">County of Portugal</a></div>
<p>An Asturian Visigothic noble named <a href="/wiki/Pelagius_of_Asturias" title="Pelagius of Asturias">Pelagius of Asturias</a> in 718 AD was elected leader by many of the ousted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visigoth" title="Visigoth">Visigoth</a> nobles. Pelagius called for the remnant of the Christian Visigothic armies to rebel against the Moors and regroup in the unconquered northern Asturian highlands, better known today as the <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_Mountains" title="Cantabrian Mountains">Cantabrian Mountains</a>, in what is today the small mountain region in North-western <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>.</p>
<p>Pelagius' plan was to use the Cantabrian mountains as a place of refuge and protection from the invading Moors. He then aimed to regroup the Iberian Peninsula's Christian armies and use the Cantabrian mountains as a springboard from which to regain their lands. In the process, after defeating the Moors in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Covadonga" title="Battle of Covadonga">Battle of Covadonga</a> in 722 AD, Pelagius was proclaimed king, thus founding the Christian <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Kingdom of Asturias</a> and starting the war of Christian reconquest known in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> as the <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista Cristã</a></i>.</p>
<p>At the end of the 9th century, the region of Portugal, between the rivers Minho and Douro, was freed or reconquered from the Moors by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vimara_Peres" title="Vimara Peres">Vimara Peres</a> on the orders of King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias" title="Alfonso III of Asturias">Alfonso III of Asturias</a>. Finding that the region had previously had two major cities—<a href="/wiki/Portus_Cale" title="Portus Cale">Portus Cale</a> in the coast and <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a> in the interior, with many towns that were now deserted—he decided to repopulate and rebuild them with Portuguese and Galician refugees and other Christians.</p>
<p>Vimara Peres organized the region he freed from the Moors, and elevated it to the status of <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">County</a>, naming it the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Portugal" title="County of Portugal">County of Portugal</a> after the region's major port city—<i>Portus Cale'</i> or modern <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>. One of the first cities Vimara Peres founded at this time is Vimaranes, known today as <a href="/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es" title="Guimarães">Guimarães</a> - the "birthplace of the Portuguese nation" or the "cradle city" (Cidade Berço in Portuguese).</p>
<p>After annexing the County of Portugal into one of the several counties that made up the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Kingdom of Asturias</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias" title="Alfonso III of Asturias">Alfonso III of Asturias</a> knighted Vimara Peres, in 868 AD, as the First Count of Portus Cale (Portugal). The region became known as <i>Portucale</i>, <i>Portugale</i>, and simultaneously <i>Portugália</i> — the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Portugal" title="County of Portugal">County of Portugal</a>.</p>

<p>Later the Kingdom of Asturias was divided into a number of Christian Kingdoms in Northern Spain due to dynastic divisions of inheritance among the king's offspring. With the forced abdication of <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias" title="Alfonso III of Asturias">Alfonso III "the Great" of Asturias</a> by his sons in 910, the Kingdom of Asturias split into three separate kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">León</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia" title="Kingdom of Galicia">Galicia</a> and Asturias. The three kingdoms were eventually reunited in 924 (León and Galicia in 914, Asturias later) under the crown of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">León</a>.</p>
<p>During the century of internecine struggles for dominance among the Northern Christians kingdoms, the County of Portugal formed the southern portion of the Kingdom of Galicia. At times the Kingdom of Galicia existed independently for short periods, but usually formed an important part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Leon" title="Kingdom of Leon">Kingdom of Leon</a>. Throughout this period, the people of County of Portugal as Galicians found themselves struggling to maintain the autonomy of Galicia with its distinct language and culture (<a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">Galician-Portuguese</a>) from the Leonese culture, whenever the status of the Kingdom of Galicia changed in relation to the Kingdom of Leon. As a result of political division, Galician-Portuguese lost its unity when the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Portugal" title="County of Portugal">County of Portugal</a> separated from the Kingdom of Galicia (a dependent kingdom of Leon) to establish the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Kingdom of Portugal</a>.</p>
<p>In 1093, <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Le%C3%B3n_and_Castile" title="Alfonso VI of León and Castile">Alfonso VI of León and Castile</a> bestowed the county to <a href="/wiki/Henry,_Count_of_Portugal" title="Henry, Count of Portugal">Henry of Burgundy</a> and married him to his daughter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theresa,_Countess_of_Portugal" title="Theresa, Countess of Portugal">Teresa of León</a>, for his role in reconquering the land from Moors. Henry based his newly formed county in Bracara Augusta (modern <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>), capital city of the ancient Roman province, and also previous capital of several kingdoms over the first millennia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independence_and_Afonsine_era">Independence and Afonsine era</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="Portugal in the Middle Ages">Portugal in the Middle Ages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_House_of_Burgundy" title="Portuguese House of Burgundy">Portuguese House of Burgundy</a></div>

<p>On 24 June 1128, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_S%C3%A3o_Mamede" title="Battle of São Mamede">Battle of São Mamede</a> occurred near <a href="/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es" title="Guimarães">Guimarães</a>. <a href="/wiki/Afonso_I_of_Portugal" title="Afonso I of Portugal">Afonso Henriques</a>, Count of Portugal, defeated his mother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teresa,_Countess_of_Portugal" title="Teresa, Countess of Portugal">Countess Teresa</a> and her lover <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Peres_de_Trava" title="Fernão Peres de Trava">Fernão Peres de Trava</a>, thereby establishing himself as sole leader. Afonso then turned his arms against the Moors in the south.</p>
<p>Afonso's campaigns were successful and, on 25 July 1139, he obtained an overwhelming victory in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ourique" title="Battle of Ourique">Battle of Ourique</a>, and straight after was unanimously proclaimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Portugal" title="King of Portugal">King of Portugal</a> by his soldiers. This is traditionally taken as the occasion when the County of Portugal, as a fief of the Kingdom of León, was transformed into the independent <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Kingdom of Portugal</a></p>
<p>Afonso then established the first of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Cortes" title="Portuguese Cortes">Portuguese Cortes</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lamego" title="Lamego">Lamego</a>, where he was crowned by the Archbishop of Braga, though the validity of the Cortes of Lamego has been disputed and called a myth created during the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War" title="Portuguese Restoration War">Portuguese Restoration War</a>. Afonso was recognized in 1143 by King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VII_of_Le%C3%B3n_and_Castile" title="Alfonso VII of León and Castile">Alfonso VII of León and Castile</a>, and in 1179 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_III" title="Pope Alexander III">Pope Alexander III</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> period, Christians reconquered the Iberian Peninsula from <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> domination. <a href="/wiki/Afonso_I_of_Portugal" title="Afonso I of Portugal">Afonso Henriques</a> and his successors, aided by military <a href="/wiki/Christian_monasticism" title="Christian monasticism">monastic orders</a>, pushed southward to drive out the Moors. At this time Portugal covered about half of its present area. In 1249, the Reconquista ended with the capture of the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> and complete expulsion of the last Moorish settlements on the southern coast, giving Portugal its present-day borders, with minor exceptions.</p>
<p>In one of these situations of conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">kingdom of Castile</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinis_I_of_Portugal" title="Dinis I of Portugal">Dinis I of Portugal</a> signed with the king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando_IV_of_Castile" title="Fernando IV of Castile">Fernando IV of Castile</a> (which was represented, when being a minor, by his mother the queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_de_Molina" title="Maria de Molina">Maria de Molina</a>) the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Alca%C3%B1ices_(1297)" title="Treaty of Alcañices (1297)">Treaty of Alcañices (1297)</a>, which stipulated that Portugal abolished agreed treaties against the kingdom of Castile for supporting the infant <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Castilla" title="Juan de Castilla">Juan de Castilla</a>. This treaty established inter alia the border demarcation between the kingdom of Portugal and the kingdom of Leon, where the disputed town of <a href="/wiki/Olivenza" title="Olivenza">Olivenza</a> was included.</p>
<p>The reigns of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denis_I_of_Portugal" title="Denis I of Portugal">Dinis I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afonso_IV_of_Portugal" title="Afonso IV of Portugal">Afonso IV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Portugal" title="Peter I of Portugal">Pedro I</a> for the most part saw peace with the Christian kingdoms of Iberia.</p>
<p>In 1348 and 1349 Portugal, like the rest of Europe, was devastated by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. In 1373, Portugal made an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Portuguese Alliance">alliance with England</a>, which is the longest-standing alliance in the world. Over time this went way beyond geo-political and military cooperation (protecting both nations' interests in Africa, the Americas and Asia against French, Spanish and Dutch rivals) and maintained strong trade and cultural ties between the two old European allies. Particularly in the Oporto region, there is visible <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> influence to this day.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joanine_era_and_Age_of_Discoveries">Joanine era and Age of Discoveries</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1415%E2%80%931578)" title="History of Portugal (1415–1578)">History of Portugal (1415–1578)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">Portuguese discoveries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aviz" title="House of Aviz">House of Aviz</a></div>

<p>In 1383, <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Castile" title="John I of Castile">John I of Castile</a>, husband of <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_of_Portugal" title="Beatrice of Portugal">Beatrice of Portugal</a> and son-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Portugal" title="Ferdinand I of Portugal">Ferdinand I of Portugal</a>, claimed the throne of Portugal. A faction of petty noblemen and commoners, led by <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">John of Aviz</a> (later King John I of Portugal) and commanded by General <a href="/wiki/Nuno_%C3%81lvares_Pereira" title="Nuno Álvares Pereira">Nuno Álvares Pereira</a> defeated the Castilians in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aljubarrota" title="Battle of Aljubarrota">Battle of Aljubarrota</a>. With this battle, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aviz" title="House of Aviz">House of Aviz</a> became the ruling house of Portugal.</p>
<p>Portugal spearheaded European exploration of the world and the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>. Prince <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Navigator" title="Henry the Navigator">Henry the Navigator</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">King João I</a>, became the main sponsor and patron of this endeavour. During this period, Portugal explored the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, discovering several Atlantic archipelagos like the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Cape_Verde" title="Portuguese Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, explored the African coast, colonized selected areas of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, discovered an eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_India_Armadas" title="Portuguese India Armadas">route to India</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil#Discovery_and_early_exploitation" title="Colonial Brazil">discovered Brazil</a>, explored the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, established trading routes throughout most of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Asia" title="Southern Asia">southern Asia</a>, and sent the first direct European maritime trade and diplomatic missions to <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Pires_de_Andrade" title="Fernão Pires de Andrade">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanban_trade" title="Nanban trade">Japan</a>.</p>
<p>In 1415, Portugal acquired the first of its overseas colonies by conquering <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a>, the first prosperous Islamic trade centre in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. There followed the first discoveries in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>: <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, which led to the first <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonization</a> movements.</p>
<p>Throughout the 15th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_exploration_of_Africa#Portuguese" title="European exploration of Africa">Portuguese explorers</a> sailed the coast of Africa, establishing trading posts for <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal#Expansion_of_the_Portuguese_empire_.2815th_and_16th_centuries.29" title="Economic history of Portugal">several common types of tradable commodities at the time</a>, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> to <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a>, as they looked for a route to India and its <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, which were coveted in Europe.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a>, intended to resolve the dispute that had been created following the return of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, which was made by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a>, the mediator between Portugal and Spain. It was signed on 7 June 1494, and divided the newly discovered lands outside Europe between the two countries along a meridian 370 leagues west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands (off the west coast of Africa).</p>
<p>In 1498, <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> reached <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and brought <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal" title="Economic history of Portugal">economic prosperity to Portugal</a> and its population of 1.7 million residents, helping to start the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Renaissance" title="Portuguese Renaissance">Portuguese Renaissance</a>. In 1500, the Portuguese explorer <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Corte-Real" title="Gaspar Corte-Real">Gaspar Corte-Real</a> reached what is now <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and founded the town of <a href="/wiki/Portugal_Cove-St._Philip%27s" title="Portugal Cove-St. Philip's">Portugal Cove-St. Philip's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, long before the French and English in the 17th century, and being just one of many <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Portuguese colonization of the Americas">Portuguese Colonizations of the Americas</a>.</p>

<p>In 1500, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro Álvares Cabral">Pedro Álvares Cabral</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and claimed it for Portugal. Ten years later, <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscat,_Oman" title="Muscat, Oman">Muscat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ormuz" title="Ormuz">Ormuz</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_Strait" title="Persian Strait">Persian Strait</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a>, now a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_of_Malaysia" title="States of Malaysia">state</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>. Thus, the Portuguese empire held dominion over commerce in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and South Atlantic. Portuguese sailors set out to reach Eastern Asia by sailing eastward from Europe, landing in such places as <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the island of <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moluccas" title="Moluccas">Moluccas</a>.</p>
<p>Although for a long period it was believed the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> were the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europeans" title="Europeans">Europeans</a> to arrive in Australia, there is also some evidence that the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_the_Portuguese_discovery_of_Australia" title="Theory of the Portuguese discovery of Australia">Portuguese may have discovered Australia</a> in 1521.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Zaragoza" title="Treaty of Zaragoza">Treaty of Zaragoza</a>, signed on 22 April 1529 between Portugal and Spain, specified the anti-meridian to the line of demarcation specified in the Treaty of Tordesillas.</p>
<p>All these factors made Portugal one of the world's major economic, military, and political powers from the 15th century until the late 16th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iberian_Union.2C_Restoration_and_early_Brigantine_era">Iberian Union, Restoration and early Brigantine era</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">Iberian Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War" title="Portuguese Restoration War">Portuguese Restoration War</a>, and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Braganza" title="House of Braganza">House of Braganza</a></div>

<p>Portugal's sovereignty was interrupted between 1580 and 1640. This occurred because the last two kings of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aviz" title="House of Aviz">House of Aviz</a> – King <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_of_Portugal" title="Sebastian of Portugal">Sebastian</a>, who died in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alc%C3%A1cer_Quibir" title="Battle of Alcácer Quibir">battle of Alcácer Quibir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, and his great-uncle and successor, King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_Portugal" title="Henry I of Portugal">Henry of Portugal</a> – both died without heirs, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_succession_crisis_of_1580" title="Portuguese succession crisis of 1580">Portuguese succession crisis of 1580</a>.</p>
<p>Subsequently, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II of Spain</a> claimed the throne and so became Philip I of Portugal. Although Portugal did not lose its formal independence, it was governed by the same monarch who governed the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>, briefly forming a <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">union</a> of kingdoms. At this time Spain was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_region" title="Geographic region">geographic territory</a>. The joining of the two crowns deprived Portugal of an independent foreign policy and led to its involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a> between <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.</p>
<p>War led to a deterioration of the relations with <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Portuguese Alliance">Portugal's oldest ally, England</a>, and the loss of <a href="/wiki/Ormus" title="Ormus">Hormuz</a>, a strategic trading post located between <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>. From 1595 to 1663 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch-Portuguese_War" title="Dutch-Portuguese War">Dutch-Portuguese War</a> primarily involved the Dutch companies invading many Portuguese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonies" title="Colonies">colonies</a> and commercial interests in Brazil, Africa, India and the Far East, resulting in the loss of the Portuguese Indian sea trade monopoly.</p>

<p>In 1640, <a href="/wiki/John_IV_of_Portugal" title="John IV of Portugal">John IV</a> spearheaded an uprising backed by disgruntled nobles and was proclaimed king. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War" title="Portuguese Restoration War">Portuguese Restoration War</a> between Portugal and the Spanish Empire, in the aftermath of the 1640 revolt, ended the sixty-year period of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">Iberian Union</a> under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>. This was the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Braganza" title="House of Braganza">House of Braganza</a>, which reigned in Portugal until 1910.</p>
<p>King John IV's eldest son came to reign as <a href="/wiki/Afonso_VI_of_Portugal" title="Afonso VI of Portugal">Afonso VI</a>, however his physical and mental disabilities left him overpowered by <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Vasconcelos_e_Sousa,_3rd_Count_of_Castelo_Melhor" title="Luís de Vasconcelos e Sousa, 3rd Count of Castelo Melhor">Luís de Vasconcelos e Sousa, 3rd Count of Castelo Melhor</a>. In a palace coup organized by the King's wife, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Francisca_of_Savoy" title="Maria Francisca of Savoy">Maria Francisca of Savoy</a>, and his brother, <a href="/wiki/Peter_II_of_Portugal" title="Peter II of Portugal">Pedro, Duke of Beja</a>, King Afonso VI was declared mentally incompetent and exiled first to the Azores and then to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sintra_National_Palace" title="Sintra National Palace">Royal Palace of Sintra</a>, outside Lisbon. After Afonso's death, Pedro came to the throne as King Pedro II. Pedro's reign saw the consolidation of national independence, imperial expansion, and investment in domestic production.</p>
<p>Pedro II's son, <a href="/wiki/John_V_of_Portugal" title="John V of Portugal">John V</a>, saw a reign characterized by the influx of gold into the coffers of the royal treasury, supplied largely by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_fifth" title="Royal fifth">royal fifth</a> (a tax on precious metals) that was received from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_empire" title="Portuguese empire">Portuguese colonies</a> of <a href="/wiki/State_of_Brazil" title="State of Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_of_Maranh%C3%A3o_(colonial)" title="State of Maranhão (colonial)">Maranhão</a>. Acting as an absolute monarch, John nearly depleted his country's tax revenues on ambitious architectural works, most notably <a href="/wiki/Mafra_National_Palace" title="Mafra National Palace">Mafra Palace</a>, and on commissions and additions for his sizable art and literary collections.</p>
<p>Official estimates – and most estimates made so far – place the number of Portuguese migrants to <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">Colonial Brazil</a> during the gold rush of the 18th century at 600,000. This represented one of the largest movements of European populations to their colonies in the Americas during colonial times.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pombaline_era_and_Enlightenment">Pombaline era and Enlightenment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1640%E2%80%931777)" title="History of Portugal (1640–1777)">History of Portugal (1640–1777)</a></div>

<p>In 1738, <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo,_1st_Marquis_of_Pombal" title="Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquis of Pombal">Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquis of Pombal</a>, began a diplomatic career as the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">Ambassador</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and later in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">Queen consort</a> of Portugal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary_Anne_of_Austria" title="Mary Anne of Austria">Archduchess Maria Anne Josefa of Austria</a>, was fond of Melo; and after his first wife died, she arranged the widowed de Melo's second marriage to the daughter of the Austrian Field Marshal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Josef,_Count_von_Daun" title="Leopold Josef, Count von Daun">Leopold Josef, Count von Daun</a>. King <a href="/wiki/John_V_of_Portugal" title="John V of Portugal">John V of Portugal</a>, however, was not pleased and recalled Melo to Portugal in 1749. John V died the following year and his son, Joseph I of Portugal, was crowned. In contrast to his father, Joseph I was fond of de Melo, and with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Mother" title="Queen Mother">Queen Mother</a>'s approval, he appointed Melo as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_minister" title="Political minister">Minister</a> of Foreign Affairs.</p>
<p>As the King's confidence in de Melo increased, the King entrusted him with more control of the state. By 1755, Sebastião de Melo was made Prime Minister. Impressed by British economic success that he had witnessed from the Ambassador, he successfully implemented similar <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic</a> policies in Portugal. He abolished slavery in Portugal and in the Portuguese colonies in India; reorganized the army and the navy; restructured the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Coimbra" title="University of Coimbra">University of Coimbra</a>, and ended discrimination against different <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> sects in Portugal.</p>
<p>But Sebastião de Melo's greatest reforms were economic and financial, with the creation of several companies and guilds to regulate every commercial activity. He demarcated the region for production of <a href="/wiki/Port_wine" title="Port wine">Port</a> to ensure the wine's quality, and this was the first attempt to control wine quality and production in Europe. He ruled with a strong hand by imposing strict law upon all classes of Portuguese society from the high nobility to the poorest working class, along with a widespread review of the country's tax system. These reforms gained him enemies in the upper classes, especially among the high nobility, who despised him as a social upstart.</p>
<p>Disaster fell upon Portugal in the morning of 1 November 1755, when <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> was struck by <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">a violent earthquake</a> with an estimated <a href="/wiki/Richter_magnitude_scale" title="Richter magnitude scale">Richter scale</a> magnitude of 9. The city was razed to the ground by the earthquake and the subsequent tsunami and ensuing fires. Sebastião de Melo survived by a stroke of luck and then immediately embarked on rebuilding the city, with his famous quote: "What now? We bury the dead and take care of the living."</p>
<p>Despite the calamity and huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_toll" title="Death toll">death toll</a>, Lisbon suffered no epidemics and within less than one year was already being rebuilt. The new city centre of Lisbon was designed to resist subsequent earthquakes. Architectural models were built for tests, and the effects of an earthquake were simulated by marching troops around the models. The buildings and big squares of the Pombaline City Centre still remain as one of Lisbon's tourist attractions. Sebastião de Melo also made an important contribution to the study of <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a> by designing an inquiry that was sent to every parish in the country.</p>

<p>Following the earthquake, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_I_of_Portugal" title="Joseph I of Portugal">Joseph I</a> gave his Prime Minister even more power, and Sebastião de Melo became a powerful, progressive dictator. As his power grew, his enemies increased in number, and bitter disputes with the high nobility became frequent. In 1758 Joseph I was wounded in an attempted assassination. The <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1vora_affair" title="Távora affair">Távora family</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dukes_of_Aveiro" title="Dukes of Aveiro">Duke of Aveiro</a> were implicated and executed after a quick trial. The <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuits</a> were expelled from the country and their assets confiscated by the crown. Sebastião de Melo prosecuted every person involved, even women and children. This was the final stroke that broke the power of the aristocracy. Joseph I made his loyal minister Count of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oeiras_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Oeiras Municipality, Portugal">Oeiras</a> in 1759.</p>
<p>In 1762, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93Portuguese_War,_1761%E2%80%931763" title="Spanish–Portuguese War, 1761–1763">Spain invaded Portuguese territory</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, but by 1763 the <i>status quo</i> between Spain and Portugal before the war had been restored.</p>
<p>Following the Távora affair, the new Count of Oeiras knew no opposition. Made "Marquis of Pombal" in 1770, he effectively ruled Portugal until Joseph I's death in 1779. However, historians also argue that Pombal’s "enlightenment," while far-reaching, was primarily a mechanism for enhancing autocracy at the expense of individual liberty and especially an apparatus for crushing opposition, suppressing criticism, and furthering colonial economic exploitation as well as intensifying book censorship and consolidating personal control and profit.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Napoleonic_era">Napoleonic era</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1777%E2%80%931834)" title="History of Portugal (1777–1834)">History of Portugal (1777–1834)</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves" title="United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves">United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves</a></div>

<p>The new ruler, Queen <a href="/wiki/Maria_I_of_Portugal" title="Maria I of Portugal">Maria I of Portugal</a>, disliked the Marquis because of the power he amassed, and never forgave him for the ruthlessness with which he dispatched the Távora family, and upon her accession to the throne, she withdrew all his political offices. Pombal died on his estate at <a href="/wiki/Pombal,_Portugal" title="Pombal, Portugal">Pombal</a> in 1782.</p>
<p>In the autumn of 1807, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> moved French troops through Spain to invade Portugal. From 1807 to 1811, British-Portuguese forces would successfully fight against the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">French invasion of Portugal</a>, while the royal family and the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, including Maria I, relocated to the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">Portuguese territory of Brazil</a>, at that time a colony of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a>, in South America. This episode is known as the <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_Court_to_Brazil" title="Transfer of the Portuguese Court to Brazil">Transfer of the Portuguese Court to Brazil</a>.</p>
<p>With the occupation by Napoleon, Portugal began a slow but inexorable decline that lasted until the 20th century. This decline was hastened by the independence in 1822 of the country's largest colonial possession, Brazil. In 1807, as Napoleon's army closed in on Lisbon, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Regent" title="Prince Regent">Prince Regent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_VI_of_Portugal" title="João VI of Portugal">João VI of Portugal</a> <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_Court_to_Brazil" title="Transfer of the Portuguese Court to Brazil">transferred his court</a> to Brazil and established <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> as the capital of the Portuguese Empire. In 1815, Brazil was declared a Kingdom and the Kingdom of Portugal was united with it, forming a pluricontinental State, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves" title="United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves">United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves</a>.</p>

<p>As a result of the change in its status and the arrival of the Portuguese royal family, Brazilian administrative, civic, <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Brazil#The_economy_at_independence.2C_1822" title="Economic history of Brazil">economical</a>, military, <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Brazil#History" title="Education in Brazil">educational</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_science_and_technology#History" title="Brazilian science and technology">scientific</a> apparatus were expanded and highly modernized. Portuguese and their allied British troops fought against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Invasion_of_Portugal" title="French Invasion of Portugal">French Invasion of Portugal</a> and by 1815 the situation in Europe had cooled down sufficiently that João VI would have been able to return safely to Lisbon. However, the King of Portugal remained in Brazil until the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Revolution_of_1820" title="Liberal Revolution of 1820">Liberal Revolution of 1820</a>, which started in <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, demanded his return to Lisbon in 1821.</p>
<p>Thus he returned to Portugal but left his son <a href="/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil" title="Pedro I of Brazil">Pedro</a> in charge of Brazil. When the Portuguese Government attempted the following year to return the Kingdom of Brazil to subordinate status, his son Pedro, with the overwhelming support of the Brazilian elites, declared <a href="/wiki/War_of_Independence_of_Brazil" title="War of Independence of Brazil">Brazil's independence</a> from Portugal. <a href="/wiki/Cisplatina" title="Cisplatina">Cisplatina</a> (today's sovereign state of Uruguay), in the south, was one of the last additions to the territory of Brazil under Portuguese rule.</p>
<p>Brazilian independence was recognized in 1825, whereby Emperor Pedro I granted unto his father the titular honour of <i>Emperor of Brazil</i>. John VI's death in 1826 caused serious questions in his succession. Though Pedro was his heir, and reigned briefly as Pedro IV, his status as a Brazilian monarch was seen as an impediment to holding the Portuguese throne by both nations. Pedro abdicated in favour of his daughter, <a href="/wiki/Maria_II_of_Portugal" title="Maria II of Portugal">Maria II</a>. However, Pedro's brother, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_I_of_Portugal" title="Miguel I of Portugal">Infante Miguel</a>, claimed the throne in protest. After a proposal for Miguel and Maria to marry failed, Miguel seized power as King Miguel I, in 1828. In order to defend his daughter's rights to the throne, Pedro launched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Civil_War" title="Portuguese Civil War">Liberal Wars</a> to reinstall his daughter and establish a constitutional monarchy in Portugal. The war ended in 1834, with Miguel's defeat, the promulgation of a constitution, and the reinstatement of Queen Maria II.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_monarchy">Constitutional monarchy</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1834%E2%80%931910)" title="History of Portugal (1834–1910)">History of Portugal (1834–1910)</a></div>
<p>Queen Maria II and King <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Portugal" title="Ferdinand II of Portugal">Ferdinand II</a>'s son, King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_V" title="Pedro V">Pedro V</a> modernized the country during his short reign (1853-1861). Under his reign, roads, telegraphs, and railways were constructed and improvements in public health advanced. His popularity increased when, during the <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> outbreak of 1853–1856, he visited hospitals handing out gifts and comforting the sick. Pedro's reign was short, as he died of cholera in 1861, after a series of deaths in the royal family, including his two brothers <a href="/wiki/Infante_Fernando_of_Portugal" title="Infante Fernando of Portugal">Infante Fernando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infante_Jo%C3%A3o,_Duke_of_Beja" title="Infante João, Duke of Beja">Infante João, Duke of Beja</a>, and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Stephanie_of_Hohenzollern-Sigmaringen" title="Stephanie of Hohenzollern-Sigmaringen">Stephanie of Hohenzollern-Sigmaringen</a>. Not having children, his brother, <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_I_of_Portugal" title="Luís I of Portugal">Luís I of Portugal</a> ascended the throne and continued his modernization.</p>
<p>At the height of European colonialism in the 19th century, Portugal had already lost its territory in South America and all but a few bases in Asia. <a href="/wiki/Luanda" title="Luanda">Luanda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benguela" title="Benguela">Benguela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bissau" title="Bissau">Bissau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maputo" title="Maputo">Lourenço Marques</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto_Amboim" title="Porto Amboim">Porto Amboim</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Mozambique" title="Island of Mozambique">Island of Mozambique</a> were among the oldest Portuguese-founded port cities in its African territories. During this phase, Portuguese colonialism focused on expanding its outposts in Africa into nation-sized territories to compete with other European powers there.</p>
<p>With the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Conference_(1884)" title="Berlin Conference (1884)">Conference of Berlin</a> of 1884, Portuguese Africa territories had their borders formally established on request of Portugal in order to protect the centuries-long Portuguese interests in the continent from rivalries enticed by the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">Scramble for Africa</a>. Portuguese Africa's cities and towns like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nova_Lisboa" title="Nova Lisboa">Nova Lisboa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lubango" title="Lubango">Sá da Bandeira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuito" title="Kuito">Silva Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malanje" title="Malanje">Malanje</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tete" title="Tete">Tete</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vila_Junqueiro" title="Vila Junqueiro">Vila Junqueiro</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vila_Pery" title="Vila Pery">Vila Pery</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vila_Cabral" title="Vila Cabral">Vila Cabral</a> were founded or redeveloped inland during this period and beyond. New coastal towns like <a href="/wiki/Beira,_Mozambique" title="Beira, Mozambique">Beira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namibe" title="Namibe">Moçâmedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobito" title="Lobito">Lobito</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Belo" title="João Belo">João Belo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nacala" title="Nacala">Nacala</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Am%C3%A9lia" title="Porto Amélia">Porto Amélia</a> were also founded. Even before the turn of the 20th century, railway tracks as the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_railway" title="Benguela railway">Benguela railway</a> in Angola, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beira_railway" title="Beira railway">Beira railway</a> in Mozambique, started to be built to link coastal areas and selected inland regions.</p>
<p>Other episodes during this period of the Portuguese presence in Africa include the <a href="/wiki/1890_British_Ultimatum" title="1890 British Ultimatum">1890 British Ultimatum</a>. This forced the Portuguese military to retreat from the land between the Portuguese colonies of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Mozambique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Angola</a> (most of present-day <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>), which had been claimed by Portugal and included in its "<a href="/wiki/Pink_Map" title="Pink Map">Pink Map</a>", which clashed with British aspirations to create a <a href="/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Railway" title="Cape to Cairo Railway">Cape to Cairo Railway</a>.</p>
<p>The Portuguese territories in Africa were <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Cape_Verde" title="Portuguese Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Portuguese São Tomé and Príncipe">São Tomé and Príncipe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Guinea" title="Portuguese Guinea">Portuguese Guinea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angola_(Portugal)" title="Angola (Portugal)">Angola</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozambique_(Portugal)" title="Mozambique (Portugal)">Mozambique</a>. The tiny fortress of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Jo%C3%A3o_Baptista_de_Ajud%C3%A1" title="São João Baptista de Ajudá">São João Baptista de Ajudá</a> on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Dahomey" title="Dahomey">Dahomey</a>, was also under Portuguese rule. In addition, Portugal still ruled the Asian territories of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">Portuguese India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Timor" title="Portuguese Timor">Portuguese Timor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>.</p>
<p>On 1 February 1908, the king Dom <a href="/wiki/Carlos_I_of_Portugal" title="Carlos I of Portugal">Carlos I of Portugal</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_Filipe,_Prince_Royal_of_Portugal" title="Luis Filipe, Prince Royal of Portugal">Prince Royal Dom Luís Filipe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Braganza" title="Duke of Braganza">Duke of Braganza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Regicide" title="Lisbon Regicide">were murdered in Lisbon</a>. Under his rule, Portugal had twice been declared <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankrupt" title="Bankrupt">bankrupt</a> – on 14 June 1892, and again on 10 May 1902 – causing social turmoil, economic disturbances, protests, revolts and criticism of the monarchy. <a href="/wiki/Manuel_II_of_Portugal" title="Manuel II of Portugal">Manuel II of Portugal</a> became the new king, but was eventually overthrown by the <a href="/wiki/5_October_1910_revolution" title="5 October 1910 revolution">5 October 1910 revolution</a>, which abolished the regime and instated <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republicanism</a> in Portugal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Republic_and_Estado_Novo">First Republic and Estado Novo</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_First_Republic" title="Portuguese First Republic">Portuguese First Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo (Portugal)</a></div>
<p>Political instability and economic weaknesses were fertile ground for chaos and unrest during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_First_Republic" title="Portuguese First Republic">Portuguese First Republic</a>. These conditions would lead to the failed <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_North" title="Monarchy of the North">Monarchy of the North</a>, <a href="/wiki/28_May_1926_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="28 May 1926 coup d'état">28 May 1926 coup d'état</a>, and the creation of the National Dictatorship (<i><a href="/wiki/Ditadura_Nacional" title="Ditadura Nacional">Ditadura Nacional</a></i>). This in turn led to the establishment of the right-wing dictatorship of the <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo</a> under <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">António de Oliveira Salazar</a> in 1933.</p>
<p>Portugal was one of only five European countries to remain neutral in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. From the 1940s to the 1960s, Portugal was a founding member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association" title="European Free Trade Association">European Free Trade Association</a> (EFTA). Gradually, new economic development projects and relocation of mainland Portuguese citizens into the overseas provinces in Africa were initiated, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angola_(Portugal)" title="Angola (Portugal)">Angola</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozambique_(Portugal)" title="Mozambique (Portugal)">Mozambique</a>, as the largest and richest overseas territories, being the main targets of those initiatives. These actions were used to affirm Portugal's status as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_transcontinental_countries" title="List of transcontinental countries">transcontinental</a> nation and not as a colonial empire.</p>
<p>After India attained independence in 1947, pro-Indian residents of <a href="/wiki/Dadra_and_Nagar_Haveli" title="Dadra and Nagar Haveli">Dadra and Nagar Haveli</a>, with the support of the Indian government and the help of pro-independence organisations, separated the territories of Dadra and Nagar Haveli from Portuguese rule in 1954. In 1961, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Jo%C3%A3o_Baptista_de_Ajud%C3%A1" title="São João Baptista de Ajudá">São João Baptista de Ajudá</a>'s annexation by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Dahomey" title="Republic of Dahomey">Republic of Dahomey</a> was the start of a process that led to the final dissolution of the centuries-old Portuguese Empire.</p>

<p>According to the census of 1921 São João Baptista de Ajudá had 5 inhabitants and, at the moment of the ultimatum by the Dahomey Government, it had only 2 inhabitants representing Portuguese Sovereignty.</p>
<p>Another forcible retreat from overseas territories occurred in December 1961 when Portugal refused to relinquish the territories of <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daman_and_Diu" title="Daman and Diu">Daman and Diu</a>. As a result, the Portuguese army and navy were involved in armed conflict in its colony of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">Portuguese India</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces" title="Indian Armed Forces">Indian Armed Forces</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Vijay_(1961)" title="Operation Vijay (1961)">operations resulted in the defeat</a> and surrender of the limited Portuguese defensive garrison, which was forced to surrender to a much larger military force. The outcome was the loss of the remaining Portuguese territories in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. The Portuguese regime refused to recognize Indian sovereignty over the annexed territories, which continued to be represented in Portugal's National Assembly until the military coup of 1974.</p>
<p>Also in the early 1960s, independence movements in the Portuguese overseas provinces of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Angola" title="Portuguese Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique" title="Portuguese Mozambique">Mozambique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Guinea" title="Portuguese Guinea">Guinea</a> in Africa, resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a> (1961–1974).</p>
<p>Throughout the colonial war period Portugal had to deal with increasing dissent, arms embargoes and other punitive sanctions imposed by most of the international community. However, the authoritarian and conservative <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo</a> regime, first installed and governed by <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">António de Oliveira Salazar</a> and from 1968 onwards led by <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Caetano" title="Marcelo Caetano">Marcelo Caetano</a>, tried to preserve a vast centuries-long intercontinental empire with a total area of 2,168,071 km.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnation_Revolution_and_European_integration">Carnation Revolution and European integration</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_transition_to_democracy" title="Portuguese transition to democracy">Portuguese transition to democracy</a></div>

<p>The Portuguese government and army resisted the <a href="/wiki/Decolonization" title="Decolonization">decolonization</a> of its overseas territories until April 1974, when a bloodless left-wing military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup">coup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a>, led the way for the independence of the overseas territories in Africa and Asia, as well as for the restoration of democracy after two years of a transitional period known as PREC (<i><a href="/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso" title="Processo Revolucionário Em Curso">Processo Revolucionário Em Curso</a></i>). This period was characterized by social turmoil and power disputes between left- and right-wing political forces. The retreat from the overseas territories and the acceptance of its independence terms by Portuguese head representatives for overseas negotiations, which would create independent states in 1975, prompted a mass exodus of Portuguese citizens from Portugal's African territories (mostly from Portuguese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overseas_Province_of_Angola" title="Overseas Province of Angola">Angola</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overseas_Province_of_Mozambique" title="Overseas Province of Mozambique">Mozambique</a>).</p>
<p>Over one million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_African#Portuguese_in_Africa" title="White African">Portuguese refugees</a> fled the former Portuguese provinces as white settlers were usually not considered part of the new identities of the former Portuguese colonies in Africa and Asia. <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1rio_Soares" title="Mário Soares">Mário Soares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Almeida_Santos" title="António de Almeida Santos">António de Almeida Santos</a> were charged with organising the independence of Portugal's overseas territories. By 1975, all the Portuguese African territories were independent and Portugal held <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constituent_Assembly_election,_1975" title="Portuguese Constituent Assembly election, 1975">its first democratic elections</a> in 50 years.</p>
<p>Portugal continued to be governed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junta_de_Salva%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Nacional" title="Junta de Salvação Nacional">Junta de Salvação Nacional</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_legislative_election,_1976" title="Portuguese legislative election, 1976">Portuguese legislative election of 1976</a>. It was won by the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal)" title="Socialist Party (Portugal)">Portuguese Socialist Party</a> (PS) and Mário Soares, its leader, became Prime Minister of the 1st Constitutional Government on 23 July. Mário Soares would be Prime Minister from 1976 to 1978 and again from 1983 to 1985. In this capacity Soares tried to resume the economic growth and development record that had been achieved before the Carnation Revolution, during the last decade of the previous regime. He initiated the process of accession to the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (EEC) by starting accession negotiations as early as 1977.</p>

<p>Portugal bounced between <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> and adherence to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberal" title="Neoliberal">neoliberal</a> model. <a href="/wiki/Land_reform" title="Land reform">Land reform</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">nationalizations</a> were enforced; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution" title="Portuguese Constitution">Portuguese Constitution</a> (approved in 1976) was rewritten in order to accommodate socialist and communist principles. Until the constitutional revisions of 1982 and 1989, the constitution was a highly charged ideological document with numerous references to socialism, the rights of workers, and the desirability of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_economy" title="Socialist economy">socialist economy</a>. Portugal's economic situation after its transition to democracy, obliged the government to pursue <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> (IMF)-monitored stabilization programs in 1977–78 and 1983–85.</p>
<p>In 1986, Portugal joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (EEC) that later became the European Union (EU). In the following years Portugal's economy progressed considerably as a result of EEC/EU <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_and_cohesion_funds" title="Structural and cohesion funds">structural and cohesion funds</a> and Portuguese companies' easier access to foreign markets.</p>
<p>Portugal's last overseas territory, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, was peacefully handed over to the People's Republic of China (PRC) in 1999, under the 1987 joint declaration that set the terms for Macau's handover from Portugal to the PRC. In 2002, the independence of <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> (Asia) was formally recognized by Portugal, after an incomplete decolonization process that was started in 1975 because of the Carnation Revolution, but interrupted by an <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> armed invasion and <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_occupation_of_East_Timor" title="Indonesian occupation of East Timor">occupation</a>.</p>

<p>On 26 March 1995, Portugal started to implement <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area" title="Schengen Area">Schengen Area</a> rules, eliminating border controls with other Schengen members while simultaneously strengthening border controls with non-member states. In 1996 the country was a co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries" title="Community of Portuguese Language Countries">Community of Portuguese Language Countries</a> (CPLP) headquartered in Lisbon. <a href="/wiki/Expo_%2798" title="Expo '98">Expo '98</a> took place in Portugal and in 1999 it was one of the founding countries of the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a>.</p>
<p>On 5 July 2004, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso" title="José Manuel Barroso">José Manuel Barroso</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">Prime Minister of Portugal</a>, was nominated <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Commission" title="President of the European Commission">President of the European Commission</a>, the most powerful office in the European Union. On 1 December 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> entered into force, after it had been signed by the European Union member states on 13 December 2007 in the <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimos_Monastery" title="Jerónimos Monastery">Jerónimos Monastery</a>, in Lisbon, enhancing the efficiency and democratic legitimacy of the Union and improving the coherence of its action.</p>
<p>Economic disruption and an unsustainable growth in <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">borrowing costs</a> in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">late-2000s financial crisis</a> led the country to negotiate in 2011 with the IMF and the European Union, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Financial_Stability_Mechanism" title="European Financial Stability Mechanism">European Financial Stability Mechanism</a> (EFSM) and the <a href="/wiki/European_Financial_Stability_Facility" title="European Financial Stability Facility">European Financial Stability Facility</a> (EFSF), a loan to help the country stabilise its finances.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Portugal" title="Geography of Portugal">Geography of Portugal</a></div>

<p>The territory of Portugal includes an area in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> (referred to as <i>the continent</i> by most Portuguese) and two archipelagos in the Atlantic Ocean: the archipelagos of Madeira and the Azores. It lies between latitudes <a href="/wiki/32nd_parallel_north" title="32nd parallel north">32°</a> and <a href="/wiki/43rd_parallel_north" title="43rd parallel north">43° N</a>, and longitudes <a href="/wiki/32nd_meridian_west" title="32nd meridian west">32°</a> and <a href="/wiki/6th_meridian_west" title="6th meridian west">6° W</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Continental_Portugal" title="Continental Portugal">Mainland Portugal</a> is split by its main river, the <a href="/wiki/Tagus" title="Tagus">Tagus</a> that flows from Spain and disgorges in Tagus Estuary, in Lisbon, before escaping into the Atlantic. The northern landscape is mountainous towards the interior with several plateaus indented by river valleys, whereas the south, that includes the <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a> regions, is characterized by rolling plains.</p>
<p>Portugal's highest peak is the similarly named <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pico" title="Mount Pico">Mount Pico</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Pico_Island" title="Pico Island">Pico</a> in the Azores. This ancient volcano, which measures 2,351 m (7,713 ft) is an iconic symbol of the Azores, while the <a href="/wiki/Serra_da_Estrela" title="Serra da Estrela">Serra da Estrela</a> on the mainland (the summit being 1,991 m (6,532 ft) above sea level) is an important seasonal attraction for skiers and winter sports enthusiasts.</p>
<p>The archipelagos of Madeira and the Azores are scattered within the Atlantic Ocean: the Azores straddling the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge" title="Mid-Atlantic Ridge">Mid-Atlantic Ridge</a> on a tectonic triple junction, and Madeira along a range formed by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">in-plate</a> <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> geology. Geologically, these islands were formed by volcanic and seismic events. The last terrestrial volcanic eruption occurred in 1957–58 (<a href="/wiki/Capelinhos" title="Capelinhos">Capelinhos</a>) and minor earthquakes occur sporadically, usually of low intensity.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone_of_Portugal" title="Exclusive economic zone of Portugal">Portugal's exclusive economic zone</a>, a sea zone over which the Portuguese have special rights over the exploration and use of marine resources, has 1,727,408 km. This is the 3rd largest <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> of the European Union and the 11th largest in the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_of_Portugal" title="Climate of Portugal">Climate of Portugal</a></div>
<p>Portugal is defined as a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> (<i>Csa</i> in the South, interior, and Douro region; <i>Csb</i> in the North, Central Portugal and coastal Alentejo; mixed <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> along the northern half of the coastline and also <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">Semi-arid climate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steppe_climate" title="Steppe climate">Steppe climate</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_semi-arid_climate" title="Cold semi-arid climate">BSk</a></i> in certain parts of Beja district far South) according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen-Geiger Climate Classification</a>), and is one of the warmest European countries: the annual average temperature in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainland_Portugal" title="Mainland Portugal">mainland Portugal</a> varies from 8–12 °C (46.4–53.6 °F) in the mountainous interior north to 16–19 °C (60.8–66.2 °F) in the south and on the <a href="/wiki/Guadiana" title="Guadiana">Guadiana</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_basin" title="River basin">river basin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, separated from the <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a> region by mountains reaching up to 900 metres (3,000 ft) in Alto de Fóia, has a climate similar to that of the southern coastal areas of Spain or Southwest Australia.</p>
<p>Annual average rainfall in the mainland varies from just over 3,200 mm (126.0 in) in the northern mountains to less than 300 mm (11.8 in) in the area of the Massueime River, near Côa, along the <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> river. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pico" title="Mount Pico">Mount Pico</a> is recognized as receiving the largest annual rainfall (over 6,250 mm (246.1 in) per year) in Portugal, according to <i><a href="/wiki/Instituto_Portugu%C3%AAs_do_Mar_e_da_Atmosfera" title="Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera">Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera</a></i> (English: Portuguese Institute of the Sea and the Atmosphere).</p>
<p>In some areas, such as the Guadiana basin, annual average temperatures can be as high as 28 °C (82 °F), and summer highest temperatures routinely are over 40 °C (104 °F). The record high of 47.4 °C (117.3 °F) was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Amareleja" title="Amareleja">Amareleja</a>, although this might not be the hottest spot in summer, according to satellite readings.</p>
<p>Snowfalls occur regularly in the winter in the interior North and Centre of the country in districts such as <a href="/wiki/Vila_Real_District" title="Vila Real District">Vila Real</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_District" title="Bragança District">Bragança</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viseu" title="Viseu">Viseu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guarda_District" title="Guarda District">Guarda</a>. In winter temperatures may drop below −10.0 °C (14.0 °F) in particular in <a href="/wiki/Serra_da_Estrela" title="Serra da Estrela">Serra da Estrela</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serra_do_Ger%C3%AAs" title="Serra do Gerês">Serra do Gerês</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serra_do_Mar%C3%A3o" title="Serra do Marão">Serra do Marão</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montesinho_Natural_Park" title="Montesinho Natural Park">Serra de Montesinho</a> and the lowest temperature ever has been -17 C near Miranda do Douro. In these places snow can fall any time from October to May. In the South of the country snowfalls are rare but still occur in the highest elevations.</p>
<p>Portugal has around 2500 to 3200 hours of sunshine a year, an average of 4–6 h in winter and 10–12 h in the summer, with higher values in the south-east and lower in the north-west.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> on the west coast of mainland Portugal varies from 13–15 °C (55.4–59.0 °F) in winter to 18–22 °C (64.4–71.6 °F) in the summer while on the south coast it ranges from 15 °C (59.0 °F) in winter and rises in the summer to about 23 °C (73.4 °F) occasionally reaching 24 °C (75.2 °F).</p>
<p>Both the archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira have a subtropical climate, although variations between islands exist, making weather predictions very difficult (owing to rough topography). The Madeira and Azorean archipelagos have a narrower temperature range, with annual average temperatures exceeding 20 °C (68 °F) along the coast (according to the Portuguese Meteorological Institute). Some islands in Azores do have drier months in the summer. Consequently, the island of the Azores have been identified as having a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> climate (both <i>Csa</i> and <i>Csb</i> types), while some islands (such as <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">Flores</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corvo_Island" title="Corvo Island">Corvo</a>) are classified as Maritime Temperate (<i>Cfb</i>) and Humid subtropical (<i>Cfa</i>), respectively, according to Köppen-Geiger classification.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Santo" title="Porto Santo">Porto Santo</a> island in Madeira has a semi-arid steppe climate (<i>BSh</i>). The <a href="/wiki/Savage_Islands" title="Savage Islands">Savage Islands</a>, which are part of the regional territory of Madeira and a nature reserve are unique in being classified as a desert climate (<i>BWh</i>) with an annual average rainfall of approximately 150 mm (5.9 in). The sea surface temperature in the archipelagos varies from 17–18 °C (62.6–64.4 °F) in winter to 24–25 °C (75.2–77.0 °F) in the summer occasionally reaching 26 °C (78.8 °F).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span></h3>

<p>Despite the fact that humans have occupied the territory of Portugal for thousands of years, something still remains of the original vegetation. In Gerês both mature deciduous and coniferous forests can be found, an extremely rare worldwide mature Mediterranean forest remain in some parts of the Arrábida mountain and a subtropical laurissilva forest, dating back to the Tertiary period, covers its largest continuous area in the world in the Madeira main island. Due to the human population decrease and rural exodus, Pyrenean oak and other local native trees are colonizing many abandoned areas.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">Boar</a>, Iberian red deer, roe deer, and the Iberian wild goat, are reported to have expanded greatly during recent decades. Boars were found recently roaming at night inside large urban areas, like in Setubal. <a href="/wiki/Protected_areas_of_Portugal" title="Protected areas of Portugal">Protected areas of Portugal</a> include one national park (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Parque Nacional</i></span>), 12 natural parks (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Parque Natural</i></span>), nine natural reserves (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Reserva Natural</i></span>), five natural monuments (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Monumento Natural</i></span>), and seven protected landscapes (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Paisagem Protegida</i></span>), which include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parque_Nacional_da_Peneda-Ger%C3%AAs" title="Parque Nacional da Peneda-Gerês">Parque Nacional da Peneda-Gerês</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parque_Natural_da_Serra_da_Estrela" title="Parque Natural da Serra da Estrela">Parque Natural da Serra da Estrela</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paul_de_Arzila" title="Paul de Arzila">Paul d'Arzila</a>.</p>
<p>These natural environments are shaped by diverse flora, and include widespread species of pine (especially the <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_pinaster" title="Pinus pinaster">Pinus pinaster</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinus_pinea" title="Pinus pinea">Pinus pinea</a></i> species), the English oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">Quercus robur</a></i>), the Pyrenean oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_pyrenaica" title="Quercus pyrenaica">Quercus pyrenaica</a></i>) the chestnut (<i><a href="/wiki/Castanea_sativa" title="Castanea sativa">Castanea sativa</a></i>), the cork-oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_suber" title="Quercus suber">Quercus suber</a></i>), the holm oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_ilex" title="Quercus ilex">Quercus ilex</a></i>) or the Portuguese oak (<i><a href="/wiki/Quercus_faginea" title="Quercus faginea">Quercus faginea</a></i>). Due to their economic value, some species of the <i>Eucalyptus</i> genus were introduced and are now common, despite their environmental impact.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurisilva" title="Laurisilva">Laurisilva</a> is a unique type of subtropical rainforest found in few areas of Europe and the world: in the Azores, and in particular on the island of Madeira, there are large forests of endemic <i>Laurisilva</i> forests (the latter protected as a natural heritage preserve). There are several species of diverse <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> fauna, including the <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">fox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badger" title="Badger">badger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_lynx" title="Iberian lynx">iberian lynx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_wolf" title="Iberian wolf">iberian wolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_goat" title="Wild goat">wild goat</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capra_pyrenaica" title="Capra pyrenaica">Capra pyrenaica</a></i>), wild cat (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felis_silvestris" title="Felis silvestris">Felis silvestris</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weasel" title="Weasel">weasel</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_polecat" title="European polecat">polecat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chameleon" title="Chameleon">chameleon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongoose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civet" title="Civet">civet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bear</a> (spotted near Rio Minho, close to Peneda-Gerês) and many others. Portugal is an important stopover for migratory birds, in places such as <a href="/wiki/Cape_St._Vincent" title="Cape St. Vincent">Cape St. Vincent</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Serra_de_Monchique" title="Serra de Monchique">Monchique</a> mountains, where thousands of birds cross from Europe to Africa during the autumn or in the spring (return migration).</p>

<p>Most of the avian species congregate along the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> since it is the closest stopover between Northern Europe and Africa. Six hundred bird species occur in Portugal (either for nesting or during the course of migration), and annually there are new registries of nesting species. The archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira are transient stopover for American, European, and African birds, while continental Portugal mostly encounters European and African bird species.</p>
<p>There are more than 100 freshwater fish species, varying from the giant European catfish (in the Tagus International Natural Park) to some small and endemic species that live only in small lakes (along the western portion of country, for example). Some of these rare and specific species are highly endangered because of habitat loss, pollution and drought. Up-welling along the west coast of Portugal makes the sea extremely rich in nutrients and diverse species of marine fish; the Portuguese marine waters are one of the richest in the world. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_fish" title="Marine fish">Marine fish</a> species are more common, and include thousands of species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardine</a> (<i>Sardina pilchardus</i>), <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_mackerel" title="Atlantic mackerel">Atlantic mackerel</a>. Bioluminescent species are also well represented (including species in different colour spectrum and forms), like the glowing plankton that are possible to observe in some beaches.</p>
<p>There are many endemic insect species, most only found in certain parts of Portugal, while other species are more widespread like the <a href="/wiki/Stag_beetle" title="Stag beetle">stag beetle</a> (<i>Lucanus cervus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Cicada" title="Cicada">cicada</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesian</a> islands (Azores and Madeira) have many endemic species (like birds, reptiles, bats, insects, snails and slugs) that evolved independently from other regions of Portugal. In Madeira, for example, it is possible to observe more than 250 species of land <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropods" title="Gastropods">gastropods</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_administration">Government and administration</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">Government of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Portugal" title="Politics of Portugal">Politics of Portugal</a></div>

<p>Portugal has been a <a href="/wiki/Semi-presidential_system" title="Semi-presidential system">semi-presidential</a> <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">representative democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a> since the ratification of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution_of_1976" title="Portuguese Constitution of 1976">Constitution of 1976</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, the nation's largest city, as its capital. The Constitution grants the division or separation of powers among four bodies referred as "organs of Sovereignty": the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">President of the Republic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">Government</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_parliament" title="Portuguese parliament">Assembly of the Republic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_of_Portugal" title="Judiciary of Portugal">Courts</a>.</p>
<p>The President, who is elected to a five-year term, has an executive role: the current President is <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Rebelo_de_Sousa" title="Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa">Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa</a>. The Assembly of the Republic is a single chamber parliament composed of 230 deputies elected for a four-year term. The Government is headed by the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Portugal" title="List of Prime Ministers of Portugal">Prime Minister</a> (currently <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Costa" title="António Costa">António Costa</a>) and includes Ministers and Secretaries of State. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courts" title="Courts">Courts</a> are organized into several levels, among the judicial, administrative and fiscal branches. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Supreme_Court" title="Portuguese Supreme Court">Supreme Courts</a> are institutions of last resort/appeal. A thirteen-member <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitutional_Court" title="Portuguese Constitutional Court">Constitutional Court</a> oversees the constitutionality of the laws.</p>
<p>Portugal operates a multi-party system of competitive legislatures/local administrative governments at the national, regional and local levels. The Assembly of the Republic, Regional Assemblies and local municipalities and parishes, are dominated by two political parties, the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal)" title="Socialist Party (Portugal)">Socialist Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(Portugal)" title="Social Democratic Party (Portugal)">Social Democratic Party</a>, in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_Democratic_Coalition" title="Unitary Democratic Coalition">Unitary Democratic Coalition</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Communist_Party" title="Portuguese Communist Party">Portuguese Communist Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Party_%22The_Greens%22" title='Ecologist Party "The Greens"'>Ecologist Party "The Greens"</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Left_Bloc" title="Left Bloc">Left Bloc</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_and_Social_Centre_%E2%80%93_People%27s_Party" title="Democratic and Social Centre – People's Party">Democratic and Social Centre – People's Party</a>, which garner between 5 and 15% of the vote regularly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency_of_the_Republic">Presidency of the Republic</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">President of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Portugal" title="List of Presidents of Portugal">List of Presidents of Portugal</a></div>
<p>The Head of State of Portugal is the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Portugal" title="President of Portugal">President of the Republic</a>, elected to a five-year term by direct, <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a>. He or she has also supervision and <a href="/wiki/Reserve_power" title="Reserve power">reserve powers</a>. Presidential powers include the appointment of the Prime Minister and the other members of the Government (where the President takes into account the results of legislative elections); dismissing the Prime Minister; dissolving the Assembly of the Republic (to call early elections); <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">vetoing</a> legislation (which may be overridden by the Assembly); and declaring a state of war or siege. The President is also the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex_officio" title="Ex officio">ex officio</a></i> Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces.</p>
<p>The President is advised on issues of importance by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Council_of_State" title="Portuguese Council of State">Council of State</a>, which is composed of six senior civilian officers, any former Presidents elected under the 1976 Constitution, five-members chosen by the Assembly, and five selected by the president.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">Government of Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">Prime Minister of Portugal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Portugal" title="List of Prime Ministers of Portugal">List of Prime Ministers of Portugal</a></div>

<p>The Government is headed by the presidentially appointed <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">Prime Minister</a>, also including (may have one or more Deputy Prime Ministers), Ministers, Secretaries of State and Under-Secretaries of State.</p>
<p>The Government is both the organ of sovereignty that conducts the general politics of the country and the superior body of the public administration.</p>
<p>It has essentially Executive powers, but has also limited Legislative powers. The Government can legislate about its own organization, about areas covered by legislative authorizations conceded by the Assembly of the Republic and about the specific regulation of generalist laws issued by the Assembly.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Portugal)" title="Council of Ministers (Portugal)">Council of Ministers</a> – under the presidency of the Prime Minister (or the President of Portugal at the latter's request) and the Ministers (may also include one or more Deputy Prime Ministers) – acts as the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a>. Each government is required to define the broad outline of its policies in a programme, and present it to the Assembly for a mandatory period of debate. The failure of the Assembly to reject the government programme by an absolute majority of deputies confirms the cabinet in office.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliament">Parliament</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Assembly of the Republic</a> is the national <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a> of Portugal. It is the main Legislative body, although the Government also has limited legislative powers.</p>
<p>The Assembly of the Republic is a unicameral body composed of up to 230 deputies. Elected by universal suffrage according to a system of proportional representation, deputies serve four-year terms of office, unless the President dissolves the Assembly and calls for new elections.</p>
<p>Currently the Government (PS) and its unofficial coalition (BE, PCP, PEV) controls parliament with the most seats. The PSD and CDS-PP form the opposition to the government alongside a single seat held by the PAN.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_and_criminal_justice">Law and criminal justice</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_of_Portugal" title="Judiciary of Portugal">Judiciary of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Portugal" title="Law of Portugal">Law of Portugal</a></div>
<p>The Portuguese legal system is part of the civil law legal system, also called the continental family legal system. The main laws include the Constitution (1976, as amended), the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Civil_Code" title="Portuguese Civil Code">Portuguese Civil Code</a> (1966, as amended) and the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Code_of_Portugal" title="Penal Code of Portugal">Penal Code of Portugal</a> (1982, as amended). Other relevant laws are the <i>Commercial Code</i> (1888, as amended) and the <i>Civil Procedure Code</i> (1961, as amended).</p>
<p>The supreme national courts are the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Justice_(Portugal)" title="Supreme Court of Justice (Portugal)">Supreme Court of Justice</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Portugal" title="Constitutional Court of Portugal">Constitutional Court</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Public_Ministry_(Portugal)" title="Public Ministry (Portugal)">Public Ministry</a>, headed by the Attorney General of the Republic, constitutes the independent body of public prosecutors.</p>
<p>Portuguese law applied in the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonies" title="Portuguese colonies">colonies and territories</a> and continues to be the major influence for those countries.</p>
<p>Portugal was one of the first countries in the world to abolish the death penalty. Maximum jail sentences are limited to 25 years.</p>
<p>Portugal is also known for having decriminalized the usage of all common drugs in 2001, the first country in the world to do so. Portugal decriminalized possession of effectively all drugs that are still illegal in other developed nations including <a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">cannabis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a>. While possession is legal, trafficking and possession of more than "10 days worth of personal use" are still punishable by jail time and fines. People caught with small amounts of any drug are given the choice to go to a rehab facility, and may refuse treatment without consequences. Despite criticism from other European nations, who stated Portugal's drug consumption would tremendously increase, overall drug use has declined along with the number of <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> infection cases, which had dropped 50 percent by 2009. Drug use among 16- to 18-year-olds also declined, however the use of marijuana rose only slightly among that age group.</p>
<p>On 31 May 2010, Portugal became the sixth country in Europe and the eighth country in the world to legally recognize <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Portugal" title="Same-sex marriage in Portugal">same-sex marriage</a> on the national level. The law came into force on 5 June 2010.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement">Law enforcement</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_Portugal" title="Law enforcement in Portugal">Law enforcement in Portugal</a></div>
<p>Portugal's main police organizations are the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guarda_Nacional_Republicana" title="Guarda Nacional Republicana">Guarda Nacional Republicana</a> – GNR</i> (<a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Guard_(Portugal)" title="National Republican Guard (Portugal)">National Republican Guard</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Gendarmerie" title="Gendarmerie">gendarmerie</a>; the <i><a href="/wiki/Pol%C3%ADcia_de_Seguran%C3%A7a_P%C3%BAblica" title="Polícia de Segurança Pública">Polícia de Segurança Pública</a> – PSP</i> (Public Security Police), a civilian police force who work in urban areas; and the <i><a href="/wiki/Pol%C3%ADcia_Judici%C3%A1ria" title="Polícia Judiciária">Polícia Judiciária</a> – PJ</i> (Judicial Police), a highly specialized criminal investigation police that is overseen by the <a href="/wiki/Public_Ministry_(Portugal)" title="Public Ministry (Portugal)">Public Ministry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_divisions">Administrative divisions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Portugal" title="Administrative divisions of Portugal">Administrative divisions of Portugal</a></div>
<p>Administratively, Portugal is divided into 308 <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Portugal" title="Municipalities of Portugal">municipalities</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>municípios or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concelhos" title="Concelhos">concelhos</a></i></span>), which after a reform in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_local_election,_2013" title="Portuguese local election, 2013">2013</a> are subdivided into 3,092 civil parishes (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i><a href="/wiki/Freguesia" title="Freguesia">freguesia</a></i></span>). Operationally, the municipality and civil parish, along with the national government, are the only legally identifiable <a href="/wiki/Local_administrative_unit" title="Local administrative unit">local administrative units</a> identified by the government of Portugal (for example, cities, towns or villages have no standing in law, although may be used as catchment for the defining services). For statistical purposes the Portuguese government also identifies <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">NUTS</a>, inter-municipal communities and informally, the district system, used until European integration (and being phased-out by the national government). Continental Portugal is agglomerated into 18 districts, while the archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira are governed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_area" title="Autonomous area">autonomous regions</a>; the largest units, established since 1976, are either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainland_Portugal" title="Mainland Portugal">mainland Portugal</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>Portugal Continental</i></span>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_Portugal" title="Autonomous regions of Portugal">autonomous regions of Portugal</a> (<a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>).</p>
<p>The 18 districts of mainland Portugal are: <a href="/wiki/Aveiro_District" title="Aveiro District">Aveiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beja_District" title="Beja District">Beja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braga_District" title="Braga District">Braga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_District" title="Bragança District">Bragança</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castelo_Branco_District" title="Castelo Branco District">Castelo Branco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coimbra_District" title="Coimbra District">Coimbra</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vora_District" title="Évora District">Évora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro_District" title="Faro District">Faro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guarda_District" title="Guarda District">Guarda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leiria_District" title="Leiria District">Leiria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_District" title="Lisbon District">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portalegre_District" title="Portalegre District">Portalegre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto_District" title="Porto District">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m_District" title="Santarém District">Santarém</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal_District" title="Setúbal District">Setúbal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viana_do_Castelo_District" title="Viana do Castelo District">Viana do Castelo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vila_Real_District" title="Vila Real District">Vila Real</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viseu_District" title="Viseu District">Viseu</a> – each district takes the name of the district capital.</p>
<p>Within the European Union NUTS (<a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics</a>) system, Portugal is divided into seven regions: the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algarve" title="Algarve">Algarve</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centro,_Portugal" title="Centro, Portugal">Centro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisboa_Region" title="Lisboa Region">Lisboa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norte,_Portugal" title="Norte, Portugal">Norte</a>, and with the exception of the Azores and Madeira, these NUTS areas are subdivided into 28 subregions.</p>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span></h3>


<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Portugal" title="Foreign relations of Portugal">Foreign relations of Portugal</a></div>
<p>A member state of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> since 1955, Portugal is also a founding member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> (1949), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> (1961) and <a href="/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association" title="European Free Trade Association">EFTA</a> (1960); it left the latter in 1986 to join the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, which became the European Union in 1993. In 1996 it co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries" title="Community of Portuguese Language Countries">Community of Portuguese Language Countries</a> (CPLP), which seeks to foster closer economic and cultural ties between the world's <a href="/wiki/Lusophone" title="Lusophone">Lusophone</a> nations.</p>
<p>In addition, Portugal is a full member of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Union" title="Latin Union">Latin Union</a> (1983) and the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Ibero-American_States" title="Organization of Ibero-American States">Organization of Ibero-American States</a> (1949). It has a friendship alliance and <a href="/wiki/Multiple_citizenship" title="Multiple citizenship">dual citizenship</a> treaty with its former colony, Brazil. Portugal and England (subsequently, the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland) share the world's oldest active military accord through their <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Portuguese Alliance">Anglo-Portuguese Alliance</a> (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1386)" title="Treaty of Windsor (1386)">Treaty of Windsor</a>), which was signed in 1373.</p>
<p>There are two international territorial disputes, both with Spain:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Olivenza" title="Olivenza">Olivenza</a>. Under Portuguese sovereignty since 1297, the municipality of Olivenza was ceded to Spain under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Badajoz_(1801)" title="Treaty of Badajoz (1801)">Treaty of Badajoz</a> in 1801, after the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Oranges" title="War of the Oranges">War of the Oranges</a>. Portugal claimed it back in 1815 under the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Treaty of Vienna</a>. However, since the 19th century, it has been continuously ruled by Spain which considers the territory theirs not only <i>de facto</i> but also <i>de jure</i>.</li>
<li>The Ilhas Selvagens (<a href="/wiki/Savage_Islands" title="Savage Islands">Savage Islands</a>). The archipelago is under Portuguese domination but is geographically closer to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> (165 km) than to <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> (280 km). Found in 1364 by Italian navigators, the islands belonged to private owners until 1971, when the Portuguese government bought them and established a natural reserve area covering the whole archipelago. The islands have been claimed by Spain since 1911 and the dispute has caused some periods of political tension between the two countries. The main problem is not so much their intrinsic value but the fact that they expand considerably to the south the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_Economic_Zone" title="Exclusive Economic Zone">Exclusive Economic Zone</a> of Portugal.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces" title="Portuguese Armed Forces">Portuguese Armed Forces</a></div>

<p>The armed forces have three branches: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Navy" title="Portuguese Navy">Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Army" title="Portuguese Army">Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Air_Force" title="Portuguese Air Force">Air Force</a>. They serve primarily as a self-defense force whose mission is to protect the territorial integrity of the country and provide humanitarian assistance and security at home and abroad. As of 2008, the three branches numbered 39,200 active personnel including 7,500 women. <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" title="List of countries by military expenditures">Portuguese military expenditure</a> in 2009 was $5.2 billion, representing 2.1 percent of GDP. Military conscription was abolished in 2004. The minimum age for voluntary recruitment is 18 years.</p>
<p>The Army (21,000 personnel) comprises three brigades and other small units. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Intervention_Brigade" title="Portuguese Intervention Brigade">infantry brigade</a> (mainly equipped with <a href="/wiki/Pandur_II" title="Pandur II">Pandur II</a> <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">APC</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Mechanized_Brigade" title="Portuguese Mechanized Brigade">mechanized brigade</a> (mainly equipped with <a href="/wiki/Leopard_2" title="Leopard 2">Leopard 2 A6</a> tanks and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M113" title="M113">M113</a> APC) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Rapid_Reaction_Brigade" title="Portuguese Rapid Reaction Brigade">Rapid Reaction Brigade</a> (consisting of paratroopers, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Army_Commandos" title="Portuguese Army Commandos">commandos</a> and rangers). The Navy (10,700 personnel, of which 1,580 are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Marines" title="Portuguese Marines">marines</a>) has five frigates, seven corvettes, two submarines, and 28 patrol and auxiliary vessels. The Air Force (7,500 personnel) has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="Lockheed F-16 Fighting Falcon">Lockheed F-16 Fighting Falcon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dassault/Dornier_Alpha_Jet" title="Dassault/Dornier Alpha Jet">Dassault/Dornier Alpha Jet</a> as the main combat aircraft.</p>
<p>In addition to the three branches of the armed forces, there is the <a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Guard_(Portugal)" title="National Republican Guard (Portugal)">National Republican Guard</a>, a security force subject to military law and organization (<a href="/wiki/Gendarmerie" title="Gendarmerie">gendarmerie</a>) comprising 25,000 personnel. This force is under the authority of both the Defense and the Interior Ministry. It has provided detachments for participation in international operations in Iraq and East Timor.</p>
<p>The United States maintains a military presence with 770 troops in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lajes_Air_Base" title="Lajes Air Base">Lajes Air Base</a> at <a href="/wiki/Terceira_Island" title="Terceira Island">Terceira Island</a>, in the Azores. The <a href="/wiki/Allied_Joint_Force_Command_Lisbon" title="Allied Joint Force Command Lisbon">Allied Joint Force Command Lisbon</a> (JFC Lisbon) – one of the three main subdivisions of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allied_Command_Operations" title="Allied Command Operations">Allied Command Operations</a> – it is based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oeiras_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Oeiras Municipality, Portugal">Oeiras</a>, near Lisbon.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, Portugal engaged in two major conflicts: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Great_War" title="Portugal in the Great War">World War I</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a> (1961–1974). After the end of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a> in 1975, the Portuguese Armed Forces have participated in peacekeeping missions in East Timor, Bosnia, Kosovo, Afghanistan, Somalia, Iraq (<a href="/wiki/Nasiriyah" title="Nasiriyah">Nasiriyah</a>) and Lebanon. Portugal also conducted several independent unilateral military operations abroad, as were the cases of the interventions of the Portuguese Armed Forces in Angola in 1992 and in Guinea-Bissau in 1998 with the main objectives of protecting and withdrawing of Portuguese and foreign citizens threatened by local civil conflicts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_finance">Government finance</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9314_Portuguese_financial_crisis" title="2010–14 Portuguese financial crisis">2010–14 Portuguese financial crisis</a></div>

<p>The Portuguese government is heavily indebted, and received a 78 billion euro bailout from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> in May 2011. The ratio of Portugal’s debt to its overall economy, was 107 percent when it received the bailout. As part of the deal, the country agreed to cut its budget deficit from 9.8 percent of GDP in 2010 to 5.9 percent in 2011, 4.5 percent in 2012 and 3 percent in 2013.</p>
<p>After the bailout was announced, the Portuguese government headed by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Passos_Coelho" title="Pedro Passos Coelho">Pedro Passos Coelho</a> managed to implement measures with the intention of improving the state's financial situation, including tax hikes, a freeze of civil service-related lower-wages and cuts of higher-wages by 14.3%, on top of the government's spending cuts. The Portuguese government also agreed to eliminate its <a href="/wiki/Golden_share" title="Golden share">golden share</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portugal_Telecom" title="Portugal Telecom">Portugal Telecom</a> which gave it veto power over vital decisions. In 2012, all public servants had already seen an average wage cut of 20% relative to their 2010 baseline, with cuts reaching 25% for those earning more than 1,500 euro per month.</p>
<p>The IMF, the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> (EC) and the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">European Central Bank</a> (ECB) said in September 2012 that Portugal’s debt would peak at 124 percent of gross domestic product in 2014. The IMF previously said in July 2012 that Portugal’s debt would peak at about 118.5 percent of GDP in 2013. In September 2013, the Portuguese Government reviewed again the public debt of Portugal for 2013 to 127.8 percent, after a peak of 130.9 percent in that month.</p>
<p>A report released in January 2011 by the <a href="/wiki/Di%C3%A1rio_de_Not%C3%ADcias" title="Diário de Notícias">Diário de Notícias</a> and published in Portugal by <a href="/wiki/Gradiva" title="Gradiva">Gradiva</a>, had demonstrated that in the period between the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> in 1974 and 2010, the democratic <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Portugal" title="Government of Portugal">Portuguese Republic governments</a> encouraged over-expenditure and investment bubbles through unclear <a href="/wiki/Public%E2%80%93private_partnership" title="Public–private partnership">Public–private partnerships</a> and funding of numerous ineffective and unnecessary external consultancy and advisory of committees and firms. This allowed considerable <a href="/wiki/Slippage_(finance)" title="Slippage (finance)">slippage</a> in state-managed <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a> and inflated top management and head officer bonuses and wages. Persistent and lasting recruitment policies boosted the number of redundant public servants. Risky <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_debt" title="Public debt">public debt</a> creation, and European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_and_cohesion_funds" title="Structural and cohesion funds">structural and cohesion funds</a> were mismanaged across almost four decades.</p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">financial crisis of 2007–08</a>, it was known in 2008–2009 that two Portuguese banks (<a href="/wiki/Banco_Portugu%C3%AAs_de_Neg%C3%B3cios" title="Banco Português de Negócios">Banco Português de Negócios</a> (BPN) and <a href="/wiki/Banco_Privado_Portugu%C3%AAs" title="Banco Privado Português">Banco Privado Português</a> (BPP)) had been accumulating losses for years due to bad investments, embezzlement and accounting fraud. The case of BPN was particularly serious because of its size, market share, and the political implications - Portugal's then current President, Cavaco Silva, and some of his political allies, maintained personal and business relationships with the bank and its CEO, who was eventually charged and arrested for fraud and other crimes. In the grounds of avoiding a potentially serious financial crisis in the Portuguese economy, the Portuguese government decided to give them a bailout, eventually at a future loss to taxpayers and to the Portuguese people in general.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Portugal" title="Economy of Portugal">Economy of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal" title="Economic history of Portugal">Economic history of Portugal</a></div>

<p>Portugal is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_income_countries" title="High income countries">high income country</a>, with its GDP per capita in 2014 being 78% of the EU27 average - increasing from 76% in 2012. The Portuguese currency is the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> (€), which replaced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Escudo" title="Portuguese Escudo">Portuguese Escudo</a>, and the country was one of the original member states of the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a>. Portugal's central bank is the <i><a href="/wiki/Banco_de_Portugal" title="Banco de Portugal">Banco de Portugal</a></i>, an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/European_System_of_Central_Banks" title="European System of Central Banks">European System of Central Banks</a>. Most industries, businesses and financial institutions are concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Metropolitan_Area" title="Lisbon Metropolitan Area">Lisbon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Porto" title="Greater Metropolitan Area of Porto">Porto</a> metropolitan areas—the <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal_District" title="Setúbal District">Setúbal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aveiro_District" title="Aveiro District">Aveiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braga_District" title="Braga District">Braga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coimbra_District" title="Coimbra District">Coimbra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leiria_District" title="Leiria District">Leiria</a> districts are the biggest economic centres outside these two main areas. According to World Travel Awards, Portugal is the Europe's Leading Golf Destination 2012 and 2013.</p>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> of 1974, which culminated in the end of one of Portugal's most notable <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal" title="Economic history of Portugal">phases of economic expansion</a> (that started in the 1960s), a significant change has occurred in the nation's annual economic growth. After the turmoil of the 1974 revolution and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PREC" title="PREC">PREC</a> period, Portugal tried to adapt to a changing <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">modern global economy</a>, a process that continues in 2013. Since the 1990s, Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">public consumption</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> model has been slowly changing to a system that is focused on exports, <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">private investment</a> and the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-tech" title="High-tech">high-tech</a> sector. Consequently, business services have overtaken more traditional industries such as textiles, clothing, footwear and <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a> (Portugal is the world's leading cork producer), wood products and beverages.</p>

<p>In the second decade of the 21st century the Portuguese economy suffered its most severe recession since the 1970s resulting in the country having to be bailed out by the European Commission, European Central Bank and International Monetary Fund. The bailout, agreed to in 2011, required Portugal to enter into a range of austerity measures in exchange for funding support of €78 billion. In May 2014 the country exited the bailout but reaffirmed its commitment to maintaining its reformist momentum. At the time of exiting the bailout the economy had contracted by 0.7% in the first quarter of 2014, however unemployment, while still high had fallen to 15.3 percent.</p>
<p>The average wage in Portugal is 910 € per month (net), excluding self-employed individuals and the <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">minimum wage</a>, which is regulated/ref by law, is €530 per month (paid 14 times per annum).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Global_Competitiveness_Report" title="Global Competitiveness Report">Global Competitiveness Report</a> for 2014-2015, published by the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a>, placed Portugal on the 36th position on the economic index. This represents a sharp increase from the 51st position where Portugal appeared in 2013-2014.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a>'s quality of life index placed Portugal as the country with the 19th-best quality of life in the world for 2005, ahead of other economically and technologically advanced countries like France, Germany, the United Kingdom and South Korea, but 9 places behind its only neighbour, Spain. This is despite the fact that Portugal remains one of the countries with the lowest per capita GDP in Western Europe.</p>

<p>Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" title="Government-owned corporation">State-owned companies</a> include <a href="/wiki/%C3%81guas_de_Portugal" title="Águas de Portugal">Águas de Portugal</a> (water), <a href="/wiki/Caixa_Geral_de_Dep%C3%B3sitos" title="Caixa Geral de Depósitos">Caixa Geral de Depósitos</a> (banking), <a href="/wiki/Comboios_de_Portugal" title="Comboios de Portugal">Comboios de Portugal</a> (railways), <a href="/wiki/Companhia_das_Lez%C3%ADrias" title="Companhia das Lezírias">Companhia das Lezírias</a> (agriculture) and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A1dio_e_Televis%C3%A3o_de_Portugal" title="Rádio e Televisão de Portugal">RTP</a> (media). Some former state-owned entities are managed by state-run <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> <a href="/wiki/Parp%C3%BAblica" title="Parpública">Parpública</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholder</a> of several public and private companies. Among former State owned companies recently privatized are <a href="/wiki/CTT_Correios_de_Portugal,_S.A." title="CTT Correios de Portugal, S.A.">CTT</a> (postal service), <a href="/wiki/TAP_Portugal" title="TAP Portugal">TAP Portugal</a> (airline) and <a href="/wiki/ANA_Aeroportos_de_Portugal" title="ANA Aeroportos de Portugal">ANA</a> (airports).</p>
<p>Companies listed on <a href="/wiki/Euronext_Lisbon" title="Euronext Lisbon">Euronext Lisbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a> like <a href="/wiki/Energias_de_Portugal" title="Energias de Portugal">EDP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galp_Energia" title="Galp Energia">Galp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Martins" title="Jerónimo Martins">Jerónimo Martins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mota-Engil" title="Mota-Engil">Mota-Engil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novabase" title="Novabase">Novabase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semapa" title="Semapa">Semapa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portucel_Soporcel" title="Portucel Soporcel">Portucel Soporcel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal_Telecom" title="Portugal Telecom">Portugal Telecom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonae" title="Sonae">Sonae</a>, are among the largest corporations of Portugal by number of employees, <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">net income</a> or international <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a>. The Euronext Lisbon is the major stock exchange of Portugal and is part of the <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Euronext" title="NYSE Euronext">NYSE Euronext</a>, the first global stock exchange. The <a href="/wiki/PSI-20" title="PSI-20">PSI-20</a> is Portugal's most selective and widely known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_index" title="Stock index">stock index</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_sector">Primary sector</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Portugal" title="Agriculture in Portugal">Agriculture in Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_Portugal" title="Fishing in Portugal">Fishing in Portugal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Portugal" title="Mining in Portugal">Mining in Portugal</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Portugal" title="Agriculture in Portugal">Agriculture in Portugal</a> is based on small to medium-sized family-owned dispersed units. However, the sector also includes larger scale <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a> export-oriented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrobusiness" title="Agrobusiness">agrobusinesses</a> backed by companies (like <a href="/wiki/Grupo_RAR" title="Grupo RAR">Grupo RAR</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Vitacress" title="Vitacress">Vitacress</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovena" title="Sovena">Sovena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactogal" title="Lactogal">Lactogal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vale_da_Rosa" title="Vale da Rosa">Vale da Rosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Companhia_das_Lez%C3%ADrias" title="Companhia das Lezírias">Companhia das Lezírias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valouro" title="Valouro">Valouro</a>). The country produces a wide variety of crops and livestock products, including <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_vegetables" title="Green vegetables">green vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olives</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oilseed" title="Oilseed">oilseeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilberry" title="Bilberry">bilberry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_grapes" title="Table grapes">table grapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">edible mushrooms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dairy_products" title="Dairy products">dairy products</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">Forestry</a> has also played an important economic role among the rural communities and industry (namely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_industry" title="Paper industry">paper industry</a> that includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portucel_Soporcel_Group" title="Portucel Soporcel Group">Portucel Soporcel Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineered_wood" title="Engineered wood">engineered wood</a> that includes <a href="/wiki/Sonae_Ind%C3%BAstria" title="Sonae Indústria">Sonae Indústria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">furniture</a> that includes several manufacturing plants in and around <a href="/wiki/Pa%C3%A7os_de_Ferreira" title="Paços de Ferreira">Paços de Ferreira</a>, the core of Portugal's major industrial operations of <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a>). In 2001, the gross agricultural product accounted for 4 per cent of the national GDP.</p>
<p>Traditionally a sea-power, Portugal has had a strong tradition in <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_Portugal" title="Fishing in Portugal">the Portuguese fishing sector</a> and is one of the countries with the highest fish consumption per capita. The main landing sites in Portugal (including Azores and Madeira), according to total landings in weight by year, are the harbours of <a href="/wiki/Matosinhos" title="Matosinhos">Matosinhos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peniche,_Portugal" title="Peniche, Portugal">Peniche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olh%C3%A3o" title="Olhão">Olhão</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sesimbra" title="Sesimbra">Sesimbra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Figueira_da_Foz" title="Figueira da Foz">Figueira da Foz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sines,_Portugal" title="Sines, Portugal">Sines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portim%C3%A3o" title="Portimão">Portimão</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>. Portuguese processed fish products are exported through several companies, under a number of different brands and registered trademarks, such as <a href="/wiki/Conservas_Ramirez" title="Conservas Ramirez">Ramirez</a>, the world’s oldest active canned fish producer.</p>
<p>Portugal is a significant European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> producer and is ranked among Europe's leading <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> producers. The nation is also a notable producer of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. However, the country lacks the potential to conduct <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_exploration" title="Hydrocarbon exploration">hydrocarbon exploration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, a limitation that has hindered the development of Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_Portugal" title="Mining in Portugal">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> sectors. Although the country has vast iron and coal reserves—mainly in the north—after the 1974 revolution and the consequent <a href="/wiki/Economic_globalization" title="Economic globalization">economic globalization</a>, low competitiveness forced a decrease in the extraction activity for these minerals. The <a href="/wiki/Panasqueira" title="Panasqueira">Panasqueira</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neves-Corvo_mine" title="Neves-Corvo mine">Neves-Corvo mines</a> are among the most recognised Portuguese mines that are still in operation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_sector">Secondary sector</span></h3>

<p>Industry is diversified, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Autoeuropa" title="Volkswagen Autoeuropa">Volkswagen Autoeuropa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peugeot_Citroen" title="Peugeot Citroen">Peugeot Citroen</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_industry" title="Aerospace industry">aerospace</a> (<a href="/wiki/Embraer" title="Embraer">Embraer</a> and <a href="/wiki/OGMA" title="OGMA">OGMA</a>), <a href="/wiki/Electronics_industry" title="Electronics industry">electronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Textile_industry" title="Textile industry">textiles</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a>. Volkswagen Group's <a href="/wiki/AutoEuropa" title="AutoEuropa">AutoEuropa</a> motor vehicle assembly plant in <a href="/wiki/Palmela" title="Palmela">Palmela</a> is among the largest <a href="/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment" title="Foreign direct investment">foreign direct investment</a> projects in Portugal. Modern non-traditional technology-based industries, such as <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, have been developed in several locations across the country. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alverca" title="Alverca">Alverca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covilh%C3%A3" title="Covilhã">Covilhã</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vora" title="Évora">Évora</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ponte_de_Sor" title="Ponte de Sor">Ponte de Sor</a> are the main centres of the Portuguese aerospace industry, which is led by Brazil-based company Embraer and the Portuguese company OGMA. Following the turn of the 21st century, many major biotechnology and information technology industries have been founded, and are concentrated in the metropolitan areas of <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Metropolitan_Area" title="Lisbon Metropolitan Area">Lisbon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Metropolitan_Area" title="Porto Metropolitan Area">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aveiro,_Portugal" title="Aveiro, Portugal">Aveiro</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tertiary_sector">Tertiary sector</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Portugal" title="Tourism in Portugal">Tourism in Portugal</a></div>

<p>The banking and insurance sectors performed well until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">late-2000s financial crisis</a>, and this partly reflected a rapid deepening of the market in Portugal. While sensitive to various types of market and underwriting <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risks</a>, it has been estimated that overall both the life and non-life sectors will be able to withstand a number of severe shocks, even though the impact on individual insurers varies widely.</p>
<p>Travel and <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Portugal" title="Tourism in Portugal">tourism</a> continue to be extremely important for Portugal. It has been necessary for the country to focus upon its niche attractions, such as health, nature and rural tourism, to stay ahead of its competitors.</p>
<p>Portugal is among the 20 most visited countries in the world, receiving an average of 13 million foreign tourists each year. In 2014, Portugal was elected <i>The Best European Country</i> by the <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a>.</p>
<p>Tourist hotspots in Portugal are Lisbon, Algarve, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, Porto and the city of <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a>. Lisbon attracts the 16th most tourists among European cities (with seven million tourists occupying the city's hotels in 2006).</p>
<p>Also, between 4-5 million religious pilgrims visit <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A1tima,_Portugal" title="Fátima, Portugal">Fátima</a> each year, where apparitions of the Blessed Virgin Mary to three shepherd children reportedly took place in 1917. The <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_of_F%C3%A1tima" title="Sanctuary of Fátima">Sanctuary of Fátima</a> is one of the largest Roman Catholic shrines in the world. The Portuguese government continues to promote and develop new tourist destinations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro Valley</a>, the island of <a href="/wiki/Porto_Santo_Island" title="Porto Santo Island">Porto Santo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alentejo" title="Alentejo">Alentejo</a>.</p>
<p>The legend of the Rooster of Barcelos tells the story of a dead rooster's miraculous intervention in proving the innocence of a man who had been falsely accused and sentenced to death. The story is associated with the 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Calvary_(sculpture)" title="Calvary (sculpture)">calvary</a> that is part of the collection of the Archeological Museum located in Paço dos Condes, a gothic-style palace in <a href="/wiki/Barcelos,_Portugal" title="Barcelos, Portugal">Barcelos</a>, a city in northwest Portugal. The <a href="/wiki/Rooster_of_Barcelos" title="Rooster of Barcelos">Rooster of Barcelos</a> is bought by many tourists as a <a href="/wiki/Souvenir" title="Souvenir">souvenir</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaternary_sector">Quaternary sector</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_in_Portugal" title="Science and technology in Portugal">Science and technology in Portugal</a></div>
<p>Scientific and technological research activities in Portugal are mainly conducted within a network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26D" title="R&amp;D">R&amp;D</a> units belonging to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Portugal" title="List of universities in Portugal">public universities</a> and state-managed autonomous research institutions like the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Engenharia,_Tecnologia_e_Inova%C3%A7%C3%A3o" title="Instituto Nacional de Engenharia, Tecnologia e Inovação">INETI – Instituto Nacional de Engenharia, Tecnologia e Inovação</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_dos_Recursos_Biol%C3%B3gicos" title="Instituto Nacional dos Recursos Biológicos">INRB – Instituto Nacional dos Recursos Biológicos</a>. The funding and management of this research system is mainly conducted under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science,_Technology_and_Higher_Education" title="Ministry of Science, Technology and Higher Education">Ministry of Science, Technology and Higher Education</a> (MCTES) itself and the MCTES's <a href="/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_para_a_Ci%C3%AAncia_e_Tecnologia" title="Fundação para a Ciência e Tecnologia">Fundação para a Ciência e Tecnologia</a> (FCT).</p>
<p>The largest R&amp;D units of the public universities by volume of research grants and peer-reviewed publications, include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosciences" title="Biosciences">biosciences</a> research institutions like the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_de_Medicina_Molecular" title="Instituto de Medicina Molecular">Instituto de Medicina Molecular</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Neuroscience_and_Cell_Biology" title="Centre for Neuroscience and Cell Biology">Centre for Neuroscience and Cell Biology</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPATIMUP" title="IPATIMUP">IPATIMUP</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_de_Biologia_Molecular_e_Celular" title="Instituto de Biologia Molecular e Celular">Instituto de Biologia Molecular e Celular</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Abel_Salazar_Biomedical_Sciences_Institute" title="Abel Salazar Biomedical Sciences Institute">Abel Salazar Biomedical Sciences Institute</a>.</p>
<p>Among the largest non-state-run research institutions in Portugal are the <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Gulbenkian_de_Ci%C3%AAncia" title="Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência">Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Champalimaud_Foundation" title="Champalimaud Foundation">Champalimaud Foundation</a>, a neuroscience and oncology research centre, which in addition awards every year one of the highest monetary prizes of any science prize in the world. A number of both national and multinational high-tech and industrial companies, are also responsible for research and development projects. One of the oldest learned societies of Portugal is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciences_Academy_of_Lisbon" title="Sciences Academy of Lisbon">Sciences Academy of Lisbon</a>, founded in 1779.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian</a> bilateral state-supported research efforts include the <a href="/wiki/International_Iberian_Nanotechnology_Laboratory" title="International Iberian Nanotechnology Laboratory">International Iberian Nanotechnology Laboratory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ibercivis" title="Ibercivis">Ibercivis</a> <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a> platform, which are joint research programmes of both Portugal and Spain. Portugal is a member of several pan-European scientific organizations. These include the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Laboratory_for_Particle_Physics" title="European Laboratory for Particle Physics">European Laboratory for Particle Physics</a> (CERN), <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">European Southern Observatory</a> (ESO).</p>
<p>Portugal has the largest <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquarium</a> in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Oceanarium" title="Lisbon Oceanarium">Lisbon Oceanarium</a>, and the Portuguese have several other notable organizations focused on science-related exhibits and divulgation, like the state agency <i>Ciência Viva</i>, a programme of the Portuguese Ministry of Science and Technology to the promotion of a scientific and technological culture among the Portuguese population, the <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum_of_the_University_of_Coimbra" title="Science Museum of the University of Coimbra">Science Museum of the University of Coimbra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_history_museums#Portugal" title="List of natural history museums">National Museum of Natural History</a> at the University of Lisbon, and the <a href="/wiki/Visionarium_(Portugal)" title="Visionarium (Portugal)">Visionarium</a>. With the emergence and growth of several <a href="/wiki/Science_park" title="Science park">science parks</a> throughout the world that helped create many thousands of scientific, technological and knowledge-based businesses, Portugal started to develop several science parks across the country. These include the <a href="/wiki/Taguspark" title="Taguspark">Taguspark</a> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oeiras_Municipality,_Portugal" title="Oeiras Municipality, Portugal">Oeiras</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Coimbra_iParque" title="Coimbra iParque">Coimbra iParque</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Biocant" title="Biocant">biocant</a> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantanhede_Municipality" title="Cantanhede Municipality">Cantanhede</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Madeira_Tecnopolo" title="Madeira Tecnopolo">Madeira Tecnopolo</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Funchal" title="Funchal">Funchal</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sines_Tecnopolo" title="Sines Tecnopolo">Sines Tecnopolo</a> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sines,_Portugal" title="Sines, Portugal">Sines</a>), Tecmaia (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maia_Municipality" title="Maia Municipality">Maia</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Parkurbis" title="Parkurbis">Parkurbis</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Covilh%C3%A3" title="Covilhã">Covilhã</a>). Companies locate in the Portuguese science parks to take advantage of a variety of services ranging from financial and legal advice through to marketing and technological support.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egas_Moniz" title="Egas Moniz">Egas Moniz</a>, a Portuguese physician who developed the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_angiography" title="Cerebral angiography">cerebral angiography</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leucotomy" title="Leucotomy">leucotomy</a>, received in 1949 the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> – he is the first Portuguese recipient of a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> and the only in the sciences.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Innovation_Scoreboard" title="European Innovation Scoreboard">European Innovation Scoreboard</a> 2011, placed Portugal-based innovation in the 15th position, with an impressive increase in innovation expenditure and output.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Portugal" title="Transport in Portugal">Transport in Portugal</a></div>

<p>By the early 1970s Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal" title="Economic history of Portugal">fast economic growth</a> with increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_consumption" title="Mass consumption">consumption</a> and purchase of new automobiles set the priority for improvements in transportation. Again in the 1990s, after joining the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, the country built many new motorways. Today, the country has a 68,732 km (42,708 mi) road network, of which almost 3,000 km (1,864 mi) are part of system of 44 motorways. Opened in 1944, the first motorway (which linked Lisbon to the National Stadium) was an innovative project that made Portugal among one of the first countries in the world to establish a motorway (this roadway eventually became the Lisbon-Cascais highway, or A5).</p>
<p>Although a few other tracts were created (around 1960 and 1970), it was only after the beginning of the 1980s that large-scale motorway construction was implemented. In 1972, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BRISA_Auto-estradas_de_Portugal_S.A." title="BRISA Auto-estradas de Portugal S.A.">Brisa</a>, the highway concessionaire, was founded to handle the management of many of the regions motorways. On many highways, toll needs to be paid, see <a href="/wiki/Via_Verde" title="Via Verde">Via Verde</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama_bridge" title="Vasco da Gama bridge">Vasco da Gama bridge</a> is the longest bridge in Europe.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Continental_Portugal" title="Continental Portugal">Continental Portugal</a>'s 89,015 km (34,369 sq mi) territory is serviced by four international airports located near the principal cities of <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beja_(Portugal)" title="Beja (Portugal)">Beja</a>. Lisbon's geographical position makes it a stopover for many foreign airlines at several airports within the country. The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag-carrier" title="Flag-carrier">flag-carrier</a> is <a href="/wiki/TAP_Portugal" title="TAP Portugal">TAP Portugal</a>, although many other domestic airlines provide services within and without the country. The government decided to build a new airport outside Lisbon, in <a href="/wiki/Alcochete" title="Alcochete">Alcochete</a>, to replace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisbon_Portela_Airport" title="Lisbon Portela Airport">Lisbon Portela Airport</a>, though this plan has been stalled due to the austerity. Currently, the most important airports are in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portela_Airport" title="Portela Airport">Lisbon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_S%C3%A1_Carneiro_Airport" title="Francisco Sá Carneiro Airport">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro_Airport" title="Faro Airport">Faro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira_Airport" title="Madeira Airport">Funchal</a> (Madeira), and <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Paulo_II_Airport" title="João Paulo II Airport">Ponta Delgada</a> (Azores), managed by the national airport authority group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANA_%E2%80%93_Aeroportos_de_Portugal" title="ANA – Aeroportos de Portugal">ANA – Aeroportos de Portugal</a>.</p>
<p>A national railway system that extends throughout the country and into Spain, is supported and administered by <a href="/wiki/Comboios_de_Portugal" title="Comboios de Portugal">Comboios de Portugal</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">Rail transport</a> of passengers and goods is derived using the 2,791 km (1,734 mi) of railway lines currently in service, of which 1,430 km (889 mi) are electrified and about 900 km (559 mi) allow train speeds greater than 120 km/h (75 mph). The railway network is managed by the <a href="/wiki/Rede_Ferrovi%C3%A1ria_Nacional" title="Rede Ferroviária Nacional">REFER</a> while the transport of passengers and goods are the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/Comboios_de_Portugal" title="Comboios de Portugal">Comboios de Portugal</a> (CP), both public companies. In 2006 the CP carried 133 million passengers and 9,750,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">t</a> (9,600,000 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>; 10,700,000 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a>) of goods.</p>
<p>The major seaports are located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sines,_Portugal" title="Sines, Portugal">Sines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leix%C3%B5es" title="Leixões">Leixões</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Setúbal">Setúbal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aveiro,_Portugal" title="Aveiro, Portugal">Aveiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Figueira_da_Foz" title="Figueira da Foz">Figueira da Foz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>.</p>
<p>The two largest metropolitan areas have subway systems: <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Metro" title="Lisbon Metro">Lisbon Metro</a> and <i>Metro Sul do Tejo</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Metropolitan_Area" title="Lisbon Metropolitan Area">Lisbon Metropolitan Area</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porto_Metro" title="Porto Metro">Porto Metro</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Metropolitan_Area" title="Porto Metropolitan Area">Porto Metropolitan Area</a>, each with more than 35 km (22 mi) of lines. In Portugal, <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Lisbon" title="Trams in Lisbon">Lisbon tram services</a> have been supplied by the <i>Companhia de Carris de Ferro de Lisboa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Carris" title="Carris">Carris</a>), for over a century. In <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Porto" title="Trams in Porto">a tram network</a>, of which only a tourist line on the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> remains, began construction on 12 September 1895 (a first for the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>). All major cities and towns have their own local urban transport network, as well as taxi services.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Portugal" title="Energy in Portugal">Energy in Portugal</a></div>

<p>Portugal has considerable resources of wind and river power, the two most cost-effective renewable sources. Since the turn of the 21st century, there has been a trend towards the development of a renewable resource industry and reduction of both consumption and use of fossil fuel resources. In 2006, the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar power</a> plant at that date, the <a href="/wiki/Moura_Photovoltaic_Power_Station" title="Moura Photovoltaic Power Station">Moura Photovoltaic Power Station</a>, began operating near <a href="/wiki/Moura,_Portugal" title="Moura, Portugal">Moura</a>, in the south, while the world's first commercial <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> farm, the <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a>, opened in the <a href="/wiki/Norte_Region,_Portugal" title="Norte Region, Portugal">Norte region</a> (2008). By the end of 2006, 66% of the country's electrical production was from coal and fuel power plants, while 29% were derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> dams, and 6% by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">wind energy</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, renewable energy resources were producing 43% of the nation's consumption of electricity, even as hydroelectric production decreased with severe droughts. As of June 2010, electricity exports had outnumbered imports. In the period between January and May 2010, 70% of the national production of energy came from renewable sources.</p>
<p>Portugal’s national energy transmission company, <a href="/wiki/Redes_Energ%C3%A9ticas_Nacionais" title="Redes Energéticas Nacionais">Redes Energéticas Nacionais</a> (REN), uses sophisticated modeling to predict weather, especially wind patterns, and computer programs to calculate energy from the various renewable-energy plants. Before the solar/wind revolution, Portugal had generated electricity from hydropower plants on its rivers for decades. New programs combine wind and water: wind-driven turbines pump water uphill at night, the most blustery period; then the water flows downhill by day, generating electricity, when consumer demand is highest. Portugal’s distribution system is also now a two-way street. Instead of just delivering electricity, it draws electricity from even the smallest generators, like rooftop solar panels. The government aggressively encouraged such contributions by setting a premium price for those who buy rooftop-generated solar electricity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Portugal" title="Demographics of Portugal">Demographics of Portugal</a></div>

<p>The Statistics Portugal (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt"><i>INE - <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Estat%C3%ADstica" title="Instituto Nacional de Estatística">Instituto Nacional de Estatística</a></i></span>) estimates that, according to the 2011 census, the population was 10,562,178 (of which 52% was female, 48% was male). This population has been relatively homogeneous for most of its history: a single religion (Catholicism) and a single language have contributed to this ethnic and national unity, namely after the expulsion of the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>. A considerable number of Moors and Jews, nevertheless, stayed in Portugal, under the condition that they converted to Catholicism, and afterwards they were known as <i>Mouriscos</i> (former Muslims) and <i>Cristãos Novos</i> (New Christians or former Jews) some of whom may have continued to observe rabbinic Judaism in secret, as in the case of the secret <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Belmonte" title="History of the Jews in Belmonte">Jews of Belmonte</a>, who now observe the Jewish faith openly. After 1772 the distinction between Old and New Christians was abolished by decree. Some famous Portuguese New Christians were the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Nunes" title="Pedro Nunes">Pedro Nunes</a> and the physician and naturalist <a href="/wiki/Garcia_de_Orta" title="Garcia de Orta">Garcia de Orta</a>.</p>
<p>Native Portuguese are an <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> ethnic group, whose ancestry is very similar to other <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern</a> Europeans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_people" title="Mediterranean people">Mediterranean peoples</a>, in particular Spaniards, followed by some regional French and Italians with whom they share a common ancestry, history and cultural proximity.</p>
<p>The most important demographic influence in the modern Portuguese seems to be the oldest one; current interpretation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y-chromosome" title="Y-chromosome">Y-chromosome</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> data suggests that the Portuguese have their origin in <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> peoples that began arriving to the European continent around 45,000 years ago. All subsequent migrations did leave an impact, genetically and culturally, but the main population source of the Portuguese is still Paleolithic. Genetic studies show Portuguese populations not to be significantly different from other European populations.</p>
<p>The total fertility rate (TFR) as of 2015 was estimated at 1.52 children born/woman, which is below the replacement rate of 2.1. In 2015, 50.7% of births were to unmarried women. Like most Western countries, Portugal has to deal with low fertility levels: the country has experienced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility_rate" title="Sub-replacement fertility rate">sub-replacement fertility rate</a> since the 1980s.</p>
<p>The structure of Portuguese society is characterized by an increasing inequality which at present (2015) places the country in the lowest third of the Social Justice Index for the European Union.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urbanization">Urbanization</span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_areas_and_Functional_Urban_Area_.28FUA.29.5B109.5D">Metropolitan areas and <a href="/wiki/Larger_urban_zone" title="Larger urban zone">Functional Urban Area (FUA)</a></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_areas_of_Portugal" title="Metropolitan areas of Portugal">Metropolitan areas of Portugal</a></div>

<p>There are two Greater Metropolitan Areas (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grande_%C3%81rea_Metropolitana" title="Grande Área Metropolitana">GAMs</a>): <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81rea_Metropolitana_de_Lisboa" title="Área Metropolitana de Lisboa">Lisbon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%81rea_Metropolitana_do_Porto" title="Área Metropolitana do Porto">Porto</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration">Immigration</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Portugal" title="Immigration to Portugal">Immigration to Portugal</a></div>

<p>Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">colonial history</a> has long since been a cornerstone of its national identity, as has its geographic position at the south-western corner of Europe, looking out into the Atlantic Ocean. It was one of the last western colonial European powers to give up its overseas territories (among them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_West_Africa" title="Portuguese West Africa">Angola</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_East_Africa" title="Portuguese East Africa">Mozambique</a> in 1975), turning over the administration of <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> to the People's Republic of China at the end of 1999. Consequently, it has both influenced and been influenced by cultures from former colonies or dependencies, resulting in immigration from these former territories for both economic and personal reasons. Portugal, long a country of emigration (the vast majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Brazilian" title="Portuguese Brazilian">Brazilians</a> have Portuguese ancestry), has now become a country of net immigration, and not just from the last <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">Indian</a> (Portuguese until 1961), <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_African_countries" title="Portuguese-speaking African countries">African</a> (Portuguese until 1975), and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Far East Asian</a> (Portuguese until 1999) overseas territories. An estimated 800,000 Portuguese returned to Portugal as the country's African possessions gained independence in 1975. By 2007, Portugal had 10,617,575 inhabitants of whom about 332,137 were legal <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Portugal" title="Immigration to Portugal">immigrants</a>.</p>
<p>Since the 1990s, along with a boom in <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, several new waves of <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazilians" title="Brazilians">Brazilian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_African_countries" title="Portuguese-speaking African countries">people from the former Portuguese colonies in Africa</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Africans" title="Africans">Africans</a> have settled in the country. <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moldovans" title="Moldovans">Moldovans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosovar_diaspora" title="Kosovar diaspora">Kosovar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a> have also chosen Portugal as destination. Portugal's <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> population is estimated to be at about 40,000.</p>
<p>In addition, a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_citizen" title="EU citizen">EU citizens</a>, mostly from the United Kingdom, other northern European or <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Nordic</a> countries, have become permanent residents in the country (with the British community being mostly composed of retired pensioners and choosing to live in the Algarve and Madeira).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Portugal" title="Religion in Portugal">Religion in Portugal</a></div>
<div class="barbox tright" style="border:1px solid silver; font-size:88%; padding:0.4em; width:auto; background: white;">

</div>
<p>According to the 2011 Census, 81.0% of the Portuguese population are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>. The country has small Protestant, <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">Latter-day Saint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Portugal" title="Islam in Portugal">Muslim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Portugal" title="Hinduism in Portugal">Hindu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baha%27i" title="Baha'i">Baha'i</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiritism" title="Spiritism">Spiritist</a> communities. Influences from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Traditional_Religion" title="African Traditional Religion">African Traditional Religion</a> and Chinese Traditional Religion are also felt among many people, particularly in fields related with Traditional Chinese Medicine and African Witch Doctors. Some 6.8% of the population declared themselves to be non-religious, and 8.3% did not give any answer about their religion.</p>
<p>In 2012, a study conducted by the Catholic University revealed 79.5% of the Portuguese considered themselves Catholics, and that 18% <a href="/wiki/Church_attendance" title="Church attendance">attended Mass</a> regularly. These figures represent a drop from 86.9% of Catholics in 2001, while during the same period the number of people stating that they had no religion rose from 8.2% to 14.2%.</p>

<p>Many Portuguese holidays, festivals and traditions have a Christian origin or connotation. Although relations between the Portuguese state and the Roman Catholic Church were generally amiable and stable since the earliest years of the Portuguese nation, their relative power fluctuated. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1279-1415)" title="History of Portugal (1279-1415)">13th and 14th centuries</a>, the church enjoyed both riches and power stemming from its role in the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">reconquest</a>, its close identification with early Portuguese nationalism and the foundation of the Portuguese educational system, including the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Coimbra" title="University of Coimbra">first university</a>.</p>
<p>The growth of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese overseas empire</a> made its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" title="Mission (Christian)">missionaries</a> important agents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonisation" title="Colonisation">colonization</a>, with important roles in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teaching" title="Teaching">education</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelization" title="Evangelization">evangelization</a> of people from all the inhabited continents. The growth of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Revolution_of_1820" title="Liberal Revolution of 1820">liberal</a> and nascent <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republican</a> movements during the eras leading to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic" title="First Portuguese Republic">First Portuguese Republic</a> (1910–26) changed the role and importance of organized religion.</p>
<p>Portugal is a <a href="/wiki/Secular_state" title="Secular state">secular state</a>: <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">church and state were formally separated</a> during the Portuguese First Republic, and later reiterated in the 1976 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution" title="Portuguese Constitution">Portuguese Constitution</a>. Other than the Constitution, the two most important documents relating to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_freedom" title="Religious freedom">religious freedom</a> in Portugal are: the 1940 Concordata (later amended in 1971) between Portugal and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, and the 2001 Religious Freedom Act.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Portugal" title="Languages of Portugal">Languages of Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese language</a></div>


<p>Portuguese is the official language of Portugal. Portuguese is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance language</a> that originated in what is now <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Portugal" title="Northern Portugal">Northern Portugal</a>, originating from <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">Galician-Portuguese</a>, which was the common language of the Galician and Portuguese people until the independence of Portugal. Particularly in the North of Portugal, there are still many similarities between the Galician culture and the Portuguese culture. Galicia is a consultative observer of the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries" title="Community of Portuguese Language Countries">Community of Portuguese Language Countries</a>. According to the Ethnologue of Languages, Portuguese and Spanish have a lexical similarity of 89% - educated speakers of each language can communicate easily with one another.</p>
<p>The Portuguese language is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Romanization_(cultural)" title="Romanization (cultural)">romanized</a> <a href="/wiki/Pre-Roman_peoples_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula" title="Pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula">Pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula</a> around 2000 years ago—particularly the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tartessians" title="Tartessians">Tartessians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lusitanians" title="Lusitanians">Lusitanians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a>. In the 15th and 16th centuries, the language spread worldwide as Portugal established a colonial and commercial empire between 1415 and 1999. Portuguese is now spoken as a native language in five different continents, with Brazil accounting for the largest number of native Portuguese speakers of any country (200 million speakers in 2012).</p>
<p>In 2013 the Portuguese language is the official language spoken in Brazil, Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, São Tomé and Príncipe, Guinea-Bissau, Equatorial Guinea, and <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a>. These countries, plus <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> Special Administrative Region (People's Republic of China) where Portuguese is co-official with Cantonese, make up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lusosphere" title="Lusosphere">Lusosphere</a>, a term derived from the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitania</a>", which currently matches the Portuguese territory south of the <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> river.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mirandese_language" title="Mirandese language">Mirandese</a> is also recognized as a co-official regional language in some municipalities of North-Eastern Portugal. An estimate of between 6,000 and 7,000 Mirandese speakers has been documented for Portugal.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/EF_English_Proficiency_Index" title="EF English Proficiency Index">International English Proficiency Index</a>, Portugal has a high proficiency level in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, proficiency higher than in countries like <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> or <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Portugal" title="Education in Portugal">Education in Portugal</a></div>

<p>The educational system is divided into preschool (for those under age 6), basic education (9 years, in three stages, compulsory), secondary education (3 years, compulsory since 2010), and higher education (subdivided in university and <a href="/wiki/Polytechnic_(Portugal)" title="Polytechnic (Portugal)">polytechnic</a> education).</p>
<p>The total adult literacy rate is 99 percent. Portuguese primary school enrollments are close to 100 percent. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Programme_for_International_Student_Assessment" title="Programme for International Student Assessment">Programme for International Student Assessment</a> (PISA) 2009, the average Portuguese 15-year-old student, when rated in terms of reading literacy, mathematics and science knowledge, is placed at the same level as those students from the United States, Sweden, Germany, Ireland, France, Denmark, United Kingdom, Hungary and <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>, with 489 points (493 is the average).</p>
<p>Over 35% of college-age citizens (20 years old) attend one of Portugal's higher education institutions (compared with 50% in the United States and 35% in the OECD countries). In addition to being a destination for <a href="/wiki/International_student" title="International student">international students</a>, Portugal is also among the top places of origin for international students. All higher education students, both domestic and international, totaled 380,937 in 2005.</p>
<p>Portuguese universities have existed since 1290. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Coimbra" title="University of Coimbra">oldest Portuguese university</a> was first established in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> before moving to <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a>. Historically, within the scope of the Portuguese Empire, the Portuguese founded the oldest engineering school of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> (the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_de_Artilharia,_Fortifica%C3%A7%C3%A3o_e_Desenho" title="Real Academia de Artilharia, Fortificação e Desenho">Real Academia de Artilharia, Fortificação e Desenho</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>) in 1792, as well as the oldest medical college in Asia (the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escola_M%C3%A9dico-Cir%C3%BArgica_de_Goa" title="Escola Médico-Cirúrgica de Goa">Escola Médico-Cirúrgica</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a>) in 1842. The largest university in Portugal is the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lisbon" title="University of Lisbon">University of Lisbon</a>.</p>
<p>Universities are usually organized into <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(division)" title="Faculty (division)">faculties</a>. Institutes and schools are also common designations for autonomous subdivisions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_colleges_and_universities_in_Portugal" title="List of colleges and universities in Portugal">Portuguese higher education institutions</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bologna_process" title="Bologna process">Bologna process</a> has been adopted, since 2006, by Portuguese universities and poly-technical institutes. Higher education in state-run educational establishments is provided on a competitive basis, a system of <i><a href="/wiki/Numerus_clausus" title="Numerus clausus">numerus clausus</a></i> is enforced through a national database on student admissions. However, every higher education institution offers also a number of additional vacant places through other extraordinary admission processes for sportsmen, mature applicants (over 23 years old), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_students" title="International students">international students</a>, foreign students from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lusosphere" title="Lusosphere">Lusosphere</a>, degree owners from other institutions, students from other institutions (<a href="/wiki/Transfer_admissions_in_the_United_States" title="Transfer admissions in the United States">academic transfer</a>), former students (readmission), and course change, which are subject to specific standards and regulations set by each institution or course department.</p>
<p>Most student costs are supported with public money. However, with the increasing tuition fees a student has to pay to attend a Portuguese state-run higher education institution and the attraction of new types of students (many as part-time students or in evening classes) like employees, businessmen, parents, and pensioners, many departments make a substantial profit from every additional student enrolled in courses, with benefits for the college or university's gross tuition revenue and without loss of educational quality (teacher per student, computer per student, classroom size per student, etc.).</p>
<p>Portugal has entered into <a href="/wiki/Higher_education_in_Portugal#International_partnership_agreements" title="Higher education in Portugal">cooperation agreements with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and other US institutions</a> to further develop and increase the effectiveness of Portuguese higher education and research.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_in_Portugal" title="Health in Portugal">Health in Portugal</a></div>

<p>According to the latest <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Report" title="Human Development Report">Human Development Report</a>, the average <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a> in 2015 was 81.0 years.</p>
<p>Portugal ranks 12th in the best public health systems in the world, ahead of high developed countries like the United Kingdom, Germany or Sweden.</p>
<p>The Portuguese health system is characterized by three coexisting systems: the National Health Service (<i>Serviço Nacional de Saúde'</i>, SNS), special social health insurance schemes for certain professions (health subsystems) and voluntary private health insurance. The SNS provides universal coverage. In addition, about 25% of the population is covered by the health subsystems, 10% by private insurance schemes and another 7% by mutual funds.</p>
<p>The Ministry of Health is responsible for developing health policy as well as managing the SNS. Five regional health administrations are in charge of implementing the national health policy objectives, developing guidelines and protocols and supervising health care delivery. Decentralization efforts have aimed at shifting financial and management responsibility to the regional level. In practice, however, the autonomy of regional health administrations over budget setting and spending has been limited to primary care.</p>
<p>The SNS is predominantly funded through general taxation. Employer (including the state) and employee contributions represent the main funding sources of the health subsystems. In addition, direct payments by the patient and voluntary health insurance premiums account for a large proportion of funding.</p>
<p>Similar to the other Eur-A countries, most Portuguese die from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noncommunicable_diseases" title="Noncommunicable diseases">noncommunicable diseases</a>. Mortality from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_diseases" title="Cardiovascular diseases">cardiovascular diseases</a> (CVD) is higher than in the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a>, but its two main components, ischaemic heart disease and cerebrovascular disease, display inverse trends compared with the Eur-A, with <a href="/wiki/Cerebrovascular_disease" title="Cerebrovascular disease">cerebrovascular disease</a> being the single biggest killer in Portugal (17%). Portuguese people die 12% less often from cancer than in the Eur-A, but mortality is not declining as rapidly as in the Eur-A. Cancer is more frequent among children as well as among women younger than 44 years. Although lung cancer (slowly increasing among women) and breast cancer (decreasing rapidly) are scarcer, cancer of the cervix and the prostate are more frequent. Portugal has the highest mortality rate for diabetes in the Eur-A, with a sharp increase since the 1980s.</p>

<p>Portugal's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infant_mortality_rate" title="Infant mortality rate">infant mortality rate</a> has dropped sharply since the late 1970s, when 24 of 1000 newborns died in the first year of life. It is now around 2 deaths per a 1000 newborns. This improvement was mainly due to the decrease in neonatal mortality, from 15.5 to 2.4 per 1000 live births.</p>
<p>People are usually well informed about their health status, the positive and negative effects of their behaviour on their health and their use of health care services. Yet their perceptions of their health can differ from what administrative and examination-based data show about levels of illness within populations. Thus, survey results based on self-reporting at the household level complement other data on health status and the use of services.</p>
<p>Only one third of adults rated their health as good or very good in Portugal (Kasmel et al., 2004). This is the lowest of the Eur-A countries reporting and reflects the relatively adverse situation of the country in terms of mortality and selected morbidity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Portugal" title="Culture of Portugal">Culture of Portugal</a></div>

<p>Portugal has developed a specific culture while being influenced by various civilizations that have crossed the Mediterranean and the European continent, or were introduced when it played an active role during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Age_of_Discovery" title="Portugal in the Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>. In the 1990s and 2000s (decade), Portugal modernized its public cultural facilities, in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Calouste_Gulbenkian_Foundation" title="Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation">Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation</a> established in 1956 in Lisbon.</p>
<p>These include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bel%C3%A9m_Cultural_Centre" title="Belém Cultural Centre">Belém Cultural Centre</a> in Lisbon, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serralves_Foundation" title="Serralves Foundation">Serralves Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Casa_da_M%C3%BAsica" title="Casa da Música">Casa da Música</a>, both in <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, as well as new public cultural facilities like municipal libraries and concert halls that were built or renovated in many municipalities across the country. Portugal is home to <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Portugal" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Portugal">fifteen</a> UNESCO <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a>, ranking it <a href="/wiki/Table_of_World_Heritage_Sites_by_country" title="Table of World Heritage Sites by country">8th in Europe and 17th in the world.</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Portugal" title="Architecture of Portugal">Architecture of Portugal</a></div>
<p>Traditional architecture is distinctive and include the <a href="/wiki/Manueline" title="Manueline">Manueline</a>, also known as Portuguese late Gothic, a sumptuous, composite Portuguese style of architectural ornamentation of the first decades of the 16th century. A 20th-century interpretation of traditional architecture, <a href="/wiki/Soft_Portuguese_style" title="Soft Portuguese style">Soft Portuguese style</a>, appears extensively in major cities, especially Lisbon. Modern Portugal has given the world renowned architects like <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Souto_de_Moura" title="Eduardo Souto de Moura">Eduardo Souto de Moura</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Siza_Vieira" title="Álvaro Siza Vieira">Álvaro Siza Vieira</a> (both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pritzker_Prize" title="Pritzker Prize">Pritzker Prize</a> winners) and <a href="/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Byrne" title="Gonçalo Byrne">Gonçalo Byrne</a>. In Portugal <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Taveira" title="Tomás Taveira">Tomás Taveira</a> is also noteworthy, particularly for stadium design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema">Cinema</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Portugal" title="Cinema of Portugal">Cinema of Portugal</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Portugal" title="Cinema of Portugal">Portuguese cinema</a> has a long tradition, reaching back to the birth of the medium in the late 19th century. Portuguese film directors such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Duarte" title="Arthur Duarte">Arthur Duarte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Lopes_Ribeiro" title="António Lopes Ribeiro">António Lopes Ribeiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Reis" title="António Reis">António Reis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Costa" title="Pedro Costa">Pedro Costa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manoel_de_Oliveira" title="Manoel de Oliveira">Manoel de Oliveira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_C%C3%A9sar_Monteiro" title="João César Monteiro">João César Monteiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_P%C3%AAra" title="Edgar Pêra">Edgar Pêra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio-Pedro_Vasconcelos" title="António-Pedro Vasconcelos">António-Pedro Vasconcelos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Lopes_(filmmaker)" title="Fernando Lopes (filmmaker)">Fernando Lopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Botelho" title="João Botelho">João Botelho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonel_Vieira" title="Leonel Vieira">Leonel Vieira</a>, are among those that gained notability. Noted Portuguese film actors include <a href="/wiki/Joaquim_de_Almeida" title="Joaquim de Almeida">Joaquim de Almeida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniela_Ruah" title="Daniela Ruah">Daniela Ruah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_de_Medeiros" title="Maria de Medeiros">Maria de Medeiros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Infante" title="Diogo Infante">Diogo Infante</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soraia_Chaves" title="Soraia Chaves">Soraia Chaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ribeirinho" title="Ribeirinho">Ribeirinho</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BAcia_Moniz" title="Lúcia Moniz">Lúcia Moniz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Morgado" title="Diogo Morgado">Diogo Morgado</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_literature" title="Portuguese literature">Portuguese literature</a></div>

<p>Portuguese literature, one of the earliest Western literatures, developed through text as well as song. Until 1350, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese-Galician" title="Portuguese-Galician">Portuguese-Galician</a> <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">troubadours</a> spread their literary influence to most of the Iberian Peninsula. <a href="/wiki/Gil_Vicente" title="Gil Vicente">Gil Vicente</a> (c. 1465–c. 1536), was one of the founders of both Portuguese and Spanish dramatic traditions.</p>
<p>Adventurer and poet <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Luís de Camões">Luís de Camões</a> (c. 1524–1580) wrote the epic poem <i>"Os Lusíadas"</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lusiads" title="The Lusiads">The Lusiads</a>), with <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a> as his main influence. Modern Portuguese poetry is rooted in neoclassic and contemporary styles, as exemplified by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a> (1888–1935). Modern Portuguese literature is represented by authors such as <a href="/wiki/Almeida_Garrett" title="Almeida Garrett">Almeida Garrett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Castelo_Branco" title="Camilo Castelo Branco">Camilo Castelo Branco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E%C3%A7a_de_Queiroz" title="Eça de Queiroz">Eça de Queiroz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sophia_de_Mello_Breyner_Andresen" title="Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen">Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Lobo_Antunes" title="António Lobo Antunes">António Lobo Antunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Torga" title="Miguel Torga">Miguel Torga</a>. Particularly popular and distinguished is <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago" title="José Saramago">José Saramago</a>, recipient of the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_cuisine" title="Portuguese cuisine">Portuguese cuisine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_wine" title="Portuguese wine">Portuguese wine</a></div>
<p>Portuguese cuisine is diverse. The Portuguese consume a lot of dry <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bacalhau" title="Bacalhau">bacalhau</a></i> in Portuguese), for which there are hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Bacalhau#Bacalhau_dishes" title="Bacalhau">recipes</a>. There are more than enough <i><a href="/wiki/Bacalhau" title="Bacalhau">bacalhau</a></i> dishes for each day of the year. Two other popular fish recipes are grilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sardines" title="Sardines">sardines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caldeirada" title="Caldeirada">caldeirada</a>, a potato-based <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">stew</a> that can be made from several types of fish. Typical Portuguese meat recipes, that may be made out of beef, pork, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep">lamb</a>, or chicken, include <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cozido_%C3%A0_portuguesa" title="Cozido à portuguesa">cozido à portuguesa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Feijoada" title="Feijoada">feijoada</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frango_de_churrasco" title="Frango de churrasco">frango de churrasco</a></i>, <i>leitão</i> (roast <a href="/wiki/Suckling_pig" title="Suckling pig">suckling pig</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Carne_de_porco_%C3%A0_alentejana" title="Carne de porco à alentejana">carne de porco à alentejana</a></i>. A very popular northern dish is the arroz de sarrabulho (rice stewed in pigs blood) or the arroz de cabidela (rice and chickens meat stewed in chickens blood).</p>

<p>Typical fast food dishes include the <a href="/wiki/Francesinha" title="Francesinha">Francesinha</a> (Frenchie) from Porto, and <i>bifanas</i> (grilled pork) or <i>prego</i> (grilled beef) <a href="/wiki/Sandwich" title="Sandwich">sandwiches</a>, which are well known around the country. The Portuguese art of <a href="/wiki/Pastry" title="Pastry">pastry</a> has its origins in the many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> Catholic monasteries spread widely across the country. These monasteries, using very few ingredients (mostly almonds, flour, eggs and some liquor), managed to create a spectacular wide range of different pastries, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past%C3%A9is_de_Bel%C3%A9m" title="Pastéis de Belém">pastéis de Belém</a> (or <i>pastéis de nata</i>) originally from Lisbon, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovos_moles" title="Ovos moles">ovos moles</a></i> from Aveiro are examples. Portuguese cuisine is very diverse, with different regions having their own traditional dishes. The Portuguese have a culture of good food, and throughout the country there are myriads of good restaurants and typical small <i>tasquinhas</i>.</p>
<p>Portuguese wines have enjoyed international recognition since the times of the Romans, who associated Portugal with their god <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Bacchus</a>. Today, the country is known by wine lovers and its wines have won several international prizes. Some of the best Portuguese wines are: <a href="/wiki/Vinho_Verde" title="Vinho Verde">Vinho Verde</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinho_Alvarinho" title="Vinho Alvarinho">Vinho Alvarinho</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinho_do_Douro" title="Vinho do Douro">Vinho do Douro</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinho_do_Alentejo" title="Vinho do Alentejo">Vinho do Alentejo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinho_do_D%C3%A3o" title="Vinho do Dão">Vinho do Dão</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinho_da_Bairrada" title="Vinho da Bairrada">Vinho da Bairrada</a> and the sweet: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Wine" title="Port Wine">Port Wine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madeira_Wine" title="Madeira Wine">Madeira Wine</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscatel" title="Moscatel">Moscatel</a> from <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Setúbal">Setúbal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Favaios" title="Favaios">Favaios</a>. Port and Madeira are particularly appreciated in a wide range of places around the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Portugal" title="Music of Portugal">Music of Portugal</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Music_of_Portugal" title="Music of Portugal">Portuguese music</a> encompasses a wide variety of genres. The most renowned is <a href="/wiki/Fado" title="Fado">Fado</a>, a melancholy urban music originated in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, usually associated with the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_guitar" title="Portuguese guitar">Portuguese guitar</a> and <i>saudade</i>, or longing. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coimbra_fado" title="Coimbra fado">Coimbra fado</a>, a unique type of "serenading" fado, is also noteworthy. Internationally notable performers include <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A1lia_Rodrigues" title="Amália Rodrigues">Amália Rodrigues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Paredes" title="Carlos Paredes">Carlos Paredes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Afonso" title="José Afonso">José Afonso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariza" title="Mariza">Mariza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_do_Carmo" title="Carlos do Carmo">Carlos do Carmo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Chainho" title="António Chainho">António Chainho</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADsia" title="Mísia">Mísia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Madredeus" title="Madredeus">Madredeus</a>.</p>
<p>In addition to <a href="/wiki/Fado" title="Fado">Fado</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">Folk</a>, the Portuguese listen to pop and other types of modern music, particularly from North America and the United Kingdom, as well as a wide range of Portuguese, Caribbean and Brazilian artists and bands. Artists with international recognition include <a href="/wiki/Dulce_Pontes" title="Dulce Pontes">Dulce Pontes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moonspell" title="Moonspell">Moonspell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buraka_Som_Sistema" title="Buraka Som Sistema">Buraka Som Sistema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blasted_Mechanism" title="Blasted Mechanism">Blasted Mechanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Gift_(band)" title="The Gift (band)">The Gift</a>, with the two latter being nominees for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Award" title="MTV Europe Music Award">MTV Europe Music Award</a>.</p>
<p>Portugal has several summer music festivals, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Festival_Sudoeste" title="Festival Sudoeste">Festival Sudoeste</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Zambujeira_do_Mar" title="Zambujeira do Mar">Zambujeira do Mar</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Festival_de_Paredes_de_Coura" title="Festival de Paredes de Coura">Festival de Paredes de Coura</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Paredes_de_Coura" title="Paredes de Coura">Paredes de Coura</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Festival_Vilar_de_Mouros" title="Festival Vilar de Mouros">Festival Vilar de Mouros</a></i> near <a href="/wiki/Caminha" title="Caminha">Caminha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boom_Festival" title="Boom Festival">Boom Festival</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idanha-a-Nova_Municipality" title="Idanha-a-Nova Municipality">Idanha-a-Nova Municipality</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimus_Alive!" title="Optimus Alive!">Optimus Alive!</a></i>, <i>Sumol Summer Fest</i> in <a href="/wiki/Ericeira" title="Ericeira">Ericeira</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_in_Rio#Lisboa.2C_Portugal.2C_and_Madrid.2C_Spain" title="Rock in Rio">Rock in Rio Lisboa</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Bock_Super_Rock" title="Super Bock Super Rock">Super Bock Super Rock</a></i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Lisbon" title="Greater Lisbon">Greater Lisbon</a>. Out of the summer season, Portugal has a large number of festivals, designed more to an urban audience, like Flowfest or Hip Hop Porto. Furthermore, one of the largest international <a href="/wiki/Goa_trance" title="Goa trance">Goa trance</a> festivals takes place in central Portugal every two years, the <a class="external text" href="http://boomfestival.org/boom2012/" rel="nofollow">Boom Festival</a>, that is also the only festival in Portugal to win international awards: European Festival Award 2010 – Green'n'Clean Festival of the Year and the Greener Festival Award Outstanding 2008 and 2010. There is also the student festivals of <i><a href="/wiki/Queima_das_Fitas" title="Queima das Fitas">Queima das Fitas</a></i> are major events in a number of cities across Portugal. In 2005, Portugal held the <a href="/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Awards" title="MTV Europe Music Awards">MTV Europe Music Awards</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavilh%C3%A3o_Atl%C3%A2ntico" title="Pavilhão Atlântico">Pavilhão Atlântico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.</p>
<p>In the classical music domain, Portugal is represented by names as the pianists <a href="/wiki/Artur_Pizarro" title="Artur Pizarro">Artur Pizarro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Jo%C3%A3o_Pires" title="Maria João Pires">Maria João Pires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sequeira_Costa" title="Sequeira Costa">Sequeira Costa</a>, the violinists Carlos Damas, <a href="/wiki/Gerardo_Ribeiro" title="Gerardo Ribeiro">Gerardo Ribeiro</a> and in the past by the great cellist <a href="/wiki/Guilhermina_Suggia" title="Guilhermina Suggia">Guilhermina Suggia</a>. Notable composers include <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Vianna_da_Motta" title="José Vianna da Motta">José Vianna da Motta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Seixas" title="Carlos Seixas">Carlos Seixas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Domingos_Bomtempo" title="João Domingos Bomtempo">João Domingos Bomtempo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Sousa_Carvalho" title="João de Sousa Carvalho">João de Sousa Carvalho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Freitas_Branco" title="Luís de Freitas Branco">Luís de Freitas Branco</a> and his student <a href="/wiki/Joly_Braga_Santos" title="Joly Braga Santos">Joly Braga Santos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Lopes-Gra%C3%A7a" title="Fernando Lopes-Graça">Fernando Lopes-Graça</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Nunes" title="Emmanuel Nunes">Emmanuel Nunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9rgio_Azevedo" title="Sérgio Azevedo">Sérgio Azevedo</a>. Similarly, contemporary composers such as Nuno Malo and Miguel d'Oliveira have achieved some international success writing <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">original music for film and television</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_arts">Visual arts</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_art" title="Portuguese art">Portuguese art</a></div>
<p>Portugal has a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Portuguese_artists" title="List of Portuguese artists">rich history in painting</a>. The first well-known painters date back to the 15th century – like <a href="/wiki/Nuno_Gon%C3%A7alves" title="Nuno Gonçalves">Nuno Gonçalves</a> – were part of the Gothic painting period. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Malhoa" title="José Malhoa">José Malhoa</a>, known for his work <i>Fado</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Columbano_Bordalo_Pinheiro" title="Columbano Bordalo Pinheiro">Columbano Bordalo Pinheiro</a> (who painted the portraits of <a href="/wiki/Te%C3%B3filo_Braga" title="Teófilo Braga">Teófilo Braga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antero_de_Quental" title="Antero de Quental">Antero de Quental</a>) were both references in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalist_school_of_painting" title="Naturalist school of painting">naturalist painting</a>.</p>
<p>The 20th century saw the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a>, and along with it came the most prominent Portuguese painters: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amadeo_de_Souza-Cardoso" title="Amadeo de Souza-Cardoso">Amadeo de Souza-Cardoso</a>, who was heavily influenced by French painters, particularly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delaunays" title="Delaunays">Delaunays</a>. Among his best-known works is <i>Canção Popular a Russa e o Fígaro</i>. Another great modernist painters/writers were <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Botelho" title="Carlos Botelho">Carlos Botelho</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almada_Negreiros" title="Almada Negreiros">Almada Negreiros</a>, friend to the poet <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a>, who painted his (Pessoa's) portrait. He was deeply influenced by both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubist" title="Cubist">Cubist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futurism_(art)" title="Futurism (art)">Futurist</a> trends.</p>
<p>Prominent international figures in visual arts nowadays include painters <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vieira_da_Silva" title="Vieira da Silva">Vieira da Silva</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BAlio_Pomar" title="Júlio Pomar">Júlio Pomar</a>, Helena Almeida, <a href="/wiki/Joana_Vasconcelos" title="Joana Vasconcelos">Joana Vasconcelos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juli%C3%A3o_Sarmento" title="Julião Sarmento">Julião Sarmento</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paula_Rego" title="Paula Rego">Paula Rego</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_Portugal" title="Sport in Portugal">Sport in Portugal</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Football</a> is the most popular sport in Portugal. There are several football competitions ranging from local amateur to world-class professional level. The legendary <a href="/wiki/Eus%C3%A9bio" title="Eusébio">Eusébio</a> is still a major symbol of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_football" title="Portuguese football">Portuguese football</a> history. <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Player_of_the_Year" title="FIFA World Player of the Year">FIFA World Player of the Year</a> winners <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Figo" title="Luís Figo">Luís Figo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cristiano_Ronaldo" title="Cristiano Ronaldo">Cristiano Ronaldo</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Ballon_d%27Or" title="FIFA Ballon d'Or">FIFA Ballon d'Or</a>, are two world-class Portuguese football players. Portuguese football managers are also noteworthy, with <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mourinho" title="José Mourinho">José Mourinho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Santos_(Portuguese_footballer)" title="Fernando Santos (Portuguese footballer)">Fernando Santos</a> are among the most renowned.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portugal national football team</a> – <i>Seleção Nacional</i> – have won one <a href="/wiki/UEFA_European_Championship" title="UEFA European Championship">UEFA European Championship</a> title: the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016" title="UEFA Euro 2016">UEFA Euro 2016</a>, with a 1–0 victory in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016_Final" title="UEFA Euro 2016 Final">final</a> over <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a>, the tournament hosts. In addition, Portugal finished second in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_European_Football_Championship" title="2004 European Football Championship">Euro 2004</a> (held in Portugal), third in the <a href="/wiki/1966_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1966 FIFA World Cup">1966 FIFA World Cup</a>, and fourth in the <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 FIFA World Cup</a>. At youth level, Portugal have won two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Youth_Championship" title="FIFA World Youth Championship">FIFA World Youth Championships</a> (in <a href="/wiki/1989_FIFA_World_Youth_Championship" title="1989 FIFA World Youth Championship">1989</a> and <a href="/wiki/1991_FIFA_World_Youth_Championship" title="1991 FIFA World Youth Championship">1991</a>) and several <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> European Youth Championships.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/S.L._Benfica" title="S.L. Benfica">S.L. Benfica</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporting_CP" title="Sporting CP">Sporting CP</a> and <a href="/wiki/FC_Porto" title="FC Porto">FC Porto</a> are the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports_clubs" title="Sports clubs">sports clubs</a> by popularity and by number of trophies won, often known as "<i>os três grandes</i>" (<a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(Portugal)" title="Big Three (Portugal)">"the big three"</a>). They have won eight titles in the European <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> club competitions, were present in many finals and have been regular contenders in the last stages almost every season. Other than football, many Portuguese sports clubs, including the "big three", compete in several other sports events with a varying level of success and popularity, these may include <a href="/wiki/Roller_hockey_(quad)" title="Roller hockey (quad)">roller hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futsal" title="Futsal">futsal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_handball" title="Team handball">handball</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Football_Federation" title="Portuguese Football Federation">Portuguese Football Federation</a> (FPF)  – <i>Federação Portuguesa de Futebol</i> – annually hosts the <a href="/wiki/Algarve_Cup" title="Algarve Cup">Algarve Cup</a>, a prestigious <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_association_football" title="Women's association football">women`s football</a> tournament that has been celebrated in the Algarvian part of Portugal.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_rugby_union_team" title="Portugal national rugby union team">Portuguese national rugby union team</a> qualified for the <a href="/wiki/2007_Rugby_World_Cup" title="2007 Rugby World Cup">2007 Rugby World Cup</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_rugby_sevens_team" title="Portugal national rugby sevens team">Portuguese national rugby sevens team</a> has played in the <a href="/wiki/World_Rugby_Sevens_Series" title="World Rugby Sevens Series">World Rugby Sevens Series</a>.</p>

<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a>, the Portuguese have won a number of gold, silver and bronze medals in the European, World and Olympic Games competitions. <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">Cycling</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Volta_a_Portugal" title="Volta a Portugal">Volta a Portugal</a> being the most important race, is also a popular sports event and include professional cycling teams such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporting_CP" title="Sporting CP">Sporting CP</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boavista_(cycling_team)" title="Boavista (cycling team)">Boavista</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clube_de_Ciclismo_de_Tavira" title="Clube de Ciclismo de Tavira">Clube de Ciclismo de Tavira</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uni%C3%A3o_Ciclista_da_Maia" title="União Ciclista da Maia">União Ciclista da Maia</a>.</p>
<p>The country has also achieved notable performances in sports like <a href="/wiki/Fencing" title="Fencing">fencing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitesurf" title="Kitesurf">kitesurf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a>, sailing, <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a>, shooting, <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windsurf" title="Windsurf">windsurf</a>, owning several European and world titles. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paralympic" title="Paralympic">paralympic</a> athletes have also conquered many medals in sports like swimming, <a href="/wiki/Boccia" title="Boccia">boccia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>.</p>
<p>In motorsport, Portugal is internationally noted for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rally_of_Portugal" title="Rally of Portugal">Rally of Portugal</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estoril_Circuit" title="Estoril Circuit">Estoril</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algarve_Circuit" title="Algarve Circuit">Algarve Circuits</a> and the revived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porto_Street_Circuit" title="Porto Street Circuit">Porto Street Circuit</a> which holds a stage of the WTCC every two years, as well as for a number of internationally noted pilots in varied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorsports" title="Motorsports">motorsports</a>.</p>
<p>In equestrian sports, Portugal won the only Horseball-Pato World Championship (in 2006), achieved the third position in the First <a href="/wiki/Horseball" title="Horseball">Horseball</a> World Cup (organized in Ponte de Lima, Portugal, in 2008), and has achieved several victories in the European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_Equitation" title="Working Equitation">Working Equitation</a> Championship.</p>
<p>In water sports, Portugal has two major sports: <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_polo" title="Water polo">water polo</a>. Northern Portugal has its own original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martial_art" title="Martial art">martial art</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jogo_do_Pau" title="Jogo do Pau">Jogo do Pau</a></i>, in which the fighters use staffs to confront one or several opponents. Other popular sport-related recreational outdoor activities with thousands of enthusiasts nationwide include <a href="/wiki/Airsoft" title="Airsoft">airsoft</a>, fishing, <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, hiking, hunting and <a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">orienteering</a>.</p>
<p>Portugal is one of the world's best <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> destinations. It has received several awards by the World Golf Awards. Its weather allows play all year round.</p>
