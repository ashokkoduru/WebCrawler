<p><b>Methane</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a></small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<sub>4</sub></span> (one atom of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and four atoms of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>). It is a <a href="/wiki/Group_14_hydride" title="Group 14 hydride">group 14 hydride</a> and the simplest <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a>, and is the main component of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. The relative abundance of methane on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> makes it an attractive <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>, though capturing and storing it poses challenges due to its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaseous" title="Gaseous">gaseous</a> state under <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">normal conditions for temperature and pressure</a>.</p>
<p>In its natural state, methane is found both below ground and under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a>. When it finds its way to the surface and the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, it is known as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a>. The Earth's atmospheric methane concentration has increased by about 150% since 1750, and it accounts for 20% of the total <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> from all of the long-lived and globally mixed <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> (these gases don't include water vapor which is by far the largest component of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>). Methane <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane#Removal_processes" title="Atmospheric methane">breaks down</a> in the atmosphere and creates <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">CH<sub>3</sub><b></b></a> with water vapor.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>

<p>In November 1776, methane was first scientifically identified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Volta" title="Alessandro Volta">Alessandro Volta</a> in the marshes of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a> straddling <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. Volta was inspired to search for the substance after reading a paper written by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> about "flammable air". Volta captured the gas rising from the marsh, and by 1778 had isolated the pure gas. He also demonstrated means to ignite the gas with an electric spark.</p>
<p>The name "methane" was coined in 1866 by the German chemist <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann" title="August Wilhelm von Hofmann">August Wilhelm von Hofmann</a>. The name was deriven from <a href="/wiki/Methanol#History" title="Methanol">methanol</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_bonding">Properties and bonding</span></h2>
<p>Methane is a <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a> molecule with four equivalent <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond" title="Carbon–hydrogen bond">C–H bonds</a>. Its electronic structure is described by four bonding molecular orbitals (MOs) resulting from the overlap of the valence orbitals on <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a>. The lowest energy MO is the result of the overlap of the 2s orbital on carbon with the in-phase combination of the 1s orbitals on the four hydrogen atoms. Above this energy level is a triply degenerate set of MOs that involve overlap of the 2p orbitals on carbon with various linear combinations of the 1s orbitals on hydrogen. The resulting "three-over-one" bonding scheme is consistent with photoelectron spectroscopic measurements.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_pressure" title="Standard pressure">standard pressure</a>, methane is a colorless, odorless gas. The familiar smell of natural gas as used in homes is achieved by the addition of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odorant" title="Odorant">odorant</a>, usually blends containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tert-butylthiol" title="Tert-butylthiol">tert-butylthiol</a>, as a safety measure. Methane has a boiling point of −161 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> (−257.8 °<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">F</a>) at a pressure of one <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere</a>. As a gas it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a> over a range of concentrations (5.4–17%) in air at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_pressure" title="Standard pressure">standard pressure</a>.</p>
<p>Solid methane exists in several <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">modifications</a>. Presently nine are known. Cooling methane at normal pressure results in the formation of methane I. This substance crystallizes in the cubic system (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> Fm<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span>m). The positions of the hydrogen atoms are not fixed in methane I, i.e. methane molecules may rotate freely. Therefore, it is a <a href="/wiki/Plastic_crystal" title="Plastic crystal">plastic crystal</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span></h2>
<p>The primary chemical reactions of methane are <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> to <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">halogenation</a>. In general, methane reactions are difficult to control. Partial oxidation to <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, for example, is challenging because the reaction typically progresses all the way to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> even with an insufficient supply of oxygen. The enzyme <a href="/wiki/Methane_monooxygenase" title="Methane monooxygenase">methane monooxygenase</a> produces methanol from methane, but cannot be used for industrial-scale reactions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid-base_reactions">Acid-base reactions</span></h3>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, methane is a very weak acid. Its pKa in <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">DMSO</a> is estimated to be 56. It cannot be <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonated</a> in solution, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> with <a href="/wiki/Methyllithium" title="Methyllithium">methyllithium</a> is known.</p>
<p>A variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">positive ions</a> derived from methane have been observed, mostly as unstable species in low-pressure gas mixtures. These include <a href="/wiki/Methenium" title="Methenium">methenium</a> or methyl cation <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
3</span></span>, methane cation <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span>, and <a href="/wiki/Methanium" title="Methanium">methanium</a> or protonated methane <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
5</span></span>. Some of these have been <a href="/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules" title="List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules">detected in outer space</a>. Methanium can also be produced as diluted solutions from methane with <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacids</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">Cations</a> with higher charge, such as <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2+<br/>
6</span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3+<br/>
7</span></span>, have been studied theoretically and conjectured to be stable.</p>
<p>Despite the strength of its C–H bonds, there is intense interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalysts" title="Catalysts">catalysts</a> that facilitate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%E2%80%93H_bond_activation" title="C–H bond activation">C–H bond activation</a> in methane (and other lower numbered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion">Combustion</span></h3>
<p>Methane's <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">heat of combustion</a> is 55.5 MJ/kg. <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">Combustion</a> of methane is a multiple step reaction. The following equations are part of the process, with the net result being:</p>

<li>CH<sub>4</sub>+ M → CH<sub>3</sub> + H + M</li>
<li>CH<sub>4</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>3</sub> + HO<sub>2</sub></li>
<li>CH<sub>4</sub> + HO<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>3</sub> + 2 OH</li>
<li>CH<sub>4</sub> + OH → CH<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</li>
<li>O<sub>2</sub> + H → O + OH</li>
<li>CH<sub>4</sub> + O → CH<sub>3</sub> + OH</li>
<li>CH<sub>3</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>2</sub>O + OH</li>
<li>CH<sub>2</sub>O + O → CHO + OH</li>
<li>CH<sub>2</sub>O + OH → CHO + H<sub>2</sub>O</li>
<li>CH<sub>2</sub>O + H → CHO + H<sub>2</sub></li>
<li>CHO + O → CO + OH</li>
<li>CHO + OH → CO + H<sub>2</sub>O</li>
<li>CHO + H → CO + H<sub>2</sub></li>
<li>H<sub>2</sub> + O → H + OH</li>
<li>H<sub>2</sub> + OH → H + H<sub>2</sub>O</li>
<li>CO + OH → CO<sub>2</sub> + H</li>
<li>H + OH + M → H<sub>2</sub>O + M</li>
<li>H + H + M → H<sub>2</sub> + M</li>
<li>H + O<sub>2</sub> + M → HO<sub>2</sub> + M</li>
</ol>
<p>The species M signifies an energetic third body, from which <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> is transferred during a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_collision&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Molecular collision (page does not exist)">molecular collision</a>. <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">Formaldehyde</a> (HCHO or <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>CO</span>) is an early intermediate (reaction 7). Oxidation of formaldehyde gives the formyl <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> (HCO; reactions 8–10), which then give <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO) (reactions 11, 12 &amp; 13). Any resulting H<sub>2</sub> oxidizes to H<sub>2</sub>O or other intermediates (reaction 14, 15). Finally, the CO <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizes" title="Oxidizes">oxidizes</a>, forming CO<sub>2</sub> (reaction 16). In the final stages (reactions 17–19), <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> is transferred back to other third <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">bodies</a>. The overall <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> of reaction is a function of the concentration of the various entities during the combustion process. The higher the temperature, the greater the concentration of radical species and the more rapid the combustion process.</p>

<p>where X is a halogen: <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> (F), <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl), <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> (Br), or <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> (I). This mechanism for this process is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical_halogenation" title="Free radical halogenation">free radical halogenation</a>. It is initiated with UV light or some other <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> initiator. A chlorine atom is generated from elemental chlorine, which abstracts a hydrogen atom from methane, resulting in the formation of hydrogen chloride. The resulting methyl radical, CH<sub>3</sub>•, can combine with another chlorine molecule to give <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>Cl) and a chlorine atom. Similar reactions can produce <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> (CH<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> (CHCl<sub>3</sub>), and, ultimately, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> (CCl<sub>4</sub>), depending upon reaction conditions and the chlorine to methane ratio.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<p>Methane is used in industrial chemical processes and may be transported as a refrigerated liquid (liquefied natural gas, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG" title="LNG">LNG</a>). While leaks from a refrigerated liquid container are initially heavier than air due to the increased density of the cold gas, the gas at ambient temperature is lighter than air. <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">Gas pipelines</a> distribute large amounts of natural gas, of which methane is the principal component.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel">Fuel</span></h3>
<p>Methane is used as a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> for ovens, homes, water heaters, kilns, automobiles, turbines, and other things. It <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combusts</a> with oxygen to create fire.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a></div>
<p>Methane is important for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generation" title="Electrical generation">electrical generation</a> by burning it as a fuel in a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boiler_(power_generation)" title="Boiler (power generation)">steam generator</a>. Compared to other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">hydrocarbon fuels</a>, methane produces less <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> for each unit of heat released. At about 891 kJ/mol, methane's <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">heat of combustion</a> is lower than any other hydrocarbon but the ratio of the heat of combustion (891 kJ/mol) to the molecular mass (16.0 g/mol, of which 12.0 g/mol is carbon) shows that methane, being the simplest hydrocarbon, produces more heat per mass unit (55.7 kJ/g) than other complex hydrocarbons. In many cities, methane is piped into homes for domestic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">heating</a> and cooking. In this context it is usually known as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, which is considered to have an energy content of 39 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">megajoules</a> per cubic meter, or 1,000 <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTU</a> per <a href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_foot" title="Standard cubic foot">standard cubic foot</a>.</p>
<p>Methane in the form of <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicle_fuel" title="Vehicle fuel">vehicle fuel</a> and is claimed to be more environmentally friendly than other fossil fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">petrol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>. Research into <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> methods of methane storage for use as an automotive fuel has been conducted.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquefied_natural_gas">Liquefied natural gas</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">Liquefied natural gas</a></div>
<p>Liquefied natural gas (LNG) is <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (predominantly methane, CH<sub>4</sub>) that has been converted to liquid form for ease of storage or transport.</p>
<p>Liquefied natural gas takes up about 1/600th the volume of natural gas in the gaseous state. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odorless" title="Odorless">odorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">colorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">non-toxic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corrosive_substance" title="Corrosive substance">non-corrosive</a>. Hazards include flammability after vaporization into a gaseous state, freezing, and <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">liquefaction</a> process involves removal of certain components, such as dust, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_gases" title="Acid gases">acid gases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, water, and heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>, which could cause difficulty downstream. The natural gas is then <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensed</a> into a liquid at close to atmospheric pressure (maximum transport pressure set at around 25 kPa or 3.6 psi) by cooling it to approximately −162 °C (−260 °F).</p>
<p>LNG achieves a higher reduction in volume than <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> (CNG) so that the <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of LNG is 2.4 times greater than that of CNG or 60% that of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>. This makes LNG cost efficient to transport over long distances where pipelines do not exist. Specially designed <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> sea vessels (<a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a>) or cryogenic road tankers are used for its transport.</p>
<p>LNG, when it is not highly refined for special uses, is principally used for transporting natural gas to markets, where it is regasified and distributed as pipeline natural gas. It is also beginning to be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicles" title="Natural gas vehicles">LNG-fueled road vehicles</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer_truck" title="Semi-trailer truck">trucks</a> in commercial operation have been achieving <a href="/wiki/Payback_period" title="Payback period">payback periods</a> of approximately four years on the higher initial investment required in LNG equipment on the trucks and LNG infrastructure to support fueling. However, it remains more common to design vehicles to use <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a>. As of 2002, the relatively higher cost of LNG production and the need to store LNG in more expensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewar_flask" title="Dewar flask">cryogenic tanks</a> had slowed widespread commercial use.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_methane_rocket_fuel">Liquid methane rocket fuel</span></h4>
<p>In a highly refined form, liquid methane is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket_propellants#Bipropellants" title="Liquid rocket propellants">used as</a> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuel</a>.</p>
<p>Though methane has been investigated for decades, no production methane engines have yet been used on <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital</a> spaceflights. This promises to change as liquid methane has recently been selected for the <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">active development</a> of a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipropellant" title="Bipropellant">bipropellant</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_engines" title="Rocket engines">rocket engines</a>.</p>
<p>Since the 1990s, a number of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> rockets using liquid methane have been proposed. One 1990s Russian engine proposal was the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RD-192&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RD-192 (page does not exist)">RD-192</a>, a methane/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LOX" title="LOX">LOX</a> variant of the <a href="/wiki/RD-191" title="RD-191">RD-191</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005, US companies, Orbitech and <a href="/wiki/XCOR_Aerospace" title="XCOR Aerospace">XCOR Aerospace</a>, developed a demonstration <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a>/liquid methane rocket engine and a larger 7,500 pounds-force (33 kN)-thrust engine in 2007 for potential use as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">CEV</a> lunar return engine, before the CEV program was later cancelled.</p>
<p>More recently the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> company <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> announced in 2012 an initiative to develop <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_rocket_engine_family#Methane-based_engines" title="SpaceX rocket engine family">liquid methane rocket engines</a>, including initially, the very large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raptor_(rocket_engine)" title="Raptor (rocket engine)">Raptor</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a>. Raptor is being designed to produce 4.4 meganewtons (1,000,000 lbf) of thrust with a vacuum <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> (I<sub>sp</sub>) of 363 seconds and a sea-level I<sub>sp</sub> of 321 seconds, and began component-level testing in 2014. In February 2014, the Raptor engine design was shown to be of the highly efficient and theoretically more reliable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-flow_staged_combustion" title="Full-flow staged combustion">full-flow staged combustion</a> cycle type, where both propellant streams—oxidizer and fuel—are completely in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase" title="Gas phase">gas phase</a> before they enter the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine#Combustion_chamber" title="Rocket engine">combustion chamber</a>. Prior to 2014, only two full-flow rocket engines had ever progressed sufficiently to be tested on test stands, but neither engine completed development or flew on a flight vehicle. In 2016, a development Raptor engine was tested.</p>
<p>In October 2013, the <a href="/wiki/China_Aerospace_Science_and_Technology_Corporation" title="China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation">China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation</a>, a state-owned contractor for the <a href="/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration" title="China National Space Administration">Chinese space program</a>, announced that it had completed a first ignition test on a new LOX methane rocket engine. No engine size was provided.</p>
<p>In September 2014, another <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> company—<a href="/wiki/Blue_Origin" title="Blue Origin">Blue Origin</a>— publicly announced that they were into their third year of development work on a large methane rocket engine. The new engine, the <i>Blue Engine 4</i>, or <b><a href="/wiki/BE-4" title="BE-4">BE-4</a></b>, has been designed to produce 2,400 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">kilonewtons</a> (550,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">lbf</a>) of thrust. While initially planned to be used exclusively on a Blue Origin proprietary launch vehicle, it will now be used on a new <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a> (ULA) engine on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_V_successor" title="Atlas V successor">new launch vehicle</a> that is a successor to the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>. ULA indicated in 2014 that they will make the maiden flight of the new launch vehicle no earlier than 2019.</p>
<p>One advantage of methane is that it is abundant in many parts of the solar system and it could potentially be harvested on the surface of another solar-system body (in particular, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_Situ_Resource_Utilization" title="In Situ Resource Utilization">methane production from local materials</a> found on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>), providing fuel for a return journey.</p>
<p>By 2013, NASA's <a href="/wiki/Project_Morpheus" title="Project Morpheus">Project Morpheus</a> had developed a small restartable LOX methane rocket engine with 5,000 pounds-force (22 kN) thrust and a specific impulse of 321 seconds suitable for inspace applications including landers. Small LOX methane thrusters 5–15 pounds-force (22–67 N) were also developed suitable for use in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reaction_Control_System" title="Reaction Control System">Reaction Control System</a> (RCS).</p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceNews_(publication)" title="SpaceNews (publication)">SpaceNews</a></i> is reporting in early 2015 that the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> space agency <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">CNES</a> is working with <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and a few other <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> and will propose a LOX/methane engine on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RLV" title="RLV">reusable launch vehicle</a> by mid-2015, with flight testing unlikely before approximately 2026.</p>

<p>Related chemistries are exploited in the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber-Bosch Synthesis</a> of ammonia from air, which is reduced with natural gas to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>.</p>
<p>Methane is also subjected to free-radical <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">chlorination</a> in the production of chloromethanes, although methanol is a more typical precursor.</p>

Methanogenesis is a form of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a> used by organisms that occupy <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruminants" title="Ruminants">ruminants</a> (e.g., cattle), and the guts of termites.</p>
<p>It is uncertain if plants are a source of methane emissions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_gas">Power to gas</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">Power to gas</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">Power to gas</a> is a technology which converts <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> power to a gas <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>. The method is used to convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water to methane, (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) using <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>. Excess and off-peak power generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_generators" title="Wind generators">wind generators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar arrays</a> could theoretically be used for load balancing in the energy grid.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_routes">Industrial routes</span></h3>
<p>Methane can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenating</a> carbon dioxide through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabatier_process" title="Sabatier process">Sabatier process</a>. Methane is also a side product of the hydrogenation of carbon monoxide in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a>. This technology is practiced on a large scale to produce longer chain molecules than methane.</p>
<p>Natural gas is so abundant that the intentional production of methane is relatively rare. The only large scale facility of this kind is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Plains_Synfuels" title="Great Plains Synfuels">Great Plains Synfuels</a> plant, started in 1984 in Beulah, North Dakota as a way to develop abundant local resources of low grade <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, a resource which is otherwise very hard to transport for its weight, <a href="/wiki/Coal_assay#Ash" title="Coal assay">ash</a> content, low calorific value and propensity to <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_combustion" title="Spontaneous combustion">spontaneous combustion</a> during storage and transport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_synthesis">Laboratory synthesis</span></h3>
<p>Methane can also be produced by the <a href="/wiki/Destructive_distillation" title="Destructive distillation">destructive distillation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Soda_lime" title="Soda lime">soda lime</a> or similar. Acetic acid is <a href="/wiki/Decarboxylation" title="Decarboxylation">decarboxylated</a> in this process. Methane can also be prepared by reaction of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_carbide" title="Aluminium carbide">aluminium carbide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acids" title="Strong acids">strong acids</a>. It is also made by reducing a solution of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and concentrated <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> with <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> powder, giving water and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrous_chloride" title="Ferrous chloride">ferrous chloride</a> as a byproduct.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_Mars">On Mars</span></h3>
<p>Methane has been proposed as a possible rocket propellant on future Mars missions due in part to the possibility of synthesizing it on the planet via <a href="/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization#Mars" title="In situ resource utilization">in situ resource utilization</a>.</p>
<p>An adaptation of the Sabatier methanation reaction may be used via a mixed catalyst bed and a reverse water gas shift in a single reactor to produce methane from the raw materials available on Mars, utilizing water from the Martian subsoil and carbon dioxide in the Martian atmosphere.</p>
<p>Methane could also be produced by a non-biological process called <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite"><i>serpentinization</i></a> involving water, carbon dioxide, and the mineral <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>, which is known to be common on Mars.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span></h2>
<p>Methane was discovered and isolated by <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Volta" title="Alessandro Volta">Alessandro Volta</a> between 1776 and 1778 when studying marsh gas from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a>. It is the major component of natural gas, about 87% by volume. The major source of methane is extraction from geological deposits known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_fields" title="Natural gas fields">natural gas fields</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_seam_gas" title="Coal seam gas">coal seam gas</a> extraction becoming a major source (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_bed_methane_extraction" title="Coal bed methane extraction">Coal bed methane extraction</a>, a method for extracting methane from a <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> deposit, while <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_coal_bed_methane_recovery" title="Enhanced coal bed methane recovery">enhanced coal bed methane recovery</a> is a method of recovering methane from non-mineable coal seams). It is associated with other <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fuels, and sometimes accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. Methane is produced at shallow levels (low pressure) by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> and reworked methane from deep under the Earth's surface. In general, the <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> that generate natural gas are buried deeper and at higher temperatures than those that contain <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>.</p>
<p>Methane is generally transported in bulk by <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a> in its natural gas form, or LNG carriers in its liquefied form; few countries transport it by truck.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_sources">Alternative sources</span></h3>

<p>Apart from gas fields, an alternative method of obtaining methane is via <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> generated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> of organic matter including <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, wastewater sludge, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a> (including landfills), or any other biodegradable feedstock, under anaerobic conditions. <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">Rice</a> fields also generate large amounts of methane during plant growth. <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">Methane hydrates/clathrates</a> (ice-like combinations of methane and water on the sea floor, found in vast quantities) are a potential future source of methane. Cattle belch methane accounts for 16% of the world's annual methane emissions to the atmosphere. One study reported that the livestock sector in general (primarily cattle, chickens, and pigs) produces 37% of all human-induced methane. Early research has found a number of medical treatments and dietary adjustments that help slightly limit the production of methane in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruminants" title="Ruminants">ruminants</a>. A 2009 study found that at a conservative estimate, at least 51% of global greenhouse gas emissions were attributable to the life cycle and supply chain of livestock products, meaning all meat, dairy, and by-products, and their transportation. More recently, a 2013 study estimated that livestock accounted for 44 percent of human-induced methane and 14.5 percent of human-induced greenhouse gas emissions. Many efforts are underway to reduce livestock methane production and trap the gas to use as energy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</a> research published in <i><a href="/wiki/Current_Biology" title="Current Biology">Current Biology</a></i> suggests that <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinosaurs" title="Dinosaurs">dinosaurs</a> may have warmed the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_methane">Atmospheric methane</span></h3>
<p><span id="Methane_as_a_greenhouse_gas"></span></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">Atmospheric methane</a></div>

<p>Methane is created near the Earth's surface, primarily by microorganisms by the process of <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>. It is carried into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> by rising air in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. Uncontrolled build-up of methane in the atmosphere is naturally checked – although human influence can upset this natural regulation – by methane's reaction with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_radicals" title="Hydroxyl radicals">hydroxyl radicals</a> formed from <a href="/wiki/Singlet_oxygen" title="Singlet oxygen">singlet oxygen</a> atoms and with water vapor. It has a net lifetime of about 10 years, and is primarily removed by conversion to carbon dioxide and water.</p>
<p>In addition, there is a large (but unknown) amount of methane in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_clathrates" title="Methane clathrates">methane clathrates</a> in the ocean floors as well as the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crust(geology)" title="Crust(geology)">crust</a>.</p>
<p>In 2010, methane levels in the Arctic were measured at 1850 nmol/mol, a level over twice as high as at any time in the 400,000 years prior to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>. Historically, methane concentrations in the world's atmosphere have ranged between 300 and 400 nmol/mol during glacial periods commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_ages" title="Ice ages">ice ages</a>, and between 600 and 700 nmol/mol during the warm <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> periods. Recent research suggests that the Earth's oceans are a potentially important new source of Arctic methane.</p>
<p>Methane is an important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> of 34 compared to CO<sub>2</sub> over a 100-year period, and 72 over a 20-year period.</p>
<p>The Earth's atmospheric methane concentration has increased by about 150% since 1750, and it accounts for 20% of the total <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> from all of the long-lived and globally mixed greenhouse gases (these gases don't include water vapor which is by far the largest component of the greenhouse effect).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clathrates">Clathrates</span></h3>
<p>Methane is essentially insoluble in water, but it can be trapped in ice forming a similar solid. Significant deposits of <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a> have been found under sediments on the ocean floors of Earth at large depths.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a> from <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> and methane clathrates is an expected consequence and further cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane">Anaerobic oxidation of methane</span></h2>
<p>There is a group of bacteria that drive methane oxidation with nitrite as the oxidant, the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane" title="Anaerobic oxidation of methane">anaerobic oxidation of methane</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>Methane is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nontoxic" title="Nontoxic">nontoxic</a>, yet it is extremely flammable and may form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a> mixtures with air. Methane is violently reactive with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a>, and some halogen-containing compounds. Methane is also an <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiant</a> and may displace <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in an enclosed space. <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">Asphyxia</a> may result if the oxygen concentration is reduced to below about 16% by displacement, as most people can <a href="/wiki/Cabin_pressurization#need_for_cabin_pressurisation" title="Cabin pressurization">tolerate a reduction from 21% to 16% without ill effects</a>. The concentration of methane at which asphyxiation risk becomes significant is much higher than the 5–15% concentration in a flammable or explosive mixture. Methane off-gas can penetrate the interiors of buildings near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">landfills</a> and expose occupants to significant levels of methane. Some buildings have specially engineered recovery systems below their basements to actively capture this gas and vent it away from the building.</p>
<p>Methane gas explosions are responsible for many deadly mining disasters. A methane gas explosion was the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Big_Branch_Mine_disaster" title="Upper Big Branch Mine disaster">Upper Big Branch coal mine disaster</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> on April 5, 2010, killing 25.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_methane">Extraterrestrial methane</span></h2>
<p>Methane has been detected or is believed to exist on all planets of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a> and most of the larger moons. With the possible exceptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet)" title="Life on Mars (planet)">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, it is believed to have come from <a href="/wiki/Abiogenic_petroleum_origin" title="Abiogenic petroleum origin">abiotic</a> processes.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> – the tenuous atmosphere contains trace amounts of methane.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> – the atmosphere contains a large amount of methane from 60 km (37 mi) to the surface according to data collected by the <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus_project" title="Pioneer Venus project">Pioneer Venus</a> Large Probe Neutral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_Spectrometer" title="Mass Spectrometer">Mass Spectrometer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> – traces are outgassed from the surface</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars#Methane" title="Atmosphere of Mars">Martian atmosphere</a> contains 10 nmol/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a> methane. The source of methane on Mars has not been determined. Recent research suggests that methane may come from <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault_line" title="Fault line">fault lines</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a>, that it may be a byproduct of electrical discharges from <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">dust devils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a>, or that it may be the result of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. In January 2009, NASA scientists announced that they had discovered that the planet often vents methane into the atmosphere in specific areas, leading some to speculate this may be a sign of biological activity below the surface. Studies of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_Research_and_Forecasting_model" title="Weather Research and Forecasting model">Weather Research and Forecasting model</a> for Mars (MarsWRF) and related <a href="/wiki/Mars_general_circulation_model" title="Mars general circulation model">Mars general circulation model (MGCM)</a> suggests that <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars#Methane" title="Atmosphere of Mars">methane plume source</a>s may be located within tens of kilometers, which is within the roving capabilities of <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">future Mars rovers</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_rover" title="Curiosity rover">Curiosity rover</a>, which landed on Mars in August 2012, can distinguish between different <a href="/wiki/Isotopologue" title="Isotopologue">isotopologues</a> of methane; but even if the mission determines that microscopic Martian life is the source of the methane, it probably resides far below the surface, beyond the rover's reach. Curiosity's <a href="/wiki/Sample_Analysis_at_Mars" title="Sample Analysis at Mars">Sample Analysis at Mars</a> (SAM) instrument is capable of tracking the presence of methane over time to determine if it is constant, variable, seasonal, or random, providing further clues about its source. The first measurements with the <a href="/wiki/Sample_Analysis_at_Mars" title="Sample Analysis at Mars">Tunable Laser Spectrometer (TLS)</a> indicated that there is less than 5 ppb of methane at the landing site.

The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Trace_Gas_Mission" title="Mars Trace Gas Mission">Mars Trace Gas Mission</a> orbiter planned for launch in 2016 would further study Mars' methane and its decomposition products such as <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. Alternatively, these compounds may instead be replenished by volcanic or other geological means, such as serpentinization. On July 19, 2013, NASA scientists reported finding "not much methane" (i.e., "an upper limit of 2.7 parts per billion of methane") around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gale_Crater" title="Gale Crater">Gale Crater</a> where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_rover" title="Curiosity rover">Curiosity rover</a> landed in August 2012. On September 19, 2013, from further measurements by Curiosity, NASA scientists reported no detection of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a> with a value of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999180000000000000♠</span>0.18<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.67</span> ppbv corresponding to an upper limit of only 1.3 ppbv (95% confidence limit), and as a result, concluded that the probability of current methanogenic microbial activity on Mars is reduced. On 16 December 2014, NASA reported the <i>Curiosity</i> rover detected a "tenfold spike", likely localized, in the amount of methane in the Martian atmosphere. Sample measurements taken "a dozen times over 20 months" showed increases in late 2013 and early 2014, averaging "7 parts of methane per billion in the atmosphere." Before and after that, readings averaged around one-tenth that level.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> – the atmosphere contains 4500 ± 2000 ppm methane

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">Enceladus</a> – the atmosphere contains 1.7% methane</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">Iapetus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> – the atmosphere contains 1.6% methane and thousands of methane lakes have been detected on the surface. In the upper atmosphere, methane is converted into more complex molecules including <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>, a process that also produces molecular <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. There is evidence that acetylene and hydrogen are recycled into methane near the surface. This suggests the presence either of an exotic catalyst, possibly an unknown form of methanogenic life. Methane showers, probably prompted by changing seasons, have also been observed. On October 24, 2014, methane was found in polar clouds on Titan.</li>
</ul>

Research indicates that <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a> slamming against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">exoplanet</a> atmospheres could add hydrocarbon gases such as methane, making the exoplanets look as though they are inhabited by life, even if they are not.</li>
