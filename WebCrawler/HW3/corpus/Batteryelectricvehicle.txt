<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For electric vehicles other than battery powered vehicles, see <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a>. For passenger electric vehicles, see <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a>. For the batteries themselves, see <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_battery" title="Electric vehicle battery">electric vehicle battery</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>battery electric vehicle</b> (<b>BEV</b>), <b>battery-only electric vehicle</b> (<b>BOEV</b>) or <b>all-electric vehicle</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> (EV) that uses <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> stored in <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable battery</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_pack" title="Battery pack">packs</a>. BEVs use <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">motor controllers</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> (ICEs) for propulsion. They derive all power from battery packs and thus have no internal combustion engine, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tank</a>. BEVs include bicycles, scooters, skateboards, rail cars, watercraft, forklifts, buses, trucks and cars. Since the introduction of the all-electric Nissan Leaf in December 2010, about 1 million highway legal <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">plug-in electric vehicles</a> have been sold worldwide by mid-September 2015, of which about 620,000 are all-electric passenger cars and light-duty trucks. As of June 2016, the best-selling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-electric_car" title="All-electric car">all-electric car</a> in history is the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a>, with global sales of more than 228,000 units, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">Tesla Model S</a>, with global sales of almost 130,000 units.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">Hybrid electric vehicle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">Plug-in hybrid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">Plug-in electric vehicle</a></div>
<p>Vehicles using both electric motors and internal combustion engines are examples of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid electric vehicles</a>, and are not considered pure or all-electric vehicles because they cannot be externally charged (operate in charge-sustaining mode) and instead they are continually recharged with power from the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking">regenerative braking</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">Hybrid vehicles</a> with batteries that can be charged externally to displace some or all of their internal combustion engine power and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> fuel are called <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> electric vehicles (PHEV), and run as BEVs during their <a href="/wiki/Charge-depleting" title="Charge-depleting">charge-depleting</a> mode. PHEVs with a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle_drivetrain#Series_hybrid" title="Hybrid vehicle drivetrain">series</a> <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">powertrain</a> are also called range-extended electric vehicles (REEVs), such as the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Volt" title="Chevrolet Volt">Chevrolet Volt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fisker_Karma" title="Fisker Karma">Fisker Karma</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">Plug-in electric vehicles</a> (PEVs) are a subcategory of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> that includes battery electric vehicles (BEVs), plug-in hybrid vehicles, (PHEVs), and <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_conversion" title="Electric vehicle conversion">electric vehicle conversions</a> of hybrid electric vehicles and conventional <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> vehicles.</p>
<p>In China, plug-in electric vehicles, together with hybrid electric vehicles are called new energy vehicles (NEVs). However, in the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a> (NEVs) are battery electric vehicles that are legally limited to roads with posted speed limits no higher than 45 miles per hour (72 km/h), are usually built to have a top speed of 30 miles per hour (48 km/h), and have a maximum loaded weight of 3,000 lbs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles_by_type">Vehicles by type</span></h2>
<p>The concept of battery electric vehicles is to use charged <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_battery" title="Electric vehicle battery">batteries</a> on board vehicles for propulsion. Battery electric cars are becoming more and more attractive with the advancement of new battery technology (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_Ion" title="Lithium Ion">Lithium Ion</a>) that have higher power and <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> (i.e., greater possible acceleration and more range with fewer batteries) and higher oil prices.</p>
<p>BEVs include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truck#Light_trucks" title="Truck">light trucks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_bus">Electric bus</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_bus" title="Battery electric bus">Battery electric bus</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a> operates nine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-fare" title="Zero-fare">zero-fare</a> electric buses, which have been in operation since 1992 and have carried 11.3 million passengers and covered a distance of 3,100,000 kilometres (1,900,000 mi), They were made locally by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Vehicle_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Advanced Vehicle Systems (page does not exist)">Advanced Vehicle Systems</a>. Two of these buses were used for the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996 Summer Olympics</a> in Atlanta.</p>
<p>Beginning in the summer of 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Airport" title="Hong Kong Airport">Hong Kong Airport</a> began operating a 16-passenger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Rosa" title="Mitsubishi Rosa">Mitsubishi Rosa</a> electric shuttle bus, and in the fall of 2000, New York City began testing a 66-passenger battery-powered <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school bus</a>, an all-electric version of the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Bird_TC/2000" title="Blue Bird TC/2000">Blue Bird TC/2000</a>. A similar bus was operated in <a href="/wiki/Napa_County,_California" title="Napa County, California">Napa Valley, California</a> for 14 months ending in April, 2004.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Beijing Olympics</a> used a fleet of 50 electric buses, which have a range of 130 km (81 mi) with the air conditioning on. They use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-ion" title="Lithium-ion">Lithium-ion</a> batteries, and consume about 1 kW·h/mi (0.62 kW·h/km; 2.2 MJ/km). The buses were designed by the Beijing Institute of Technology and built by the Jinghua Coach Co. Ltd. The batteries are replaced with fully charged ones at the recharging station to allow 24-hour operation of the buses.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the bus electric phenomenon is in development, but we already can find some of them in operation in numerous cities of France. PVI, a medium company located in the Paris region, is one of the leader of the market with its brand <a href="/wiki/Gepebus" title="Gepebus">Gepebus</a> (offering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oreos_2X" title="Oreos 2X">Oreos 2X</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oreos_4X" title="Oreos 4X">Oreos 4X</a>).</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the first battery-electric, fast-charge bus has been in operation in <a href="/wiki/Pomona,_California" title="Pomona, California">Pomona, California</a> since September 2010 at <a href="/wiki/Foothill_Transit" title="Foothill Transit">Foothill Transit</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Proterra,_Inc." title="Proterra, Inc.">Proterra</a> EcoRide BE35 uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-titanate_batteries" title="Lithium-titanate batteries">lithium-titanate batteries</a> and is able to fast-charge in less than 10 minutes.</p>
<p>In 2014, the first production model all-electric school bus was delivered to the Kings Canyon Unified School District in California’s <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a>. The bus was one of four the district ordered. This battery electric school bus, which has 4 sodium nickel batteries, is the first modern electric school bus approved for student transportation by any state.</p>

<p>The same technology is used to power the Mountain View Community Shuttles. This technology was supported by the California Energy Commission, and the shuttle program is being supported by Google.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunder_Sky">Thunder Sky</span></h4>
<p>Thunder Sky (based in Hong Kong) builds <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_batteries" title="Lithium-ion batteries">lithium-ion batteries</a> used in submarines and has three models of electric buses, the 10/21 passenger EV-6700 with a range of 280 km (170 mi) under 20 mins quick-charge, the EV-2009 city buses, and the 43 passenger EV-2008 highway bus, which has a range of 300 km (190 mi) under quick-charge (20 mins to 80 percent), and 350 km (220 mi) under full charge (25 mins). The buses will also be built in the United States and Finland.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_Tindo">Free Tindo</span></h4>
<p>Tindo is an all-electric bus from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adelaide,_Australia" title="Adelaide, Australia">Adelaide, Australia</a>. The Tindo (aboriginal word for sun) is made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designline" title="Designline">Designline International</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and gets its electricity from a <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">solar PV</a> system on Adelaide's central <a href="/wiki/Bus_station" title="Bus station">bus station</a>. Rides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-fare" title="Zero-fare">zero-fare</a> as part of Adelaide's public transport system.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Fast-Charge.2C_Battery-Electric_Transit_Bus">First Fast-Charge, Battery-Electric Transit Bus</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Proterra,_Inc." title="Proterra, Inc.">Proterra</a>'s EcoRide BE35 transit bus, called the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ecoliner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ecoliner (page does not exist)">Ecoliner</a> by Foothill Transit in West Covina, California, is a heavy duty, fast charge, battery-electric bus. Proterra's ProDrive drive-system uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UQM" title="UQM">UQM</a> motor and regenerative braking that captures 90 percent of the available energy and returns it to the TerraVolt energy storage system, which in turn increases the total distance the bus can drive by 31–35 percent. It can travel 30–40 miles on a single charge, is up to 600 percent more fuel-efficient than a typical diesel or CNG bus, and produces 44 percent less carbon than CNG.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_cars">Electric cars</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">Electric car</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_production_battery_electric_vehicles" title="List of production battery electric vehicles">List of production battery electric vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_car_use_by_country" title="Electric car use by country">electric car use by country</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a> is a plug-in battery powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> which is <a href="/wiki/Ground_propulsion" title="Ground propulsion">propelled</a> by <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a>(s). Although electric cars often give good acceleration and have generally acceptable top speed, the lower <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> of production batteries available in 2015 compared with <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_fuel" title="Carbon-based fuel">carbon-based fuels</a> means that electric cars need batteries that are fairly large fraction of the vehicle mass but still often give relatively low range between charges. Recharging can also take significant lengths of time. For journeys within a single battery charge, rather than long journeys, electric cars are practical forms of transportation and can be recharged overnight.</p>

<p>Electric cars have the potential of significantly reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_pollution" title="City pollution">city pollution</a> by having <a href="/wiki/Zero-emissions_vehicle" title="Zero-emissions vehicle">zero tail pipe emissions</a>. Vehicle <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> savings depend on how the electricity is generated. With the current <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">US energy mix</a>, using an electric car would result in a 30 percent reduction in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions. Given the current energy mixes in other countries, it has been predicted that such emissions would decrease by 40 percent in the UK, 19 percent in China, and as little as 1 percent in Germany.</p>
<p>Electric cars are expected to have a major impact in the auto industry given advantages in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_emissions" title="Motor vehicle emissions">city pollution</a>, less dependence on oil, and expected rise in gasoline prices. World governments are pledging billions to fund development of electric vehicles and their components. The US has pledged <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$2.4 billion</span> in federal grants for electric cars and batteries. China has announced it will provide <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$15 billion</span> to initiate an electric car industry.</p>
<p>Since 2008, about 620,000 highway-capable all-electric passenger cars and light utility vehicles have been sold worldwide by mid-September 2015, out of total global sales of about 1 million light-duty plug-in electric vehicles. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renault-Nissan_Alliance" title="Renault-Nissan Alliance">Renault-Nissan Alliance</a> is the leading all-electric vehicle manufacturer. The Alliance achieved the sales milestone of 350,000 all-electric vehicles delivered globally in August 2016. Ranking second is <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Motors" title="Tesla Motors">Tesla Motors</a> with over 139,000 electric cars sold between 2008 and June 2016.</p>

<p>As of June 2016, the world's all-time top selling highway legal electric car is the Nissan Leaf, released in December 2010, with global sales of over 228,000 units, followed by the Tesla Model S (about 129,400), the Renault Zoe (51,193), the BMW i3 (around 49,500), and the Mitsubishi i-MiEV family (about 37,600). The Renault Kangoo Z.E. utility van is the leader of the light-duty all-electric segment with global sales of 23,219 units through June 2016.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Formula_E" title="Formula E">Formula E</a> is a fully electric international single seater championship. The series was conceived in 2012, and the inaugural championship started in Beijing on 13 September 2014. The series is sanctioned by the FIA. Alejandro Agag is the current CEO of Formula E.</p>
<p>The Formula E championship is currently contested by ten teams with two drivers each (after the withdrawal of Team Trulli, there are temporarily only nine teams competing). Racing generally takes place on temporary city-center street circuits which are approximately 2 to 3.4 km (1.2 to 2.1 mi) long. Currently, only the Mexico City ePrix takes place on a road course, a modified version of the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special-purpose_vehicles">Special-purpose vehicles</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Electrathon" title="Electrathon">Electrathon</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special-purpose_vehicle" title="Special-purpose vehicle">Special-purpose vehicles</a> come in a wide range of types, ranging from relatively common ones such as <a href="/wiki/Golf_cart" title="Golf cart">golf carts</a>, things like <a href="/wiki/Electric_golf_trolley" title="Electric golf trolley">electric golf trolleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk_float" title="Milk float">milk floats</a>, <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">all-terrain vehicles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a>, and a wide range of other devices. Certain manufacturers specialize in electric-powered "in plant" work machines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-_and_three-wheeled_vehicles">Two- and three-wheeled vehicles</span></h3>
<p>An increasing variety of two-wheeled electric vehicles is being made for transportation and entertainment. These include devices for sitting on such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_motorcycles_and_scooters" title="Electric motorcycles and scooters">electric motorcycles and scooters</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_bicycles" title="Electric bicycles">electric bicycles</a>, as well as devices for standing on such as the <a href="/wiki/Segway_PT" title="Segway PT">Segway PT</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-balancing_two-wheeled_board" title="Self-balancing two-wheeled board">self-balancing two-wheeled boards</a>.</p>
<p>Three-wheeled vehicles include <a href="/wiki/Electric_rickshaw" title="Electric rickshaw">electric rickshaws</a>, a powered variant of the <a href="/wiki/Cycle_rickshaw" title="Cycle rickshaw">cycle rickshaw</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span></h2>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motors">Motors</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Traction_motor" title="Traction motor">Traction motor</a></div>
<p>Electric cars have traditionally used series wound DC motors, a form of <a href="/wiki/Brushed_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushed DC electric motor">brushed DC electric motor</a>. Separately excited and permanent magnet are just two of the types of DC motors available. More recent electric vehicles have made use of a variety of <a href="/wiki/AC_motor" title="AC motor">AC motor</a> types, as these are simpler to build and have no brushes that can wear out. These are usually <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">induction motors</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brushless_AC_electric_motor" title="Brushless AC electric motor">brushless AC electric motors</a> which use permanent magnets. There are several variations of the permanent magnet motor which offer simpler drive schemes and/or lower cost including the <a href="/wiki/Brushless_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushless DC electric motor">brushless DC electric motor</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_controllers">Motor controllers</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">Motor controller</a></div>
<p>The motor controller regulates the power to the motor, supplying either variable pulse width DC or variable frequency variable amplitude AC, depending on the motor type, DC or AC.</p>
