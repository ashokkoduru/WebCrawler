<p><b>Framing</b> may refer to:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frameup" title="Frameup">Framing (crime)</a>, providing false evidence or false testimony in order to prove someone guilty of a crime falsely</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(construction)" title="Framing (construction)">Framing (construction)</a>, the most common carpentry work</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Timber_framing" title="Timber framing">Timber framing</a>, the standard type of building framing until the mid-19th century</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">Framing (social sciences)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Framing_effect_(psychology)" title="Framing effect (psychology)">Framing effect (psychology)</a>, terminology used in communication theory, sociology, and other disciplines where it relates to the construction and presentation of a fact or issue "framed" from a particular <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Perspective_(cognition)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Perspective (cognition) (page does not exist)">perspective</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(visual_arts)" title="Framing (visual arts)">Framing (visual arts)</a>, a technique used to bring the focus to the subject</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(World_Wide_Web)" title="Framing (World Wide Web)">Framing (World Wide Web)</a>, where it relates to the use of multiple panes within a web page</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Framing_device" title="Framing device">Framing device</a>, a narrative tool in which the setting of a story or another element is repeated at the beginning and end of a story</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frame_synchronization" title="Frame synchronization">Frame synchronization</a> in telecommunications</li>
</ul>
