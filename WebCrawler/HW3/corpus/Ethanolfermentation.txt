<p><b>Ethanol fermentation</b>, also called <b>alcoholic fermentation</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Biological_process" title="Biological process">biological process</a> which converts <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> into <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">cellular energy</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a side-effect. Because <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeasts</a> perform this conversion in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, alcoholic <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> is considered an <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism#Metabolism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> process.</p>
<p>Ethanol fermentation has many uses, including the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>, the production of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> baking.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_oxygen">Effect of oxygen</span></h2>
<p>Fermentation does not require oxygen. If oxygen is present, some species of yeast (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Kluyveromyces_lactis" title="Kluyveromyces lactis">Kluyveromyces lactis</a></i> or <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kluyveromyces_lipolytica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kluyveromyces lipolytica (page does not exist)">Kluyveromyces lipolytica</a></i>) will oxidize <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvate</a> completely to carbon dioxide and water. This process is called <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>. But these species of yeast will produce ethanol only in an anaerobic environment (not cellular respiration).</p>
<p>However, many yeasts such as the commonly used baker's yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, or fission yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces_pombe" title="Schizosaccharomyces pombe">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</a></i>, prefer fermentation to respiration. These yeasts will produce ethanol even under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic</a> conditions, if they are provided with the right kind of nutrition. During batch fermentation, the rate of ethanol production per milligram of cell protein is maximal for a brief period early in this process and declines progressively as ethanol accumulates in the surrounding broth. Studies demonstrate that the removal of this accumulated ethanol does not immediately restore fermentative activity, and they provide evidence that the decline in metabolic rate is due to physiological changes (including possible ethanol damage) rather than to the presence of ethanol. Several potential causes for the decline in fermentative activity have been investigated. Viability remained at or above 90%, internal pH remained near neutrality, and the specific activities of the glycolytic and alcohologenic enzymes (measured in vitro) remained high throughout batch fermentation. None of these factors appears to be causally related to the fall in fermentative activity during batch fermentation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bread_baking">Bread baking</span></h2>

<p>Ethanol fermentation causes bread dough to rise. Yeast organisms consume sugars in the dough and produce ethanol and carbon dioxide as waste products. The carbon dioxide forms bubbles in the dough, expanding it into something of a foam. Less than 2% ethanol remains after baking.  </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcoholic_beverages">Alcoholic beverages</span></h2>

<p>All ethanol contained in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> (including ethanol produced by <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_maceration" title="Carbonic maceration">carbonic maceration</a>) is produced by means of fermentation induced by yeast.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">Wine</a> is produced by fermentation of the natural sugars present in grapes; <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">cider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perry" title="Perry">perry</a> are produced by similar fermentation of natural sugar in <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pears</a>, respectively; and other <a href="/wiki/Fruit_wine" title="Fruit wine">fruit wines</a> are produced from the fermentation of the sugars in any other kinds of fruit. <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">Brandy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eau_de_vie" title="Eau de vie">eaux de vie</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Slivovitz" title="Slivovitz">slivovitz</a>) are produced by <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of these fruit-fermented beverages.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">Mead</a> is produced by fermentation of the natural sugars present in <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">Beer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiskey" title="Whiskey">whiskey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">vodka</a> are produced by fermentation of grain starches that have been converted to sugar by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, which is present in grain kernels that have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting">malted</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinated</a>). Other sources of starch (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> and unmalted grain) may be added to the mixture, as the amylase will act on those starches as well. Whiskey and vodka are also distilled; <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a> and related beverages are produced by the addition of flavoring agents to a vodka-like feedstock during distillation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rice_wine" title="Rice wine">Rice wines</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a>) are produced by the fermentation of grain starches converted to sugar by the mold <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_oryzae" title="Aspergillus oryzae">Aspergillus oryzae</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Baijiu" title="Baijiu">Baijiu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dch%C5%AB" title="Shōchū">shōchū</a></i> are distilled from the product of such fermentation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">Rum</a> and some other beverages are produced by fermentation and distillation of <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>. Rum is usually produced from the sugarcane product <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In all cases, fermentation must take place in a vessel that allows carbon dioxide to escape but prevents outside air from coming in. This is because exposure to oxygen would prevent the formation of ethanol, while a buildup of carbon dioxide creates a risk the vessel will rupture or fail catastrophically, causing injury and property damage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedstocks_for_fuel_production">Feedstocks for fuel production</span></h2>
<p>Yeast fermentation of various carbohydrate products is also used to produce the ethanol that is added to <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.</p>
<p>The dominant ethanol feedstock in warmer regions is <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>. In temperate regions, <a href="/wiki/Corn_ethanol" title="Corn ethanol">corn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a> are used.</p>
<p>In the United States, the main feedstock for the production of ethanol is currently corn. Approximately 2.8 gallons of ethanol are produced from one bushel of corn (0.42 liter per kilogram). While much of the corn turns into ethanol, some of the corn also yields by-products such as <a href="/wiki/Distillers_grains" title="Distillers grains">DDGS</a> (distillers dried grains with solubles) that can be used as feed for livestock. A bushel of corn produces about 18 pounds of DDGS (320 kilograms of DDGS per metric ton of maize). Although most of the fermentation plants have been built in corn-producing regions, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> is also an important feedstock for ethanol production in the Plains states. <a href="/wiki/Pearl_millet" title="Pearl millet">Pearl millet</a> is showing promise as an ethanol feedstock for the southeastern U.S. and the potential of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duckweed" title="Duckweed">duckweed</a> is being studied.</p>
<p>In some parts of Europe, particularly France and Italy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapes" title="Grapes">grapes</a> have become a <i>de facto</i> feedstock for fuel ethanol by the <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of surplus <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>. In Japan, it has been proposed to use rice normally made into <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a> as an ethanol source.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cassava_as_ethanol_feedstock">Cassava as ethanol feedstock</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol can be made from <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a> or from sugars or starches. Starches are cheapest. The starchy crop with highest energy content per acre is <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>, which grows in tropical countries.</p>
<p>Thailand already had a large cassava industry in the 1990s, for use as cattle feed and as a cheap admixture to wheat flour. Nigeria and Ghana are already establishing cassava-to-ethanol plants. Production of ethanol from cassava is currently economically feasible when crude oil prices are above US$120 per barrel.</p>
<p>New varieties of cassava are being developed, so the future situation remains uncertain. Currently, cassava can yield between 25-40 tonnes per hectare (with irrigation and fertilizer), and from a tonne of cassava roots, circa 200 liters of ethanol can be produced (assuming cassava with 22% starch content). A liter of ethanol contains circa 21.46 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">MJ</a> of energy. The overall energy efficiency of cassava-root to ethanol conversion is circa 32%.</p>
<p>The yeast used for processing cassava is <i>Endomycopsis fibuligera</i>, sometimes used together with bacterium <i>Zymomonas mobilis</i>.</p>

<p>Ethanol fermentation produces unharvested byproducts such as heat, carbon dioxide, food for livestock, and water.</p>
