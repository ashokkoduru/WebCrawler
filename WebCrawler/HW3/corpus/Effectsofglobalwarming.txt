<p>The <b>effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></b> are the environmental and social changes caused (directly or indirectly) by human emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. There is <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">a scientific consensus that climate change is occurring</a>, and that human activities are the primary driver. Many impacts of climate change have already been observed, including <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glacier retreat</a>, changes in the timing of seasonal events (e.g., earlier flowering of plants), and changes in agricultural productivity.</p>
<p>Future effects of climate change will vary depending on <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">climate change policies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_development" title="Social development">social development</a>. The two main policies to address climate change are reducing human greenhouse gas emissions (<a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">adapting to the impacts</a> of climate change. <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Geoengineering</a> is another policy option.</p>
<p>Near-term climate change policies could significantly affect long-term climate change impacts. Stringent mitigation policies might be able to limit global warming (in 2100) to around 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> or below, relative to pre-industrial levels. Without mitigation, increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_demand" title="Energy demand">energy demand</a> and extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> might lead to global warming of around 4 °C. Higher magnitudes of global warming would be more difficult to adapt to, and would increase the risk of negative impacts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">attribution of recent climate change</a></div>
<p>In this article, "<a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>" means a change in climate that persists over a sustained period of time. The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> defines this time period as 30 years. Examples of climate change include increases in global surface temperature (global warming), changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> patterns, and changes in the frequency of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events. Changes in climate may be due to natural causes, e.g., changes in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>'s output, or due to human activities, e.g., changing the composition of the atmosphere. Any human-induced changes in climate will occur against a background of natural climatic variations and of variations in human activity such as population growth on shores or in arid areas which increase or decrease climate vulnerability.</p>
<p>Also, the term "anthropogenic forcing" refers to the influence exerted on a habitat or chemical environment by humans, as opposed to a natural process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_changes">Temperature changes</span></h2>

<p>This article breaks down some of the impacts of climate change according to different levels of future global warming. This way of describing impacts has, for instance, been used in the IPCC (<a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>) Assessment Reports on climate change. The instrumental <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperature record</a> shows global warming of around 0.6 °C during the 20th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SRES_emissions_scenarios">SRES emissions scenarios</span></h3>
<p>The future level of global warming is uncertain, but a wide range of estimates (projections) have been made. The IPCC's "<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a>" scenarios have been frequently used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model#Projections_of_future_climate_change" title="Global climate model">projections of future climate change</a>. The SRES scenarios are "<a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#Baselines" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">baseline</a>" (or "reference") scenarios, which means that they do not take into account any current or future measures to limit GHG emissions (e.g., the UNFCCC's <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">Cancún agreements</a>). Emissions projections of the SRES scenarios are broadly comparable in range to the baseline emissions scenarios that have been developed by the scientific community.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a>, changes in future global mean temperature were projected using the six SRES "marker" emissions scenarios. Emissions projections for the six SRES "marker" scenarios are representative of the full set of forty SRES scenarios. For the lowest emissions SRES marker scenario ("B1" - see the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a> article for details on this scenario), the best estimate for global mean temperature is an increase of 1.8 °C (3.2 °F) by the end of the 21st century. This projection is relative to global temperatures at the end of the 20th century. The "likely" range (greater than 66% probability, based on expert judgement) for the SRES B1 marker scenario is 1.1–2.9 °C (2.0–5.2 °F). For the highest emissions SRES marker scenario (A1FI), the best estimate for global mean temperature increase is 4.0 °C (7.2 °F), with a "likely" range of 2.4–6.4 °C (4.3–11.5 °F).</p>
<p>The range in temperature projections partly reflects (1) the choice of <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Emissions_scenarios" title="Economics of global warming">emissions scenario</a>, and (2) the "<a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>". For (1), different scenarios make different assumptions of future social and economic development (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_projection" title="Population projection">population level</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy" title="Energy policy">energy policies</a>), which in turn affects projections of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The projected magnitude of warming by 2100 is closely related to the level of cumulative emissions over the 21st century (i.e. total emissions between 2000-2100). The higher the cumulative emissions over this time period, the greater the level of warming is projected to occur.</p>
<p>(2) reflects uncertainty in the response of the climate system to past and future GHG emissions, which is measured by the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>). Higher estimates of climate sensitivity lead to greater projected warming, while lower estimates of climate sensitivity lead to less projected warming.</p>
<p>Over the next several millennia, projections suggest that global warming could be irreversible. Even if emissions were drastically reduced, global temperatures would remain close to their highest level for at least 1,000 years (see the later section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Irreversibilities" title="Effects of global warming">irreversibilities</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_warming_in_context">Projected warming in context</span></h3>

<p>Scientists have used various "proxy" data to assess past changes in Earth's climate (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a>). Sources of proxy data include historical records (such as <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmers</a>' logs), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_pollen" title="Fossil pollen">fossil pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, and ocean and lake <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>. Analysis of these data suggest that recent warming is unusual in the past 400 years, possibly longer. By the end of the 21st century, temperatures may increase to a level not experienced since the mid-<a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>, around 3 million years ago. At that time, models suggest that mean global temperatures were about 2–3 °C warmer than pre-industrial temperatures. Even a 2 °C rise above the pre-industrial level would be outside the range of temperatures experienced by human <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_impacts">Physical impacts</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Physical impacts of climate change">Physical impacts of climate change</a></div>


<p>A broad range of evidence shows that the climate system has warmed. Evidence of global warming is shown in the graphs opposite. Some of the graphs show a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trend_estimation" title="Trend estimation">trend</a>, e.g., increasing temperature over land and the ocean, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea level rise</a>. Other graphs show a negative trend, e.g., decreased <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, and declining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent. Evidence of warming is also apparent in living <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Observed_impacts_on_biological_systems" title="Effects of global warming">(biological) systems</a>.</p>
<p>Human activities have contributed to a number of the observed changes in climate. This contribution has principally been through the burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, which has led to an increase in the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere. Another human influence on the climate are <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> emissions, which are a precursor to the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate_aerosols" title="Sulfate aerosols">sulfate aerosols</a> in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Human-induced warming could lead to <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Abrupt_or_irreversible_changes" title="Effects of global warming">large-scale, irreversible, and/or abrupt changes</a> in physical systems. An example of this is the melting of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, which contributes to sea level rise. The probability of warming having unforeseen consequences increases with the rate, magnitude, and duration of climate change.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_weather">Effects on weather</span></h3>
<p>Observations show that there have been changes in weather. As climate changes, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilities" title="Probabilities">probabilities</a> of certain types of weather events are affected.</p>

<p>Changes have been observed in the amount, intensity, frequency, and type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. Widespread increases in heavy precipitation have occurred, even in places where total rain amounts have decreased. With medium confidence (see <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Footnotes" title="Effects of global warming">footnote 1</a>), IPCC (2012) concluded that human influences had contributed to an increase in heavy precipitation events at the global scale.</p>
<p>Projections of future changes in precipitation show overall increases in the global average, but with substantial shifts in where and how precipitation falls. Projections suggest a reduction in rainfall in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>, and an increase in precipitation in subpolar latitudes and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_region" title="Equatorial region">equatorial regions</a>. In other words, regions which are dry at present will in general become even drier, while regions that are currently wet will in general become even wetter. This projection does not apply to every locale, and in some cases can be modified by local conditions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather">Extreme weather</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Global_warming" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone § Global warming</a></div>
<p>Over most land areas since the 1950s, it is very likely that there have been fewer or warmer cold days and nights. Hot days and nights have also very likely become warmer or more frequent. Human activities have very likely contributed to these trends. There may have been changes in other climate extremes (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>) but these changes are more difficult to identify.</p>
<p>Projections suggest changes in the frequency and intensity of some extreme weather events. Confidence in projections varies over time.</p>

<p>Some changes (e.g., more frequent hot days) will probably be evident in the near term, while other near-term changes (e.g., more intense droughts and tropical cyclones) are more uncertain.</p>

<p>Future climate change will be associated with more very hot days and fewer very cold days. The frequency, length and intensity of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> will very likely increase over most land areas. Higher growth in anthropogenic GHG emissions will be associated with larger increases in the frequency and severity of temperature extremes.</p>
<p>Assuming high growth in GHG emissions (IPCC scenario <a href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">RCP8.5</a>), presently dry regions may be affected by an increase in the risk of drought and reductions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" title="Soil moisture">soil moisture</a>. Over most of the mid-latitude land masses and wet tropical regions, extreme precipitation events will very likely become more intense and frequent.</p>

<p>At the global scale, the frequency of tropical cyclones will probably decrease or be unchanged. Global mean tropical cyclone maximum wind speed and precipitation rates will likely increase. Changes in tropical cyclones will probably vary by region, but these variations are uncertain.</p>

<p>The impacts of extreme events on the environment and human society will vary. Some impacts will be beneficial—e.g., fewer cold extremes will probably lead to fewer cold deaths. Overall, however, impacts will probably be mostly negative.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryosphere">Cryosphere</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a></div>


<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> is made up of areas of the Earth which are covered by snow or ice. Observed changes in the cryosphere include declines in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent, the widespread retreat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_glaciers" title="Alpine glaciers">alpine glaciers</a>, and reduced snow cover in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.</p>
<p>Solomon <i>et al.</i> (2007) assessed the potential impacts of climate change on <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summertime</a> Arctic sea ice extent. Assuming high growth in greenhouse gas emissions (<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES A2</a>), some models projected that Arctic sea ice in the summer could largely disappear by the end of the 21st century. More recent projections suggest that the Arctic summers could be ice-free (defined as ice extent less than 1 million square km) as early as 2025-2030.</p>
<p>During the 21st century, glaciers and snow cover are projected to continue their widespread retreat. In the western mountains of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, increasing temperatures and changes in precipitation are projected to lead to reduced <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpack</a>. Snowpack is the seasonal accumulation of slow-melting snow. The melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a> could contribute to sea level rise, especially over long time-scales (see the section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets" title="Effects of global warming">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</a>).</p>
<p>Changes in the cryosphere are projected to have social impacts. For example, in some regions, glacier retreat could increase the risk of reductions in seasonal water availability. Barnett <i>et al.</i> (2005) estimated that more than one-sixth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_population" title="World's population">world's population</a> rely on glaciers and snowpack for their water supply.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_oceans" title="Effects of global warming on oceans">Effects of global warming on oceans</a></div>
<p>The role of the oceans in global warming is complex. The oceans serve as a sink for carbon dioxide, taking up much that would otherwise remain in the atmosphere, but increased levels of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> have led to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>. Furthermore, as the temperature of the oceans increases, they become less able to absorb excess <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>. The ocean have also acted as a sink in absorbing extra heat from the atmosphere. The increase in ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_content" title="Heat content">heat content</a> is much larger than any other store of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in the Earth’s heat balance over the two periods 1961 to 2003 and 1993 to 2003, and accounts for more than 90% of the possible increase in heat content of the Earth system during these periods.</p>
<p>Global warming is projected to have a number of effects on the oceans. Ongoing effects include rising sea levels due to thermal expansion and melting of glaciers and ice sheets, and warming of the ocean surface, leading to increased temperature stratification. Other possible effects include large-scale changes in ocean circulation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidification">Acidification</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>

<p>About one-third of the carbon dioxide emitted by human activity has already been taken up by the oceans. As carbon dioxide dissolves in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_water" title="Sea water">sea water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> is formed, which has the effect of acidifying the ocean, measured as a change in <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. The uptake of human carbon emissions since the year 1750 has led to an average decrease in pH of 0.1 units. Projections using the SRES emissions scenarios suggest a further reduction in average global surface ocean pH of between 0.14 and 0.35 units over the 21st century.</p>
<p>The effects of ocean acidification on the marine <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> have yet to be documented. Laboratory experiments suggest beneficial effects for a few species, with potentially highly detrimental effects for a substantial number of species. With medium confidence, Fischlin <i>et al.</i> (2007) projected that future ocean acidification and climate change would impair a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> and shallow <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a> marine organisms that use <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> to make their shells or skeletons, such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and marine <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pteropoda" title="Pteropoda">pteropods</a>), with significant impacts particularly in the Southern Ocean.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_depletion">Oxygen depletion</span></h4>
<p>The amount of oxygen dissolved in the oceans may decline, with adverse consequences for ocean life.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_rise">Sea level rise</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">Current sea level rise</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Future_sea_level" title="Future sea level">Future sea level</a></div>

<p>There is strong evidence that global sea level rose gradually over the 20th century. With high confidence, Bindoff <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that between the mid-19th and mid-20th centuries, the rate of sea level rise increased. Authors of the IPCC Fourth Assessment Synthesis Report (IPCC AR4 SYR, 2007) reported that between the years 1961 and 2003, global average sea level rose at an average rate of 1.8 mm per year (mm/yr), with a range of 1.3–2.3 mm/yr. Between 1993 and 2003, the rate increased above the previous period to 3.1 mm/yr (range of 2.4–3.8 mm/yr). Authors of IPCC AR4 SYR (2007) were uncertain whether the increase in rate from 1993 to 2003 was due to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Internal_forcing_mechanisms" title="Climate change">natural variations</a> in sea level over the time period, or whether it reflected an increase in the underlying long-term trend.</p>
<p>There are two main factors that have contributed to observed sea level rise. The first is <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a>: as ocean water warms, it expands. The second is from the contribution of land-based ice due to increased melting. The major store of water on land is found in glaciers and ice sheets. Anthropogenic forces very likely (greater than 90% probability, based on expert judgement) contributed to sea level rise during the latter half of the 20th century.</p>
<p>There is a widespread consensus that substantial long-term sea level rise will continue for centuries to come. In their Fourth Assessment Report, the IPCC projected sea level rise to the end of the 21st century using the SRES emissions scenarios. Across the six SRES marker scenarios, sea level was projected to rise by 18 to 59 cm (7.1 to 23.2 in), relative to sea level at the end of the 20th century. Thermal expansion is the largest component in these projections, contributing 70-75% of the central estimate for all scenarios. Due to a lack of scientific understanding, this sea level rise estimate does not include all of the possible contributions of ice sheets (see the section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets" title="Effects of global warming">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</a>).</p>
<p>An assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a> on climate change was published in 2010 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> (US NRC, 2010). NRC (2010) described the projections in AR4 (i.e. those cited in the above paragraph) as "conservative", and summarized the results of more recent studies. Cited studies suggested a great deal of uncertainty in projections. A range of projections suggested possible sea level rise by the end of the 21st century of between 0.56 and 2 m, relative to sea levels at the end of the 20th century.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_temperature_rise">Ocean temperature rise</span></h4>

<p>From 1961 to 2003, the global ocean temperature has risen by 0.10 °C from the surface to a depth of 700 m. There is variability both year-to-year and over longer time scales, with global ocean heat content observations showing high rates of warming for 1991–2003, but some cooling from 2003 to 2007. The temperature of the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> rose by 0.17 °C (0.31 °F) between the 1950s and the 1980s, nearly twice the rate for the world's oceans as a whole. As well as having effects on ecosystems (e.g. by melting sea ice, affecting algae that grow on its underside), warming reduces the ocean's ability to absorb <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>. It is likely (greater than 66% probability, based on expert judgement) that anthropogenic forcing contributed to the general warming observed in the upper several hundred metres of the ocean during the latter half of the 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">Regional effects of global warming</a></div>

<p>Regional effects of global warming vary in nature. Some are the result of a generalised global change, such as rising temperature, resulting in local effects, such as melting ice. In other cases, a change may be related to a change in a particular ocean current or weather system. In such cases, the regional effect may be disproportionate and will not necessarily follow the global trend.</p>
<p>There are three major ways in which global warming will make changes to regional climate: melting or forming ice, changing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>) and changing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents in the oceans</a> and air flows in the atmosphere. The coast can also be considered a region, and will suffer severe impacts from <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts">Observed impacts</span></h3>
<p>With very high confidence, Rosenzweig <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that physical and biological systems on all continents and in most oceans had been affected by recent climate changes, particularly regional temperature increases. Impacts include earlier leafing of <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> over many regions; movements of species to higher latitudes and altitudes in the Northern Hemisphere; changes in <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">bird migrations</a> in Europe, North America and Australia; and shifting of the oceans' <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> from cold- to warm-adapted communities.</p>
<p>The human influence on the climate can be seen in the geographical pattern of observed warming, with greater temperature increases over land and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a> rather than over the oceans. Using models, it is possible to identify the human "signal" of global warming over both land and ocean areas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_impacts">Projected impacts</span></h3>
<p>Projections of future climate changes at the regional scale do not hold as high a level of scientific confidence as projections made at the global scale. It is, however, expected that future warming will follow a similar geographical pattern to that seen already, with greatest warming over land and high northern <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, and least over the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>. Nearly all land areas will very likely warm more than the global average.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, small islands and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">megadeltas</a> are regions that are likely to be especially affected by climate change. <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Low-latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">less-developed</a> areas are at most risk of experiencing negative impacts due to climate change. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">Developed countries</a> are also vulnerable to climate change. For example, developed countries will be negatively affected by increases in the severity and frequency of some <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>. In all regions, some people can be particularly at risk from climate change, such as the <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poor</a>, young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children" title="Children">children</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elderly" title="Elderly">elderly</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_systems">Social systems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans" title="Effects of climate change on humans">Effects of climate change on humans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change,_industry_and_society" title="Climate change, industry and society">Climate change, industry and society</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_gender" title="Climate change and gender">Climate change and gender</a></div>
<p>The impacts of climate change can be thought of in terms of sensitivity and vulnerability. "Sensitivity" is the degree to which a particular system or sector might be affected, positively or negatively, by climate change and/or <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Causes" title="Climate change">climate variability</a>. "Vulnerability" is the degree to which a particular system or sector might be adversely affected by climate change.</p>
<p>The sensitivity of human society to climate change varies. Sectors sensitive to climate change include water resources, coastal zones, human settlements, and human health. Industries sensitive to climate change include <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_and_climate_change" title="Fisheries and climate change">fisheries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_supply">Food supply</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Climate change and agriculture</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">Food security</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" title="Food vs fuel">Food vs fuel</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_world_food_price_crisis" title="2007–2008 world food price crisis">2007–2008 world food price crisis</a></div>

<p>Climate change will impact agriculture and food production around the world due to: the effects of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere, higher temperatures, altered precipitation and <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> regimes, increased frequency of extreme events, and modified <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> pressure. In general, low-latitude areas are at most risk of having decreased crop yields.</p>
<p>As of 2007, the effects of regional climate change on agriculture have been small. Changes in crop <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a> provide important evidence of the response to recent regional climate change. Phenology is the study of natural phenomena that recur periodically, and how these phenomena relate to climate and seasonal changes. A significant advance in phenology has been observed for agriculture and forestry in large parts of the Northern Hemisphere.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections">Projections</span></h4>


<p>With low to medium confidence, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007) projected that for about a 1 to 3 °C increase in global mean temperature (by the years 2090-2100, relative to average temperatures in the years 1990–2000), there would be productivity decreases for some <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a> in low latitudes, and productivity increases in high latitudes. With medium confidence, global production potential was projected to:</p>
<ul>
<li>increase up to around 3 °C,</li>
<li>very likely decrease above about 3 °C.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most of the studies on global agriculture assessed by Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007) had not incorporated a number of critical factors, including changes in extreme events, or the spread of pests and diseases. Studies had also not considered the development of specific practices or technologies to aid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming#Agricultural_production" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to climate change</a>.</p>
<p>The graphs opposite show the projected effects of climate change on selected crop yields. Actual changes in yields may be above or below these central estimates.</p>
<p>The projections above can be expressed relative to pre-industrial (1750) temperatures. 0.6 °C of warming is estimated to have occurred between 1750 and 1990-2000. Add 0.6 °C to the above projections to convert them from a 1990-2000 to pre-industrial baseline.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_security">Food security</span></h5>
<p>Easterling <i>et al.</i> (2007) assessed studies that made quantitative projections of climate change impacts on <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>. It was noted that these projections were highly uncertain and had limitations. However, the assessed studies suggested a number of fairly robust findings. The first was that climate change would likely increase the number of people at risk of <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> compared with reference scenarios with no climate change. Climate change impacts depended strongly on projected future social and economic development. Additionally, the magnitude of climate change impacts was projected to be smaller compared to the impact of social and economic development. In 2006, the global estimate for the number of people <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">undernourished</a> was 820 million. Under the SRES A1, B1, and B2 scenarios (see the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios#Scenario_families" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES article</a> for information on each scenario group), projections for the year 2080 showed a reduction in the number of people undernourished of about 560-700 million people, with a global total of undernourished people of 100-240 million in 2080. By contrast, the SRES A2 scenario showed only a small decrease in the risk of hunger from 2006 levels. The smaller reduction under A2 was attributed to the higher projected future population level in this scenario.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Droughts_and_agriculture">Droughts and agriculture</span></h4>
<p>Some evidence suggests that droughts have been occurring more frequently because of global warming and they are expected to become more frequent and intense in Africa, southern Europe, the Middle East, most of the Americas, Australia, and Southeast Asia. However, other research suggests that there has been little change in drought over the past 60 years. Their impacts are aggravated because of increased water demand, population growth, urban expansion, and environmental protection efforts in many areas. Droughts result in crop failures and the loss of pasture grazing land for livestock.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">Effects of global warming on human health</a></div>
<p>Human beings are exposed to climate change through changing weather patterns (temperature, precipitation, sea-level rise and more frequent extreme events) and indirectly through changes in water, air and food quality and changes in ecosystems, agriculture, industry and settlements and the economy (Confalonieri <i>et al.</i>, 2007:393). According to an assessment of the scientific literature by Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007:393), the effects of climate change to date have been small, but are projected to progressively increase in all countries and regions.</p>
<p>A study by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO, 2009) estimated the effect of climate change on human health. Not all of the effects of climate change were included in their estimates, for example, the effects of more frequent and extreme storms were excluded. Climate change was estimated to have been responsible for 3% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrhoea" title="Diarrhoea">diarrhoea</a>, 3% of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, and 3.8% of <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a> deaths worldwide in 2004. Total attributable mortality was about 0.2% of deaths in 2004; of these, 85% were child deaths.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections_2">Projections</span></h4>
<p>With high confidence, authors of the IPCC AR4 Synthesis report projected that climate change would bring some benefits in temperate areas, such as fewer deaths from cold exposure, and some mixed effects such as changes in range and transmission potential of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Benefits were projected to be outweighed by negative health effects of rising temperatures, especially in developing countries.</p>
<p>With very high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that economic development was an important component of possible adaptation to climate change. Economic growth on its own, however, was not judged to be sufficient to insulate the world's population from disease and injury due to climate change. Future vulnerability to climate change will depend not only on the extent of social and economic change, but also on how the benefits and costs of change are distributed in society. For example, in the 19th century, rapid <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a> lead to a plummeting in population health. Other factors important in determining the health of populations include <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, the availability of health services, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public-health</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_resources">Water resources</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Water_security" title="Water security">Water security</a></div>

<p>A number of climate-related trends have been observed that affect <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>. These include changes in precipitation, the crysosphere and <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface waters</a> (e.g., changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_flows" title="River flows">river flows</a>). Observed and projected impacts of climate change on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> systems and their management are mainly due to changes in temperature, sea level and precipitation variability. Sea level rise will extend areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salinization" title="Salinization">salinization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, resulting in a decrease in freshwater availability for humans and ecosystems in coastal areas. In an assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a>, Kundzewicz <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded, with high confidence, that:</p>
<ul>
<li>the negative impacts of climate change on freshwater systems outweigh the benefits. All of the regions assessed in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> (<a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">Polar regions</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>), and small islands) showed an overall net negative impact of climate change on water resources and freshwater ecosystems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">Semi-arid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> areas are particularly exposed to the impacts of climate change on freshwater. With very high confidence, it was judged that many of these areas, e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, and north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, would suffer a decrease in water resources due to climate change.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_and_conflict">Migration and conflict</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">Environmental migrant</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">General circulation models</a> project that the future climate change will bring wetter coasts, drier mid-continent areas, and further sea level rise. Such changes could result in the gravest effects of climate change through <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">human migration</a>. Millions might be displaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoreline_erosion" title="Shoreline erosion">shoreline erosions</a>, river and coastal <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, or severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>.</p>
<p>Migration related to climate change is likely to be predominantly from <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> to towns and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cities" title="Cities">cities</a>. In the short term climate stress is likely to add incrementally to existing migration patterns rather than generating entirely new flows of people.</p>
<p>It has been argued that environmental degradation, loss of access to resources (e.g., water resources), and resulting human migration could become a source of political and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_conflict" title="Military conflict">military conflict</a>. Factors other than climate change may, however, be more important in affecting conflict. For example, Wilbanks <i>et al.</i> (2007) suggested that major environmentally influenced conflicts in Africa were more to do with the relative abundance of resources, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamonds</a>, than with resource scarcity. Scott <i>et al.</i> (2001) placed only low confidence in predictions of increased conflict due to climate change.</p>
<p>A 2013 study found that significant climatic changes were associated with a higher risk of conflict worldwide, and predicted that "amplified rates of human conflict could represent a large and critical social impact of anthropogenic climate change in both low- and high-income countries." Similarly, a 2014 study found that higher temperatures were associated with a greater likelihood of violent crime, and predicted that global warming would cause millions of such crimes in the United States alone during the 21st century.</p>
<p>Military planners are concerned that global warming is a "threat multiplier". "Whether it is poverty, food and water scarcity, diseases, economic instability, or threat of natural disasters, the broad range of changing climatic conditions may be far reaching. These challenges may threaten stability in much of the world".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_impacts">Aggregate impacts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Economic impacts of climate change">Economic impacts of climate change</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aggregate_data" title="Aggregate data">Aggregating</a> impacts adds up the total impact of climate change across sectors and/or regions. Examples of aggregate measures include <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Distribution_of_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">economic cost</a> (e.g., changes in <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Marginal_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">social cost of carbon</a>), changes in ecosystems (e.g., changes over land area from one type of vegetation to another), human health impacts, and the number of people affected by climate change. Aggregate measures such as economic cost require researchers to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_judgement" title="Value judgement">value judgements</a> over the importance of impacts occurring in different regions and at different times.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts_2">Observed impacts</span></h4>
<p>Global losses reveal rapidly rising costs due to extreme weather-related events since the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomics" title="Socioeconomics">Socio-economic</a> factors have contributed to the observed trend of global losses, e.g., population growth, increased <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>. Part of the growth is also related to regional climatic factors, e.g., changes in precipitation and flooding events. It is difficult to quantify the relative impact of socio-economic factors and climate change on the observed trend. The trend does, however, suggest increasing vulnerability of social systems to climate change.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_impacts_2">Projected impacts</span></h4>
<p>The total economic impacts from climate change are highly uncertain. With medium confidence, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001) concluded that world GDP would change by plus or minus a few percent for a small increase in global mean temperature (up to around 2 °C relative to the 1990 temperature level). Most studies assessed by Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001) projected losses in world GDP for a medium increase in global mean temperature (above 2-3 °C relative to the 1990 temperature level), with increasing losses for greater temperature increases. This assessment is consistent with the findings of more recent studies, as reviewed by Hitz and Smith (2004).</p>
<p>Economic impacts are expected to vary regionally. For a medium increase in global mean temperature (2-3 °C of warming, relative to the average temperature between 1990–2000), market sectors in low-latitude and less-developed areas might experience net costs due to climate change. On the other hand, market sectors in high-latitude and developed regions might experience net benefits for this level of warming. A global mean temperature increase above about 2-3 °C (relative to 1990-2000) would very likely result in market sectors across all regions experiencing either declines in net benefits or rises in net costs.</p>
<p>Aggregate impacts have also been quantified in non-economic terms. For example, climate change over the 21st century is likely to adversely affect hundreds of millions of people through increased coastal flooding, reductions in water supplies, increased malnutrition and increased health impacts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_systems">Biological systems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems" title="Climate change and ecosystems">Climate change and ecosystems</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_global_warming" title="Extinction risk from global warming">Extinction risk from global warming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_climate_change_on_plant_biodiversity" title="Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity">Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_terrestrial_animals" title="Effects of climate change on terrestrial animals">Effects of climate change on terrestrial animals</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_marine_mammals" title="Effects of climate change on marine mammals">Effects of climate change on marine mammals</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts_on_biological_systems">Observed impacts on biological systems</span></h3>

<p>With very high confidence, Rosenzweig <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that recent warming had strongly affected natural biological systems. Hundreds of studies have documented responses of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> to the climate changes that have already occurred. For example, in the Northern Hemisphere, species are almost uniformly moving their ranges northward and up in elevation in search of cooler temperatures. Humans are very likely causing changes in regional temperatures to which plants and animals are responding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_impacts_on_biological_systems">Projected impacts on biological systems</span></h3>
<p>By the year 2100, ecosystems will be exposed to atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> levels substantially higher than in the past 650,000 years, and global temperatures at least among the highest of those experienced in the past 740,000 years. Significant disruptions of ecosystems are projected to increase with future climate change. Examples of disruptions include disturbances such as fire, drought, pest <a href="/wiki/Infestation" title="Infestation">infestation</a>, invasion of species, storms, and <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> events. The stresses caused by climate change, added to other stresses on ecological systems (e.g., land conversion, <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvesting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>), threaten substantial damage to or complete loss of some unique ecosystems, and <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of some critically endangered species.</p>
<p>Climate change has been estimated to be a major driver of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> loss in cool <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a> forests, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">mediterranean-climate</a> systems, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" title="Arctic tundra">Arctic tundra</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>. In other ecosystems, <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land-use</a> change may be a stronger driver of biodiversity loss at least in the near-term. Beyond the year 2050, climate change may be the major driver for biodiversity loss globally.</p>
<p>A literature assessment by Fischlin <i>et al.</i> (2007) included a quantitative estimate of the number of species at increased risk of extinction due to climate change. With medium confidence, it was projected that approximately 20 to 30% of plant and animal species assessed so far (in an unbiased <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(statistics)" title="Sampling (statistics)">sample</a>) would likely be at increasingly high risk of extinction should global mean temperatures exceed a warming of 2 to 3 °C above pre-industrial temperature levels. The uncertainties in this estimate, however, are large: for a rise of about 2 °C the percentage may be as low as 10%, or for about 3 °C, as high as 40%, and depending on <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a> (all living <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> of an area, the <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> considered as a unit) the range is between 1% and 80%. As global average temperature exceeds <a href="/wiki/4_Degrees_and_Beyond_International_Climate_Conference" title="4 Degrees and Beyond International Climate Conference">4 °C</a> above pre-industrial levels, model projections suggested that there could be significant extinctions (40-70% of species that were assessed) around the globe.</p>
<p>Assessing whether future changes in ecosystems will be beneficial or detrimental is largely based on how ecosystems are valued by human society. For increases in global average temperature exceeding 1.5 to 2.5 °C (relative to global temperatures over the years 1980-1999) and in concomitant atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> concentrations, projected changes in ecosystems will have predominantly negative consequences for biodiversity and ecosystems goods and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">services</a>, e.g., water and food supply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abrupt_or_irreversible_changes">Abrupt or irreversible changes</span></h2>
<p>Physical, ecological and social systems may respond in an abrupt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a> or irregular way to climate change. This is as opposed to a smooth or regular response. A quantitative entity behaves "irregularly" when its <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">dynamics</a> are <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">discontinuous</a> (i.e., not smooth), nondifferentiable, unbounded, wildly varying, or otherwise ill-defined. Such behaviour is often termed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_singularity" title="Mathematical singularity">singular</a>". Irregular behaviour in Earth systems may give rise to certain thresholds, which, when crossed, may lead to a large change in the system.</p>
<p>Some singularities could potentially lead to severe impacts at regional or global scales. Examples of "large-scale" singularities are discussed in the articles on <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">runaway climate change</a>. It is possible that human-induced climate change could trigger large-scale singularities, but the probabilities of triggering such events are, for the most part, poorly understood.</p>
<p>With low to medium confidence, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001) concluded that a rapid warming of more than 3 °C above 1990 levels would exceed thresholds that would lead to large-scale discontinuities in the climate system. Since the assessment by Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001), improved scientific understanding provides more guidance for two large-scale singularities: the role of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> feedbacks in future climate change (discussed below in the section on biogeochemical cycles) and the melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeochemical_cycles">Biogeochemical cycles</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a></div>
<p>Climate change may have an effect on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> in an interactive "feedback" process. A feedback exists where an initial process triggers changes in a second process that in turn influences the initial process. A positive feedback intensifies the original process, and a negative feedback reduces it. Models suggest that the interaction of the climate system and the carbon cycle is one where the feedback effect is positive.</p>
<p>Using the A2 SRES emissions scenario, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007) found that this effect led to additional warming by the years 2090-2100 (relative to the 1990–2000) of 0.1–1.5 °C. This estimate was made with high confidence. The climate projections made in the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report summarized earlier of 1.1–6.4 °C account for this feedback effect. On the other hand, with medium confidence, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007) commented that additional releases of GHGs were possible from permafrost, peat lands, wetlands, and large stores of marine hydrates at high latitudes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</span></h3>
<p>With medium confidence, authors of AR4 concluded that with a global average temperature increase of 1–4 °C (relative to temperatures over the years 1990–2000), at least a partial deglaciation of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a>, and possibly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a> would occur. The estimated timescale for partial deglaciation was centuries to millennia, and would contribute 4 to 6 metres (13 to 20 ft) or more to sea level rise over this period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a></div>

<p>The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is an important component of the Earth's climate system, characterized by a northward flow of warm, salty water in the upper layers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic</a> and a southward flow of colder water in the deep Atlantic. The AMOC is equivalently known as the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> (THC). Potential impacts associated with MOC changes include reduced warming or (in the case of abrupt change) absolute cooling of northern high-latitude areas near <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and north-western Europe, an increased warming of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> high-latitudes, tropical drying, as well as changes to <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a>, terrestrial vegetation, oceanic <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> uptake, oceanic oxygen concentrations, and shifts in fisheries. According to an assessment by the US <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="Climate Change Science Program">Climate Change Science Program</a> (CCSP, 2008b), it is very likely (greater than 90% probability, based on expert judgement) that the strength of the AMOC will decrease over the course of the 21st century. Warming is still expected to occur over most of the European region downstream of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> in response to increasing GHGs, as well as over <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Although it is very unlikely (less than 10% probability, based on expert judgement) that the AMOC will collapse in the 21st century, the potential consequences of such a collapse could be severe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversibilities">Irreversibilities</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Commitment_to_radiative_forcing">Commitment to radiative forcing</span></h4>
<p>Emissions of GHGs are a potentially irreversible commitment to sustained <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> in the future. The contribution of a GHG to radiative forcing depends on the gas's ability to trap <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> (heat) radiation, the concentration of the gas in the atmosphere, and the length of time the gas resides in the atmosphere.</p>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> is the most important anthropogenic GHG. While more than half of the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> emitted is currently removed from the atmosphere within a century, some fraction (about 20%) of emitted <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> remains in the atmosphere for many thousands of years. Consequently, <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> emitted today is potentially an irreversible commitment to sustained radiative forcing over thousands of years.</p>
<p>This commitment may not be truly irreversible should techniques be developed to remove <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> or other GHGs directly from the atmosphere, or to block sunlight to induce cooling. Techniques of this sort are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a>. Little is known about the effectiveness, costs or potential <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">side-effects</a> of geoengineering options. Some geoengineering options, such as blocking sunlight, would not prevent further <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversible_impacts">Irreversible impacts</span></h4>
<p>Human-induced climate change may lead to irreversible impacts on physical, biological, and social systems. There are a number of examples of climate change impacts that may be irreversible, at least over the timescale of many human generations. These include the large-scale singularities described above – changes in carbon cycle feedbacks, the melting of the Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets, and changes to the AMOC. In biological systems, the extinction of species would be an irreversible impact. In social systems, unique <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a> may be lost due to climate change. For example, humans living on <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atoll</a> islands face risks due to sea-level rise, sea-surface warming, and increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_global_warming">Benefits of global warming<span id="Benefits_of_Global_Warming"></span></span></h2>
<p>With a large range of effects, it is unlikely that all effects will be negative. Not only have some positive effects been identified, but there is some published material indicating that a small amount of warming would be good. The IPCC cautions that "Estimates agree on the size of the impact (small relative to economic growth), and 17 of the 20 impact estimates shown in Figure 10-1 are negative. Losses accelerate with greater warming, and estimates diverge."</p>
<p>The identified benefits are listed below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_fertilisation_effect">CO2 fertilisation effect</span></h3>

<ul>
<li>Improved agriculture in some high latitude regions </li>
<li>Increased growing season in Greenland </li>
<li>Increased productivity of sour orange trees </li>
<li>Increased vegetation activity in high northern latitudes </li>
<li>Increased plankton biomass in the North Pacific Subtropical Gyre </li>
<li>Recent increase in forest growth </li>
<li>Increased Arctic tundra plant reproduction </li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Winter deaths will decline as temperatures warm </li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice-free_Northwest_Passage">Ice-free Northwest Passage</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Ships will travel on a shorter route between the Pacific and Atlantic oceans </li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_population_changes">Animal population changes</span></h3>

<ul>
<li>Increase in chinstrap and gentoo penguins </li>
<li>Bigger marmots </li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_data_and_tools">NASA data and tools</span></h3>
<p>NASA has released public data and tools to predict how temperature and rainfall patterns worldwide may change through to the year 2100 caused by increasing carbon dioxide in Earth’s atmosphere. The dataset shows projected changes worldwide on a regional level simulated by 21 climate models. The data can be viewed on a daily timescale for individual cities and towns and may be used to conduct climate risk assessments to predict the local and global effects of weather dangers, for example droughts, floods, heat waves and declines in agriculture productivity, and help plan responses to global warming effects.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footnotes">Footnotes</span></h2>
<p> The TAR and AR4 that are referred to in this article use specific and quantitative language to describe uncertainty. This language is intended to provide an indication of the level of confidence that IPCC authors have about a particular finding. The qualitative language used to describe uncertainty has a quantitative scale associated with it. The quantitative values for qualitative terms are intended to ensure that confidence levels are interpreted correctly. The is because qualitative statements, e.g., using the word "likely", can be interpreted differently in quantitative terms.</p>
<p>Confidence levels used in the TAR:</p>
<ul>
<li>Very High = 95% or greater</li>
<li>High = 67-95%</li>
<li>Medium = 33-67%</li>
<li>Low = 5-33%</li>
<li>Very Low = 5% or less</li>
</ul>
<p>Confidence statements made in AR4 are listed below:</p>
<ul>
<li>Very high confidence: At least 9 out of 10 chance of being correct</li>
<li>High confidence: About 8 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Medium confidence: About 5 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Low confidence: About 2 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Very low confidence: Less than a 1 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
</ul>
<p>IPCC (2012) uses the following terms: "very low", "low", "medium", "high", and "very high confidence". Unlike the TAR and AR4, the scale is qualitative rather than quantitative.</p>
<p>The quantitative values used by IPCC authors are "subjective" probabilities, also known as "personalist" or "<a href="/wiki/Bayesian_probability" title="Bayesian probability">Bayesian</a>" probabilities, and reflect the expert judgement of IPCC authors. In this formulation, probability is not only a function of an event, but also the state of information that is available to the person making the assessment. In this framework, assigned probabilities may change as more or different information becomes available.</p>
<p>The IPCC also uses another scale to describe the likelihood of a particular event occurring. This is different from the confidence scales described above, and it is possible to assign confidence values to statements of likelihood. For example, the judgement that an event is improbable (e.g., rolling a <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> twice and getting a six both times) may be assigned a high level of scientific confidence. Also, the probability that an event has an even chance of occurring (e.g., a tossed <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coin</a> coming up heads) may also be assigned a high level of confidence.</p>
