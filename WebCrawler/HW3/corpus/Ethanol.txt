<p><b>Ethanol</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span> (<b>EtOH</b>), also commonly called <b>alcohol</b>, <b>ethyl alcohol</b>, and <b>drinking alcohol</b>, is the principal type of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverages" title="Alcoholic beverages">alcoholic beverages</a>, produced by the <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeasts</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxic" title="Neurotoxic">neurotoxic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">psychoactive drug</a>, and one of the oldest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recreational_drugs" title="Recreational drugs">recreational drugs</a>. It can cause <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">alcohol intoxication</a> when consumed in sufficient quantity.</p>
<p>Ethanol is a <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammable</a>, colorless liquid with a slight chemical odor. It is used as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_fuel" title="Alcohol fuel">fuel</a>, and due to its low freezing point, the active fluid in many <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_thermometer" title="Alcohol thermometer">alcohol thermometers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> is a simple one, being an <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_group" title="Ethyl group">ethyl group</a> linked to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group. Its structural formula, <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span>CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>OH</span>, is often abbreviated as <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
5</span>OH</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
6</span>O</span> or <i>EtOH</i>.</p>
<p>The stem word "eth-" used in many related compounds originates with the then-German word for ethanol (<i>äthyl</i>), <a href="#Etymology">coined in the 19th century</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p><i>Ethanol</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Systematic_name" title="Systematic name">systematic name</a> defined by the <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC)</a> for a molecule with two carbon <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> (prefix "eth-"), having a single bond between them (suffix "-ane"), and an attached <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>-OH group (suffix "-ol").</p>
<p>The prefix <i>ethyl</i> was coined in 1834 by the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus Liebig</a>. <i>Ethyl</i> is a contraction of the Ancient Greek αἰθήρ (aithḗr, “upper air”) and the Greek word <i><span lang="grc">ύλη</span></i> (<i>hyle</i>, substance).</p>
<p>The name <i>ethanol</i> was coined as a result of a resolution that was adopted at the International Conference on Chemical Nomenclature that was held in April 1892 in Geneva, Switzerland.</p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>" now refers to a wider class of substances in chemistry nomenclature, but in common parlance it remains the name of ethanol. Ultimately a medieval loan from Arabic <i><a href="/wiki/Kohl_(cosmetics)" title="Kohl (cosmetics)">al-kuḥl</a></i>, use of <i>alcohol</i> in this sense is modern, introduced in the mid 18th century. Before that time, Middle Latin <i>alcohol</i> referred to "powdered ore of antimony; powdered cosmetic", by the later 17th century "any sublimated substance; <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">distilled spirit</a>" use for "the spirit of wine" (shortened from a full expression <i>alcohol of wine</i>) recorded 1753. The systematic use in chemistry dates to 1850.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiseptic">Antiseptic</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol is used in medical wipes and most common antibacterial <a href="/wiki/Hand_sanitizer" title="Hand sanitizer">hand sanitizer</a> gels as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a>. Ethanol kills organisms by <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denaturing</a> their <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and dissolving their <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and is effective against most <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, and many <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>. However, ethanol is ineffective against bacterial <a href="/wiki/Endospore" title="Endospore">spores</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitussive">Antitussive</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol is widely used, clinically and over the counter, as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antitussive" title="Antitussive">antitussive</a> agent.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Antidote">Antidote</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol may be administered as an <a href="/wiki/Antidote" title="Antidote">antidote</a> to <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> poisoning.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicinal_solvent">Medicinal solvent</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol, often in high concentrations, is used to dissolve many water-insoluble medications and related compounds. Proprietary liquid preparations of cough and cold remedies, analgesics, and mouth washes may be dissolved in 1 to 25% concentrations of ethanol and may need to be avoided in individuals with adverse reactions to ethanol such as <a href="/wiki/Alcohol-induced_respiratory_reactions" title="Alcohol-induced respiratory reactions">alcohol-induced respiratory reactions</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreational">Recreational</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is a central nervous system <a href="/wiki/Depressant" title="Depressant">depressant</a> and has significant psychoactive effects in sublethal doses. Based on its abilities to alter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_consciousness" title="Human consciousness">human consciousness</a>, ethanol is considered a <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">psychoactive drug</a>.</p>
<p>The amount of ethanol in the body is typically quantified by <a href="/wiki/Blood_alcohol_content" title="Blood alcohol content">blood alcohol content</a> (BAC), which is here taken as weight of ethanol per unit volume of blood. Small doses of ethanol, in general, produce euphoria and relaxation; people experiencing these symptoms tend to become talkative and less inhibited, and may exhibit poor judgment. At higher dosages (BAC &gt; 1 g/L), ethanol acts as a <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> <a href="/wiki/Depressant" title="Depressant">depressant</a>, producing at progressively higher dosages, impaired sensory and motor function, slowed cognition, stupefaction, unconsciousness, and possible death. Ethanol is commonly consumed as a recreational drug, especially while socializing, due to its psychoactive effects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel">Fuel</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_fuel">Engine fuel</span></h4>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">Ethanol fuel</a></div>
<p>The largest single use of ethanol is as an engine <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_additive" title="Fuel additive">fuel additive</a>. <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in particular relies heavily upon the use of ethanol as an engine fuel, due in part to its role as the globe's leading producer of ethanol. <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">Gasoline</a> sold in Brazil contains at least 25% <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> ethanol. Hydrous ethanol (about 95% ethanol and 5% water) can be used as fuel in more than 90% of new gasoline fueled cars sold in the country. Brazilian ethanol is produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a> and noted for high <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>. The US and many other countries primarily use E10 (10% ethanol, sometimes known as gasohol) and E85 (85% ethanol) ethanol/gasoline mixtures.</p>

<p>Ethanol has been used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuel</a> and is currently in <a href="/wiki/Light_aircraft" title="Light aircraft">lightweight</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark-III_X-racer" title="Mark-III X-racer">rocket-powered racing aircraft</a>.</p>
<p>Australian law limits the use of pure ethanol from sugarcane waste to 10% in automobiles. Older cars (and vintage cars designed to use a slower burning fuel) should have the engine valves upgraded or replaced.</p>
<p>According to an industry <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">advocacy group</a>, ethanol as a fuel reduces harmful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_emissions" title="Motor vehicle emissions">tailpipe emissions</a> of carbon monoxide, particulate matter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxides_of_nitrogen" title="Oxides of nitrogen">oxides of nitrogen</a>, and other ozone-forming pollutants. <a href="/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory" title="Argonne National Laboratory">Argonne National Laboratory</a> analyzed greenhouse gas emissions of many different engine and fuel combinations, and found that <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>/petrodiesel blend (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B20_(biodiesel)" title="B20 (biodiesel)">B20</a>) showed a reduction of 8%, conventional <a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a> ethanol blend a reduction of 17% and <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> 64%, compared with pure gasoline.</p>
<p>Ethanol combustion in an internal combustion engine yields many of the products of incomplete combustion produced by gasoline and significantly larger amounts of <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and related species such as acetaldehyde. This leads to a significantly larger photochemical reactivity and more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_level_ozone" title="Ground level ozone">ground level ozone</a>. These data have been assembled into <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Fuels_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clean Fuels Report (page does not exist)">The Clean Fuels Report</a> comparison of fuel emissions and show that ethanol exhaust generates 2.14 times as much ozone as gasoline exhaust. When this is added into the custom <i>Localised Pollution Index (LPI)</i> of The Clean Fuels Report, the local pollution of ethanol (pollution that contributes to smog) is rated 1.7, where gasoline is 1.0 and higher numbers signify greater pollution. The <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> formalized this issue in 2008 by recognizing control standards for formaldehydes as an emissions control group, much like the conventional <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a> and Reactive Organic Gases (ROGs).</p>
<p>World production of ethanol in 2006 was 51 gigalitres (1.3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10 US gal), with 69% of the world supply coming from Brazil and the United States. More than 20% of Brazilian cars are able to use 100% ethanol as fuel, which includes ethanol-only engines and <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">flex-fuel</a> engines. Flex-fuel engines in Brazil are able to work with all ethanol, all gasoline or any mixture of both. In the US flex-fuel vehicles can run on 0% to 85% ethanol (15% gasoline) since higher ethanol blends are not yet allowed or efficient. Brazil supports this population of ethanol-burning automobiles with large national infrastructure that produces ethanol from domestically grown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">Sugar cane</a> not only has a greater concentration of sucrose than corn (by about 30%), but is also much easier to extract. The <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a> generated by the process is not wasted, but is used in power plants to produce electricity.</p>
<p>In the United States, the ethanol fuel industry is based largely on <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>. According to the Renewable Fuels Association, as of 30 October 2007, 131 grain ethanol bio-refineries in the United States have the capacity to produce 7.0 billion US gallons (26,000,000 m) of ethanol per year. An additional 72 construction projects underway (in the U.S.) can add 6.4 billion US gallons (24,000,000 m) of new capacity in the next 18 months. Over time, it is believed that a material portion of the ≈150-billion-US-gallon (570,000,000 m) per year market for gasoline will begin to be replaced with fuel ethanol.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sweet_sorghum" title="Sweet sorghum">Sweet sorghum</a> is another potential source of ethanol, and is suitable for growing in dryland conditions. The <a href="/wiki/International_Crops_Research_Institute_for_the_Semi-Arid_Tropics" title="International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics">International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICRISAT" title="ICRISAT">ICRISAT</a>) is investigating the possibility of growing sorgham as a source of fuel, food, and animal feed in arid parts of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sweet_sorghum" title="Sweet sorghum">Sweet sorghum</a> has one-third the water requirement of sugarcane over the same time period. It also requires about 22% less water than corn (also known as maize). The world’s first sweet sorghum ethanol distillery began commercial production in 2007 in Andhra Pradesh, India.</p>
<p>Ethanol's high <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">miscibility</a> with water makes it unsuitable for shipping through modern <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a> like liquid hydrocarbons. Mechanics have seen increased cases of damage to small engines (in particular, the <a href="/wiki/Carburetor" title="Carburetor">carburetor</a>) and attribute the damage to the increased water retention by ethanol in fuel.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rocket_Fuel">Rocket Fuel</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol was commonly used as fuel in early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipropellant" title="Bipropellant">bipropellant</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> (liquid propelled) vehicles, in conjunction with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> such as liquid oxygen. The German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, credited with beginning the space age, used ethanol, mixed with 25% of water to reduce the combustion chamber temperature. The V-2's design team helped develop U.S. rockets following World War II, including the ethanol-fueled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redstone_(rocket)" title="Redstone (rocket)">Redstone rocket</a> which launched the first U.S. satellite. Alcohols fell into general disuse as more efficient rocket fuels were developed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_Cells">Fuel Cells</span></h4>
<p>Commercial fuel cells operate on reformed natural gas, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. Ethanol is an attractive alternative due to its wide availability, low cost, high purity and low toxicity. There are a wide range of fuel cell concepts that have been trialled including <a href="/wiki/Direct-ethanol_fuel_cell" title="Direct-ethanol fuel cell">direct-ethanol fuel cells</a>, auto-thermal reforming systems and thermally integrated systems. The majority of work is being conducted at a research level although there are a number of organizations at the beginning of commercialization of ethanol fuel cells.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_heating">Household heating</span></h4>
<p>Ethanol fireplaces can be used for home heating or for decoration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedstock">Feedstock</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="#Reactions">§ Reactions</a></div>
<p>Ethanol is an important industrial ingredient. It has widespread use as a precursor for other organic compounds such as ethyl <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a>, ethyl <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, diethyl ether, acetic acid, and ethyl <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent">Solvent</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> and is a good general purpose <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tincture" title="Tincture">tinctures</a>, markers, and personal care products such as mouthwashes, perfumes and deodorants. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_precipitation" title="Ethanol precipitation">precipitate</a> from aqueous solution in the presence of alcohol, and ethanol precipitation is used for this reason in the purification of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverse_effects">Adverse effects</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_and_health" title="Alcohol and health">Alcohol and health</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short-term_effects_of_alcohol" title="Short-term effects of alcohol">Short-term effects of alcohol</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_balance">Loss of balance</span></h3>
<p>When alcohol reaches the brain, it has the ability to delay signals that are sent between nerve cells that control balance, thinking and movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastrointestinal_diseases">Gastrointestinal diseases<span id="Gastrointestinal_disease"></span></span></h3>

<p>Alcohol stimulates gastric juice production, even when food is not present, and as a result, its consumption will stimulate acidic secretions normally intended to digest protein molecules. Consequently, the excess acidity may harm the inner lining of the stomach. The stomach lining is normally protected by a mucosal layer which prevents the stomach from essentially digesting itself. However, in patients who have a <a href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcer</a> disease (PUD), this mucosal layer is broken down. PUD is commonly associated with the bacteria <i>H. pylori</i>. <i>H. pylori</i> secrete a toxin that weakens the mucosal wall, which as a result lead to acid and protein enzymes penetrating the weakened barrier. Because alcohol stimulates a person's stomach to secrete acid, a person with PUD should avoid drinking alcohol on an empty stomach. Drinking alcohol would cause more acid release which would further damage the already-weakened stomach wall. Complications of this disease could include a burning pain in the abdomen, bloating and in severe cases, the presence of dark black stools indicate internal bleeding. A person who drinks alcohol regularly is strongly advised to reduce their intake to prevent PUD aggravation.</p>
<p>Ingestion of alcohol can initiate systemic pro-inflammatory changes through two intestinal routes: (1) altering intestinal microbiota composition (dysbiosis), which increases lipopolysaccharide (LPS) release, and (2) degrading intestinal barrier integrity - thus allowing this (LPS) to enter the circulatory system. The major portion of the blood supply to the liver is provided the portal vein. Therefore, while the liver is continuously fed nutrients from the intestine, it is also exposed to any bacteria and/or bacterial derivatives that breach the intestinal mucosal barrier. Consequently, LPS levels increase in the portal vein, liver and systemic circulation after alcohol intake. Immune cells in the liver respond to LPS with the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), leukotrienes, chemokines and cytokines. These factors promote tissue inflammation and contribute to organ pathology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Short-term_toxic_allergy-like_responses">Short-term toxic allergy-like responses</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol-induced_respiratory_reactions" title="Alcohol-induced respiratory reactions">Alcohol-induced respiratory reactions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_flush_reaction" title="Alcohol flush reaction">Alcohol flush reaction</a></div>
<p>Ethanol-containing beverages can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urticarial" title="Urticarial">urticarial</a> skin eruptions, systemic <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_flush_reaction" title="Alcohol flush reaction">alcohol flush reactions</a>, exacerbations of <a href="/wiki/Rhinitis" title="Rhinitis">rhinitis</a> and, more seriously and commonly, <a href="/wiki/Bronchoconstriction" title="Bronchoconstriction">bronchoconstriction</a> in patients with a history of <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>. These reactions occur within 1–60 minutes of ethanol ingestion and are due to: <b>1)</b> genetic abnormalities in the metabolism of ethanol which cause the ethanol metabolite, <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a>, to accumulate in tissues and trigger the release of <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a>, the evoker of these symptoms; <b>2)</b> true <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergy</a> reactions to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergens" title="Allergens">allergens</a> occurring naturally in, or contaminating, alcoholic beverages, particularly wines and beers, and <b>3)</b> unknown causes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term">Long-term</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_alcohol" title="Long-term effects of alcohol">Long-term effects of alcohol</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_defects">Birth defects</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_syndrome" title="Fetal alcohol syndrome">fetal alcohol syndrome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_spectrum_disorder" title="Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder">fetal alcohol spectrum disorder</a></div>
<p>Ethanol is classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogen" title="Teratogen">teratogen</a>. According to the CDC, alcohol consumption by women of child-bearing age who are not using birth control increases the risk of fetal alcohol syndrome. The CDC currently recommends complete abstinence from alcoholic beverages.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer">Cancer</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="International Agency for Research on Cancer">IARC</a> list ethanol in alcoholic beverages as <i>Group 1 carcinogens</i> and arguments "There is sufficient evidence for the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_and_cancer" title="Alcohol and cancer">carcinogenicity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> (the major metabolite of ethanol) in experimental animals.".</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_effects">Other effects</span></h4>
<p>Frequent drinking of alcoholic beverages has been shown to be a major contributing factor in cases of elevated blood levels of <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reinforcement_disorders">Reinforcement disorders</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Addiction">Addiction</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">Alcoholism</a></div>
<p>The reinforcing effects of alcohol consumption are mediated by <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> generated by <a href="/wiki/Catalase" title="Catalase">catalase</a> and other oxidizing enzymes such as <a href="/wiki/CYP2E1" title="CYP2E1">cytochrome P-4502E1</a> in the brain. Although acetaldehyde has been associated with some of the adverse and toxic effects of ethanol, it appears to play a central role in the activation of the <a href="/wiki/Mesolimbic_pathway" title="Mesolimbic pathway">mesolimbic dopamine system</a>.</p>
<p>Ethanol's rewarding and reinforcing (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Addictive" title="Addictive">addictive</a>) properties are mediated through its effects on <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> neurons in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesolimbic_reward_pathway" title="Mesolimbic reward pathway">mesolimbic reward pathway</a>, which connects the <a href="/wiki/Ventral_tegmental_area" title="Ventral tegmental area">ventral tegmental area</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens" title="Nucleus accumbens">nucleus accumbens</a> (NAcc). One of ethanol's primary effects is the allosteric inhibition of <a href="/wiki/NMDA_receptor" title="NMDA receptor">NMDA receptors</a> and facilitation of <a href="/wiki/GABAA_receptor" title="GABAA receptor">GABA<sub>A</sub> receptors</a> (e.g., enhanced GABA<sub>A</sub> receptor-mediated <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> flux through <a href="/wiki/Allosteric_regulation" title="Allosteric regulation">allosteric regulation</a> of the receptor). At high doses, ethanol inhibits most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligand_gated_ion_channel" title="Ligand gated ion channel">ligand gated ion channels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voltage_gated_ion_channel" title="Voltage gated ion channel">voltage gated ion channels</a> in neurons as well.</p>
<p>With acute alcohol consumption, <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> is released in the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synapse" title="Chemical synapse">synapses</a> of the mesolimbic pathway, in turn heightening activation of postsynaptic <a href="/wiki/Dopamine_receptor_D1" title="Dopamine receptor D1">D1 receptors</a>. The activation of these receptors triggers postsynaptic internal signaling events through <a href="/wiki/Protein_kinase_A" title="Protein kinase A">protein kinase A</a> which ultimately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphorylate" title="Phosphorylate">phosphorylate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAMP_response_element_binding_protein" title="CAMP response element binding protein">cAMP response element binding protein</a> (CREB), inducing CREB-mediated changes in <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a>.</p>
<p>With chronic alcohol intake, consumption of ethanol similarly induces CREB phosphorylation through the D1 receptor pathway, but it also alters <a href="/wiki/NMDA_receptor" title="NMDA receptor">NMDA receptor</a> function through phosphorylation mechanisms; an adaptive <a href="/wiki/Downregulation_and_upregulation" title="Downregulation and upregulation">downregulation</a> of the D1 receptor pathway and CREB function occurs as well. Chronic consumption is also associated with an effect on CREB phosphorylation and function via postsynaptic NMDA receptor signaling cascades through a <a href="/wiki/MAPK/ERK_pathway" title="MAPK/ERK pathway">MAPK/ERK pathway</a> and <a href="/wiki/CAMK" title="CAMK">CAMK</a>-mediated pathway. These modifications to CREB function in the mesolimbic pathway <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inducible_gene" title="Inducible gene">induce expression</a> (i.e., increase gene expression) of ΔFosB in the <abbr title="nucleus accumbens">NAcc</abbr>, where ΔFosB is the "master control protein" that, when overexpressed in the NAcc, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necessary_and_sufficient" title="Necessary and sufficient">necessary and sufficient</a> for the development and maintenance of an addictive state (i.e., its overexpression in the nucleus accumbens produces and then directly modulates compulsive alcohol consumption).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dependence_and_withdrawal">Dependence and withdrawal</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_withdrawal_syndrome" title="Alcohol withdrawal syndrome">Alcohol withdrawal syndrome</a></div>
<p>Discontinuing consumption of alcohol after several years of heavy drinking can also be fatal. Alcohol withdrawal can cause anxiety, autonomic dysfunction, seizures, and hallucinations. <a href="/wiki/Delirium_tremens" title="Delirium tremens">Delirium tremens</a> is a condition that requires people with a long history of heavy drinking to undertake an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_detoxification" title="Alcohol detoxification">alcohol detoxification</a> regimen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overdose">Overdose</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_consumption_and_health" title="Alcohol consumption and health">Alcohol consumption and health</a></div>

<p>Death from ethanol consumption is possible when blood alcohol levels reach 0.4%. A blood level of 0.5% or more is commonly fatal. Levels of even less than 0.1% can cause <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">intoxication</a>, with unconsciousness often occurring at 0.3–0.4%.</p>
<p>Prolonged heavy consumption of alcohol can cause significant permanent damage to the brain and other organs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions">Interactions</span></h2>
<p>Ethanol can intensify the sedation caused by other <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> <a href="/wiki/Depressant" title="Depressant">depressant</a> drugs such as <a href="/wiki/Barbiturate" title="Barbiturate">barbiturates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzodiazepine" title="Benzodiazepine">benzodiazepines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-benzodiazepine" title="Non-benzodiazepine">non-benzodiazepines</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Zolpidem" title="Zolpidem">Zolpidem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zopiclone" title="Zopiclone">Zopiclone</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antipsychotics" title="Antipsychotics">antipsychotics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Histamine_antagonist" title="Histamine antagonist">sedative antihistamines</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antidepressants" title="Antidepressants">antidepressants</a>. It interacts with <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> in vivo to produce <a href="/wiki/Cocaethylene" title="Cocaethylene">cocaethylene</a>, another psychoactive substance. Ethanol enhances the <a href="/wiki/Bioavailability" title="Bioavailability">bioavailability</a> of <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">methylphenidate</a> (elevated plasma d-MPH). In combination with cannabis, ethanol increases plasma THC levels, which suggests that ethanol may increase the absorption of THC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcohol_and_metronidazole">Alcohol and metronidazole</span></h3>
<p>One of the most important drug/food interactions that should be noted is between alcohol and <a href="/wiki/Metronidazole" title="Metronidazole">metronidazole</a>.</p>
<p>Metronidazole is an antibacterial agent that kills bacteria by damaging cellular DNA and hence cellular function. Metronidazole is usually given to people who have diarrhea caused by <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_difficile_(bacteria)" title="Clostridium difficile (bacteria)">Clostridium difficile</a></i> bacteria. <i>C. difficile</i> is one of the most common microorganisms that cause diarrhea and can lead to complications such as colon inflammation and even more severely, death.</p>
<p>Patients who are taking metronidazole are strongly advised to avoid alcohol, even after 1 hour after the last dose. The reason is that alcohol and metronidazole can lead to side effects such as flushing, headache, nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and sweating. These symptoms are often called the <a href="/wiki/Disulfiram" title="Disulfiram">disulfiram</a>-like reaction. The proposed mechanism of action for this interaction is that metronidazole can bind to an enzyme that normally metabolizes alcohol. Binding to this enzyme may impair the liver's ability to process alcohol for proper excretion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacology">Pharmacology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacodynamics">Pharmacodynamics</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication#Ethanol_and_GABAA_Receptors" title="Alcohol intoxication">Alcohol intoxication § Ethanol and GABAA Receptors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker#Ethanol" title="Calcium channel blocker">Calcium channel blocker § Ethanol</a></div>
<p>Ethanol acts in the central nervous system primarily by binding to the GABA<sub>A</sub> receptor, increasing the effects of the inhibitory <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GABA" title="GABA">GABA</a> (i.e., it is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_allosteric_modulator" title="Positive allosteric modulator">positive allosteric modulator</a>).</p>
<p>Ethanol is known to possess the following direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmacodynamic" title="Pharmacodynamic">pharmacodynamic</a> actions (most important actions are bolded):</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/GABAA_receptor" title="GABAA receptor">GABA<sub>A</sub> receptor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_allosteric_modulator" title="Positive allosteric modulator">positive allosteric modulator</a></b> (primarily of <a href="/wiki/GABRD" title="GABRD">δ subunit</a>-containing receptors)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/NMDA_receptor" title="NMDA receptor">NMDA receptor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_allosteric_modulator" title="Negative allosteric modulator">negative allosteric modulator</a></b>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mesolimbic_pathway" title="Mesolimbic pathway">Mesolimbic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> release, secondary to GABAA/NMDA receptor modulation</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogenous_opioid" title="Endogenous opioid">Endogenous opioid</a> release in the mesolimbic dopamine pathway, secondary to an unidentified mechanism in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens" title="Nucleus accumbens">NAcc</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ventral_tegmental_area" title="Ventral tegmental area">VTA</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/AMPA_receptor" title="AMPA receptor">AMPA receptor</a> negative allosteric modulator</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kainate_receptor" title="Kainate receptor">Kainate receptor</a> negative allosteric modulator</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nicotinic_acetylcholine_receptor" title="Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor">nACh receptor</a> agonist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/5-HT3_receptor" title="5-HT3 receptor">5-HT<sub>3</sub> receptor</a> agonist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glycine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Glycine reuptake inhibitor">Glycine reuptake inhibitor</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adenosine_reuptake_inhibitor" title="Adenosine reuptake inhibitor">Adenosine reuptake inhibitor</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/L-type_calcium_channel" title="L-type calcium channel">L-type calcium channel</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_blocker" title="Channel blocker">blocker</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/G_protein-coupled_inwardly-rectifying_potassium_channel" title="G protein-coupled inwardly-rectifying potassium channel">GIRK channel</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_opener" title="Channel opener">opener</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Some of its actions on <a href="/wiki/Ligand-gated_ion_channel" title="Ligand-gated ion channel">ligand-gated ion channels</a>, specifically the nACh receptors and the glycine receptor, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dose-dependent" title="Dose-dependent">dose-dependent</a>, with potentiation <i>or</i> inhibition occurring dependent on ethanol concentration. This is because ethanol's effects on these channels are a summation of positive and negative allosteric modulatory actions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacokinetics">Pharmacokinetics</span></h3>
<p>The removal of ethanol from the human body, through oxidation by <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">alcohol dehydrogenase</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, is limited. Hence, the removal of a large concentration of alcohol from <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> may follow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_order_kinetics" title="Zero order kinetics">zero-order kinetics</a>. This means that alcohol leaves the body at a constant rate, rather than having an elimination <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">half-life</a>.</p>
<p>The rate-limiting steps for one substance may be in common with other substances. As a result, the blood alcohol concentration can be used to modify the rate of metabolism of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>. Methanol itself is not highly toxic, but its metabolites <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> are; therefore, to reduce the rate of production and concentration of these harmful metabolites, ethanol can be ingested. <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">Ethylene glycol</a> poisoning can be treated in the same way.</p>
<p>Pure ethanol will irritate the skin and eyes. Nausea, <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> and intoxication are symptoms of ingestion. Long-term use by ingestion can result in serious liver damage. Atmospheric concentrations above one in a thousand are above the European Union <a href="/wiki/Occupational_exposure_limit" title="Occupational exposure limit">Occupational exposure limits</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_metabolism" title="Ethanol metabolism">Ethanol metabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">Alcohol dehydrogenase</a></div>
<p>Ethanol within the human body is converted into <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> by <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">alcohol dehydrogenase</a> and then into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl" title="Acetyl">acetyl</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl_CoA" title="Acetyl CoA">acetyl CoA</a> by <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Acetaldehyde dehydrogenase">acetaldehyde dehydrogenase</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl_CoA" title="Acetyl CoA">Acetyl CoA</a> is the final product of both carbohydrate and fat metabolism, where the acetyl can be further used to produce energy or for biosynthesis. As such, ethanol can be compared to an energy-bearing macronutrient, yielding approximately 7 kcal per gram consumed. However, the product of the first step of this breakdown, acetaldehyde, is more toxic than ethanol. Acetaldehyde is linked to most of the clinical effects of alcohol. It has been shown to increase the risk of developing cirrhosis of the liver and multiple forms of cancer.</p>
<p>During the metabolism of alcohol via the respective dehydrogenases, NAD (<a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide</a>) is converted into reduced NAD. Normally, NAD is used to metabolise fats in the liver, and as such alcohol competes with these fats for the use of NAD. Prolonged exposure to alcohol means that fats accumulate in the liver, leading to the term 'fatty liver'. Continued consumption (such as in <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>) then leads to cell death in the hepatocytes as the fat stores reduce the function of the cell to the point of death. These cells are then replaced with scar tissue, leading to the condition called <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcohol_and_digestion">Alcohol and digestion</span></h4>
<p>A part of ethyl alcohol is hydrophobic. This hydrophobic or lipophilic end can diffuse across cells that line the stomach wall. In fact, alcohol is one of the rare substances that can be absorbed in the stomach. Most food substances are absorbed in the small intestine. However, even though alcohol can be absorbed in the stomach, it is mostly absorbed in the small intestine because the small intestine has a large surface area that promotes absorption. Once alcohol is absorbed in the small intestine, it delays the release of stomach contents from emptying into the small intestine. Thus, alcohol can delay the rate of absorption of nutrients. After absorption, alcohol reaches the liver where it is metabolized.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Breathalyzers">Breathalyzers</span></h5>
<p>Alcohol that is not processed by the liver goes to the heart. The liver can process only a certain amount of alcohol per unit time. Thus, when a person drinks too much alcohol, more alcohol can reach the heart. In the heart, alcohol reduces the force of heart contractions. Consequently, the heart will pump less blood, lowering overall body blood pressure. Also, blood that reaches the heart goes to the lungs to replenish blood's oxygen concentration. It is at this stage that a person can breathe out traces of alcohol. This is the underlying principle of the alcohol breath testing (or breathalyzers) to determine if a driver has been drinking and driving.</p>
<p>From the lungs, blood returns to the heart and will be distributed throughout the body. Interestingly, alcohol increases levels of high-density lipoproteins(HDLs), which carry cholesterol. Alcohol is known to make blood less likely to clot, reducing risk of heart attack and stroke. This could be the reason that alcohol seems to produce health benefits when consumed in moderate amounts. Also, alcohol dilates blood vessels. Consequently, a person will feel warmer, and his/her skin flush and appear pink.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnitude_of_effects">Magnitude of effects</span></h4>
<p>Some individuals have less effective forms of one or both of the metabolizing enzymes, and can experience more severe symptoms from ethanol consumption than others. However, those having acquired <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_tolerance" title="Alcohol tolerance">alcohol tolerance</a> have a greater quantity of these enzymes, and metabolize ethanol more rapidly.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_(data_page)" title="Ethanol (data page)">Ethanol (data page)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_formula">Chemical formula</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is a 2-carbon alcohol. Its <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">molecular formula</a> is CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH. An alternative notation is CH<sub>3</sub>–CH<sub>2</sub>–OH, which indicates that the carbon of a <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl group</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>–) is attached to the carbon of a <a href="/wiki/Methylene_group" title="Methylene group">methylene group</a> (–CH<sub>2</sub>–), which is attached to the oxygen of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl group (–OH)</a>. It is a constitutional <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a>. Ethanol is sometimes abbreviated as <b>EtOH</b>, using the common organic chemistry notation of representing the ethyl group (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>-) with <b>Et</b>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h3>

<p>Ethanol is a volatile, colorless liquid that has a slight odor. It burns with a smokeless blue flame that is not always visible in normal light.</p>
<p>The physical properties of ethanol stem primarily from the presence of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group and the shortness of its carbon chain. Ethanol's hydroxyl group is able to participate in hydrogen bonding, rendering it more viscous and less volatile than less polar organic compounds of similar molecular weight, such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>.</p>
<p>Ethanol is slightly more refractive than water, having a <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of 1.36242 (at λ=589.3 nm and 18.35 °C or 65.03 °F).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> for ethanol is <span class="nowrap">150 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span> at a pressure of <span class="nowrap">4.3 × 10 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a></span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent_properties">Solvent properties</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is a versatile solvent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> with water and with many organic solvents, including <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>. It is also miscible with light aliphatic hydrocarbons, such as <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">pentane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a>, and with aliphatic chlorides such as <a href="/wiki/1,1,1-Trichloroethane" title="1,1,1-Trichloroethane">trichloroethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetrachloroethylene" title="Tetrachloroethylene">tetrachloroethylene</a>.</p>
<p>Ethanol's miscibility with water contrasts with the immiscibility of longer-chain alcohols (five or more carbon atoms), whose water miscibility decreases sharply as the number of carbons increases. The miscibility of ethanol with <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a> is limited to alkanes up to <a href="/wiki/Undecane" title="Undecane">undecane</a>: mixtures with <a href="/wiki/Dodecane" title="Dodecane">dodecane</a> and higher alkanes show a <a href="/wiki/Miscibility_gap" title="Miscibility gap">miscibility gap</a> below a certain temperature (about 13 °C for dodecane). The miscibility gap tends to get wider with higher alkanes and the temperature for complete miscibility increases.</p>
<p>Ethanol-water mixtures have less volume than the sum of their individual components at the given fractions. Mixing equal volumes of ethanol and water results in only 1.92 volumes of mixture. Mixing ethanol and water is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>, with up to 777 J/mol being released at 298 K.</p>
<p>Mixtures of ethanol and water form an <a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotrope</a> at about 89 mole-% ethanol and 11 mole-% water or a mixture of 95.6 percent ethanol by mass (or about 97% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>) at normal pressure, which boils at 351K (78 °C). This azeotropic composition is strongly temperature- and pressure-dependent and vanishes at temperatures below 303 K.</p>

<p>Hydrogen bonding causes pure ethanol to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> to the extent that it readily absorbs water from the air. The polar nature of the hydroxyl group causes ethanol to dissolve many ionic compounds, notably <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_chloride" title="Magnesium chloride">magnesium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_bromide" title="Ammonium bromide">ammonium bromide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bromide" title="Sodium bromide">sodium bromide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">Sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chlorides</a> are slightly soluble in ethanol. Because the ethanol molecule also has a nonpolar end, it will also dissolve nonpolar substances, including most <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oils</a> and numerous flavoring, coloring, and medicinal agents.</p>
<p>The addition of even a few percent of ethanol to water sharply reduces the <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> of water. This property partially explains the "<a href="/wiki/Tears_of_wine" title="Tears of wine">tears of wine</a>" phenomenon. When wine is swirled in a glass, ethanol evaporates quickly from the thin film of wine on the wall of the glass. As the wine's ethanol content decreases, its surface tension increases and the thin film "beads up" and runs down the glass in channels rather than as a smooth sheet.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flammability">Flammability</span></h3>
<p>An ethanol-water solution that contains 40% alcohol by weight will catch fire if heated to about 26 °C (79 °F) and if an ignition source is applied to it. This is called its <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a>. The flash point of pure ethanol is 16.60 °C (61.88 °F), less than average room temperature.</p>

<p><br/>
Dishes using burning alcohol for culinary effects are called <a href="/wiki/Flamb%C3%A9" title="Flambé">Flambé</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span></h2>
<p>Ethanol is a byproduct of the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic process</a> of yeast. As such, ethanol will be present in any yeast habitat. Ethanol can commonly be found in overripe fruit. Ethanol produced by symbiotic yeast can be found in <a href="/wiki/Palm_wine" title="Palm wine">bertam palm</a> blossoms. Although some animal species such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentailed_treeshrew" title="Pentailed treeshrew">pentailed treeshrew</a> exhibit ethanol-seeking behaviors, most show no interest or avoidance of food sources containing ethanol. Ethanol is also produced during the germination of many plants as a result of natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anerobiosis" title="Anerobiosis">anerobiosis</a>. Ethanol has been detected in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>, forming an icy coating around dust grains in <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_cloud" title="Interstellar cloud">interstellar clouds</a>. Minute quantity amounts (average 196 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a>) of endogenous ethanol and acetaldehyde were found in the exhaled breath of healthy volunteers. <a href="/wiki/Auto-brewery_syndrome" title="Auto-brewery syndrome">Auto-brewery syndrome</a>, also known as gut fermentation syndrome, is a rare medical condition in which <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">intoxicating</a> quantities of ethanol are produced through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogenous" title="Endogenous">endogenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>

<p>Ethanol is produced both as a <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a>, through the hydration of ethylene and, via biological processes, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermenting</a> sugars with <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>. Which process is more economical depends on prevailing prices of petroleum and grain feed stocks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethylene_hydration">Ethylene hydration</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol for use as an industrial feedstock or solvent (sometimes referred to as synthetic ethanol) is made from <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> feed stocks, primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>-<a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzed</a> hydration of ethylene:</p>

<p>The catalyst is most commonly <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbed</a> onto a porous support such as <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a>. This catalyst was first used for large-scale ethanol production by the <a href="/wiki/Shell_Oil_Company" title="Shell Oil Company">Shell Oil Company</a> in 1947. The reaction is carried out in the presence of high pressure steam at 300 °C (572 °F) where a 1.0:0.6 ethylene to steam ratio is maintained. In the U.S., this process was used on an industrial scale by <a href="/wiki/Union_Carbide" title="Union Carbide">Union Carbide Corporation</a> and others, but now only <a href="/wiki/LyondellBasell" title="LyondellBasell">LyondellBasell</a> uses it commercially.</p>
<p>In an older process, first practiced on the industrial scale in 1930 by Union Carbide, but now almost entirely obsolete, ethylene was hydrated indirectly by reacting it with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_sulfate" title="Ethyl sulfate">ethyl sulfate</a>, which was <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a> to yield ethanol and regenerate the sulfuric acid:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation">Fermentation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">Ethanol fermentation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Yeast_in_winemaking" title="Yeast in winemaking">Yeast in winemaking</a></div>
<p>Ethanol in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> and fuel is produced by fermentation. Certain species of <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolize</a> <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">sugar</a>, producing ethanol and carbon dioxide. The chemical equations below summarize the conversion:</p>

<p>Fermentation is the process of culturing yeast under favorable thermal conditions to produce alcohol. This process is carried out at around 35–40 °C (95–104 °F). Toxicity of ethanol to yeast limits the ethanol concentration obtainable by brewing; higher concentrations, therefore, are obtained by <a href="/wiki/Fortified_wine" title="Fortified wine">fortification</a> or <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>. The most ethanol-tolerant yeast strains can survive up to approximately 18% ethanol by volume.</p>
<p>To produce ethanol from starchy materials such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereal_grain" title="Cereal grain">cereal grains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> must first be converted into sugars. In brewing <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, this has traditionally been accomplished by allowing the grain to germinate, or <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>, which produces the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>. When the malted grain is <a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">mashed</a>, the amylase converts the remaining starches into sugars.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellulose">Cellulose</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">Cellulosic ethanol</a></div>
<p>Sugars for <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">ethanol fermentation</a> can be obtained from <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>. Deployment of this technology could turn a number of cellulose-containing agricultural by-products, such as <a href="/wiki/Corncob" title="Corncob">corncobs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sawdust" title="Sawdust">sawdust</a>, into renewable energy resources. Other agricultural residues such as sugar cane bagasse and <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switchgrass" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a> may also be a sources of fermentable sugars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span></h3>


<p>Breweries and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> plants employ two methods for measuring ethanol concentration. Infrared ethanol sensors measure the vibrational frequency of dissolved ethanol using the CH band at 2900 cm. This method uses a relatively inexpensive solid state sensor that compares the CH band with a reference band to calculate the ethanol content. The calculation makes use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer-Lambert_law" title="Beer-Lambert law">Beer-Lambert law</a>. Alternatively, by measuring the density of the starting material and the density of the product, using a <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">hydrometer</a>, the change in specific gravity during fermentation indicates the alcohol content. This inexpensive and indirect method has a long history in the beer brewing industry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purification">Purification</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distillation">Distillation</span></h3>
<p>Ethylene hydration or brewing produces an ethanol–water mixture. For most industrial and fuel uses, the ethanol must be purified. <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">Fractional distillation</a> at atmospheric pressure can concentrate ethanol to 95.6% by volume (89.5 mole%). This mixture is an <a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotrope</a> with a boiling point of 78.1 °C (172.6 °F), and <i>cannot</i> be further purified by distillation. Addition of an entraining agent, such as <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane" title="Cyclohexane">cyclohexane</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Heptane" title="Heptane">heptane</a>, allows a new ternary azeotrope comprising the ethanol, water, and the entraining agent to be formed. This lower-boiling ternary azeotrope is removed preferentially, leading to water-free ethanol.</p>
<p>At pressures less than atmospheric pressure, the composition of the ethanol-water azeotrope shifts to more ethanol-rich mixtures, and at pressures less than 70 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a> (9.333 kPa), there is no azeotrope, and it is possible to distill absolute ethanol from an ethanol-water mixture. While vacuum distillation of ethanol is not presently economical, pressure-swing distillation is a topic of current research. In this technique, a reduced-pressure distillation first yields an ethanol-water mixture of more than 95.6% ethanol. Then, fractional distillation of this mixture at atmospheric pressure distills off the 95.6% azeotrope, leaving anhydrous ethanol at the bottom.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_sieves_and_desiccants">Molecular sieves and desiccants</span></h3>

<p>Apart from distillation, ethanol may be dried by addition of a <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_sieves" title="Molecular sieves">molecular sieves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">cornmeal</a>. The desiccants can be dried and reused.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molecular_sieve" title="Molecular sieve">Molecular sieves</a> can be used to selectively absorb the water from the 95.6% ethanol solution. Synthetic <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> in pellet form can be used, as well as a variety of plant-derived absorbents, including <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">cornmeal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sawdust" title="Sawdust">sawdust</a>. The zeolite bed can be regenerated essentially an unlimited number of times by drying it with a blast of hot <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Cornmeal and other plant-derived absorbents cannot readily be regenerated, but where ethanol is made from grain, they are often available at low cost. Absolute ethanol produced this way has no residual benzene, and can be used to fortify port and sherry in traditional winery operations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membranes_and_reverse_osmosis">Membranes and reverse osmosis</span></h3>
<p>Membranes can also be used to separate ethanol and water. Membrane-based separations are not subject to the limitations of the water-ethanol azeotrope because the separations are not based on vapor-liquid equilibria. Membranes are often used in the so-called hybrid membrane distillation process. This process uses a pre-concentration distillation column as first separating step. The further separation is then accomplished with a membrane operated either in vapor permeation or pervaporation mode. Vapor permeation uses a vapor membrane feed and pervaporation uses a liquid membrane feed.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grades_of_ethanol">Grades of ethanol</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denatured_alcohol">Denatured alcohol</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Denatured_alcohol" title="Denatured alcohol">Denatured alcohol</a></div>
<p>Pure ethanol and alcoholic beverages are heavily <a href="/wiki/Sin_tax" title="Sin tax">taxed</a> as psychoactive drugs, but ethanol has many uses that do not involve its consumption. To relieve the tax burden on these uses, most jurisdictions waive the tax when an agent has been added to the ethanol to render it unfit to drink. These include <a href="/wiki/Bitterant" title="Bitterant">bittering agents</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denatonium_benzoate" title="Denatonium benzoate">denatonium benzoate</a> and toxins such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>. Products of this kind are called <i>denatured alcohol.</i></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolute_alcohol">Absolute alcohol</span></h4>
<p>Absolute or anhydrous alcohol refers to ethanol with a low water content. There are various grades with maximum water contents ranging from 1% to a few parts per million (ppm) levels. If <a href="/wiki/Azeotropic_distillation" title="Azeotropic distillation">azeotropic distillation</a> is used to remove water, it will contain trace amounts of the material separation agent (e.g. benzene). Absolute alcohol is not intended for human consumption. Absolute ethanol is used as a solvent for laboratory and industrial applications, where water will react with other chemicals, and as fuel alcohol. Spectroscopic ethanol is an absolute ethanol with a low absorbance in <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> and visible light, fit for use as a solvent in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet-visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy">ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy</a>.</p>
<p>Pure ethanol is classed as 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(alcohol)" title="Proof (alcohol)">proof</a> in the U.S., equivalent to 175 degrees proof in the UK system.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rectified_spirits">Rectified spirits</span></h4>
<p>Rectified spirit, an azeotropic composition of 96% ethanol containing 4% water, is used instead of anhydrous ethanol for various purposes. Wine spirits are about 94% ethanol (188 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proof_(alcohol)" title="Proof (alcohol)">proof</a>). The impurities are different from those in 95% (190 proof) laboratory ethanol.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">Alcohol</a>.</div>
<p>Ethanol is classified as a primary alcohol, meaning that the carbon its hydroxyl group attaches to has at least two hydrogen atoms attached to it as well. Many ethanol reactions occur at its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ester_formation">Ester formation</span></h3>

<p>This reaction, which is conducted on large scale industrially, requires the removal of the water from the reaction mixture as it is formed. Esters react in the presence of an acid or base to give back the alcohol and a salt. This reaction is known as <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a> because it is used in the preparation of soap. Ethanol can also form esters with inorganic acids. <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_sulfate" title="Diethyl sulfate">Diethyl sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triethyl_phosphate" title="Triethyl phosphate">triethyl phosphate</a> are prepared by treating ethanol with sulfur trioxide and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">phosphorus pentoxide</a> respectively. <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_sulfate" title="Diethyl sulfate">Diethyl sulfate</a> is a useful ethylating agent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_nitrite" title="Ethyl nitrite">Ethyl nitrite</a>, prepared from the reaction of ethanol with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a> and sulfuric acid, was formerly used as a <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretic</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dehydration">Dehydration</span></h3>
<p>Strong acid desiccants cause the partial dehydration of ethanol to form <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> and other byproducts. If the dehydration temperature exceeds around 160 °C (320 °F), full dehydration will occur and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> will be the main product.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol can be oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> and further oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, depending on the reagents and conditions. This oxidation is of no importance industrially, but in the human body, these oxidation reactions are catalyzed by the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liver_alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Liver alcohol dehydrogenase">liver alcohol dehydrogenase</a>. The oxidation product of ethanol, acetic acid, is a nutrient for humans, being a precursor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl_CoA" title="Acetyl CoA">acetyl CoA</a>, where the acetyl group can be spent as energy or used for biosynthesis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">Distilled beverage</a>.</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">fermentation</a> of sugar into ethanol is one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnologies</a> employed by humans. The intoxicating effects of ethanol consumption have been known since ancient times. Ethanol has been used by humans since prehistory as the intoxicating ingredient of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>. Dried residue on 9,000-year-old pottery found in China suggests that <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> people consumed alcoholic beverages.</p>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> was well known by the early Greeks and Arabs, the first recorded production of alcohol from distilled wine was by the <a href="/wiki/Schola_Medica_Salernitana" title="Schola Medica Salernitana">School of Salerno</a> alchemists in the 12th century. The first to mention absolute alcohol, in contrast with alcohol-water mixtures, was <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Llull" title="Ramon Llull">Raymond Lull</a>.</p>
<p>In 1796, German-Russian chemist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Tobias_Lowitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann Tobias Lowitz (page does not exist)">Johann Tobias Lowitz</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Tobias_Lowitz" title="de:Johann Tobias Lowitz">de</a>)</span> obtained pure ethanol by mixing partially purified ethanol (the alcohol-water azeotrope) with an excess of anhydrous alkali and then distilling the mixture over low heat. French chemist <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> described ethanol as a compound of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, and in 1807 <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Th%C3%A9odore_de_Saussure" title="Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure">Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure</a> determined ethanol's chemical formula. Fifty years later, <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Scott_Couper" title="Archibald Scott Couper">Archibald Scott Couper</a> published the structural formula of ethanol. It was one of the first structural formulas determined.</p>
<p>Ethanol was first prepared synthetically in 1825 by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>. He found that sulfuric acid could absorb large volumes of <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>. He gave the resulting solution to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hennell" title="Henry Hennell">Henry Hennell</a>, a British chemist, who found in 1826 that it contained "sulphovinic acid" (<a href="/wiki/Ethyl_sulfate" title="Ethyl sulfate">ethyl hydrogen sulfate</a>). In 1828, Hennell and the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Georges-Simon_Serullas" title="Georges-Simon Serullas">Georges-Simon Serullas</a> independently discovered that sulphovinic acid could be decomposed into ethanol. Thus, in 1825 Faraday had unwittingly discovered that ethanol could be produced from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (a component of coal gas) by <a href="/wiki/Acid_catalysis" title="Acid catalysis">acid-catalyzed</a> hydration, a process similar to current industrial ethanol synthesis.</p>
<p>Ethanol was used as lamp fuel in the United States as early as 1840, but a tax levied on industrial alcohol during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> made this use uneconomical. The tax was repealed in 1906. Use as an automotive fuel dates back to 1908, with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Ford Model T</a> able to run on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">petrol</a> (gasoline) or ethanol. It fuels some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_lamps" title="Spirit lamps">spirit lamps</a>.</p>
<p>Ethanol intended for industrial use is often produced from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>. Ethanol has widespread use as a solvent of substances intended for human contact or consumption, including scents, flavorings, colorings, and medicines. In chemistry, it is both a solvent and a feedstock for the synthesis of other products. It has a long history as a fuel for heat and light, and more recently as a fuel for <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Drinking_culture" title="Drinking culture">Drinking culture</a>.</div>
<p>A 2002 study found 41% of people fatally injured in traffic accidents were in alcohol related crashes. The risk of a fatal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident">car accident</a> increases exponentially with the level of alcohol in the driver's blood. Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drunk_driving" title="Drunk driving">drunk driving</a> laws governing the acceptable levels in the blood while driving or operating heavy machinery set typical upper limits of legal <a href="/wiki/Blood_alcohol_content" title="Blood alcohol content">blood alcohol content</a> (BAC) at 0.08%.</p>
