<p><b>Jared Mason Diamond</b> (born September 10, 1937) is an American scientist and author best known for his <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> books <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Chimpanzee" title="The Third Chimpanzee">The Third Chimpanzee</a></i> (1991); <i><a href="/wiki/Guns,_Germs,_and_Steel" title="Guns, Germs, and Steel">Guns, Germs, and Steel</a></i> (1997, awarded a <a href="/wiki/1998_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1998 Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/Collapse:_How_Societies_Choose_to_Fail_or_Succeed" title="Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed">Collapse</a></i> (2005); and <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_Until_Yesterday" title="The World Until Yesterday">The World Until Yesterday</a></i> (2012). Originally trained in <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, Diamond is known for drawing from a variety of fields, including <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>. He is a professor of geography at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a>.</p>
<p>In 2005, Diamond was ranked ninth on a poll by <i><a href="/wiki/Prospect_(magazine)" title="Prospect (magazine)">Prospect</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy_(magazine)" title="Foreign Policy (magazine)">Foreign Policy</a></i> of the world's top 100 public intellectuals.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span></h2>
<p>Diamond was born in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts. Both of his parents were from East European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> families who had emigrated to the United States. His father, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Diamond" title="Louis Diamond">Louis Diamond</a>, was a physician, and his mother, Flora Kaplan, a teacher, linguist, and concert pianist. Diamond himself began studying piano at age six; years later he would propose to his wife after playing the <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> Intermezzo in A minor for her. He attended the <a href="/wiki/Roxbury_Latin_School" title="Roxbury Latin School">Roxbury Latin School</a> and earned a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> in 1958 and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PhD" title="PhD">doctorate</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Membrane_biophysics" title="Membrane biophysics">membranes</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_bladder" title="Gall bladder">gall bladder</a> from <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in 1961.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<p>After graduating from Cambridge, Diamond returned to Harvard as a <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Society_of_Fellows#Junior_Fellows" title="Harvard Society of Fellows">Junior Fellow</a> until 1965, and, in 1968, became a professor of physiology at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles_School_of_Medicine" title="University of California, Los Angeles School of Medicine">UCLA Medical School</a>. While in his twenties he developed a second, parallel, career in <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, specialising in <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> and nearby islands. Later, in his fifties, Diamond developed a third career in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_history" title="Environmental history">environmental history</a> and became a professor of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> at UCLA, his current position. He also teaches at <a href="/wiki/Libera_Universit%C3%A0_Internazionale_degli_Studi_Sociali_Guido_Carli" title="Libera Università Internazionale degli Studi Sociali Guido Carli">LUISS Guido Carli</a> in Rome. He won the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> in 1999 and <a href="/wiki/Westfield_State_University" title="Westfield State University">Westfield State University</a> granted him an honorary doctorate in 2009.</p>
<p>Diamond originally specialized in salt absorption in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_bladder" title="Gall bladder">gall bladder</a>. He has also published scholarly works in the fields of ecology and <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithology</a>, but is arguably best known for authoring a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular-science" title="Popular-science">popular-science</a> books combining topics from diverse fields other than those he has formally studied. Because of this academic diversity, Diamond has been described as a <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span></h2>
<p>Diamond's first popular book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Third_Chimpanzee:_The_Evolution_and_Future_of_the_Human_Animal" title="The Third Chimpanzee: The Evolution and Future of the Human Animal">The Third Chimpanzee: The Evolution and Future of the Human Animal</a></i> (1991), examines <a href="/wiki/Human_evolution" title="Human evolution">human evolution</a> and its relevance to the modern world, incorporating evidence from <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, ecology, and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>. The book traces how humans evolved to be so different from other animals, despite sharing over 98% of our DNA with our closest animal relatives, the chimpanzees. The book also examines the animal origins of language, art, agriculture, smoking and drug use, and other apparently uniquely human attributes. It was well received by critics and won the 1992 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne-Poulenc_Prize_for_Science_Books" title="Rhône-Poulenc Prize for Science Books">Rhône-Poulenc Prize for Science Books</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times_Book_Prize" title="Los Angeles Times Book Prize">Los Angeles Times Book Prize</a>.</p>
<p>His second and best known popular science book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guns,_Germs,_and_Steel:_The_Fates_of_Human_Societies" title="Guns, Germs, and Steel: The Fates of Human Societies">Guns, Germs, and Steel: The Fates of Human Societies</a></i>, was published in 1997. It asks why <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_(mixed_ancestry)" title="Eurasian (mixed ancestry)">Eurasian peoples</a> conquered or displaced Native Americans, Australians, and Africans, instead of vice versa. It argues that this outcome was not due to biological advantages of Eurasian peoples themselves but instead to features of the Eurasian continent, in particular, its high diversity of wild plant and animal species suitable for domestication and its east/west major axis that favored the spread of those domesticates, people, and technologies for long distances with little change in latitude. The first part of the book focuses on reasons why only a few species of wild plants and animals proved suitable for domestication. The second part discusses how local food production based on those domesticates led to the development of dense and stratified human populations, writing, centralized political organization, and epidemic infectious diseases. The third part compares the development of food production and of human societies among different continents and world regions. <i>Guns, Germs, and Steel</i> became an international best-seller, was translated into 33 languages, and received several awards, including a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aventis_Prize_for_Science_Books" title="Aventis Prize for Science Books">Aventis Prize for Science Books</a> and the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa_Award_in_Science" title="Phi Beta Kappa Award in Science">Phi Beta Kappa Award in Science</a>. A television documentary series based on the book was produced by the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> in 2005.</p>
<p>In his third book, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Why_is_Sex_Fun%3F" title="Why is Sex Fun?">Why is Sex Fun?</a></i>, also published in 1997, Diamond discusses evolutionary factors underlying features of human sexuality that are generally taken for granted but that are highly unusual among our animal relatives. Those features include a long-term pair relationship (marriage), coexistence of economically cooperating pairs within a shared communal territory, provision of parental care by fathers as well as by mothers, having sex in private rather than in public, concealed ovulation, female sexual receptivity encompassing most of the menstrual cycle (including days of infertility), female but not male menopause, and distinctive secondary sexual characteristics.</p>
<p>Diamond's next book, <i><a href="/wiki/Collapse:_How_Societies_Choose_to_Fail_or_Succeed" title="Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed">Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed</a></i>, published in 2005, examines a range of past societies in an attempt to identify why they either collapsed or continued to thrive and considers what contemporary societies can learn from these historical examples. As in <i>Guns, Germs, and Steel</i>, he argues against explanations for the failure of past societies based primarily on cultural factors, instead focusing on ecology. Among the societies mentioned in the book are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">Norse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anasazi" title="Anasazi">Anasazi</a>, the indigenous people of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapa_Nui" title="Rapa Nui">Rapa Nui</a> (Easter Island), Japan, Haiti, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and modern <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>. The book concludes by asking why some societies make disastrous decisions, how big businesses affect the environment, what our principal environmental problems are today, and what individuals can do about those problems. Like <i>Guns, Germs, and Steel</i>, <i>Collapse</i> was translated into dozens of languages, became an international best-seller, and was the basis of a television documentary produced by the National Geographic Society. It was also nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_Prize_for_Science_Books" title="Royal Society Prize for Science Books">Royal Society Prize for Science Books</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, Diamond published an article in <i>The New Yorker</i> entitled "Vengeance Is Ours", describing the role of revenge in tribal warfare in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>. A year later two indigenous people mentioned in the article filed a lawsuit against Diamond and <i>The New Yorker</i> claiming the article defamed them. In 2013, <i>The Observer</i> reported that the lawsuit "was withdrawn by mutual consent after the sudden death of their lawyer."</p>
<p>In 2010, Diamond co-edited (with James Robinson) <i>Natural Experiments of History</i>, a collection of seven case studies illustrating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidisciplinarity" title="Multidisciplinarity">multidisciplinary</a> and comparative approach to the study of history that he advocates. The book’s title stems from the fact that it is not possible to study history by the preferred methods of the laboratory sciences, i.e., by controlled experiments comparing replicated human societies as if they were test tubes of bacteria. Instead, one must look at natural experiments in which human societies that are similar in many respects have been historically perturbed, either by different starting conditions or by different impacts. The book’s afterword classifies natural experiments, discusses the practical difficulties of studying them, and offers suggestions how to address those difficulties.</p>
<p>Diamond's most recent book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_Until_Yesterday" title="The World Until Yesterday">The World Until Yesterday</a></i>, published in 2012, asks what the western world can learn from <a href="/wiki/Traditional_society" title="Traditional society">traditional societies</a>. It surveys 39 traditional small-scale societies of farmers and hunter-gatherers with respect to how they deal with universal human problems. The problems discussed include dividing space, resolving disputes, bringing up children, treatment of elders, dealing with dangers, formulating religions, learning multiple languages, and remaining healthy. The book suggests that some practices of traditional societies could be usefully adopted in the modern industrial world today, either by individuals or else by society as a whole.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span></h2>
<p>At the end of his book <i><a href="/wiki/Collapse:_How_Societies_Choose_to_Fail_or_Succeed" title="Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed">Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed</a></i>, Jared Diamond wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>People who get depressed at such thoughts [big global environmental problems] often then ask me, "Jared, are you optimistic or pessimistic about the world’s future?" I answer, "I am a cautious optimist." By that, I mean that, on the one hand, I acknowledge the seriousness of the problems facing us. If we don’t make a determined effort to solve them, and if we don’t succeed at that effort, the world as a whole within the next few decades will face a declining standard of living, or perhaps something worse. That’s the reason why I decided to devote most of my career efforts at this stage of my life to convincing people that our problems have to be taken seriously and won’t go away otherwise. On the other hand, we shall be able to solve our problems – if we choose to do so.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
