<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">


<p>A <b>biocenosis</b> (UK English, <i>bioc<b>o</b>enosis</i>, also <b>biocenose</b>, <b>biocoenose</b>, <b>biotic community</b>, <b>biological community</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">ecological community</a></b>, <b>life assemblage,</b>) coined by <a href="/wiki/Karl_M%C3%B6bius" title="Karl Möbius">Karl Möbius</a> in 1877, describes the interacting <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> living together in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biotope" title="Biotope">biotope</a>).</p>
<p>In the palaeontological literature, the term distinguishes "life assemblages," which reflect the original living community, living together at one place and time. In other words, it is an assemblage of fossils or a community of specific time.Which is different from "death assemblages" (<a href="/wiki/Thanatocoenosis" title="Thanatocoenosis">thanatocoenoses</a>). Of course, no palaeontological assemblage will ever completely represent the original biological community (i.e. the biocoenosis, in the sense used by an ecologist); the term thus has somewhat different meanings in a palaeontological and an ecological context.</p>
<p>Based on the concept of biocenosis, ecological communities can take in various forms</p>
<ul>
<li>Zoocenosis for the <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">faunal</a> community,</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytocenosis" title="Phytocenosis">Phytocenosis</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> community,</li>
<li>Microbiocenosis for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbial</a> community.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> extent of a biocenose is limited by the requirement of a more or less uniform species composition.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystems">Ecosystems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, originally defined by Tansley (1935), is a biotic community (or biocenosis) along with its physical environment (or <i><a href="/wiki/Biotope" title="Biotope">biotope</a></i>). In ecological studies, biocenosis is the emphasis on relationships between species in an area. These relationships are an additional consideration to the interaction of each species with the physical environment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotic_communities">Biotic communities</span></h2>

<p>Biotic communities vary in size, and larger ones may contain smaller ones. Species interactions are evident in food or feeding relationships. A method of delineating biotic communities is to map the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food network</a> to identify which species feed upon which others and then determine the system boundary as the one that can be drawn through the fewest consumption links relative to the number of species within the boundary.</p>
<p>Mapping biotic communities is important identifying sites needing environmental protection, such as the British <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a> (SSSIs). The <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Environment_and_Heritage_(Australia)" title="Department of the Environment and Heritage (Australia)">Department of the Environment and Heritage</a> maintains a register of <i>Threatened Species and Threatened Ecological Communities</i> under the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Protection_and_Biodiversity_Conservation_Act_1999" title="Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999">Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999</a> (EPBC Act).</p>
