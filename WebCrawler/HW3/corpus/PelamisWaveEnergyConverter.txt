<p>The <b>Pelamis Wave Energy Converter</b> is a technology that uses the motion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface waves</a> to create electricity. The machine is made up of connected sections which flex and bend as waves pass; it is this motion which is used to generate electricity.</p>
<p>Developed by the now defunct  Scottish company <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Power" title="Pelamis Wave Power">Pelamis Wave Power</a> (formerly Ocean Power Delivery), the Pelamis became the first offshore wave machine to generate electricity into the grid, when it was first connected to the UK grid in 2004. Pelamis Wave Power then went on to build and test five additional Pelamis machines: three first-generation P1 machines, which were tested in a farm off the coast of Portugal in 2009, and two second-generation machines, the Pelamis P2, which started tests off Orkney in 2010.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span></h2>
<p>The Pelamis machine is an offshore wave energy converter, operating in water depths greater than 50m. The machine consists of a series of semi-submerged cylindrical sections linked by hinged joints. As waves pass along the length of the machine, the sections move relative to one another. The wave-induced <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a> of the sections is resisted by <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_cylinder" title="Hydraulic cylinder">hydraulic cylinders</a> which pump high pressure oil through <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_machinery" title="Hydraulic machinery">hydraulic motors</a> via smoothing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic_accumulators" title="Hydraulic accumulators">hydraulic accumulators</a>. The hydraulic motors drive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generators</a> to produce electricity. Electricity from all the joints is fed down a single <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical cable</a> to a junction on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_bed" title="Sea bed">sea bed</a>. Several devices can be connected and linked to shore through a single seabed cable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle">Principle</span></h2>
<p>The Pelamis is an attenuating wave energy converter. The machine responds to the curvature of the waves (their shape) rather than the wave height. As waves can only reach a certain curvature before naturally breaking, this limits the range of motion through which the machine must move but maintains large motion at the joints in small waves.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projects">Projects</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="P2_Pelamis_Testing_at_EMEC">P2 Pelamis Testing at EMEC</span></h3>
<p>The P2 Pelamis design was Pelamis Wave Power's second generation Pelamis machine. The Pelamis P2 is 180m long, 4m diameter and approximately 1350 tonnes in weight. Consisting of five tube sections and four flexible joints, the design is longer and fatter than the previous P1 design.</p>
<p>In 2010, Pelamis Wave Power began tests of the first Pelamis P2 machine at the <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a>, Orkney, Scotland. The machine is owned by the German utility company, <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a>, and was the UK's first commercial supply contract in the <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a> sector. In March 2010 Pelamis Wave Power announced a second order for a P2 machine, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ScottishPower" title="ScottishPower">ScottishPower</a> Renewables, part of <a href="/wiki/Iberdrola_Renovables" title="Iberdrola Renovables">Iberdrola Renovables</a>. This second machine was installed for the first time at EMEC in May 2012. The two utility companies have announced that they will work together to share and collaborate in testing of the P2 Pelamis technology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projects_Formerly_in_Development">Projects Formerly in Development</span></h3>
<p>E.ON and ScottishPower Renewables have announced plans to build larger projects using Pelamis machines in the waters off Orkney's west coast. Both companies won leases in 2010 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Crown_Estate" title="The Crown Estate">The Crown Estate</a>, who own the seabed around the UK, for projects of up to 50MW. The "Pentland Firth and Orkney Waters Leasing Round" was the world's first commercial scale wave and tidal energy leasing opportunity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_History">Development History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototype_Pelamis_Machine">Prototype Pelamis Machine</span></h3>

<p>Pelamis Wave Power tested their first full-scale prototype at the <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, Scotland between 2004 and 2007. The machine, which was rated at 750 kW, was the world's first offshore <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> machine to generate electricity into the grid system.</p>
<p>The prototype was 120m long and 3.5m in diameter. It consisted of four tube sections linked by three, shorter, power conversion modules</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agu.C3.A7adoura_Wave_Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a></div>
<p>In 2008 Pelamis tested three first generation, P1 Pelamis waves at the <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a>. Located off the northwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> near <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">Póvoa de Varzim</a>, the farm had an installed capacity of 2.25MW and was the world's first multiple machine wave power project. The project was part funded by Portuguese utility <a href="/wiki/Enersis" title="Enersis">Enersis</a>, at the time owned by Australian global investment company <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Brown" title="Babcock &amp; Brown">Babcock &amp; Brown</a>. The farm first generated electricity in July 2008 but was taken offline in November 2008 at the same time as Babcock &amp; Brown encountered financial difficulties.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelamis_platurus" title="Pelamis platurus">Pelamis platurus</a></i> is a yellow-bellied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_snake" title="Sea snake">sea snake</a> that lives in tropical and subtropical waters. It prefers shallow inshore waters.</p>
