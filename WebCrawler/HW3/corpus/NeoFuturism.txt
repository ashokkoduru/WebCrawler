<p><b>Neo-futurism</b> is a late 20th–early 21st century movement in <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>. It could be seen as a departure from the attitude of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-modernism" title="Post-modernism">post-modernism</a> and represents an <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealistic</a> belief in a better future and "a need to periodize the modern rapport with the technological".</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> movement is a futuristic rethinking of the aesthetic and functionality of rapidly growing cities. The industrialization that began worldwide following the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> gave wind to new streams of thought in life, art and architecture, leading to post-modernism, neo-modernism and then neo-futurism.</p>
<p>In the Western countries, <a href="/wiki/Futurist_architecture" title="Futurist architecture">futurist architecture</a> evolved into <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Googie_architecture" title="Googie architecture">Googie movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/High-tech_architecture" title="High-tech architecture">high-tech architecture</a>, and finally into Neo-Futurism. Neo-futuristic urbanists, architects, designers and artists believe in cities releasing emotions, driven by eco-<a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, ethical values and implementing new materials and new technologies to provide a better quality of life for city-dwellers.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span></h2>
<p>Pioneered in the late 1960s and early 70s by <a href="/wiki/Thought_leader" title="Thought leader">thought leader</a> Hal Foster; American architects <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_C._Portman,_Jr." title="John C. Portman, Jr.">John C. Portman, Jr.</a>; Finnish-American architect and industrial designer <a href="/wiki/Eero_Saarinen" title="Eero Saarinen">Eero Saarinen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archigram" title="Archigram">Archigram</a>, a British avant-garde architectural group (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Cook" title="Peter Cook">Peter Cook</a>, Warren Chalk, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Herron" title="Ron Herron">Ron Herron</a>, Dennis Crompton, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Webb_(architect)" title="Michael Webb (architect)">Michael Webb</a> and David Greene) based at the <a href="/wiki/Architectural_Association_School_of_Architecture" title="Architectural Association School of Architecture">Architectural Association</a>, London; American avant-garde architectural group ArchiGO, centered around the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Institute_of_Technology" title="Illinois Institute of Technology">Illinois Institute of Technology</a>; Danish architect <a href="/wiki/Henning_Larsen" title="Henning Larsen">Henning Larsen</a>; Czech architect <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kaplick%C3%BD" title="Jan Kaplický">Jan Kaplický</a>; Swedish artist Simon Stålenhag; Italian <a href="/wiki/Light_art" title="Light art">light sculptor</a> Marco Lodola; American concept artist <a href="/wiki/Syd_Mead" title="Syd Mead">Syd Mead</a>; American theatre screenwriter Greg Allen and Russian poets Andrei Voznesensky, Serge Segay and Rea Nikonova.</p>
<p>Although it was never built, the Fun Palace (1961) interpreted by architect <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Price" title="Cedric Price">Cedric Price</a> as a "giant neo-futurist machine" influenced other architects, notably <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rogers" title="Richard Rogers">Richard Rogers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Centre_Georges_Pompidou" title="Centre Georges Pompidou">Pompidou Centre</a> extended many of Price's ideas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>Neo-futurism was relaunched in 2007 after the dissemination of "The Neo-Futuristic City <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">Manifesto</a>" included in the candidature presented to the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_International_Expositions" title="Bureau of International Expositions">Bureau of International Expositions</a> (BIE) and written by innovation designer <a href="/wiki/Vito_Di_Bari" title="Vito Di Bari">Vito Di Bari</a>, a former executive director at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization</a> (UNESCO), to outline his vision for the city of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> at the time of the Universal <a href="/wiki/Expo_2015" title="Expo 2015">Expo 2015</a>. Di Bari defined his neo-futuristic vision as the "cross-pollination of art, cutting edge technologies and ethical values combined to create a pervasively higher quality of life"; he referenced the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development#Culture" title="Sustainable development">Fourth Pillar of Sustainable Development Theory</a> and reported that the name had been inspired by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> report <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Common_Future" title="Our Common Future">Our Common Future</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Cohen" title="Jean-Louis Cohen">Jean-Louis Cohen</a> has defined neo-futurism as "a corollary to technology, being the structures built today byproducts of new materials to create previously impossible forms." Etan J. Ilfeld wrote that in the contemporary neo-futurist aesthetics "the machine becomes an integral element of the creative process itself, and generates the emergence of artistic modes that would have been impossible prior to computer technology." Reyner Banham's definition of "une architecture autre" is a call for an architecture that technologically overcomes all previous architectures but possessing an expressive form, as Banham stated about neo-futuristic "Archigram’s Plug-in Computerized City, form does not have to follow function into oblivion."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span></h2>
<p>The relaunch of neo-futurism in the 21st century has been creatively inspired by the Iraqi-British <a href="/wiki/Pritzker_Architecture_Prize" title="Pritzker Architecture Prize">Pritzker Architecture Prize</a>-winning architect <a href="/wiki/Zaha_Hadid" title="Zaha Hadid">Zaha Hadid</a>, Spanish architect <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a>  and by Vito DiBari.</p>
<p>Neo-Futurist architects, designers and artists are French architect <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Denis_Laming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Denis Laming (page does not exist)">Denis Laming</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Laming" title="fr:Denis Laming">fr</a>)</span>; American artists Josh Hadar, Erin Sparler, Marlow Rodale, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Studio-X&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Studio-X (page does not exist)">Studio-X</a> Lawrie Masson; Panayiotis Terzis, and Miguel Ovalle; urban-noise artist Joseph Young; French designer Patrick Jouin British artist <a class="external text" href="http://www.oliviapeake.com" rel="nofollow">Olivia Peake</a>; Japanese designer Yuima Nakazato, Swedish artist Simon Stålenhag, Italian artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Luca_Bestetti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Luca Bestetti (page does not exist)">Luca Bestetti</a> and Greek artist Charis Tsevis.
