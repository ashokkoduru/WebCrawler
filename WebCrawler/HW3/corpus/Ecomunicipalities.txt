<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p>An <b>eco-municipality</b> or <b>eco-town</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> area that has adopted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> values in its <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a>. The development of eco-municipalities stems from changing systems in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, where more than seventy municipal governments have accepted varying principles of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> in their operations as well as community-wide decision making processes. The purpose of these policies is to increase the overall sustainability of the community.</p>
<p>The distinction between an eco-municipality and other <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> projects (such as <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a>) is the focus on community involvement and social transformation in a public agency as well as the use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">holistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Systems_thinking" title="Systems thinking">systems</a> approach. An eco-municipality is one that recognizes that issues of sustainability are key to all decisions made by government. </p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>In 1983 the <a href="/wiki/%C3%96vertorne%C3%A5" title="Övertorneå">Övertorneå</a> community of Sweden first adopted an Eco-municipality framework followed by a formal organization in 1995 (SEKOM).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Framework">Framework</span></h2>
<p>In becoming an eco-municipality, cities or towns typically adopt a resolution, based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_Step" title="Natural Step">Natural Step framework</a> (or Framework for Strategic Sustainable Development (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=FSSD&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="FSSD (page does not exist)">FSSD</a>)), which sets the following objectives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reduce dependence upon fossil fuels.</li>
<li>Reduce dependence upon synthetic chemicals.</li>
<li>Reduce encroachment upon nature.</li>
<li>Better meet human needs fairly and efficiently.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipalities_adopting_framework">Municipalities adopting framework</span></h2>
<p>Communities in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and Africa ranging in size from villages of 300 to cities of 700,000 have become eco-municipalities. In Sweden, over sixty municipalities have officially become eco-municipalities. They have formed a national association of eco-municipalities to assist one another and work to influence national policy. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whistler,_BC" title="Whistler, BC">Whistler, BC</a>, was awarded first place in a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>-endorsed international competition for sustainable communities. Its long-term sustainability plan, Whistler 2020, is based on the Natural Step framework.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, there is a growing eco-municipality movement which began in the Chequamegon Bay region. As of November 2007, twelve local communities had formally adopted eco-municipality resolutions. The resolutions state the community's intention to become an eco-municipality, endorsing the Natural Step sustainability principles and framework as a guide.</p>
