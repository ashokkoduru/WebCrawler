<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p><b>Earth science</b> or <b>geoscience</b> is an all-embracing term for the fields of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. It can be considered to be a branch of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>, but with a much older history. There are both <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> approaches to Earth sciences. The Earth sciences can include the study of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, and the large-scale structure of the Earth's interior, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Typically, Earth scientists use tools from <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">chronology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> to build a quantitative understanding of how the <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_science" title="Earth system science">Earth system</a> works and evolves.</p>

<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Branches_of_science" title="Branches of science">fields of science</a> are generally categorized within the Earth sciences:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physical geography</a>, covers aspects of <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">soil study</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocky</a> parts of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>) and its historic development. Major subdisciplines are <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_geology" title="Engineering geology">engineering geology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sedimentology</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a> investigate the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">shape of the Earth</a>, its reaction to forces and its <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_field" title="Gravity field">gravity fields</a>. Geophysicists explore the <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">Earth's core</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismic</a> activity of the lithosphere. Geophysics is commonly used to supplement the work of geologists in developing a comprehensive understanding of crustal geology, particularly in mineral and petroleum exploration. See <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_survey" title="Geophysical survey">geophysical survey</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_science" title="Soil science">Soil science</a> covers the outermost layer of the Earth's crust that is subject to soil formation processes (or <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>). Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Edaphology" title="Edaphology">edaphology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">pedology</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> covers the interactions between the <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>, with their natural environment. This field of study differentiates the study of the Earth, from the study of other planets in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>; the Earth being the only planet teeming with life.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">Hydrology</a> (includes <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a>) describe the marine and freshwater domains of the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">watery</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>). Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Hydrogeology" title="Hydrogeology">hydrogeology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">physical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oceanography" title="Chemical oceanography">chemical</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">biological</a> oceanography.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">Glaciology</a> covers the <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">icy</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">Atmospheric sciences</a> cover the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> parts of the Earth (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>) between the surface and the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> (about 1000 km). Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">atmospheric physics</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_interior">Earth's interior</span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> ranges, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological phenomena</a> that can be explained in terms of <a href="/wiki/Energy_transformation" title="Energy transformation">energy transformations</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>.</p>
<p>Beneath Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> lies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> which is heated by the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_elements" title="Heavy elements">heavy elements</a>. The mantle is not quite solid and consists of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> which is in a state of semi-perpetual <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convection" title="wiktionary:convection">convection</a>. This convection process causes the lithospheric plates to move, albeit slowly. The resulting process is known as <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.</p>
<p>Plate tectonics might be thought of as the process by which the Earth is resurfaced. Through a process called <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a>, new crust is created by the flow of magma from underneath the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> to the surface, through fissures, where it cools and solidifies. Through a process called <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a>, oceanic crust is pushed underground — beneath the rest of the lithosphere—where it comes into contact with magma and melts—rejoining the mantle from which it originally came.</p>
<p>Areas of the crust where new crust is created are called <i>divergent boundaries</i>, those where it is brought back into the Earth are <i>convergent boundaries</i> and those where plates slide past each other, but no new lithospheric material is created or destroyed, are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform</a> (or conservative) boundaries <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</a> result from the movement of the lithospheric plates, and they often occur near convergent boundaries where parts of the crust are forced into the Earth as part of subduction.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes" title="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</a> result primarily from the melting of subducted crust material. Crust material that is forced into the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> melts, and some portion of the melted material becomes light enough to rise to the surface—giving birth to volcanoes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_electromagnetic_field">Earth's electromagnetic field</span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a> is a magnet that is created by a current that flows around a soft <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> core. Earth has a solid iron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_core" title="Earth core">inner core</a> surrounded by semi-liquid materials of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer core</a> that move in continuous currents around the inner core; therefore, the Earth is an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a>. This is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo theory</a> of Earth's magnetism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Atmosphere of Earth</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> are the five layers which make up Earth's atmosphere. In all, the atmosphere is made up of about 78.0% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 20.9% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and 0.92% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. 75% of the gases in the atmosphere are located within the troposphere, the bottom-most layer. The remaining one percent of the atmosphere (all but the nitrogen, oxygen, and argon) contains small amounts of other gases including CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapors. Water vapors and CO<sub>2</sub> allow the Earth's atmosphere to catch and hold the Sun's energy through a phenomenon called the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>. This allows Earth's surface to be warm enough to have liquid water and support life.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> created by the internal motions of the core produces the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> which protects the Earth's atmosphere from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. As the Earth is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_the_earth" title="Age of the earth">4.5 billion</a> years old, it would have lost its atmosphere by now if there were no protective magnetosphere.</p>
<p>In addition to storing heat, the atmosphere also protects living organisms by shielding the Earth's surface from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. Note that the level of protection is high enough to prevent <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> from destroying all life on Earth, yet low enough to aid the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutations" title="Mutations">mutations</a> that have an important role in pushing forward <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">diversity</a> in the biosphere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span></h2>
<p>Methodologies vary depending on the nature of the subjects being studied. Studies typically fall into one of three categories: observational, experimental, or theoretical. Earth scientists often conduct sophisticated computer analysis or go to many of the world's most exotic locations to study Earth phenomena (e.g. Antarctica or hot spot island chains).</p>
<p>A foundational idea within the study Earth science is the notion of uniformitarianism. Uniformitarianism dictates that "ancient geologic features are interpreted by understanding active processes that are readily observed." In other words, any geologic processes at work in the present have operated in the same ways throughout geologic time. This enables those who study Earth's history to apply knowledge of how Earth processes operate in the present to gain insight into how the planet has evolved and changed throughout deep history.</p>

<p>Earth science generally recognizes four spheres, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>; these correspond to <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. Also included by some are the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>) as a distinct portion of the hydrosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>) as an active and intermixed sphere.</p>
