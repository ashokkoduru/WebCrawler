<p><b>Nature</b>, in the broadest sense, is the natural, physical, or material world or universe. "Nature" can refer to the <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomena</a> of the physical world, and also to life in general. The study of nature is a large part of science. Although humans are part of nature, human activity is often understood as a separate category from other natural phenomena.</p>
<p>The word <i>nature</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>natura</i>, or "essential qualities, innate disposition", and in ancient times, literally meant "birth". <i>Natura</i> is a Latin translation of the Greek word <i><a href="/wiki/Physis" title="Physis">physis</a></i> (φύσις), which originally related to the intrinsic characteristics that plants, animals, and other features of the world develop of their own accord. The concept of nature as a whole, the physical <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, is one of several expansions of the original notion; it began with certain core applications of the word φύσις by <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">pre-Socratic</a> philosophers, and has steadily gained currency ever since. This usage continued during the advent of modern <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> in the last several centuries.</p>
<p>Within the various uses of the word today, "nature" often refers to geology and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>. Nature can refer to the general realm of living plants and animals, and in some cases to the processes associated with inanimate objects – the way that particular types of things exist and change of their own accord, such as the weather and geology of the Earth. It is often taken to mean the "<a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a>" or <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>–wild animals, rocks, forest, and in general those things that have not been substantially altered by human intervention, or which persist despite human intervention. For example, manufactured objects and human interaction generally are not considered part of nature, unless qualified as, for example, "human nature" or "the whole of nature". This more traditional concept of natural things which can still be found today implies a distinction between the natural and the artificial, with the artificial being understood as that which has been brought into being by a human <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> or a human <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a>. Depending on the particular context, the term "natural" might also be distinguished from the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unnatural" title="wikt:unnatural">unnatural</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth science</a></div>

<p>Earth is the only <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> known to support life, and its natural features are the subject of many fields of scientific research. Within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, it is third closest to the sun; it is the largest <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planet</a> and the fifth largest overall. Its most prominent climatic features are its two large polar regions, two relatively narrow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> zones, and a wide <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> tropical to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> region. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation</a> varies widely with location, from several metres of water per year to less than a millimetre. 71 percent of the Earth's surface is covered by salt-water oceans. The remainder consists of continents and islands, with most of the inhabited land in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.</p>
<p>Earth has evolved through geological and biological processes that have left traces of the original conditions. The <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">outer surface</a> is divided into several gradually migrating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a>. The interior remains active, with a thick layer of plastic <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and an iron-filled core that generates a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. This iron core is composed of a solid inner phase, and a fluid outer phase. Convective motion in the core generates electric currents through dynamo action, and these, in turn, generate the geomagnetic field.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> conditions have been significantly altered from the original conditions by the presence of life-forms, which create an ecological balance that stabilizes the surface conditions. Despite the wide regional variations in climate by <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and other geographic factors, the long-term average global climate is quite stable during interglacial periods, and variations of a degree or two of average global temperature have historically had major effects on the ecological balance, and on the actual geography of the Earth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a></div>
<p>Geology is the science and study of the solid and liquid matter that constitutes the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. The field of geology encompasses the study of the composition, <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical properties</a>, dynamics, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">history</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth_materials" title="Earth materials">Earth materials</a>, and the processes by which they are formed, moved, and changed. The field is a major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" title="Academic discipline">academic discipline</a>, and is also important for <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mineral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geology" title="Petroleum geology">hydrocarbon</a> extraction, knowledge about and mitigation of <a href="/wiki/Natural_hazard" title="Natural hazard">natural hazards</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Geotechnical_engineering" title="Geotechnical engineering">Geotechnical engineering</a> fields, and understanding <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">past climates</a> and environments.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_evolution">Geological evolution</span></h4>

<p>The geology of an area evolves through time as rock units are deposited and inserted and deformational processes change their shapes and locations.</p>
<p>Rock units are first emplaced either by <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposition</a> onto the surface or intrude into the <a href="/wiki/Country_rock_(geology)" title="Country rock (geology)">overlying rock</a>. Deposition can occur when <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> settle onto the surface of the Earth and later <a href="/wiki/Lithification" title="Lithification">lithify</a> into <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>, or when as <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic material</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> flows, blanket the surface. Igneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">intrusions</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laccolith" title="Laccolith">laccoliths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a>, push upwards into the overlying rock, and crystallize as they intrude.</p>
<p>After the initial sequence of rocks has been deposited, the rock units can be <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">deformed</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphosed</a>. Deformation typically occurs as a result of horizontal shortening, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extension_(geology)" title="Extension (geology)">horizontal extension</a>, or side-to-side (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strike-slip" title="Strike-slip">strike-slip</a>) motion. These structural regimes broadly relate to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent boundaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">divergent boundaries</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transform_boundary" title="Transform boundary">transform boundaries</a>, respectively, between <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plates</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_perspective">Historical perspective</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">History of the Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Evolution</a></div>

<p>Earth is estimated to have formed 4.54 billion years ago from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>. The moon formed roughly 20 million years later. Initially molten, the outer layer of the Earth cooled, resulting in the solid crust. Outgassing and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> activity produced the primordial atmosphere. Condensing <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, most or all of which came from <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> delivered by <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth" title="Origin of water on Earth">produced the oceans</a> and other water sources. The highly energetic chemistry is believed to have produced a self-replicating molecule around 4 billion years ago.</p>

<p>Continents formed, then broke up and reformed as the surface of Earth reshaped over hundreds of millions of years, occasionally combining to make a <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a>. Roughly 750 million years ago, the earliest known supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a>, began to break apart. The continents later recombined to form <a href="/wiki/Pannotia" title="Pannotia">Pannotia</a> which broke apart about 540 million years ago, then finally <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, which broke apart about 180 million years ago.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a> era covered much of the Earth in <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and ice sheets. This hypothesis has been termed the "<a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a>", and it is of particular interest as it precedes the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a> in which multicellular life forms began to proliferate about 530–540 million years ago.</p>
<p>Since the Cambrian explosion there have been five distinctly identifiable <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinctions</a>. The last mass extinction occurred some 66 million years ago, when a meteorite collision probably triggered the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">non-avian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> and other large reptiles, but spared small animals such as <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. Over the past 66 million years, mammalian life diversified.</p>
<p>Several million years ago, a species of small African <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">ape</a> gained the ability to stand upright. The subsequent advent of human life, and the development of agriculture and further <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> allowed humans to affect the Earth more rapidly than any previous life form, affecting both the nature and quantity of other organisms as well as global climate. By comparison, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a>, produced by the proliferation of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Siderian" title="Siderian">Siderian</a> period, required about 300 million years to culminate.</p>
<p>The present era is classified as part of a mass <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a> event, the fastest ever to have occurred. Some, such as <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, predict that human destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> could cause the extinction of one-half of all species in the next 100 years. The extent of the current extinction event is still being researched, debated and calculated by biologists.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere.2C_climate.2C_and_weather">Atmosphere, climate, and weather</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Atmosphere of Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">Climate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">Weather</a></div>
<p>The Earth's atmosphere is a key factor in sustaining the <a href="/wiki/Nature#Ecosystems" title="Nature">ecosystem</a>. The thin layer of gases that envelops the Earth is held in place by gravity. Air is mostly <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, with much smaller amounts of carbon dioxide, argon, etc. The atmospheric pressure declines steadily with altitude. The <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> plays an important role in depleting the amount of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV) radiation that reaches the surface. As <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is readily damaged by UV light, this serves to protect life at the surface. The atmosphere also retains heat during the night, thereby reducing the daily temperature extremes.</p>
<p>Terrestrial weather occurs almost exclusively in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">lower part of the atmosphere</a>, and serves as a convective system for redistributing heat. <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">Ocean currents</a> are another important factor in determining climate, particularly the major underwater <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> which distributes heat energy from the equatorial oceans to the polar regions. These currents help to moderate the differences in temperature between winter and summer in the temperate zones. Also, without the redistributions of heat energy by the ocean currents and atmosphere, the tropics would be much hotter, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a> much colder.</p>

<p>Weather can have both beneficial and harmful effects. Extremes in weather, such as <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, can expend large amounts of energy along their paths, and produce devastation. Surface vegetation has evolved a dependence on the seasonal variation of the weather, and sudden changes lasting only a few years can have a dramatic effect, both on the vegetation and on the animals which depend on its growth for their food.</p>
<p>Climate is a measure of the long-term trends in the weather. Various factors are known to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">influence the climate</a>, including ocean currents, surface <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, variations in the solar luminosity, and changes to the Earth's orbit. Based on historical records, the Earth is known to have undergone drastic climate changes in the past, including <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>.</p>

<p>The climate of a region depends on a number of factors, especially <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. A latitudinal band of the surface with similar climatic attributes forms a climate region. There are a number of such regions, ranging from the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> at the equator to the <a href="/wiki/Polar_climate" title="Polar climate">polar climate</a> in the northern and southern extremes. Weather is also influenced by the seasons, which result from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">axis</a> being <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">tilted</a> relative to its <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">orbital plane</a>. Thus, at any given time during the summer or winter, one part of the Earth is more directly exposed to the rays of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. This exposure alternates as the Earth revolves in its orbit. At any given time, regardless of season, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern</a> hemispheres experience opposite seasons.</p>
<p>Weather is a <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic system</a> that is readily modified by small changes to the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, so accurate <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">weather forecasting</a> is limited to only a few days. Overall, two things are happening worldwide: (1) temperature is increasing on the average; and (2) regional climates have been undergoing noticeable changes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_on_Earth">Water on Earth</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a></div>
<p><b>Water</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> that is composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and is vital for all known forms of life. In typical usage, <i>water</i> refers only to its liquid form or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_of_matter" title="States of matter">state</a>, but the substance also has a solid state, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaseous" title="Gaseous">gaseous</a> state, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>. Water covers 71% of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface. On Earth, it is found mostly in oceans and other large water bodies, with 1.6% of water below ground in <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and 0.001% in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">air</a> as <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a>, clouds, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. Oceans hold 97% of surface water, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, and polar <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a> 2.4%, and other land surface water such as rivers, lakes, and ponds 0.6%. Additionally, a minute amount of the Earth's water is contained within biological bodies and manufactured products.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span></h3>


<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a></div>
<p>An ocean is a major body of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saline water</a>, and a principal component of the hydrosphere. Approximately 71% of the Earth's surface (an area of some 361 million square kilometers) is covered by ocean, a <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">continuous body of water</a> that is customarily divided into several principal oceans and smaller seas. More than half of this area is over 3,000 meters (9,800 feet) deep. Average oceanic <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> is around 35 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_thousand" title="Parts per thousand">parts per thousand</a> (ppt) (3.5%), and nearly all seawater has a salinity in the range of 30 to 38 ppt. Though generally recognized as several 'separate' oceans, these waters comprise one global, interconnected body of salt water often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a> or global ocean. This concept of a global ocean as a continuous body of water with relatively free interchange among its parts is of fundamental importance to <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>.</p>
<p>The major oceanic divisions are defined in part by the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a>, and other criteria: these divisions are (in descending order of size) the Pacific Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean, the Indian Ocean, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>, and the Arctic Ocean. Smaller regions of the oceans are called seas, <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">gulfs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bays</a> and other names. There are also <a href="/wiki/Salt_lake" title="Salt lake">salt lakes</a>, which are smaller bodies of landlocked saltwater that are not interconnected with the World Ocean. Two notable examples of salt lakes are the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes">Lakes</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">Lake</a></div>
<p>A lake (from Latin <i>lacus</i>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrain_feature" title="Terrain feature">terrain feature</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_feature" title="Physical feature">physical feature</a>), a body of liquid on the surface of a world that is localized to the bottom of <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">basin</a> (another type of landform or terrain feature; that is, it is not global) and moves slowly if it moves at all. On Earth, a body of water is considered a lake when it is inland, not part of the ocean, is larger and deeper than a pond, and is fed by a river. The only world other than Earth known to harbor lakes is <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, Saturn's largest moon, which has lakes of <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, most likely mixed with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. It is not known if Titan's lakes are fed by rivers, though Titan's surface is carved by numerous river beds. Natural lakes on Earth are generally found in mountainous areas, <a href="/wiki/Rift_zone" title="Rift zone">rift zones</a>, and areas with ongoing or recent <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. Other lakes are found in <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a> or along the courses of mature rivers. In some parts of the world, there are many lakes because of chaotic drainage patterns left over from the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. All lakes are temporary over geologic time scales, as they will slowly fill in with sediments or spill out of the basin containing them.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ponds">Ponds</span></h4>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">Pond</a></div>
<p>A <b>pond</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_stagnation" title="Water stagnation">standing water</a>, either natural or man-made, that is usually smaller than a lake. A wide variety of man-made bodies of water are classified as ponds, including <a href="/wiki/Water_garden" title="Water garden">water gardens</a> designed for aesthetic ornamentation, <a href="/wiki/Fish_pond" title="Fish pond">fish ponds</a> designed for commercial fish breeding, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_pond" title="Solar pond">solar ponds</a> designed to store thermal energy. Ponds and lakes are distinguished from streams via <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">current</a> speed. While currents in streams are easily observed, ponds and lakes possess thermally driven micro-currents and moderate wind driven currents. These features distinguish a pond from many other aquatic terrain features, such as <a href="/wiki/Stream_pool" title="Stream pool">stream pools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tide_pool" title="Tide pool">tide pools</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers">Rivers</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">River</a></div>
<p>A river is a natural <a href="/wiki/Watercourse" title="Watercourse">watercourse</a>, usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>, flowing toward an ocean, a lake, a sea or another river. In a few cases, a river simply flows into the ground or dries up completely before reaching another body of water. Small rivers may also be called by several other names, including stream, creek, brook, rivulet, and rill; there is no general rule that defines what can be called a river. Many names for small rivers are specific to geographic location; one example is <i>Burn</i> in Scotland and North-east England. Sometimes a river is said to be larger than a creek, but this is not always the case, due to vagueness in the language. A river is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>. Water within a river is generally collected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> through <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> recharge, <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">springs</a>, and the release of stored water in natural ice and snowpacks (i.e., from <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Streams">Streams</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">Stream</a></div>
<p>A stream is a flowing body of water with a <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">current</a>, confined within a <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">bed</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_bank" title="Stream bank">stream banks</a>. In the United States, a stream is classified as a watercourse less than 60 feet (18 metres) wide. Streams are important as conduits in the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, instruments in <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a>, and they serve as corridors for fish and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> migration. The biological <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> in the immediate vicinity of a stream is called a <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">riparian zone</a>. Given the status of the ongoing <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a>, streams play an important <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">corridor</a> role in connecting <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">fragmented habitats</a> and thus in conserving <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. The study of streams and waterways in general involves many branches of inter-disciplinary natural science and engineering, including <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">fluvial geomorphology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecology" title="Aquatic ecology">aquatic ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">fish biology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian" title="Riparian">riparian ecology</a>, and others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystems">Ecosystems</span></h2>



<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</a></div>
<p>Ecosystems are composed of a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abiotic" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a> components that function in an interrelated way. The structure and composition is determined by various environmental factors that are interrelated. Variations of these factors will initiate dynamic modifications to the ecosystem. Some of the more important components are: <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, atmosphere, radiation from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, water, and living organisms.</p>

<p>Central to the ecosystem concept is the idea that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_organism" title="Living organism">living organisms</a> interact with every other element in their local <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. Eugene Odum, a founder of ecology, stated: "Any unit that includes all of the organisms (ie: the "community") in a given area interacting with the physical environment so that a flow of energy leads to clearly defined trophic structure, biotic diversity, and material cycles (i.e.: exchange of materials between living and nonliving parts) within the system is an ecosystem." Within the ecosystem, species are connected and dependent upon one another in the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>, and exchange energy and <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> between themselves as well as with their environment. The human ecosystem concept is grounded in the deconstruction of the human/nature <a href="/wiki/Dichotomy" title="Dichotomy">dichotomy</a> and the premise that all species are ecologically integrated with each other, as well as with the abiotic constituents of their <a href="/wiki/Biotope" title="Biotope">biotope</a>.</p>
<p>A smaller unit of size is called a <a href="/wiki/Microecosystem" title="Microecosystem">microecosystem</a>. For example, a microsystem can be a stone and all the life under it. A <i>macroecosystem</i> might involve a whole <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>, with its <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wilderness">Wilderness</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">Wilderness</a></div>
<p><b>Wilderness</b> is generally defined as areas that have not been significantly modified by human activity. Wilderness areas can be found in preserves, estates, farms, conservation preserves, ranches, <a href="/wiki/Category:National_forests" title="Category:National forests">national forests</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a>, and even in urban areas along rivers, gulches, or otherwise undeveloped areas. Wilderness areas and protected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parks" title="Parks">parks</a> are considered important for the survival of certain <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, ecological studies, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">conservation</a>, solitude, and <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>. Some nature writers believe wilderness areas are vital for the human spirit and creativity, and some Ecologists consider wilderness areas to be an integral part of the Earth's self-sustaining natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>). They may also preserve historic <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> traits and that they provide <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> for wild <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fauna_(animals)" title="Fauna (animals)">fauna</a> that may be difficult to recreate in <a href="/wiki/Zoo" title="Zoo">zoos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arboretum" title="Arboretum">arboretums</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratories</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">Biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">Biosphere</a></div>
<p>Although there is no universal agreement on the definition of life, scientists generally accept that the biological manifestation of life is characterized by <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a>, and response to <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>. Life may also be said to be simply the characteristic state of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>.</p>
<p>Properties common to terrestrial organisms (plants, animals, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, and bacteria) are that they are cellular, carbon-and-water-based with complex organization, having a metabolism, a capacity to grow, respond to stimuli, and reproduce. An entity with these properties is generally considered life. However, not every definition of life considers all of these properties to be essential. Human-made <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">analogs of life</a> may also be considered to be life.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> is the part of Earth's outer shell – including land, surface rocks, water, air and the atmosphere – within which life occurs, and which <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biotic</a> processes in turn alter or transform. From the broadest <a href="/wiki/Geophysiology" title="Geophysiology">geophysiological</a> point of view, the biosphere is the global ecological system integrating all living beings and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> (rocks), <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a> (water), and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> (air). The entire Earth contains over 75 billion tons (150 <i>trillion</i> pounds or about 6.8×10 kilograms) of <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> (life), which lives within various environments within the biosphere.</p>
<p>Over nine-tenths of the total biomass on Earth is plant life, on which animal life depends very heavily for its existence. More than 2 million species of plant and animal life have been identified to date, and estimates of the actual number of existing species range from several million to well over 50 million. The number of individual species of life is constantly in some degree of flux, with new species appearing and others ceasing to exist on a continual basis. The total number of species is in rapid decline.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Evolution</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a> on Earth is not well understood, but it is known to have occurred at least 3.5 billion years ago, during the <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">hadean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">archean</a> eons on a <a href="/wiki/Early_Earth" title="Early Earth">primordial Earth</a> that had a substantially different environment than is found at present. These life forms possessed the basic traits of self-replication and inheritable traits. Once life had appeared, the process of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> resulted in the development of ever-more diverse life forms.</p>
<p>Species that were unable to adapt to the changing environment and competition from other life forms became extinct. However, the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> record retains evidence of many of these older species. Current fossil and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> evidence shows that all existing species can trace a continual ancestry back to the first primitive life forms.</p>
<p>The advent of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> in very basic forms of plant life worldwide allowed the sun's energy to be harvested to create conditions allowing for more complex life. The resultant <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> accumulated in the atmosphere and gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. The incorporation of smaller cells within larger ones resulted in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory" title="Endosymbiotic theory">development of yet more complex cells</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a>. Cells within colonies became increasingly specialized, resulting in true multicellular organisms. With the ozone layer absorbing harmful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a>, life colonized the surface of Earth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbes">Microbes</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">Microbe</a></div>
<p>The first form of life to develop on the Earth were microbes, and they remained the only form of life until about a billion years ago when multi-cellular organisms began to appear. Microorganisms are single-celled organisms that are generally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">microscopic</a>, and smaller than the human eye can see. They include Bacteria, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">Fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protista" title="Protista">Protista</a>.</p>
<p>These life forms are found in almost every location on the Earth where there is liquid water, including in the Earth's interior. Their reproduction is both rapid and profuse. The combination of a high mutation rate and a <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a> ability makes them highly adaptable, and able to survive in new environments, including <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. They form an essential part of the planetary ecosystem. However, some microorganisms are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogenic" title="Pathogenic">pathogenic</a> and can post health risk to other organisms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants_and_animals">Plants and animals</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animal</a></div>


<p>Originally <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> divided all living things between plants, which generally do not move fast enough for humans to notice, and animals. In <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>' system, these became the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdoms</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetabilia" title="Vegetabilia">Vegetabilia</a> (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantae" title="Plantae">Plantae</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animalia</a>. Since then, it has become clear that the Plantae as originally defined included several unrelated groups, and the <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> and several groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a> were removed to new kingdoms. However, these are still often considered plants in many contexts. Bacterial life is sometimes included in flora, and some classifications use the term <i>bacterial flora</i> separately from <i>plant flora</i>.</p>
<p>Among the many ways of classifying plants are by regional <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">floras</a>, which, depending on the purpose of study, can also include <i>fossil flora</i>, remnants<br/>
of plant life from a previous era. People in many regions and countries take great pride in their individual arrays of characteristic flora, which can vary widely across the globe due to differences in climate and <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a>.</p>
<p>Regional floras commonly are divided into categories such as <i>native flora</i> and <i>agricultural and garden flora</i>, the lastly mentioned of which are intentionally grown and cultivated. Some types of "native flora" actually have been introduced centuries ago by people migrating from one region or continent to another, and become an integral part of the native, or natural flora of the place to which they were introduced. This is an example of how human interaction with nature can blur the boundary of what is considered nature.</p>
<p>Another category of plant has historically been carved out for <i>weeds</i>. Though the term has fallen into disfavor among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanists</a> as a formal way to categorize "useless" plants, the informal use of the word "weeds" to describe those plants that are deemed worthy of elimination is illustrative of the general tendency of people and societies to seek to alter or shape the course of nature. Similarly, animals are often categorized in ways such as <i>domestic</i>, <i>farm animals</i>, <i>wild animals</i>, <i>pests</i>, etc. according to their relationship to human life.</p>
<p>Animals as a category have several characteristics that generally set them apart from other living things. Animals are <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> and usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicellular" title="Multicellular">multicellular</a> (although see <a href="/wiki/Myxozoa" title="Myxozoa">Myxozoa</a>), which separates them from bacteria, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, and most <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophic</a>, generally digesting food in an internal chamber, which separates them from plants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a>. They are also distinguished from plants, algae, and <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> by lacking <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a>.</p>
<p>With a few exceptions, most notably the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a> (Phylum Porifera), animals have bodies differentiated into separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissues</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a>, which are able to contract and control locomotion, and a <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>, which sends and processes signals. There is also typically an internal <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestive</a> chamber. The eukaryotic cells possessed by all animals are surrounded by a characteristic extracellular matrix composed of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> and elastic <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a>. This may be calcified to form structures like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_shell" title="Animal shell">shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spicule_(sponge)" title="Spicule (sponge)">spicules</a>, a framework upon which cells can move about and be reorganized during development and maturation, and which supports the complex anatomy required for mobility.</p>

<p>Although humans comprise only a minuscule proportion of the total living biomass on Earth, the human effect on nature is disproportionately large. Because of the extent of human influence, the boundaries between what humans regard as nature and "made environments" is not clear cut except at the extremes. Even at the extremes, the amount of natural environment that is free of discernible human influence is diminishing at an increasingly rapid pace.</p>
<p>The development of technology by the human race has allowed the greater exploitation of natural resources and has helped to alleviate some of the risk from natural hazards. In spite of this progress, however, the fate of human <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> remains closely linked to changes in the environment. There exists a highly complex feedback loop between the use of advanced technology and changes to the environment that are only slowly becoming understood. Man-made threats to the Earth's natural environment include pollution, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, and disasters such as oil spills. Humans have contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of many plants and animals.</p>
<p>Humans employ nature for both leisure and economic activities. The acquisition of natural resources for industrial use remains the primary component of the world's economic system. Some activities, such as hunting and fishing, are used for both sustenance and leisure, often by different people. <a href="/wiki/Agriculture#History" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> was first adopted around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9th_millennium_BCE" title="9th millennium BCE">9th millennium BCE</a>. Ranging from food production to energy, nature influences economic wealth.</p>
<p>Although early humans gathered uncultivated plant materials for food and employed the medicinal properties of vegetation for healing, most modern human use of plants is through agriculture. The clearance of large tracts of land for crop growth has led to a significant reduction in the amount available of forestation and wetlands, resulting in the loss of habitat for many plant and animal species as well as increased erosion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aesthetics_and_beauty">Aesthetics and beauty</span></h3>

<p>Beauty in nature has historically been a prevalent theme in art and books, filling large sections of libraries and bookstores. That nature has been depicted and celebrated by so much art, photography, poetry, and other literature shows the strength with which many people associate nature and beauty. Reasons why this association exists, and what the association consists of, are studied by the branch of philosophy called <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>. Beyond certain basic characteristics that many philosophers agree about to explain what is seen as beautiful, the opinions are virtually endless. Nature and wildness have been important subjects in various eras of world history. An early tradition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_art" title="Landscape art">landscape art</a> began in China during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty_art" title="Tang Dynasty art">Tang Dynasty</a> (618–907). The tradition of representing nature <i>as it is</i> became one of the aims of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">Chinese painting</a> and was a significant influence in Asian art.</p>
<p>Although natural wonders are celebrated in the <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Book of Job</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> portrayals in art became more prevalent in the 1800s, especially in the works of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romantic_movement" title="Romantic movement">Romantic movement</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> artists <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">John Constable</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a> turned their attention to capturing the beauty of the natural world in their paintings. Before that, paintings had been primarily of religious scenes or of human beings. <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a>'s poetry described the wonder of the natural world, which had formerly been viewed as a threatening place. Increasingly the valuing of nature became an aspect of Western culture. This artistic movement also coincided with the <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">Transcendentalist movement</a> in the Western world. A common classical idea of beautiful art involves the word <a href="/wiki/Mimesis" title="Mimesis">mimesis</a>, the imitation of nature. Also in the realm of ideas about beauty in nature is that the perfect is implied through perfect mathematical <a href="/wiki/Substantial_form" title="Substantial form">forms</a> and more generally by <a href="/wiki/Patterns_in_nature" title="Patterns in nature">patterns in nature</a>. As David Rothenburg writes, "The beautiful is the root of science and the goal of art, the highest possibility that humanity can ever hope to see".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Matter_and_energy">Matter and energy</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">Matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a></div>
<p>Some fields of science see nature as matter in motion, obeying certain laws of nature which science seeks to understand. For this reason the most fundamental science is generally understood to be "<a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>" – the name for which is still recognizable as meaning that it is the study of nature.</p>
<p>Matter is commonly defined as the substance of which physical objects are composed. It constitutes the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>. The visible components of the universe are now believed to compose only 4.9 percent of the total mass. The remainder is believed to consist of 26.8 percent <a href="/wiki/Cold_dark_matter" title="Cold dark matter">cold dark matter</a> and 68.3 percent <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>. The exact arrangement of these components is still unknown and is under intensive investigation by physicists.</p>
<p>The behavior of matter and energy throughout the observable universe appears to follow well-defined <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">physical laws</a>. These laws have been employed to produce <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> models that successfully explain the structure and the evolution of the universe we can observe. The mathematical expressions of the laws of physics employ a set of twenty <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">physical constants</a> that appear to be static across the observable universe. The values of these constants have been carefully measured, but the reason for their specific values remains a mystery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beyond_Earth">Beyond Earth</span></h2>


<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">Outer space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">Extraterrestrial life</a></div>
<p>Outer space, also simply called <i>space</i>, refers to the relatively empty regions of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> outside the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheres</a> of celestial bodies. <i>Outer</i> space is used to distinguish it from <a href="/wiki/Airspace" title="Airspace">airspace</a> (and terrestrial locations). There is no discrete boundary between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> and space, as the atmosphere gradually attenuates with increasing altitude. Outer space within the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">interplanetary space</a>, which passes over into <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar space</a> at what is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a>.</p>
<p>Outer space is sparsely filled with several dozen types of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> discovered to date by <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">microwave spectroscopy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">blackbody radiation</a> left over from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_bang" title="Big bang">big bang</a> and the origin of the universe, and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>, which include <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionized</a> <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nuclei</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">subatomic particles</a>. There is also some gas, <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, and small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteors</a>. Additionally, there are signs of human life in outer space today, such as material left over from previous manned and unmanned launches which are a potential hazard to spacecraft. Some of this <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">debris</a> re-enters the atmosphere periodically.</p>
<p>Although the Earth is the only body within the solar system known to support life, evidence suggests that in the distant past the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> possessed bodies of liquid water on the surface. For a brief period in Mars' history, it may have also been capable of forming life. At present though, most of the water remaining on Mars is frozen. If life exists at all on Mars, it is most likely to be located underground where liquid water can still exist.</p>
<p>Conditions on the other terrestrial planets, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, appear to be too harsh to support life as we know it. But it has been conjectured that <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, the fourth-largest moon of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, may possess a sub-surface ocean of liquid water and could potentially host life.</p>
<p>Astronomers have started to discover extrasolar <a href="/wiki/Earth_analog" title="Earth analog">Earth analogs</a> – planets that lie in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> of space surrounding a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>, and therefore could possibly host life as we know it.</p>
