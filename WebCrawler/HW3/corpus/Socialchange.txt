<p><b>Social change</b> refers to an alteration in the <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>. Social change may include changes in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_institution" title="Social institution">social institutions</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_behaviour" title="Social behaviour">social behaviours</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_relations" title="Social relations">social relations</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>Social change may refer to the notion of <a href="/wiki/Social_progress" title="Social progress">social progress</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sociocultural_evolution" title="Sociocultural evolution">sociocultural evolution</a>, the philosophical idea that society moves forward by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialectical" title="Dialectical">dialectical</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolutionary" title="Evolutionary">evolutionary</a> means. It may refer to a paradigmatic change in the socio-economic structure, for instance a shift away from <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> and towards <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>. Accordingly, it may also refer to <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolution</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">Socialist</a> revolution presented in <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, or to other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_movements" title="Social movements">social movements</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">Women's suffrage</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">Civil rights movement</a>. Social change may be driven by cultural, religious, economic, scientific or technological forces. Developmental psychology can play a role in social change.Social change comes about with tangible/intangible resource inputs as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_investment" title="Social investment">social investment</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prominent_theories">Prominent theories</span></h2>

<p>Change comes from two sources. One source is random or unique factors such as climate, weather, or the presence of specific groups of people. Another source is systematic factors. For example, successful development has the same general requirements, such as a stable and flexible government, enough free and available resources, and a diverse social organization of society. On the whole, social change is usually a combination of systematic factors along with some random or unique factors.</p>
<p>There are many theories of social change. Generally, a <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_change" title="Theory of change">theory of change</a> should include elements such as structural aspects of change (like population shifts), processes and mechanisms of social change, and directions of change.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Hegelian</b>: The classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegelian" title="Hegelian">Hegelian</a> dialectic model of change is based on the interaction of opposing forces. Starting from a point of momentary stasis, Thesis countered by Antithesis first yields conflict, then it subsequently results in a new Synthesis.</li>
<li><b>Marxist</b>: <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> presents a dialectical and materialist concept of history; Humankind's history is a fundamental struggle between <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social classes</a>.</li>
<li><b>Kuhnian</b>: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosopher_of_science" title="Philosopher of science">philosopher of science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn" title="Thomas Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a> argues in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Structure_of_Scientific_Revolutions" title="The Structure of Scientific Revolutions">The Structure of Scientific Revolutions</a></i> with respect to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernicus" title="Copernicus">Copernican</a> Revolution that people are likely to continue utilizing an apparently unworkable paradigm until a better paradigm is commonly accepted .</li>
<li><b>Heraclitan</b>: The Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a> used the metaphor of a river to speak of change thus, "On those stepping into rivers staying the same other and other waters flow" (DK22B12). What Heraclitus seems to be suggesting here, later interpretations notwithstanding, is that, in order for the river to remain the river, change must constantly be taking place. Thus one may think of the Heraclitan model as parallel to that of a living organism, which, in order to remain alive, must constantly be changing. A contemporary application of this approach is shown in the social change theory <a href="/wiki/SEED-SCALE" title="SEED-SCALE">SEED-SCALE</a> which builds off of the <a href="/wiki/Complexity_economics" title="Complexity economics">complexity theory</a> subfield of <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">Emergence</a>.</li>
<li><b>Daoist</b>: The Chinese philosophical work <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dao_De_Jing" title="Dao De Jing">Dao De Jing</a></i>, I.8 and II.78 uses the metaphor of water as the ideal agent of change. Water, although soft and yielding, will eventually wear away stone. Change in this model is to be natural, harmonious and steady, albeit imperceptible.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_social_changes">Current social changes</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_demographic_shifts">Global demographic shifts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_the_world" title="Demographics of the world">Demographics of the world</a></div>
<p>One of the most obvious changes currently occurring is the change in the relative global population distribution between countries. In the recent decades, developing countries became a larger proportion of world population, increasing from 68% in 1950 to 82% in 2010, while population of the developed countries has declined from 32% of total world population in 1950 to 18% in 2010. China and India continue to be the largest countries, followed by the US as a distant third. However, population growth throughout the world is slowing. Population growth among developed countries has been slowing since the 1950s, and is now at 0.3% annual growth. Population growth among the less developed countries excluding the least developed has also been slowing, since 1960, and is now at 1.3% annual growth. Population growth among the least developed countries has slowed relatively little, and is the highest at 2.7% annual growth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gendered_patterns_of_work_and_care">Gendered patterns of work and care</span></h3>
<p>In much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">developed world</a>, changes from distinct men's and <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_work" title="Women's work">women's work</a> to more gender equal patterns have been economically important since the mid 20th century. Both men and women are to be considered to be great contributor's to social change world wide.</p>
