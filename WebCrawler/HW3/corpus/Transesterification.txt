<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, <b>transesterification</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the process of exchanging the organic group R″ of an <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> with the organic group R′ of an alcohol. These reactions are often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyzed</a> by the addition of an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> catalyst. The reaction can also be accomplished with the help of enzymes (biocatalysts) particularly lipases (E.C.3.1.1.3).</p>
<div class="center">

</div>
<p>Strong acids catalyse the reaction by donating a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group, thus making it a more potent <a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophile</a>, whereas bases catalyse the reaction by removing a proton from the alcohol, thus making it more <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophilic</a>. Esters with larger alkoxy groups can be made from methyl or ethyl esters in high purity by heating the mixture of ester, acid/base, and large alcohol and evaporating the small alcohol to drive equilibrium.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span></h2>
<p>In the transesterification mechanism, the carbonyl carbon of the starting ester (RCOOR) undergoes nucleophilic attack by the incoming alkoxide (RO) to give a tetrahedral intermediate, which either reverts to the starting material, or proceeds to the transesterified product (RCOOR). The various species exist in equilibrium, and the product distribution depends on the relative energies of the reactant and product.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyester_production">Polyester production</span></h3>
<p>The largest scale application of transesterification is in the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyesters</a>. In this application diesters undergo transesterification with diols to form macromolecules. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> react to form <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, which is evaporated to drive the reaction forward.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methanolysis_and_biodiesel_production">Methanolysis and biodiesel production</span></h3>
<p>The reverse reaction, methanolysis, is also an example of transesterification. This process has been used to recycle polyesters into individual monomers (see <a href="/wiki/Plastic_recycling" title="Plastic recycling">plastic recycling</a>). It is also used to convert fats (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triglycerides" title="Triglycerides">triglycerides</a>) into <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. This conversion was one of the first uses. Transesterified <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>) was used to power heavy-duty vehicles in South Africa before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>It was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patented" title="Patented">patented</a> in the US in the 1950s by <a href="/wiki/Colgate-Palmolive" title="Colgate-Palmolive">Colgate</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Biolipid" title="Biolipid">biolipid</a> transesterification may have been discovered much earlier. In the 1940s, researchers were looking for a method to more readily produce <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, which was used to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosives" title="Explosives">explosives</a> for World War II. Many of the methods used today by producers and home brewers have their origin in the original 1940s research.</p>
<p>Biolipid transesterification has also been recently shown by Japanese researchers to be possible using a super-critical methanol methodology, whereby high temperature, high-pressure vessels are used to physically catalyze the biolipid/methanol reaction into fatty-acid methyl esters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-pressure_transesterification">High-pressure transesterification</span></h3>
<p>Base-catalyzed transesterification is characterized by a negative activation volume (approx. −12 cm/mol) and therefore it proceeds faster under high-pressure conditions. It has been shown that amine-catalyzed alcoholysis of sterically hindered esters (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Protecting_group" title="Protecting group">protecting groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiral_auxiliary" title="Chiral auxiliary">chiral auxiliaries</a>) proceeds rapidly at room temperature under 10 kbar pressure, giving quantitative yields.</p>
