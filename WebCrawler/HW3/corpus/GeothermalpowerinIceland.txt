<p>Due to the geological location of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> (over a rift in continental plates), the high concentration of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> in the area is often an advantage in the generation of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>, the heating and production of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. During <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, pavements near these areas (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reykjavik" title="Reykjavik">Reykjavik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akureyri" title="Akureyri">Akureyri</a>) are heated up.</p>
<p>Five major <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a> power plants exist in Iceland, which produce approximately 26.2% (2010) of the nation's electricity. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a> meets the heating and hot water requirements of approximately 87% of all buildings in Iceland. Apart from geothermal energy, 73.8% of the nation’s electricity is generated by <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydro power</a>, and 0.1% from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.</p>
<p>Consumption of primary geothermal energy in 2004 was 79.7 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petajoule" title="Petajoule">petajoules (PJ)</a>, approximately 53.4% of the total national consumption of <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">primary energy</a>, 149.1 PJ. The corresponding share for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydro power</a> was 17.2%, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> was 26.3%, and coal was 3%. Plans are underway to turn Iceland into a 100% fossil-fuel-free nation in the near future. For example, Iceland's abundant geothermal energy has enabled renewable energy initiatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a>'s carbon dioxide to methanol fuel process. The following are the five largest power stations in Iceland.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Nesjavellir_Geothermal_Power_Station" title="Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station">Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station</a> (120 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Svartsengi_Power_Station" title="Svartsengi Power Station">Svartsengi Power Station</a> (76.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
