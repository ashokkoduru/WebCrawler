<p><b>Nitrogen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>N</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 7. It is the lightest <a href="/wiki/Pnictogen" title="Pnictogen">pnictogen</a>, and at room temperature it is a transparent, odorless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecules" title="Diatomic molecules">diatomic</a> gas. Nitrogen is a common element in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, estimated at about seventh in total abundance in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. Nitrogen is the most abundant element in <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>, making up about 78% of the atmosphere by volume. It is also the most common uncombined element on Earth, since all the more common elements occur primarily as compounds. The element nitrogen was discovered as a separable component of air by Scottish physician <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Rutherford" title="Daniel Rutherford">Daniel Rutherford</a> in 1772.</p>
<p>Many industrially important compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, organic <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanides</a>, contain nitrogen. The extremely strong triple bond in elemental nitrogen (N≡N) dominates nitrogen chemistry, causing difficulty for both organisms and industry in converting the N<sub>2</sub> into useful <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a>, but at the same time causing release of large amounts of often useful energy when the compounds burn, explode, or decay back into nitrogen gas. Synthetically produced ammonia and nitrates are key industrial <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, and fertilizer nitrates are key <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of water systems.</p>
<p>Apart from its use in fertilizers and energy-stores, nitrogen is a constituent of organic compounds as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a> fabric and <a href="/wiki/Cyanoacrylate" title="Cyanoacrylate">cyanoacrylate</a> "super" glue. Nitrogen is a constituent of every major pharmacological drug class, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>. Many drugs are mimics or <a href="/wiki/Prodrug" title="Prodrug">prodrugs</a> of natural nitrogen-containing <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">signal molecules</a>: for example, the organic nitrates <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitroprusside" title="Nitroprusside">nitroprusside</a> control <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> by metabolizing into <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>. Plant <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a> (often defense chemicals) contain nitrogen by definition, and many notable nitrogen-containing drugs, such as <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, are either alkaloids or synthetic mimics that act (as many plant alkaloids do) on receptors of animal <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a> (for example, synthetic <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">amphetamines</a>).</p>
<p>Nitrogen occurs in all organisms, primarily in <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> (and thus <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>), in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> (<a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>) and in the energy transfer molecule <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Composition_of_the_human_body" title="Composition of the human body">human body contains</a> about 3% by mass of nitrogen, the fourth most abundant element in the body after oxygen, carbon, and hydrogen. The <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a> describes movement of the element from the air, into the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and organic compounds, then back into the atmosphere.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_etymology">History and etymology</span></h2>
<p>The existence of nitrogen is formally considered to have been discovered by Scottish physician <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Rutherford" title="Daniel Rutherford">Daniel Rutherford</a> in 1772, who called it <i>noxious air</i>. Though he did not recognize it as an entirely different chemical substance, he clearly distinguished it from Joseph Black's <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Black#Carbon_Dioxide" title="Joseph Black">"fixed air"</a>, or carbon dioxide. The fact that there was a component of air that does not support <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> was clear to Rutherford. Nitrogen was also studied at about the same time by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>, who referred to it as <i>burnt air</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">phlogisticated air</a></i>. Nitrogen gas was <a href="/wiki/Inert" title="Inert">inert</a> enough that <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> referred to it as "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mephitic_air" title="wikt:mephitic air">mephitic air</a>" or <i>azote</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <span lang="el">ἄζωτος</span> <i>azotos</i>, "lifeless". In an atmosphere of pure nitrogen, animals died and flames were extinguished. This "mephitic air" consisted mostly of N<sub>2</sub>, but might have included more than 1% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>.</p>
<p>Lavoisier's name for nitrogen is used in many languages (French, Italian, Portuguese, Polish, Russian, Albanian, Turkish, etc.) and still remains in English in the common names of many compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> and compounds of the <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a> ion. The English word nitrogen (1794) entered the language from the French <i>nitrogène</i>, coined in 1790 by French chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean-Antoine_Chaptal" title="Jean-Antoine Chaptal">Jean-Antoine Chaptal</a> (1756–1832), from the French <i><a href="/wiki/Niter" title="Niter">nitre</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a>, also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltpeter" title="Saltpeter">saltpeter</a>) and the French <i>-gène</i>, "producing", from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> -γενής (<i>-genes</i>, "producer, begetter"). Chaptal's meaning was that nitrogen gas is the essential part of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, which in turn was produced from niter. In earlier times, niter had been confused with Egyptian "natron" (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>) — called <i>nitron</i> (νίτρον) in Greek — which contained no nitrate.</p>
<p>Nitrogen compounds were well known by the Middle Ages. <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">Alchemists</a> knew nitric acid as <i><a href="/wiki/Aqua_fortis" title="Aqua fortis">aqua fortis</a></i> (strong water). The mixture of nitric and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acids</a> was known as <i><a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a></i> (royal water), celebrated for its ability to dissolve <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> (the <i>king</i> of metals). The earliest military, industrial, and agricultural applications of nitrogen compounds used saltpeter (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">sodium nitrate</a> or potassium nitrate), most notably in <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a>, and later as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>. In 1910, <a href="/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> discovered that an electrical discharge in nitrogen gas produced "active nitrogen", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic">monatomic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotrope</a> of nitrogen. The "whirling cloud of brilliant yellow light" produced by his apparatus reacted with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">quicksilver</a> to produce explosive <a href="/wiki/Mercury_nitride" title="Mercury nitride">mercury nitride</a>.</p>
<p>For a long time, sources of nitrogen compounds were limited. Natural sources originated either from biology or deposits of nitrates produced by atmospheric reactions. <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">Nitrogen fixation</a> by industrial processes like the <a href="/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Caro_process" title="Frank–Caro process">Frank–Caro process</a> (1895–1899) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber%E2%80%93Bosch_process" title="Haber–Bosch process">Haber–Bosch process</a> (1908–1913) eased this shortage of nitrogen compounds, to the extent that half of global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a> (see Applications) now relies on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers. At the same time, use of the <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_process" title="Ostwald process">Ostwald process</a> (1902) to produce nitrates from industrial nitrogen fixation allowed the large-scale industrial production of nitrates as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a> in the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosives" title="Explosives">explosives</a> in the <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">World War</a>s of the 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">Air separation</a></div>
<p>Nitrogen gas is an <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gas</a> produced by the fractional <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>, or by mechanical means using gaseous air (i.e., pressurized reverse <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_pressure" title="Osmotic pressure">osmosis membrane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorption</a>). Nitrogen gas generators using membranes or PSA are typically more cost and energy efficient than bulk delivered nitrogen. Commercial nitrogen is often a byproduct of air-processing for industrial concentration of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for steelmaking and other purposes. When supplied compressed in cylinders it is often called OFN (oxygen-free nitrogen).</p>
<p>In a chemical laboratory, it is prepared by treating an aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a>.</p>

<p>Small amounts of impurities NO and HNO<sub>3</sub> are also formed in this reaction. The impurities can be removed by passing the gas through aqueous sulfuric acid containing <a href="/wiki/Potassium_dichromate" title="Potassium dichromate">potassium dichromate</a>. Very pure nitrogen can be prepared by the thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Barium_azide" title="Barium azide">barium azide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_azide" title="Sodium azide">sodium azide</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span></h2>
<p>Nitrogen is a <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal" title="Nonmetal">nonmetal</a>, with an <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of 3.04. It has five <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell" title="Electron shell">outer shell</a> and is, therefore, <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">trivalent</a> in most compounds. The <a href="/wiki/Triple_bond" title="Triple bond">triple bond</a> in molecular nitrogen (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>) is one of the strongest. The resulting difficulty of converting <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> into other compounds, and the ease (and associated high energy release) of converting nitrogen compounds into elemental <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>, have dominated the role of nitrogen in both nature and human economic activities.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, molecular nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condenses</a> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquefies</a>) at 77 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (−195.79 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezes</a> at 63 K (−210.01 °C) into the beta <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_close-packed" title="Hexagonal close-packed">hexagonal close-packed</a> crystal <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropic</a> form. Below 35.4 K (−237.6 °C) nitrogen assumes the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> crystal allotropic form (called the alpha phase). <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</a>, a fluid resembling water in appearance, but with 80.8% of the density (the density of liquid nitrogen at its boiling point is 0.808 g/mL), is a common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogen" title="Cryogen">cryogen</a>.</p>
<p>Unstable allotropes of nitrogen consisting of more than two nitrogen atoms, such as <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span> and <a href="/wiki/Tetranitrogen" title="Tetranitrogen"><span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span></a>, have been produced in the laboratory. Under extremely high pressures (1.1 million <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>) and high temperatures (2000 K), as produced in a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cell</a>, nitrogen polymerizes into the single-bonded cubic gauche crystal structure. This structure is similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, and both have extremely strong <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span> is nicknamed "nitrogen diamond".</p>
<p>Other (theoretical but not yet synthesized) allotropes include <a href="/wiki/Hexazine" title="Hexazine">hexazine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
6</span></span>, a <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> analog) and <a href="/wiki/Octaazacubane" title="Octaazacubane">octaazacubane</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
8</span></span>, a <a href="/wiki/Cubane-type_cluster" title="Cubane-type cluster">cubane-type cluster</a>). The former is predicted to be highly unstable, while the latter is predicted to be kinetically stable, by reason of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_symmetry" title="Orbital symmetry">orbital symmetry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_nitrogen" title="Isotopes of nitrogen">Isotopes of nitrogen</a></div>
<p>There are two stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of nitrogen: N and N. By far the most common is N (99.634%), which is produced in the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Of the ten isotopes produced synthetically, N has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of ten minutes and the remaining isotopes have half-lives on the order of seconds or less.</p>
<p>Biologically mediated reactions (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(biology)" title="Assimilation (biology)">assimilation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrification" title="Nitrification">nitrification</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a>) strongly control nitrogen dynamics in the soil. These reactions typically result in N enrichment of the <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a> and depletion of the <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">product</a>.</p>
<p>A small part (0.73%) of the molecular nitrogen in Earth's atmosphere is the <a href="/wiki/Isotopologue" title="Isotopologue">isotopologue</a> NN, and almost all the rest is N<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotope</a> N is the dominant <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclide</a> in the coolant of <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boiling_water_reactor" title="Boiling water reactor">boiling water reactors</a> during normal operation. It is produced from O (in water) via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Np_reaction" title="Np reaction">(n,p) reaction</a>. It has a short half-life of about 7.1 s, but during its decay back to O produces high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a> (5 to 7 MeV).</p>
<p>Because of this, access to the primary coolant piping in a <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactor</a> must be restricted during <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">reactor</a> power operation. N is a sensitive and immediate indicator of leaks from the primary coolant system to the secondary steam cycle, and is the primary means of detection for such leaks.</p>
<p>Similarly, access to any of the steam cycle components in a <a href="/wiki/Boiling_water_reactor" title="Boiling water reactor">boiling water reactor</a> nuclear power plant must be restricted during operation. Condensate is typically retained for 10 minutes to allow for decay of N to radioactive depletion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetic_spectrum">Electromagnetic spectrum</span></h3>

<p>Molecular nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) is largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">transparent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a> radiation because it is a <a href="/wiki/Homonuclear_molecule" title="Homonuclear molecule">homonuclear molecule</a> and has no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">dipole moment</a> to couple to <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> at these <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>. Significant <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> occurs at extreme <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> wavelengths, beginning around 100 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a>. This is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_transition" title="Electronic transition">electronic transitions</a> in the molecule to states in which charge is not distributed evenly between nitrogen atoms. Nitrogen absorption leads to significant absorption of ultraviolet radiation in the Earth's upper atmosphere and the atmospheres of other planetary bodies. For similar reasons, pure molecular <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_laser" title="Nitrogen laser">nitrogen lasers</a> typically emit light in the ultraviolet range.</p>
<p>Nitrogen also makes a contribution to visible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_glow" title="Air glow">air glow</a> from the Earth's upper atmosphere, through electron impact excitation followed by emission. This visible blue air glow (seen in the polar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurora</a> and in the re-entry glow of returning spacecraft) typically results not from molecular nitrogen but rather from free nitrogen atoms combining with oxygen to form <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO).</p>
<p>Nitrogen gas also exhibits <a href="/wiki/Scintillation_(physics)" title="Scintillation (physics)">scintillation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span></h3>


<p>In general, nitrogen is unreactive at standard temperature and pressure. N<sub>2</sub> reacts spontaneously with few <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a>, being resilient to <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> as well as oxidants and most reductants. When nitrogen reacts spontaneously with a reagent, the net transformation is often called <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>.</p>
<p>Elemental <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> reacts ("burns") in an atmosphere of N<sub>2</sub> to give <a href="/wiki/Lithium_nitride" title="Lithium nitride">lithium nitride</a>:</p>

<p>When heated to 800C, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Magnesium</a> also burns in nitrogen, forming <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_nitride" title="Magnesium nitride">magnesium nitride</a>.</p>

<p>N<sub>2</sub> forms a variety of <a href="/wiki/Adduct" title="Adduct">adducts</a> with transition metals. The first example of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_complex" title="Dinitrogen complex">dinitrogen complex</a> is [Ru(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub>(N<sub>2</sub>)] (see figure at right). Notably, N<sub>2</sub> ligand was obtained by the decomposition of hydrazine, and not coordination of free dinitrogen. Many such compounds are now known, including IrCl(N<sub>2</sub>)(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, W(N<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dppe" title="Dppe">Ph<sub>2</sub>PCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>PPh<sub>2</sub></a>)<sub>2</sub>, and [(η-C<sub>5</sub>Me<sub>4</sub>H)<sub>2</sub>Zr]<sub>2</sub>(<a href="/wiki/Bridging_ligand" title="Bridging ligand">μ</a><sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Hapticity" title="Hapticity">η</a>,η-N<sub>2</sub>). These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complexes</a> illustrate how N<sub>2</sub> might bind to the metal(s) in <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenase</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>. A catalytic process to <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduce</a> N<sub>2</sub> to ammonia with the use of a <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> complex in the presence of a proton source was published in 2005.</p>
<p>The starting point for industrial production of nitrogen compounds is the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>, in which nitrogen is fixed by reacting <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> over an iron(II, III) oxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span>) catalyst at about 500 °C and 200 atmospheres pressure. Biological nitrogen fixation in free-living <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">root nodules</a> of plants also produces ammonia from molecular nitrogen. The reaction, which is the source of the bulk of nitrogen in the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, is catalyzed by the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> complex that contains Fe and Mo atoms, using energy derived from hydrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP) into <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">adenosine diphosphate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> (−20.5 kJ/mol).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote selfreference">See also: the categories <wbr></wbr><a href="/wiki/Category:Nitrate_minerals" title="Category:Nitrate minerals">Nitrate minerals</a>, and <wbr></wbr><a href="/wiki/Category:Ammonium_minerals" title="Category:Ammonium minerals">Ammonium minerals</a>.</div>
<p>Nitrogen gas (N<sub>2</sub>) is the largest constituent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> (78.082% by volume of dry air, 75.3% by weight in dry air). However, this high concentration does not reflect nitrogen's overall low abundance in the makeup of the Earth, from which most of the element escaped by solar evaporation, early in the planet's formation.</p>
<p>Nitrogen is a common element in the universe, and is considered the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">seventh most abundant</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> by mass in the universe, the Milky Way, and the Solar System. According to physical cosmology theory, it was originally created by <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">fusion</a> processes from carbon and hydrogen in <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">Molecular</a> nitrogen and nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> have been detected in <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar space</a> by astronomers using the <a href="/wiki/Far_Ultraviolet_Spectroscopic_Explorer" title="Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer">Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer</a>.</p>
<p>Due to the volatility of elemental nitrogen and its common compounds with hydrogen and oxygen, nitrogen was driven out of the <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a> in the early Solar System by the heat of the Sun and lost to the rocky planets of the inner Solar System. Nitrogen is consequently a relatively rare element on these inner planets, including Earth. Neon, which occurs in equal abundance in the universe, is rare in the inner Solar System for the same reason. Nitrogen is estimated at 31st <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in earth's crust">element in crustal abundance</a>. Nitrogen also occurs in some relatively uncommon minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">saltpeter</a> (potassium nitrate), <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">Chile saltpeter</a> (sodium nitrate) and <a href="/wiki/Sal_ammoniac" title="Sal ammoniac">sal ammoniac</a> (ammonium chloride). Because of the ready solubility of naturally-occurring nitrogen compounds, these minerals were concentrated from the crust by the leaching of rivers and collected in ancient seas which then evaporated, leaving the deposits of present day. A similar process occurred with the water-soluble compounds of the uncommon light element <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>.</p>
<p>Nitrogen and its compounds occur far more commonly as gases in the atmospheres of planets and moons that are large enough to have atmospheres. Molecular nitrogen is a major constituent of not only Earth's atmosphere, but also the <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturnian</a> moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>'s thick atmosphere. Also, held by gravity at colder temperatures, nitrogen and its compounds occur in variable amounts in the planetary atmospheres of the gas giant planets.</p>
<p>Nitrogen is present in all known living organisms, in proteins, nucleic acids, and other molecules. It typically makes up around 4% of the dry weight of plant matter, and around 3% of the weight of the human body. It is a large component of animal waste (for example, <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a>), usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> compounds, and derivatives of these nitrogenous products, which are essential <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> for all plants that cannot <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">fix atmospheric nitrogen</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote selfreference">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Nitrogen_compounds" title="Category:Nitrogen compounds">Category:Nitrogen compounds</a>.</div>
<p>The main neutral <a href="/wiki/Hydride#Appendix_on_nomenclature" title="Hydride">hydride</a> of nitrogen is <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span>), although <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span>) is also commonly used. Ammonia is more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" title="Basic (chemistry)">basic</a> than <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> by 6 orders of magnitude. In <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>, ammonia forms the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/>
4</span></span>). Liquid ammonia (boiling point 240 K) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiprotic" title="Amphiprotic">amphiprotic</a> (displaying either <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted–Lowry</a> acidic or basic character) and forms ammonium and the less common <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a> ions (<span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
2</span></span>); both amides and <a href="/wiki/Nitride" title="Nitride">nitride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">3−<br/></span></span>) <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salts</a> are known, Many of these salts <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">decompose</a> in water, but there are exceptions like <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boron_nitride" title="Boron nitride">boron nitride</a> that have strong polar covalent network structures. Singly, doubly, triply and quadruply substituted alkyl compounds of ammonia are called <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a> (four substitutions, to form commercially and biologically important quaternary amines, results in a positively charged nitrogen, and thus a water-soluble, or at least <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiphilic" title="Amphiphilic">amphiphilic</a>, compound). Larger chains, rings and structures of nitrogen hydrides are also known, but are generally unstable.</p>
<p>Other classes of nitrogen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> (negatively charged ions) are the poisonous <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azides</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span>), which are linear and <a href="/wiki/Isoelectronicity" title="Isoelectronicity">isoelectronic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, but which bind to important iron-containing enzymes in the body in a manner more resembling <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a>. Another <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> of the same structure is the colorless and relatively inert anesthetic gas <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> (dinitrogen monoxide, <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O</span>), also known as laughing gas. This is one of a variety of nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> that form a family often abbreviated as <b>NOx</b>. <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">Nitric oxide</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_monoxide" title="Nitrogen monoxide">nitrogen monoxide</a>, NO), is a natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radical</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signal transduction</a> in both plants and animals. It is used, for example, in <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a>, causing the smooth muscle of blood vessels to relax. The reddish and poisonous <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> contains an unpaired <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and is an important component of <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>. Nitrogen molecules containing unpaired electrons show a tendency to <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimerize</a> (thus pairing the electrons), and are, in general, highly reactive. The corresponding acids are <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">nitrous</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span>, with the corresponding salts called <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a>.</p>
<p>The higher oxides <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_trioxide" title="Dinitrogen trioxide">dinitrogen trioxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span> and <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_pentoxide" title="Dinitrogen pentoxide">dinitrogen pentoxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
5</span></span>, are unstable and explosive, a consequence of the low energy state of <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>. Nearly every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> rocket engine uses <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span> as the oxidizer; the fuels, one of the various forms of <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a>, are also nitrogen compounds. These engines are extensively used on spacecraft such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a> and those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Program" title="Apollo Program">Apollo Program</a> because their propellants are liquids at room temperature and ignition occurs spontaneously without an ignition system, allowing many precisely controlled burns. Some launch vehicles such as the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ariane_(rocket_family)" title="Ariane (rocket family)">Ariane</a> 1 through 4 also use hypergolic fuels, although the trend is away from such engines for cost and safety reasons. <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span></span> is an intermediate in the manufacture of nitric acid <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span>, a strong acid and a fairly strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>.</p>
<p>Nitrogen is notable for the range of explosively unstable compounds. <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_triiodide" title="Nitrogen triiodide">Nitrogen triiodide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">NI<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span></span> is an extremely sensitive <a href="/wiki/Contact_explosive" title="Contact explosive">contact explosive</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">Nitrocellulose</a>, produced by nitration of cellulose with nitric acid, is also known as guncotton. <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">Nitroglycerin</a>, made by nitration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycerin" title="Glycerin">glycerin</a>, is the dangerously unstable explosive ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a>. The comparatively stable, but less powerful explosive <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">trinitrotoluene</a> (TNT) is the standard explosive against which the power of nuclear explosions are measured.</p>
<p>Nitrogen can also be found in many <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>. Common nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a> include: <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitro_compound" title="Nitro compound">nitro</a> groups, <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">imines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Enamine" title="Enamine">enamines</a>. The amount of nitrogen in a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> can be determined by the <a href="/wiki/Kjeldahl_method" title="Kjeldahl method">Kjeldahl method</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_gas">Nitrogen gas</span></h3>
<p>Nitrogen gas has a variety of applications, including serving as an <a href="/wiki/Inert" title="Inert">inert</a> replacement for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> where <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> is undesirable;</p>
<ul>
<li>As a <a href="/wiki/Modified_atmosphere" title="Modified atmosphere">modified atmosphere</a>, pure or mixed with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, to nitrogenate and preserve the freshness of packaged or bulk foods (by delaying <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">rancidity</a> and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidative damage</a>). Pure nitrogen as food additive is labeled in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> with the <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E number</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E941" title="E941">E941</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulbs</a> as an inexpensive alternative to <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Gaseous_fire_suppression" title="Gaseous fire suppression">fire suppression systems for Information technology (IT) equipment</a></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Photolithography" title="Photolithography">photolithography</a> in deep ultraviolet, nitrogen is used to avoid the strong oxygen absorption of UV at these wavelengths.</li>
<li>Dried and pressurized, it is used as an <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> for <a href="/wiki/High_voltage" title="High voltage">high voltage</a> equipment.</li>
<li>In the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>.</li>
<li>In some aircraft fuel systems to reduce fire hazard, (see <a href="/wiki/Inerting_system" title="Inerting system">inerting system</a>).</li>
<li>In packaging <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" title="Explosive material">liquid explosives</a> as a safety measure.</li>
<li>To inflate race car and aircraft <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tires</a>, reducing the problems caused by moisture and <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxygen</a> in natural air.</li>
</ul>
<p>Nitrogen is commonly used during sample preparation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_Analysis" title="Chemical Analysis">chemical analysis</a>. It is used to concentrate and reduce the volume of liquid samples. Directing a pressurized stream of nitrogen gas perpendicular to the surface of the liquid causes the solvent to evaporate while leaving the solute(s) and un-evaporated solvent behind.</p>
<p>Nitrogen can be used as a replacement, or in combination with, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to pressurize kegs of some <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beers</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">stouts</a> and British <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ales</a>, due to the smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_bubble" title="Liquid bubble">bubbles</a> it produces, which makes the dispensed beer smoother and <a href="/wiki/Beer_head" title="Beer head">headier</a>. A pressure-sensitive nitrogen capsule known commonly as a "<a href="/wiki/Widget_(beer)" title="Widget (beer)">widget</a>" allows nitrogen-charged beers to be packaged in <a href="/wiki/Beverage_can" title="Beverage can">cans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottles</a>.</p>
<p>Nitrogen tanks are also replacing carbon dioxide as the main power source for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paintball_gun" title="Paintball gun">paintball guns</a>. Nitrogen must be kept at higher pressure than CO<sub>2</sub>, making N<sub>2</sub> tanks heavier and more expensive.</p>
<p>Nitrogen gas has become the inert gas of choice for <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas_asphyxiation" title="Inert gas asphyxiation">inert gas asphyxiation</a>, and is under consideration as a replacement for lethal injection in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. Nitrogen is promoted by euthanasia advocate <a href="/wiki/Philip_Nitschke" title="Philip Nitschke">Philip Nitschke</a> to end life in a "peaceful, reliable [and] totally legal" manner.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</a></div>
<p>Liquid nitrogen is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogen" title="Cryogen">cryogenic liquid</a>. At atmospheric pressure, it boils at −195.8 °C (−320.4 °F). When insulated in proper containers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewar_flask" title="Dewar flask">Dewar flasks</a>, it can be transported without much <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporative loss</a>.</p>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>, the main use of liquid nitrogen is as a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a>. Among other things, it is used in the <a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation" title="Cryopreservation">cryopreservation</a> of blood, reproductive cells (<a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">egg</a>), and other biological samples and materials. It is used in the clinical setting in <a href="/wiki/Cryotherapy" title="Cryotherapy">cryotherapy</a> to remove cysts and warts on the skin. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Cold_trap" title="Cold trap">cold traps</a> for certain laboratory equipment and to cool <a href="/wiki/Infrared_detector" title="Infrared detector">infrared detectors</a> or <a href="/wiki/X-ray_detector" title="X-ray detector">X-ray detectors</a>. It has also been used to cool <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing units</a> and other devices in computers that are <a href="/wiki/Overclocking" title="Overclocking">overclocked</a>, and that produce more heat than during normal operation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_nitrogen">Solid nitrogen</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solid_nitrogen" title="Solid nitrogen">Solid nitrogen</a></div>
<p>The solid form of the element nitrogen forms a significant dynamic surface coverage on <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> and outer moons of the Solar System such as <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>. Even at the low temperatures of solid nitrogen it is fairly volatile and can <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublime</a> to form an atmosphere, or condense back into nitrogen frost. It is very weak and flows in the form of glaciers and on Triton <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geysers</a> of nitrogen gas come from the polar ice cap region.</p>
<p>There are a variety of crystalline forms of solid nitrogen known, not all with discrete dinitrogen molecular structures. Such non-molecular forms of solid nitrogen are of interest as they possess a higher energy density than any other non-nuclear material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_compounds">Nitrogen compounds</span></h3>
<p>Molecular nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) in the atmosphere is relatively non-reactive due to its strong triple bond, N≡N, and molecular nitrogen plays an inert role in the human body, being neither produced nor destroyed. In nature, nitrogen is converted into biologically (and industrially) useful compounds by lightning, and by some living organisms, notably certain <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen-fixing_bacteria" title="Nitrogen-fixing bacteria">nitrogen-fixing bacteria</a>—see <i><a href="#Biological_role">Biological role</a></i> below). Molecular nitrogen is released into the atmosphere in the process of <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decay</a>, in dead plant and animal tissues.</p>
<p>The ability to combine, or fix, molecular nitrogen is a key feature of modern industrial chemistry. Previously to the 20th century, access to nitrogen compounds for fertilizers and gunpowder had been through deposits of natural nitrates, such as Chilean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltpeter" title="Saltpeter">saltpeter</a>. However, first the <a href="/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Caro_process" title="Frank–Caro process">Frank–Caro process</a> for producing <a href="/wiki/Cyanamide" title="Cyanamide">cyanamide</a>, and then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber%E2%80%93Bosch_process" title="Haber–Bosch process">Haber–Bosch process</a> for producing ammonia from air and natural gas (developed just before the first world war) eased this shortage of nitrogen compounds, to the extent that half of global food production now relies on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_process" title="Ostwald process">Ostwald process</a>, developed a few years before the Haber process, allowed large-scale production of nitric acid and nitrate from ammonia, thus freeing large-scale industrial production of nitrate explosives and weapons propellants from the need to mine nitrate salt deposits. The organic and inorganic <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> of nitric acid have been important historically as convenient stores of chemical energy for warfare and rocket fuels. Historically, such compounds included important compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a>, used in <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> which was often produced by biological means (bacterial fermentation) before natural mineral sources were discovered. Later, all such sources were displaced by industrial production, in the early 1900s.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">Ammonium nitrate</a> has been used as both fertilizer and explosive (see <a href="/wiki/ANFO" title="ANFO">ANFO</a>). Various other nitrated organic compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">trinitrotoluene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>, are used as explosives and propellants for modern firearms. <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">Nitric acid</a> is used as an <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> in liquid fueled <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">Hydrazine</a> and hydrazine derivatives find use as rocket <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">monopropellants</a>. In most of these compounds, the basic instability and tendency to burn or explode is derived from the fact that nitrogen is present as an oxide, and not as the far more stable nitrogen molecule (N<sub>2</sub>), which is a product of the compounds' thermal decomposition. When nitrates burn or explode, the formation of the powerful triple bond in the N<sub>2</sub> produces most of the energy of the reaction.</p>
<p>Nitrogen is a constituent of molecules in every major drug class in pharmacology and medicine. <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O) was discovered early in the 19th century to be a partial anesthetic, though it was not used as a surgical anesthetic until later. Called "<a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">laughing gas</a>", it was found capable of inducing a state of social disinhibition resembling drunkenness. Other notable nitrogen-containing drugs are drugs derived from plant <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> (there exist many alkaloids known to have pharmacological effects; in some cases, they appear as natural chemical defenses of plants against predation). Drugs that contain nitrogen include all major classes of antibiotics and organic nitrate drugs like <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitroprusside" title="Nitroprusside">nitroprusside</a> that regulate blood pressure and heart action by mimicking the action of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">Nitrogen cycle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_impacts_on_the_nitrogen_cycle" title="Human impacts on the nitrogen cycle">Human impacts on the nitrogen cycle</a></div>
<p>Nitrogen is an essential building block of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, essential to life on Earth.</p>
<p>Elemental nitrogen in the atmosphere cannot be used directly by either plants or animals, and must be converted to a reduced (or 'fixed') state to be useful for higher plants and animals. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation</a> often contains substantial quantities of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, thought to result from <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and other atmospheric electric phenomena. This was first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Liebig</a> in 1827 and later confirmed. However, because <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> is preferentially retained by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_canopy" title="Forest canopy">forest canopy</a> relative to atmospheric nitrate, most fixed nitrogen reaches the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> surface under trees as nitrate. Soil nitrate is preferentially assimilated by tree <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> relative to soil ammonium, however this is not the case for all species.</p>
<p>Specific <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizobia" title="Rhizobia">Rhizobium</a> trifolium</i>) possess <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> that can fix atmospheric nitrogen (see <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>) into a form (<a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ion) that is chemically useful to higher organisms. This process requires a large amount of energy and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anoxia" title="wikt:anoxia">anoxic</a> conditions. Such bacteria may live freely in soil (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Azotobacter" title="Azotobacter">Azotobacter</a></i>) but normally exist in a <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> relationship in the <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">root nodules</a> of <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">leguminous</a> plants (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>, <i>Trifolium</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> plant, <i>Glycine max</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer_tree" title="Fertilizer tree">fertilizer trees</a>. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria are also symbiotic with a number of unrelated plant species such as alders (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alnus" title="Alnus">Alnus</a></i>) spp., lichens, <i><a href="/wiki/Casuarina" title="Casuarina">Casuarina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Myrica" title="Myrica">Myrica</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Marchantiophyta" title="Marchantiophyta">liverworts</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Gunnera" title="Gunnera">Gunnera</a></i>.</p>
<p>As part of the symbiotic relationship, the plant converts the 'fixed' ammonium ion to nitrogen oxides and amino acids to form <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and other molecules, (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaloids" title="Alkaloids">alkaloids</a>). In return for the 'fixed' nitrogen, the plant secretes sugars to the symbiotic bacteria. <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">Legumes</a> maintain an anaerobic (oxygen free) environment for their nitrogen-fixing bacteria.</p>
<p>Plants are able to assimilate nitrogen directly in the form of nitrates that may be present in soil from natural mineral deposits, artificial fertilizers, animal waste, or organic decay (as the product of bacteria, but not bacteria specifically associated with the plant). Nitrates absorbed in this fashion are converted to nitrites by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Nitrate_reductase" title="Nitrate reductase"><i>nitrate</i> reductase</a>, and then converted to ammonia by another enzyme called <a href="/wiki/Nitrite_reductase" title="Nitrite reductase"><i>nitrite</i> reductase</a>.</p>
<p>Nitrogen compounds are basic building blocks in animal biology as well. Animals use nitrogen-containing <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> from plant sources as starting materials for all nitrogen-compound animal biochemistry, including the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>. Plant-feeding insects are dependent on nitrogen in their diet, such that varying the amount of nitrogen fertilizer applied to a plant can affect the reproduction rate of insects feeding on fertilized plants.</p>
<p>Soluble nitrate is an important limiting factor in the growth of certain bacteria in ocean waters. In many places in the world, artificial <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> applied to crop-lands to increase yields result in run-off delivery of soluble nitrogen to oceans at river mouths. This process can result in <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of the water, as nitrogen-driven bacterial growth depletes water oxygen to the point that all higher organisms die. Well-known <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">"dead zone"</a> areas in the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> are due to this important polluting process.</p>
<p>Many saltwater fish manufacture large amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trimethylamine_oxide" title="Trimethylamine oxide">trimethylamine oxide</a> to protect them from the high <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmotic</a> effects of their environment; conversion of this compound to <a href="/wiki/Dimethylamine" title="Dimethylamine">dimethylamine</a> is responsible for the early odor in unfresh saltwater fish. In animals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radical</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (<b>NO</b>) (derived from an <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a>), serves as an important regulatory molecule for circulation.</p>
<p>Nitric oxide's rapid reaction with water in animals results in production of its metabolite <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a>. Animal <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of nitrogen in proteins, in general, results in <a href="/wiki/Excretion" title="Excretion">excretion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, while animal metabolism of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleic_acids" title="Nucleic acids">nucleic acids</a> results in excretion of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>. The characteristic odor of animal flesh decay is caused by the creation of long-chain, nitrogen-containing <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Putrescine" title="Putrescine">putrescine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadaverine" title="Cadaverine">cadaverine</a>, which are breakdown products of the amino acids <a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">ornithine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>, respectively, in decaying proteins.</p>
<p>Decay of organisms and their waste products may produce small amounts of nitrate, but most decay eventually returns nitrogen content to the atmosphere, as molecular nitrogen. The circulation of nitrogen from atmosphere, to organic compounds, then back to the atmosphere, is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>Although nitrogen is non-toxic, when released into an enclosed space it can displace oxygen, and therefore presents an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_asphyxiation" title="Nitrogen asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a> hazard. This may happen with few warning symptoms, since the human <a href="/wiki/Carotid_body" title="Carotid body">carotid body</a> is a relatively poor and slow low-oxygen (hypoxia) sensing system. An example occurred shortly before the launch of the first Space Shuttle mission in 1981, when two technicians died from asphyxiation after they walked into a space located in the Shuttle's <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Launcher_Platform" title="Mobile Launcher Platform">Mobile Launcher Platform</a> that was pressurized with pure nitrogen as a precaution against fire.</p>
<p>A closed space, initially filled with air, will ultimately contain only nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and trace gases if some oxidative process, such as the rusting of iron, consumes all the oxygen.</p>
<p>When inhaled at high <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressures</a> (more than about 4 bar, encountered at depths below about 30 m in <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a>), nitrogen is an anesthetic agent, causing <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis" title="Nitrogen narcosis">nitrogen narcosis</a>, a temporary state of mental impairment similar to <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> intoxication.</p>
<p>Nitrogen dissolves in the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and body fats. Rapid decompression (as when divers ascend too quickly or astronauts decompress too quickly from cabin pressure to spacesuit pressure) can lead to a potentially fatal condition called <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> (formerly known as caisson sickness or <i>the bends</i>), when nitrogen bubbles form in the bloodstream, nerves, joints, and other sensitive or vital areas. Bubbles from other "inert" gases (gases other than carbon dioxide and oxygen) cause the same effects, so replacement of nitrogen in <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gases</a> may prevent nitrogen narcosis, but does not prevent decompression sickness.</p>
<p>Direct skin contact with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> will cause severe <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a> (cryogenic "burns"). This may happen almost instantly on contact, or after a second or more, depending on the form of liquid nitrogen. Bulk liquid nitrogen causes less rapid freezing than a spray of nitrogen mist (such as is used to freeze certain skin growths in the practice of <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatology</a>). The extra surface area provided by nitrogen-soaked materials is also important, with soaked clothing or cotton causing far more rapid damage than a spill of direct liquid to skin. Full "contact" between naked skin and large collected-droplets or pools of liquid nitrogen may be prevented for a second or two, by a layer of insulating gas from the <a href="/wiki/Leidenfrost_effect" title="Leidenfrost effect">Leidenfrost effect</a>. This may give the skin a second of protection from nitrogen bulk liquid. However, liquid nitrogen applied to skin in mists, and on fabrics, bypasses this effect, and causes local frostbite immediately.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oxygen_sensor" title="Oxygen sensor">Oxygen sensors</a> are sometimes used as a safety precaution when working with liquid nitrogen to alert workers of gas spills into a confined space. Nitrogen that evaporates from liquid is still colder and heavier than (oxygenated) air, therefore any spill of liquid nitrogen tends to stay at the bottom of a closed space, expelling oxygen.</p>
