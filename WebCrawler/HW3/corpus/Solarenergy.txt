<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">Solar power</a></div>
<p><b>Solar energy</b> is radiant <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> that is harnessed using a range of ever-evolving technologies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_heating" title="Solar heating">solar heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">solar thermal energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_architecture" title="Solar architecture">solar architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_photosynthesis" title="Artificial photosynthesis">artificial photosynthesis</a>.</p>
<p>It is an important source of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and its technologies are broadly characterized as either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">passive solar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">active solar</a> depending on how they capture and distribute solar energy or convert it into <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>. Active solar techniques include the use of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> to harness the energy. Passive solar techniques include orienting a building to the Sun, selecting materials with favorable <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">thermal mass</a> or light-dispersing properties, and designing spaces that <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">naturally circulate air</a>.</p>
<p>The large magnitude of solar energy available makes it a highly appealing source of electricity. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> in its 2000 World Energy Assessment found that the annual potential of solar energy was 1,575–49,837 <a href="/wiki/Joule#Multiples" title="Joule">exajoules</a> (EJ). This is several times larger than the total <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">world energy consumption</a>, which was 559.8 EJ in 2012.</p>
<p>In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> said that "the development of affordable, inexhaustible and clean solar energy technologies will have huge longer-term benefits. It will increase countries’ energy security through reliance on an indigenous, inexhaustible and mostly import-independent resource, enhance <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, reduce pollution, lower the costs of mitigating <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and keep <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> prices lower than otherwise. These advantages are global. Hence the additional costs of the incentives for early deployment should be considered learning investments; they must be wisely spent and need to be widely shared".</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential">Potential</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">Solar radiation</a></div>

<p>The Earth receives 174,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terawatt" title="Terawatt">terawatts</a> (TW) of incoming solar radiation (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>) at the upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Approximately 30% is reflected back to space while the rest is absorbed by clouds, oceans and land masses. The <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectrum</a> of solar light at the Earth's surface is mostly spread across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-infrared" title="Near-infrared">near-infrared</a> ranges with a small part in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-ultraviolet" title="Near-ultraviolet">near-ultraviolet</a>. Most of the world's population live in areas with insolation levels of 150-300 watts/m², or 3.5-7.0 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" title="Kilowatt-hour">kWh</a>/m² per day.</p>
<p>Solar radiation is absorbed by the Earth's land surface, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> – which cover about 71% of the globe – and atmosphere. Warm air containing evaporated water from the oceans rises, causing <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. When the air reaches a high altitude, where the temperature is low, water vapor condenses into clouds, which rain onto the Earth's surface, completing the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> of water condensation amplifies convection, producing atmospheric phenomena such as wind, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-cyclone" title="Anti-cyclone">anti-cyclones</a>. Sunlight absorbed by the oceans and land masses keeps the surface at an average temperature of 14 °C. By <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, green plants convert solar energy into chemically stored energy, which produces food, wood and the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> from which fossil fuels are derived.</p>
<p>The total solar energy absorbed by Earth's atmosphere, oceans and land masses is approximately 3,850,000 <a href="/wiki/Joule#Multiples" title="Joule">exajoules</a> (EJ) per year. In 2002, this was more energy in one hour than the world used in one year. Photosynthesis captures approximately 3,000 EJ per year in biomass. The amount of solar energy reaching the surface of the planet is so vast that in one year it is about twice as much as will ever be obtained from all of the Earth's non-renewable resources of coal, oil, natural gas, and mined uranium combined,</p>

<p>The potential solar energy that could be used by humans differs from the amount of solar energy present near the surface of the planet because factors such as geography, time variation, cloud cover, and the land available to humans limit the amount of solar energy that we can acquire.</p>
<p>Geography affects solar energy potential because areas that are closer to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> have a greater amount of solar radiation. However, the use of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> that can follow the position of the sun can significantly increase the solar energy potential in areas that are farther from the equator. Time variation effects the potential of solar energy because during the nighttime there is little solar radiation on the surface of the Earth for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_panels" title="Solar panels">solar panels</a> to absorb. This limits the amount of energy that solar panels can absorb in one day. <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">Cloud cover</a> can affect the potential of solar panels because clouds block incoming light from the sun and reduce the light available for solar cells.</p>
<p>In addition, land availability has a large effect on the available solar energy because solar panels can only be set up on land that is otherwise unused and suitable for solar panels. Roofs have been found to be a suitable place for solar cells, as many people have discovered that they can collect energy directly from their homes this way. Other areas that are suitable for solar cells are lands that are not being used for businesses where solar plants can be established.</p>
<p>Solar technologies are characterized as either passive or active depending on the way they capture, convert and distribute sunlight and enable solar energy to be harnessed at different levels around the world, mostly depending on distance from the equator. Although solar energy refers primarily to the use of solar radiation for practical ends, all renewable energies, other than <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">Geothermal power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">Tidal power</a>, derive their energy either directly or indirectly from the Sun.</p>
<p>Active solar techniques use photovoltaics, <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar thermal collectors</a>, pumps, and fans to convert sunlight into useful outputs. Passive solar techniques include selecting materials with favorable thermal properties, designing spaces that naturally circulate air, and referencing the position of a building to the Sun. Active solar technologies increase the supply of energy and are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supply_side" title="Supply side">supply side</a> technologies, while passive solar technologies reduce the need for alternate resources and are generally considered demand side technologies.</p>
<p>In 2000, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a>, UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs, and <a href="/wiki/World_Energy_Council" title="World Energy Council">World Energy Council</a> published an estimate of the potential solar energy that could be used by humans each year that took into account factors such as insolation, cloud cover, and the land that is usable by humans. The estimate found that solar energy has a global potential of 1,575–49,837 EJ per year <i>(see table below)</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_energy">Thermal energy</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">Solar thermal energy</a></div>
<p>Solar thermal technologies can be used for water heating, space heating, space cooling and process heat generation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_commercial_adaptation">Early commercial adaptation</span></h3>

<p>In 1897, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Shuman" title="Frank Shuman">Frank Shuman</a>, a U.S. inventor, engineer and solar energy pioneer built a small demonstration solar engine that worked by reflecting solar energy onto square boxes filled with ether, which has a lower boiling point than water, and were fitted internally with black pipes which in turn powered a steam engine. In 1908 Shuman formed the Sun Power Company with the intent of building larger solar power plants. He, along with his technical advisor A.S.E. Ackermann and British physicist Sir <a href="/wiki/C._V._Boys" title="C. V. Boys">Charles Vernon Boys</a>, developed an improved system using mirrors to reflect solar energy upon collector boxes, increasing heating capacity to the extent that water could now be used instead of ether. Shuman then constructed a full-scale steam engine powered by low-pressure water, enabling him to patent the entire solar engine system by 1912.</p>
<p>Shuman built the world’s first <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">solar thermal power station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maadi" title="Maadi">Maadi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, between 1912 and 1913. His plant used <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic troughs</a> to power a 45–52 kilowatts (60–70 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a>) engine that pumped more than 22,000 litres (4,800 imp gal; 5,800 US gal) of water per minute from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a> to adjacent cotton fields. Although the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">cheap oil</a> in the 1930s discouraged the advancement of solar energy, Shuman’s vision and basic design were resurrected in the 1970s with a new wave of interest in solar thermal energy. In 1916 Shuman was quoted in the media advocating solar energy's utilization, saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We have proved the commercial profit of sun power in the tropics and have more particularly proved that after our stores of oil and coal are exhausted the human race can receive unlimited power from the rays of the sun.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Frank Shuman, New York Times, 2 July 1916</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_heating">Water heating</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" title="Solar hot water">Solar hot water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_combisystem" title="Solar combisystem">Solar combisystem</a></div>

<p>Solar hot water systems use sunlight to heat water. In low geographical latitudes (below 40 degrees) from 60 to 70% of the domestic hot water use with temperatures up to 60 °C can be provided by solar heating systems. The most common types of solar water heaters are evacuated tube collectors (44%) and glazed flat plate collectors (34%) generally used for domestic hot water; and unglazed plastic collectors (21%) used mainly to heat swimming pools.</p>
<p>As of 2007, the total installed capacity of solar hot water systems was approximately 154 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GWth" title="GWth">thermal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">gigawatt</a> (GW<sub>th</sub>). China is the world leader in their deployment with 70 GW<sub>th</sub> installed as of 2006 and a long-term goal of 210 GW<sub>th</sub> by 2020. <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> are the per capita leaders in the use of solar hot water systems with over 90% of homes using them. In the United States, Canada, and Australia, heating swimming pools is the dominant application of solar hot water with an installed capacity of 18 GW<sub>th</sub> as of 2005.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating.2C_cooling_and_ventilation">Heating, cooling and ventilation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_heating" title="Solar heating">Solar heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">Thermal mass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_chimney" title="Solar chimney">Solar chimney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_conditioning" title="Solar air conditioning">Solar air conditioning</a></div>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">heating, ventilation and air conditioning</a> (HVAC) systems account for 30% (4.65 EJ/yr) of the energy used in commercial buildings and nearly 50% (10.1 EJ/yr) of the energy used in residential buildings. Solar heating, cooling and ventilation technologies can be used to offset a portion of this energy.</p>

<p>Thermal mass is any material that can be used to store heat—heat from the Sun in the case of solar energy. Common thermal mass materials include stone, cement and water. Historically they have been used in arid climates or warm temperate regions to keep buildings cool by absorbing solar energy during the day and radiating stored heat to the cooler atmosphere at night. However, they can be used in cold temperate areas to maintain warmth as well. The size and placement of thermal mass depend on several factors such as climate, daylighting and shading conditions. When properly incorporated, thermal mass maintains space temperatures in a comfortable range and reduces the need for auxiliary heating and cooling equipment.</p>
<p>A solar chimney (or thermal chimney, in this context) is a passive solar ventilation system composed of a vertical shaft connecting the interior and exterior of a building. As the chimney warms, the air inside is heated causing an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a> that pulls air through the building. Performance can be improved by using glazing and thermal mass materials in a way that mimics greenhouses.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">Deciduous</a> trees and plants have been promoted as a means of controlling solar heating and cooling. When planted on the southern side of a building in the northern hemisphere or the northern side in the southern hemisphere, their leaves provide shade during the summer, while the bare limbs allow light to pass during the winter. Since bare, leafless trees shade 1/3 to 1/2 of incident solar radiation, there is a balance between the benefits of summer shading and the corresponding loss of winter heating. In climates with significant heating loads, deciduous trees should not be planted on the Equator-facing side of a building because they will interfere with winter solar availability. They can, however, be used on the east and west sides to provide a degree of summer shading without appreciably affecting winter solar gain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_cooker" title="Solar cooker">Solar cooker</a></div>

<p>Solar cookers use sunlight for cooking, drying and <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">pasteurization</a>. They can be grouped into three broad categories: box cookers, panel cookers and reflector cookers. The simplest solar cooker is the box cooker first built by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace_de_Saussure" title="Horace de Saussure">Horace de Saussure</a> in 1767. A basic box cooker consists of an insulated container with a transparent lid. It can be used effectively with partially overcast skies and will typically reach temperatures of 90–150 °C (194–302 °F). Panel cookers use a reflective panel to direct sunlight onto an insulated container and reach temperatures comparable to box cookers. Reflector cookers use various concentrating geometries (dish, trough, Fresnel mirrors) to focus light on a cooking container. These cookers reach temperatures of 315 °C (599 °F) and above but require direct light to function properly and must be repositioned to track the Sun.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_heat">Process heat</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_pond" title="Solar pond">Solar pond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_evaporation_pond" title="Salt evaporation pond">Salt evaporation pond</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_furnace" title="Solar furnace">Solar furnace</a></div>
<p>Solar concentrating technologies such as parabolic dish, trough and Scheffler reflectors can provide process heat for commercial and industrial applications. The first commercial system was the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Total_Energy_Project" title="Solar Total Energy Project">Solar Total Energy Project</a> (STEP) in Shenandoah, Georgia, USA where a field of 114 parabolic dishes provided 50% of the process heating, air conditioning and electrical requirements for a clothing factory. This grid-connected cogeneration system provided 400 kW of electricity plus thermal energy in the form of 401 kW steam and 468 kW chilled water, and had a one-hour peak load thermal storage. Evaporation ponds are shallow pools that concentrate dissolved solids through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. The use of evaporation ponds to obtain salt from seawater is one of the oldest applications of solar energy. Modern uses include concentrating brine solutions used in leach mining and removing dissolved solids from waste streams. <a href="/wiki/Clothes_line" title="Clothes line">Clothes lines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clotheshorse" title="Clotheshorse">clotheshorses</a>, and clothes racks dry clothes through evaporation by wind and sunlight without consuming electricity or gas. In some states of the United States legislation protects the "right to dry" clothes. Unglazed transpired collectors (UTC) are perforated sun-facing walls used for preheating ventilation air. UTCs can raise the incoming air temperature up to 22 °C (40 °F) and deliver outlet temperatures of 45–60 °C (113–140 °F). The short payback period of transpired collectors (3 to 12 years) makes them a more cost-effective alternative than glazed collection systems. As of 2003, over 80 systems with a combined collector area of 35,000 square metres (380,000 sq ft) had been installed worldwide, including an 860 m (9,300 sq ft) collector in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> used for drying coffee beans and a 1,300 m (14,000 sq ft) collector in <a href="/wiki/Coimbatore" title="Coimbatore">Coimbatore</a>, India, used for drying marigolds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_treatment">Water treatment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_still" title="Solar still">Solar still</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_disinfection" title="Solar water disinfection">Solar water disinfection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_desalination" title="Solar desalination">Solar desalination</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Powered_Desalination_Unit" title="Solar Powered Desalination Unit">Solar Powered Desalination Unit</a></div>

<p>Solar distillation can be used to make <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a> potable. The first recorded instance of this was by 16th-century Arab alchemists. A large-scale solar distillation project was first constructed in 1872 in the <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> mining town of Las Salinas. The plant, which had solar collection area of 4,700 m (51,000 sq ft), could produce up to 22,700 L (5,000 imp gal; 6,000 US gal) per day and operate for 40 years. Individual <a href="/wiki/Still" title="Still">still</a> designs include single-slope, double-slope (or greenhouse type), vertical, conical, inverted absorber, multi-wick, and multiple effect. These stills can operate in passive, active, or hybrid modes. Double-slope stills are the most economical for decentralized domestic purposes, while active multiple effect units are more suitable for large-scale applications.</p>
<p>Solar water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfection" title="Disinfection">disinfection</a> (SODIS) involves exposing water-filled plastic <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> (PET) bottles to sunlight for several hours. Exposure times vary depending on weather and climate from a minimum of six hours to two days during fully overcast conditions. It is recommended by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> as a viable method for household water treatment and safe storage. Over two million people in developing countries use this method for their daily drinking water.</p>
<p>Solar energy may be used in a water stabilization pond to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_water" title="Waste water">waste water</a> without chemicals or electricity. A further environmental advantage is that <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> grow in such ponds and consume <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in photosynthesis, although algae may produce toxic chemicals that make the water unusable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_production">Electricity production</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">Solar power</a></div>

<p>Solar power is the conversion of sunlight into <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, either directly using <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> (PV), or indirectly using <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> (CSP). CSP systems use lenses or mirrors and tracking systems to focus a large area of sunlight into a small beam. PV converts light into electric current using the <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">photoelectric effect</a>.</p>
<p>Solar power is anticipated to become the world's largest source of electricity by 2050, with solar photovoltaics and concentrated solar power contributing 16 and 11 percent to the global overall consumption, respectively.</p>
<p>Commercial CSP plants were first developed in the 1980s. Since 1985 the eventually 354 MW <a href="/wiki/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems" title="Solar Energy Generating Systems">SEGS</a> CSP installation, in the Mojave Desert of California, is the largest solar power plant in the world. Other large CSP plants include the 150 MW <a href="/wiki/Solnova_Solar_Power_Station" title="Solnova Solar Power Station">Solnova Solar Power Station</a> and the 100 MW <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andasol_solar_power_station" title="Andasol solar power station">Andasol solar power station</a>, both in Spain. The 250 MW <a href="/wiki/Agua_Caliente_Solar_Project" title="Agua Caliente Solar Project">Agua Caliente Solar Project</a>, in the United States, and the 221 MW <a href="/wiki/Gujarat_Solar_Park" title="Gujarat Solar Park">Charanka Solar Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, are the <a href="/wiki/List_of_photovoltaic_power_stations" title="List of photovoltaic power stations">world’s largest</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_plant" title="Photovoltaic plant">photovoltaic plants</a>. Solar projects exceeding 1 GW are being developed, but most of the deployed photovoltaics are in small rooftop arrays of less than 5 kW, which are connected to the grid using net metering and/or a feed-in tariff. In 2013 solar generated less than 1% of the world's total grid electricity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photovoltaics">Photovoltaics</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">Photovoltaics</a></div>

<p>In the last two decades, <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> (PV), also known as solar PV, has evolved from a pure niche market of small scale applications towards becoming a mainstream electricity source. A <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cell</a> is a device that converts light directly into electricity using the photoelectric effect. The first solar cell was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fritts" title="Charles Fritts">Charles Fritts</a> in the 1880s. In 1931 a German engineer, Dr Bruno Lange, developed a photo cell using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver_selenide" title="Silver selenide">silver selenide</a> in place of <a href="/wiki/Copper_oxide" title="Copper oxide">copper oxide</a>. Although the prototype <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> cells converted less than 1% of incident light into electricity, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Werner_von_Siemens" title="Ernst Werner von Siemens">Ernst Werner von Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> recognized the importance of this discovery. Following the work of <a href="/wiki/Russell_Ohl" title="Russell Ohl">Russell Ohl</a> in the 1940s, researchers Gerald Pearson, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_Fuller" title="Calvin Fuller">Calvin Fuller</a> and Daryl Chapin created the <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">crystalline silicon</a> solar cell in 1954. These early solar cells cost 286 USD/watt and reached efficiencies of 4.5–6%. By 2012 available efficiencies exceeded 20%, and the maximum efficiency of research photovoltaics was in excess of 40%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concentrated_solar_power">Concentrated solar power</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">Concentrated solar power</a></div>
<p>Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) systems use lenses or mirrors and tracking systems to focus a large area of sunlight into a small beam. The concentrated heat is then used as a heat source for a conventional power plant. A wide range of concentrating technologies exists; the most developed are the parabolic trough, the concentrating linear fresnel reflector, the Stirling dish and the solar power tower. Various techniques are used to track the Sun and focus light. In all of these systems a <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a> is heated by the concentrated sunlight, and is then used for power generation or energy storage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_urban_planning">Architecture and urban planning</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">Passive solar building design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">Urban heat island</a></div>

<p>Sunlight has influenced building design since the beginning of architectural history. Advanced solar architecture and urban planning methods were first employed by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feng_shui#Archaeology" title="Feng shui">Chinese</a>, who oriented their buildings toward the south to provide light and warmth.</p>
<p>The common features of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">passive solar</a> architecture are orientation relative to the Sun, compact proportion (a low surface area to volume ratio), selective shading (overhangs) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_mass_(Building)" title="Thermal mass (Building)">thermal mass</a>. When these features are tailored to the local climate and environment they can produce well-lit spaces that stay in a comfortable temperature range. <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>' Megaron House is a classic example of passive solar design. The most recent approaches to solar design use computer modeling tying together <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">solar lighting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_heating" title="Solar heating">heating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_conditioning" title="Solar air conditioning">ventilation</a> systems in an integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_design" title="Solar design">solar design</a> package. <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">Active solar</a> equipment such as pumps, fans and switchable windows can complement passive design and improve system performance.</p>
<p>Urban heat islands (UHI) are metropolitan areas with higher temperatures than that of the surrounding environment. The higher temperatures result from increased absorption of solar energy by urban materials such as asphalt and concrete, which have lower <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedos</a> and higher <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacities</a> than those in the natural environment. A straightforward method of counteracting the UHI effect is to paint buildings and roads white, and to plant trees in the area. Using these methods, a hypothetical "cool communities" program in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> has projected that urban temperatures could be reduced by approximately 3 °C at an estimated cost of US$1 billion, giving estimated total annual benefits of US$530 million from reduced air-conditioning costs and healthcare savings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture_and_horticulture">Agriculture and horticulture</span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a> seek to optimize the capture of solar energy in order to optimize the productivity of plants. Techniques such as timed planting cycles, tailored row orientation, staggered heights between rows and the mixing of plant varieties can improve crop yields. While sunlight is generally considered a plentiful resource, the exceptions highlight the importance of solar energy to agriculture. During the short growing seasons of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>, French and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Solar power in the United Kingdom">English</a> farmers employed fruit walls to maximize the collection of solar energy. These walls acted as thermal masses and accelerated ripening by keeping plants warm. Early fruit walls were built perpendicular to the ground and facing south, but over time, sloping walls were developed to make better use of sunlight. In 1699, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Fatio_de_Duillier" title="Nicolas Fatio de Duillier">Nicolas Fatio de Duillier</a> even suggested using a <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">tracking mechanism</a> which could pivot to follow the Sun. Applications of solar energy in agriculture aside from growing crops include pumping water, drying crops, brooding chicks and drying chicken manure. More recently the technology has been embraced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winemakers" title="Winemakers">vintners</a>, who use the energy generated by solar panels to power grape presses.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">Greenhouses</a> convert solar light to heat, enabling year-round production and the growth (in enclosed environments) of specialty crops and other plants not naturally suited to the local climate. Primitive greenhouses were first used during Roman times to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cucumbers" title="Cucumbers">cucumbers</a> year-round for the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a>. The first modern greenhouses were built in Europe in the 16th century to keep exotic plants brought back from explorations abroad. Greenhouses remain an important part of horticulture today, and plastic transparent materials have also been used to similar effect in <a href="/wiki/Polytunnel" title="Polytunnel">polytunnels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Row_cover" title="Row cover">row covers</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_vehicle" title="Solar vehicle">Solar vehicle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar-charged_vehicle" title="Solar-charged vehicle">Solar-charged vehicle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_boat" title="Electric boat">Electric boat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_balloon" title="Solar balloon">Solar balloon</a></div>

<p>Development of a solar-powered car has been an engineering goal since the 1980s. The <a href="/wiki/World_Solar_Challenge" title="World Solar Challenge">World Solar Challenge</a> is a biannual solar-powered car race, where teams from universities and enterprises compete over 3,021 kilometres (1,877 mi) across central Australia from <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>. In 1987, when it was founded, the winner's average speed was 67 kilometres per hour (42 mph) and by 2007 the winner's average speed had improved to 90.87 kilometres per hour (56.46 mph). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Solar_Challenge" title="North American Solar Challenge">North American Solar Challenge</a> and the planned <a href="/wiki/South_African_Solar_Challenge" title="South African Solar Challenge">South African Solar Challenge</a> are comparable competitions that reflect an international interest in the engineering and development of solar powered vehicles.</p>
<p>Some vehicles use solar panels for auxiliary power, such as for air conditioning, to keep the interior cool, thus reducing fuel consumption.</p>
<p>In 1975, the first practical solar boat was constructed in England. By 1995, passenger boats incorporating PV panels began appearing and are now used extensively. In 1996, <a href="/wiki/Kenichi_Horie" title="Kenichi Horie">Kenichi Horie</a> made the first solar-powered crossing of the Pacific Ocean, and the <i>Sun21</i> catamaran made the first solar-powered crossing of the Atlantic Ocean in the winter of 2006–2007. There were plans to circumnavigate the globe in 2010.</p>
<p>In 1974, the unmanned <a href="/wiki/AstroFlight_Sunrise" title="AstroFlight Sunrise">AstroFlight Sunrise</a> airplane made the first solar flight. On 29 April 1979, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Riser" title="Solar Riser">Solar Riser</a></i> made the first flight in a solar-powered, fully controlled, man-carrying flying machine, reaching an altitude of 40 feet (12 m). In 1980, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gossamer_Albatross#Solar-powered_variants" title="Gossamer Albatross">Gossamer Penguin</a></i> made the first piloted flights powered solely by photovoltaics. This was quickly followed by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Challenger" title="Solar Challenger">Solar Challenger</a></i> which crossed the English Channel in July 1981. In 1990 <a href="/wiki/Eric_Scott_Raymond" title="Eric Scott Raymond">Eric Scott Raymond</a> in 21 hops flew from California to North Carolina using solar power. Developments then turned back to unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV) with the <i><a href="/wiki/NASA_Pathfinder" title="NASA Pathfinder">Pathfinder</a></i> (1997) and subsequent designs, culminating in the <i><a href="/wiki/Helios_Prototype" title="Helios Prototype">Helios</a></i> which set the altitude record for a non-rocket-propelled aircraft at 29,524 metres (96,864 ft) in 2001. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QinetiQ_Zephyr" title="QinetiQ Zephyr">Zephyr</a></i>, developed by <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>, is the latest in a line of record-breaking solar aircraft, making a 54-hour flight in 2007, and month-long flights were envisioned by 2010. As of 2016, <a href="/wiki/Solar_Impulse" title="Solar Impulse">Solar Impulse</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electric_aircraft" title="Electric aircraft">electric aircraft</a>, is currently circumnavigating the globe. It is a single-seat plane powered by <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> and capable of taking off under its own power. The design allows the aircraft to remain airborne for several days.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Solar_balloon" title="Solar balloon">solar balloon</a> is a black balloon that is filled with ordinary air. As sunlight shines on the balloon, the air inside is heated and expands causing an upward <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> force, much like an artificially heated <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloon</a>. Some solar balloons are large enough for human flight, but usage is generally limited to the toy market as the surface-area to payload-weight ratio is relatively high.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_production">Fuel production</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_chemical" title="Solar chemical">Solar chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_fuel" title="Solar fuel">Solar fuel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_photosynthesis" title="Artificial photosynthesis">Artificial photosynthesis</a></div>
<p>Solar chemical processes use solar energy to drive chemical reactions. These processes offset energy that would otherwise come from a fossil fuel source and can also convert solar energy into storable and transportable fuels. Solar induced chemical reactions can be divided into thermochemical or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical" title="Photochemical">photochemical</a>. A variety of fuels can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Artificial_photosynthesis" title="Artificial photosynthesis">artificial photosynthesis</a>. The multielectron catalytic chemistry involved in making carbon-based fuels (such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>) from reduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is challenging; a feasible alternative is <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> production from protons, though use of water as the source of electrons (as plants do) requires mastering the multielectron oxidation of two water molecules to molecular oxygen. Some have envisaged working solar fuel plants in coastal metropolitan areas by 2050 –  the splitting of sea water providing hydrogen to be run through adjacent fuel-cell electric power plants and the pure water by-product going directly into the municipal water system. Another vision involves all human structures covering the earth's surface (i.e., roads, vehicles and buildings) doing photosynthesis more efficiently than plants.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">Hydrogen production</a> technologies have been a significant area of solar chemical research since the 1970s. Aside from electrolysis driven by photovoltaic or photochemical cells, several thermochemical processes have also been explored. One such route uses concentrators to split water into oxygen and hydrogen at high temperatures (2,300–2,600 °C or 4,200–4,700 °F). Another approach uses the heat from solar concentrators to drive the <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reformation</a> of natural gas thereby increasing the overall hydrogen yield compared to conventional reforming methods. Thermochemical cycles characterized by the decomposition and regeneration of reactants present another avenue for hydrogen production. The Solzinc process under development at the <a href="/wiki/Weizmann_Institute_of_Science" title="Weizmann Institute of Science">Weizmann Institute of Science</a> uses a 1 MW solar furnace to decompose <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a> (ZnO) at temperatures above 1,200 °C (2,200 °F). This initial reaction produces pure zinc, which can subsequently be reacted with water to produce hydrogen.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_storage_methods">Energy storage methods</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">Energy storage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">Seasonal thermal energy storage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_change_material" title="Phase change material">Phase change material</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">Grid energy storage</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V2G" title="V2G">V2G</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">Thermal mass</a> systems can store solar energy in the form of heat at domestically useful temperatures for daily or <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">interseasonal durations</a>. Thermal storage systems generally use readily available materials with high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat" title="Specific heat">specific heat</a> capacities such as water, earth and stone. Well-designed systems can lower <a href="/wiki/Peak_demand" title="Peak demand">peak demand</a>, shift time-of-use to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/off-peak" title="wikt:off-peak">off-peak</a> hours and reduce overall heating and cooling requirements.</p>
<p>Phase change materials such as <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin wax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate#Thermal_storage" title="Sodium sulfate">Glauber's salt</a> are another thermal storage medium. These materials are inexpensive, readily available, and can deliver domestically useful temperatures (approximately 64 °C or 147 °F). The "Dover House" (in <a href="/wiki/Dover,_Massachusetts" title="Dover, Massachusetts">Dover, Massachusetts</a>) was the first to use a Glauber's salt heating system, in 1948. Solar energy can also be stored at high temperatures using <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">molten salts</a>. Salts are an effective storage medium because they are low-cost, have a high specific heat capacity and can deliver heat at temperatures compatible with conventional power systems. The <a href="/wiki/The_Solar_Project#Solar_Two" title="The Solar Project">Solar Two</a> project used this method of energy storage, allowing it to store 1.44 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">terajoules</a> (400,000 kWh) in its 68 m³ storage tank with an annual storage efficiency of about 99%.</p>
<p>Off-grid <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">PV systems</a> have traditionally used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rechargeable_batteries" title="Rechargeable batteries">rechargeable batteries</a> to store excess electricity. With grid-tied systems, excess electricity can be sent to the transmission <a href="/wiki/Grid-tied_electrical_system" title="Grid-tied electrical system">grid</a>, while standard grid electricity can be used to meet shortfalls. <a href="/wiki/Net_metering" title="Net metering">Net metering</a> programs give household systems a credit for any electricity they deliver to the grid. This is handled by 'rolling back' the meter whenever the home produces more electricity than it consumes. If the net electricity use is below zero, the utility then rolls over the kilowatt hour credit to the next month. Other approaches involve the use of two meters, to measure electricity consumed vs. electricity produced. This is less common due to the increased installation cost of the second meter. Most standard meters accurately measure in both directions, making a second meter unnecessary.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">Pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a> stores energy in the form of water pumped when energy is available from a lower elevation reservoir to a higher elevation one. The energy is recovered when demand is high by releasing the water, with the pump becoming a hydroelectric power generator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development.2C_deployment_and_economics">Development, deployment and economics</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deployment_of_solar_power_to_energy_grids" title="Deployment of solar power to energy grids">Deployment of solar power to energy grids</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_electricity_by_source" title="Cost of electricity by source">Cost of electricity by source</a></div>
<p>Beginning with the surge in <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> use which accompanied the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, energy consumption has steadily transitioned from wood and biomass to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. The early development of solar technologies starting in the 1860s was driven by an expectation that coal would soon become scarce. However, development of solar technologies stagnated in the early 20th century in the face of the increasing availability, economy, and utility of coal and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil embargo</a> and <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">1979 energy crisis</a> caused a reorganization of energy policies around the world and brought renewed attention to developing solar technologies. Deployment strategies focused on incentive programs such as the Federal Photovoltaic Utilization Program in the U.S. and the Sunshine Program in Japan. Other efforts included the formation of research facilities in the U.S. (SERI, now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NREL" title="NREL">NREL</a>), Japan (<a href="/wiki/New_Energy_and_Industrial_Technology_Development_Organization" title="New Energy and Industrial Technology Development Organization">NEDO</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Germany" title="Solar power in Germany">Germany</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Institute for Solar Energy Systems ISE</a>).</p>
<p>Commercial solar water heaters began appearing in the United States in the 1890s. These systems saw increasing use until the 1920s but were gradually replaced by cheaper and more reliable heating fuels. As with photovoltaics, <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> attracted renewed attention as a result of the oil crises in the 1970s but interest subsided in the 1980s due to falling petroleum prices. Development in the solar water heating sector progressed steadily throughout the 1990s and annual growth rates have averaged 20% since 1999. Although generally underestimated, solar water heating and cooling is by far the most widely deployed solar technology with an estimated capacity of 154 GW as of 2007.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> has said that solar energy can make considerable contributions to solving some of the most urgent problems the world now faces:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The development of affordable, inexhaustible and clean solar energy technologies will have huge longer-term benefits. It will increase countries’ energy security through reliance on an indigenous, inexhaustible and mostly import-independent resource, enhance sustainability, reduce pollution, lower the costs of mitigating climate change, and keep fossil fuel prices lower than otherwise. These advantages are global. Hence the additional costs of the incentives for early deployment should be considered learning investments; they must be wisely spent and need to be widely shared.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 2011, a report by the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> found that solar energy technologies such as photovoltaics, solar hot water and concentrated solar power could provide a third of the world’s energy by 2060 if politicians commit to limiting <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The energy from the sun could play a key role in de-carbonizing the global economy alongside improvements in <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> and imposing costs on <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emitters. "The strength of solar is the incredible variety and flexibility of applications, from small scale to big scale".</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We have proved ... that after our stores of oil and coal are exhausted the human race can receive unlimited power from the rays of the sun.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Frank_Shuman" title="Frank Shuman">Frank Shuman</a>, New York Times, 2 July 1916</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ISO_standards">ISO standards</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> has established several standards relating to solar energy equipment. For example, ISO 9050 relates to glass in building while ISO 10217 relates to the materials used in solar water heaters.</p>
