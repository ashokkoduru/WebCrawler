<p><b>Grindavík</b> is a fishing town at the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Peninsula_(Iceland)" title="Southern Peninsula (Iceland)">Southern Peninsula</a> at the southwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.</p>
<p>It is one of the few cities with a harbour at this coast. Most of the inhabitants work in the fishing industry. The <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lagoon_(geothermal_spa)" title="Blue Lagoon (geothermal spa)">Blue Lagoon</a>, Grindavík's premiere attraction, is located 3 miles (4.8 km) from the town centre.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landn%C3%A1ma" title="Landnáma">Landnáma</a> or <i>The Book of Settlements</i> mentions that around 934 two Viking settlers, Molda-Gnúpur Hrólfsson and Þórir Haustmyrkur Vígbjóðsson, arrived in the <a href="/wiki/Reykjanes" title="Reykjanes">Reykjanes</a> area. Þórir settled in Selvogur, and Krísuvík and Molda-Gnúpur in Grindavík.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.grindavik.is" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<p>The sons of Moldar-Gnúpur established three settlements; Þórkötlustaðahverfi, Járngerðarstaðarhverfi and Staðarhverfi. The modern version of Grindavik is situated mainly in what was Járngerðarstaðarhverfi.</p>
<p>The origins of the municipality can be traced to Einar Einarsson's decision to move there to build and run a shop in 1897. During that time the population was only around 360. Fishing had for centuries been a crucial element in the survival of Grindavik's population, but fishing trips were often dangerous. Men were frequently lost at sea and the catch not always stable. However, when a safer access point to land was created at Hópið in 1939, fishing conditions changed dramatically. From 1950 serious development in the fishing industry had begun to take place. Grindavik was declared a municipality in 1974.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span></h2>
<p>A short distance to the north, there is the <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Lagoon_(geothermal_spa)" title="Blue Lagoon (geothermal spa)">Blue Lagoon</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>: <span lang="is"><i>Bláa Lónið</i></span>), a geothermal spa using hot and mineralized waters from the nearby Svartsengi power station.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ungmennaf%C3%A9lag_Grindav%C3%ADkur" title="Ungmennafélag Grindavíkur">Ungmennafélag Grindavíkur</a> (Umfg) is the town's sport club, and the town contains the <a href="/wiki/Grindav%C3%ADkurv%C3%B6llur" title="Grindavíkurvöllur">Grindavíkurvöllur</a> stadium.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leif_the_Lucky_Bridge" title="Leif the Lucky Bridge">Leif the Lucky Bridge</a> spans the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lfagj%C3%A1_rift_valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Álfagjá rift valley (page does not exist)">Álfagjá rift valley</a> that marks the boundary of the Eurasian and North American continental tectonic plates. It was built in 2002 and named in honor of Icelandic explorer <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a>, who travelled from Europe to explore North America 500 years before Columbus.</p>
<p>The Icelandic Saltfish Museum in Grindavík opened in September 2002. It displays the story of salt fish production and its importance for the Icelandic economy throughout the centuries in a specially designed building of 650 square metres (7,000 sq ft).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_residents">Notable residents</span></h2>
<p>The Icelandic writer <a href="/wiki/Gu%C3%B0bergur_Bergsson" title="Guðbergur Bergsson">Guðbergur Bergsson</a> was born here, and <a href="/wiki/Kalli_Bjarni" title="Kalli Bjarni">Kalli Bjarni</a>, the first winner of the Icelandic version of <a href="/wiki/Pop_Idol" title="Pop Idol">Pop Idol</a>, lives in the town. The Spanish publisher and writer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jaime_Salinas_Bonmat%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jaime Salinas Bonmatí (page does not exist)">Jaime Salinas Bonmatí</a>, engaged to Guðbergur Bergsson, lived, died and is buried here. Icelandic footballer <a href="/wiki/Alfre%C3%B0_Finnbogason" title="Alfreð Finnbogason">Alfreð Finnbogason</a> was also born in Grindavík.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_Communication_Facility">Naval Communication Facility</span></h2>
<p>Near Grindavík, the United States Navy operates <a href="/wiki/Naval_Radio_Transmitter_Facility_Grindavik" title="Naval Radio Transmitter Facility Grindavik">Naval Radio Transmitter Facility Grindavik</a>. It uses several antennas, including two guyed masts. The mast situated at 63°51′1″N 22°28′0″W was built in 1993 and is 304.8 meters tall. The other mast at 63°51′3″N 22°27′6″W was built in 1983 and is 182.9 meters tall. The taller mast replaced a 243.8 meter tall mast, and the second replaced a mast of same height.</p>
