<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es"><i>España</i></span> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[esˈpaɲa]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Es-Espa%C3%B1a.ogg" title="File:Es-España.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/64/Es-Espa%C3%B1a.ogg" title="Es-España.ogg">listen</a>)</small>), officially the <b>Kingdom of Spain</b> (Spanish: <span lang="es"><i>Reino de España</i></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> largely located on the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southwestern Europe</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, and several small territories on and near the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> coast. <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spain" title="Peninsular Spain">Its mainland</a> is bordered to the south and east by the Mediterranean Sea except for a small land boundary with <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>; to the north and northeast by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a>; and to the west and northwest by <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and the Atlantic Ocean. Along with France and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, it is one of only three countries to have both Atlantic and Mediterranean coastlines. Extending to 1,214 km (754 mi), the <a href="/wiki/Portugal%E2%80%93Spain_border" title="Portugal–Spain border">Portugal–Spain border</a> is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_territories_by_land_borders" title="List of countries and territories by land borders">longest uninterrupted border</a> within the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</p>
<p>Spanish territory includes two archipelagos: the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, in the Mediterranean Sea, and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, in the Atlantic Ocean off the African coast. It also includes two major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclaves" title="Exclaves">exclaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>, in continental North Africa; and the islands and <i><a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n" title="Peñón">peñones</a></i> (rocks) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_de_Albor%C3%A1n" title="Isla de Alborán">Alborán</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_Alhucemas" title="Peñón de Alhucemas">Alhucemas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chafarinas_Islands" title="Chafarinas Islands">Chafarinas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera" title="Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera">Vélez de la Gomera</a>. With an area of 505,990 km (195,360 sq mi), Spain is the largest country in Southern Europe, the second largest country in Western Europe and the European Union, and the <a href="/wiki/Area_and_population_of_European_countries" title="Area and population of European countries">fourth largest country in the European continent</a>. By population, Spain is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_countries_by_population" title="List of European countries by population">sixth</a> largest in Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union#List" title="Member state of the European Union">fifth</a> in the European Union, after <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Spain's capital and largest city is <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, other major <a href="/wiki/List_of_metropolitan_areas_in_Spain" title="List of metropolitan areas in Spain">urban areas</a> include <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" title="Anatomically modern humans">Modern humans</a> first arrived in the Iberian Peninsula around 35,000 years ago. <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberian</a> cultures along with ancient <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carthaginian_Iberia" title="Carthaginian Iberia">Carthaginian</a> settlements developed on the peninsula until it came under Roman rule around 200 BCE, after which the region was named <i><a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a></i>. In the Middle Ages, the area was conquered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_tribes" title="Germanic tribes">Germanic tribes</a> and later by the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>. Spain emerged as a unified country in the 15th century, following the marriage of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> and the completion of the centuries-long reconquest, or <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i>, of the peninsula from the Moors in 1492. In the early modern period, Spain became one of history's first <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_empires" title="List of largest empires">global colonial empires</a>, leaving a vast cultural and linguistic legacy that includes over 500 million <a href="/wiki/Hispanophone" title="Hispanophone">Spanish speakers</a>, making Spanish the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_number_of_native_speakers" title="List of languages by number of native speakers">second most spoken first language</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> and before <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.</p>
<p>Spain is a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> organised in the form of a parliamentary government under a constitutional <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Spain" title="Monarchy of Spain">monarchy</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Middle_power" title="Middle power">middle power</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed country</a> with the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(nominal)" title="List of countries by GDP (nominal)">fourteenth largest economy</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominal_GDP" title="Nominal GDP">nominal GDP</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP)">sixteenth largest</a> by <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a>. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN), the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU), the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> (CoE), the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Ibero-American_States" title="Organization of Ibero-American States">Organization of Ibero-American States</a> (OEI), the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD), the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> (WTO) and many other international organisations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The origins of the Roman name <i><a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a></i>, from which the modern name <i>España</i> was derived, are uncertain due to inadequate evidence. Down the centuries there have been a number of accounts and hypotheses:</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">Antonio de Nebrija</a> proposed that the word <i>Hispania</i> evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_language" title="Iberian language">Iberian</a> word <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispalis" title="Hispalis">Hispalis</a></i>, meaning "city of the western world".</p>
<p>Jesús Luis Cunchillos argues that the root of the term <i>span</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_language" title="Phoenician language">Phoenician</a> word <i>spy</i>, meaning "to forge metals". Therefore, <i>i-spn-ya</i> would mean "the land where metals are forged". It may be a derivation of the Phoenician <i>I-Shpania</i>, meaning "island of rabbits", "land of rabbits" or "edge", a reference to Spain's location at the end of the Mediterranean; Roman coins struck in the region from the reign of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> show a female figure with a <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a> at her feet, and <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> called it the "land of the rabbits".</p>
<p><i>Hispania</i> may derive from the poetic use of the term <i>Hesperia</i>, reflecting the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> perception of <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> as a "western land" or "land of the setting sun" (<i>Hesperia</i>, <i>Ἑσπερία</i> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>) and Spain, being still further west, as <i>Hesperia ultima</i>.</p>
<p>There is the claim that "Hispania" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> word <i>Ezpanna</i> meaning "edge" or "border", another reference to the fact that the Iberian Peninsula constitutes the southwest corner of the European continent.</p>
<p>Two 15th-century Spanish Jewish scholars, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Isaac_Abrabanel" title="Don Isaac Abrabanel">Don Isaac Abrabanel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solomon_ibn_Verga" title="Solomon ibn Verga">Solomon ibn Verga</a>, gave an explanation now considered folkloric. Both men wrote in two different published works that the first Jews to reach Spain were brought by ship by Phiros who was confederate with the king of Babylon when he laid siege to Jerusalem. This man was a <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Grecian</a> by birth, but who had been given a kingdom in Spain. He became related by marriage to Espan, the nephew of king Heracles, who also ruled over a kingdom in Spain. Heracles later renounced his throne in preference for his native Greece, leaving his kingdom to his nephew, Espan, from whom the country of <i>España</i> (Spain) took its name. Based upon their testimonies, this eponym would have already been in use in Spain by c. 350 BCE.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Spain" title="History of Spain">History of Spain</a></div>

<p>Iberia enters written records as a land populated largely by the <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>. After an <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Roman conquest of Hispania">arduous conquest</a>, the peninsula came under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. During the early Middle Ages it came under Germanic rule but later, much of it was conquered by Moorish invaders from North Africa. In a process that took centuries, the small Christian kingdoms in the north gradually regained control of the peninsula. The last Moorish kingdom fell in the same year Columbus reached the Americas. A global empire began which saw Spain become the strongest kingdom in Europe, the leading world power for a century and a half, and the largest overseas empire for three centuries.</p>
<p>Continued wars and other problems eventually led to a diminished status. The Napoleonic invasions of Spain led to chaos, triggering independence movements that tore apart most of the empire and left the country politically unstable. Prior to the Second World War, Spain suffered a devastating civil war and came under the rule of an authoritarian government, which oversaw a period of stagnation that was followed by a surge in the growth of the economy. Eventually democracy was peacefully restored in the form of a parliamentary constitutional monarchy. Spain joined the European Union, experiencing a cultural renaissance and steady economic growth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_pre-Roman_peoples">Prehistory and pre-Roman peoples</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Iberia" title="Prehistoric Iberia">Prehistoric Iberia</a></div>

<p>Archaeological research at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological_Site_of_Atapuerca" title="Archaeological Site of Atapuerca">Atapuerca</a> indicates the Iberian Peninsula was populated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominids</a> 1.2 million years ago. In <a href="/wiki/Atapuerca_Mountains" title="Atapuerca Mountains">Atapuerca</a> fossils have been found of the earliest known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominins" title="Hominins">hominins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Homo_antecessor" title="Homo antecessor">Homo antecessor</a>. Modern humans first arrived in Iberia, from the north on foot, about 35,000 years ago. The best known artefacts of these prehistoric human settlements are the famous paintings in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altamira_(cave)" title="Altamira (cave)">Altamira cave</a> of Cantabria in northern Iberia, which were created from 35,600 to 13,500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Common_Era" title="Before Common Era">BCE</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cro-Magnon" title="Cro-Magnon">Cro-Magnon</a>. Archaeological and genetic evidence suggests that the Iberian Peninsula acted as one of several major refugia from which northern Europe was repopulated following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">last ice age</a>.</p>
<p>The largest groups inhabiting the Iberian Peninsula before the Roman conquest were the <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">Iberians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>. The Iberians inhabited the Mediterranean side of the peninsula, from the northeast to the southeast. The Celts inhabited much of the inner and Atlantic sides of the peninsula, from the northwest to the southwest. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">Basques</a> occupied the western area of the Pyrenees mountain range and adjacent areas, the <a href="/wiki/Tartessos" title="Tartessos">Tartessians</a> were in the southwest and the <a href="/wiki/Lusitanians" title="Lusitanians">Lusitanians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vettones" title="Vettones">Vettones</a> occupied areas in the central west. A number of trading settlements of <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_colonies" title="Greek colonies">Greeks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthaginians" title="Carthaginians">Carthaginians</a> developed on the Mediterranean coast.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Empire_and_the_Gothic_Kingdom">Roman Empire and the Gothic Kingdom</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic Kingdom</a></div>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">Second Punic War</a>, an expanding <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> captured Carthaginian trading colonies along the Mediterranean coast from roughly 210 to 205 BC. It took the Romans nearly two centuries to complete the conquest of the Iberian Peninsula, though they had control of it for over six centuries. Roman rule was bound together by law, language, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_road" title="Roman road">Roman road</a>.</p>

<p>The cultures of the Celtic and Iberian populations were gradually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanisation_(cultural)" title="Romanisation (cultural)">Romanised</a> (Latinised) at differing rates in different parts of <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Baetica" title="Hispania Baetica">Hispania</a>. Local leaders were admitted into the Roman aristocratic class. Hispania served as a granary for the Roman market, and its harbours exported <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>. Agricultural production increased with the introduction of irrigation projects, some of which remain in use. Emperors <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>, and the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca</a> were born in Hispania. <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> was introduced into Hispania in the 1st century AD and it became popular in the cities in the 2nd century AD. Most of Spain's present languages and religion, and the basis of its laws, originate from this period.</p>

<p>The weakening of the Western Roman Empire's jurisdiction in Hispania began in 409, when the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">Vandals</a>, together with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmatian" title="Sarmatian">Sarmatian</a> <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>, crossed the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and ravaged <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> drove them into Iberia that same year. The Suebi established a kingdom in what is today modern <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. As the western empire disintegrated, the social and economic base became greatly simplified: but even in modified form, the successor regimes maintained many of the institutions and laws of the late empire, including Christianity and assimilation to the evolving Roman culture.</p>
<p>The Alans' allies, the <a href="/wiki/Hasdingi" title="Hasdingi">Hasdingi</a> Vandals, established a kingdom in <a href="/wiki/Gallaecia" title="Gallaecia">Gallaecia</a>, too, occupying largely the same region but extending farther south to the <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> river. The <a href="/wiki/Silingi" title="Silingi">Silingi</a> Vandals occupied the region that still bears a form of their name—<i>Vandalusia</i>, modern <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, in Spain. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantines</a> established an enclave, <a href="/wiki/Spania" title="Spania">Spania</a>, in the south, with the intention of reviving the Roman empire throughout Iberia. Eventually, however, Hispania was reunited under <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic rule</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">archbishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, was an influential philosopher and was much studied in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Also, his theories were vital to the conversion of the <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic Kingdom</a> to a Catholic one, in the <a href="/wiki/Councils_of_Toledo" title="Councils of Toledo">Councils of Toledo</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> kingdom was the first Christian kingdom ruling in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> it was the referent for the different kingdoms fighting against the Muslim rule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages:_Muslim_invasion_and_Reconquista">Middle Ages: Muslim invasion and Reconquista</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></div>

<p>In the 8th century, nearly all of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> was <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">conquered</a> (711–718) by largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moorish" title="Moorish">Moorish</a> <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> armies from North Africa. These conquests were part of the expansion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a>. Only a small area in the mountainous north-west of the peninsula managed to resist the initial invasion.</p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>, Christians and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> were given the subordinate status of <a href="/wiki/Dhimmi" title="Dhimmi">dhimmi</a>. This status permitted Christians and Jews to practice their religions as <i><a href="/wiki/People_of_the_Book" title="People of the Book">People of the Book</a></i> but they were required to pay a special tax and had legal and social rights inferior to those of Muslims.</p>
<p>Conversion to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> proceeded at an increasing pace. The <i><a href="/wiki/Muladi" title="Muladi">muladíes</a></i> (Muslims of ethnic <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> origin) are believed to have comprised the majority of the population of Al-Andalus by the end of the 10th century.</p>
<p>The Muslim community in the Iberian Peninsula was itself diverse and beset by social tensions. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber people</a> of North Africa, who had provided the bulk of the invading armies, <a href="/wiki/Berber_Revolt" title="Berber Revolt">clashed with the Arab leadership</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. Over time, large Moorish populations became established, especially in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guadalquivir_River" title="Guadalquivir River">Guadalquivir River</a> valley, the coastal plain of <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">Valencia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebro_River" title="Ebro River">Ebro River</a> valley and (towards the end of this period) in the mountainous region of <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">Córdoba</a>, the capital of the caliphate since <a href="/wiki/Abd-ar-Rahman_III" title="Abd-ar-Rahman III">Abd-ar-Rahman III</a>, was the largest, richest and most sophisticated city in western Europe. Mediterranean trade and cultural exchange flourished. Muslims imported a rich intellectual tradition from the Middle East and North Africa. Muslim and Jewish scholars played an important part in reviving and expanding classical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_culture" title="Greek culture">Greek learning</a> in Western Europe. Some important philosophers at the time were <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Arabi" title="Ibn Arabi">Ibn Arabi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanisation_(cultural)" title="Romanisation (cultural)">Romanised</a> cultures of the Iberian Peninsula interacted with Muslim and Jewish cultures in complex ways, giving the region a distinctive culture. Outside the cities, where the vast majority lived, the land ownership system from Roman times remained largely intact as Muslim leaders rarely dispossessed landowners and the introduction of new crops and techniques led to an expansion of agriculture.</p>

<p>In the 11th century, the Muslim holdings fractured into rival <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">Taifa</a> kingdoms, allowing the small Christian states the opportunity to greatly enlarge their territories. The arrival from <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> of the Islamic ruling sects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravids" title="Almoravids">Almoravids</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohads" title="Almohads">Almohads</a> restored unity upon the Muslim holdings, with a stricter, less tolerant application of Islam, and saw a revival in Muslim fortunes. This re-united Islamic state experienced more than a century of successes that partially reversed Christian gains.</p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i> (Reconquest) was the centuries-long period in which Christian rule was re-established over the Iberian Peninsula. The Reconquista is viewed as beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Covadonga" title="Battle of Covadonga">Battle of Covadonga</a> won by <a href="/wiki/Pelagius_of_Asturias" title="Pelagius of Asturias">Don Pelayo</a> in 722 and was concurrent with the period of Muslim rule on the Iberian Peninsula. The Christian army's victory over Muslim forces led to the creation of the Christian <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Kingdom of Asturias</a> along the northwestern coastal mountains. Shortly after, in 739, Muslim forces were driven from <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, which was to eventually host one of medieval Europe's holiest sites, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a> and was incorporated into the new Christian kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">Kingdom of León</a> was the strongest Christian kingdom for centuries. In 1188 the first modern parliamentary session in Europe was held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n_(Spain)" title="León (Spain)">León</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cortes_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Cortes of León">Cortes of León</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">Kingdom of Castile</a>, formed from Leonese territory, was its successor as strongest kingdom. The kings and the nobility fought for power and influence in this period. The example of the Roman emperors influenced the political objective of the Crown, while the nobles benefited from <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>.</p>
<p>Muslim armies had also moved north of the Pyrenees but they were defeated by Frankish forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tours" title="Battle of Tours">Battle of Poitiers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Frankia</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> forces established <a href="/wiki/Marca_Hispanica" title="Marca Hispanica">Christian counties</a> on the southern side of the Pyrenees. These areas were to grow into the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">Navarre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Aragon</a>. For several centuries, the fluctuating frontier between the Muslim and Christian controlled areas of Iberia was along the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> valleys.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/County_of_Barcelona" title="County of Barcelona">County of Barcelona</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">Kingdom of Aragon</a> entered in a dynastic union and gained territory and power in the mediterranean. In 1229 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mallorca" title="Mallorca">Mallorca</a> was conquered, so was <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> in 1238.</p>
<p>The break-up of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> into the competing taifa kingdoms helped the long embattled Iberian Christian kingdoms gain the initiative. The capture of the strategically central city of <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a> in 1085 marked a significant shift in the balance of power in favour of the Christian kingdoms. Following a great Muslim resurgence in the 12th century, the great Moorish strongholds in the south fell to Christian Spain in the 13th century—Córdoba in 1236 and <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> in 1248. The last Nasrid sultanate of <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>, a Muslim <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tributary_(political)" title="Tributary (political)">tributary state</a> would finally surrender in 1492 to the Catholic monarchs <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Queen Isabella I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">King Ferdinand II</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Arag%C3%B3n" title="Crown of Aragón">Aragon</a>.</p>

<p>From the mid 13th century, literature and philosophy started to flourish again in the Christian peninsular kingdoms, based on Roman and Gothic traditions. An important philosopher from this time is <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Llull" title="Ramon Llull">Ramon Llull</a>. <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Cresques" title="Abraham Cresques">Abraham Cresques</a> was a prominent Jewish cartographer. <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> and its institutions were the model for the legislators. The king <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">Alfonso X of Castile</a> focused on strengthening this Roman and Gothic past, and also on linking the Iberian Christian kingdoms with the rest of medieval European Christendom. He worked for being elected emperor of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and published the <a href="/wiki/Siete_Partidas" title="Siete Partidas">Siete Partidas</a> code. The <a href="/wiki/Toledo_School_of_Translators" title="Toledo School of Translators">Toledo School of Translators</a> is the name that commonly describes the group of scholars who worked together in the city of Toledo during the 12th and 13th centuries, to translate many of the philosophical and scientific works from <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">Classical Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Hebrew" title="Ancient Hebrew">Ancient Hebrew</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmission_of_the_Classics" title="Transmission of the Classics">Islamic transmission of the classics</a> is the main <a href="/wiki/Islamic_contributions_to_Medieval_Europe" title="Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe">Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">Castilian</a> language—more commonly known (especially later in history and at present) as "Spanish" after becoming the national language and <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> of Spain—evolved from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a>, as did other <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a> of Spain like the <a href="/wiki/Old_Catalan" title="Old Catalan">Catalan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese" title="Astur-Leonese">Asturian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">Galician</a> languages, as well as other Romance languages in Latin Europe. <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>, the only non-Romance language in Spain, continued evolving from Early Basque to Medieval. The <a href="/wiki/Glosas_Emilianenses" title="Glosas Emilianenses">Glosas Emilianenses</a> founded in the <a href="/wiki/Monasteries_of_San_Mill%C3%A1n_de_la_Cogolla" title="Monasteries of San Millán de la Cogolla">monasteries</a> of <a href="/wiki/San_Mill%C3%A1n_de_la_Cogolla" title="San Millán de la Cogolla">San Millán de la Cogolla</a> contain the first written words in both Basque and Spanish, having the first become an influence in the formation of the second as an evolution of Latin.</p>

<p>In the 13th and 14th centuries, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinid" title="Marinid">Marinid</a> Muslim sect based in North Africa invaded and established some enclaves on the southern coast but failed in their attempt to re-establish Muslim rule in Iberia and were soon driven out. The 13th century also witnessed the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>, centred in Spain's north east, expand its reach across islands in the Mediterranean, to <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and even Athens. Around this time the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Palencia" title="University of Palencia">Palencia</a> (1212/1263) and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salamanca" title="University of Salamanca">Salamanca</a> (1218/1254) were established. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> of 1348 and 1349 devastated Spain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_Spain">Imperial Spain</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a></div>

<p>In 1469, the crowns of the Christian kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a> and Aragon were united by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">marriage</a> of <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella I of Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>. 1478 commenced the completion of the conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and in 1492, the combined forces of Castile and Aragon captured the Emirate of Granada, ending the last remnant of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Muslim_presence_in_the_Iberian_peninsula" title="Timeline of the Muslim presence in the Iberian peninsula">781-year presence</a> of Islamic rule in Iberia. That same year, <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Spain's Jews</a> were <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">ordered to convert</a> to <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> or face <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">expulsion</a> from Spanish territories during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">Spanish Inquisition</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Granada" title="Treaty of Granada">Treaty of Granada</a> guaranteed religious tolerance towards Muslims, and although the tolerance was only partial, it was not until the beginning of the 17th century, following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morisco_rebellions_in_Granada" title="Morisco rebellions in Granada">Revolt of the Alpujarras</a>, that Muslims were finally expelled.</p>
<p>The year 1492 also marked the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, during a voyage funded by Isabella. Columbus's first voyage crossed the Atlantic and reached the Caribbean Islands, beginning the European exploration and conquest of the Americas, although he remained convinced that he had reached the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a>. The colonisation of the Americas started, with <i><a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadores</a></i> like <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Pizarro" title="Francisco Pizarro">Francisco Pizarro</a>. <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">Miscegenation</a> was the rule between the native and the European cultures and people.</p>

<p>As <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Monarchs" title="New Monarchs">New Monarchs</a>, Isabella and Ferdinand centralised royal power at the expense of local nobility, and the word <i>España</i>, whose root is the ancient name <i>Hispania</i>, began to be commonly used to designate the whole of the two kingdoms. With their wide-ranging political, legal, religious and military reforms, Spain emerged as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_powers#Spanish_Empire" title="Historical powers">world power</a>.</p>
<p>The unification of the crowns of Aragon and Castile by the marriage of their sovereigns laid the basis for modern Spain and the Spanish Empire, although each kingdom of Spain remained a separate country, in social, political, laws, currency and language.</p>
<p>There were two big revolts against the new Habsburg monarch and the more authoritarian and imperial-style crown: <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Comuneros" title="Revolt of the Comuneros">Revolt of the Comuneros</a> in <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Brotherhoods" title="Revolt of the Brotherhoods">Revolt of the Brotherhoods</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mallorca" title="Mallorca">Mallorca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>. After years of combat, Comuneros <a href="/wiki/Juan_L%C3%B3pez_de_Padilla" title="Juan López de Padilla">Juan López de Padilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Bravo" title="Juan Bravo">Juan Bravo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Maldonado" title="Francisco Maldonado">Francisco Maldonado</a> were executed and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Pacheco" title="Maria Pacheco">Maria Pacheco</a> went into exile. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germana_de_Foix" title="Germana de Foix">Germana de Foix</a> also finished with the revolt in the mediterranean.</p>

<p>Spain was Europe's leading power throughout the 16th century and most of the 17th century, a position reinforced by trade and wealth from colonial possessions and became the world's leading <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy" title="Spanish Navy">maritime power</a>. It reached its apogee during the reigns of the first two <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish Habsburgs</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Spain" title="Charles I of Spain">Charles I</a> (1516–1556) and <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a> (1556–1598). This period saw the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Comuneros" title="Revolt of the Comuneros">Revolt of the Comuneros</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt" title="Dutch Revolt">Dutch Revolt</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morisco_rebellions_in_Granada" title="Morisco rebellions in Granada">Morisco Revolt</a>, clashes with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman-Habsburg_wars" title="Ottoman-Habsburg wars">Ottomans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585–1604)">Anglo-Spanish War</a> and wars with <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France#The_French_Renaissance" title="Early modern France">France</a>.</p>

<p>Through exploration and conquest or royal marriage alliances and inheritance, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> expanded to include vast areas in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, islands in the Asia-Pacific area, areas of Italy, cities in Northern Africa, as well as parts of what are now France, Germany, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Magellan%27s_circumnavigation" title="Timeline of Magellan's circumnavigation">first circumnavigation</a> of the world was carried out in 1519–1521. It was the first empire on which it was said that <a href="/wiki/The_empire_on_which_the_sun_never_sets" title="The empire on which the sun never sets">the sun never set</a>. This was an <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>, with daring explorations by sea and by land, the opening-up of new <a href="/wiki/Trade_route" title="Trade route">trade routes</a> across oceans, conquests and the beginnings of European <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>. Spanish explorers brought back <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>, spices, luxuries, and previously unknown plants, and played a leading part in transforming the European understanding of the globe. The cultural efflorescence witnessed during this period is now referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">Spanish Golden Age</a>. The expansion of the empire caused immense upheaval in the Americas as the collapse of societies and empires and new diseases from Europe devastated American indigenous populations. The rise of <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> and new geographical discoveries and conquests raised issues that were addressed by the intellectual movement now known as the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Salamanca" title="School of Salamanca">School of Salamanca</a>, which developed the first modern theories of what are now known as <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>.</p>

<p>In the late 16th century and first half of the 17th century, Spain was confronted by unrelenting challenges from all sides. <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">Barbary pirates</a>, under the aegis of the rapidly growing <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, disrupted life in many coastal areas through their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slave</a> raids and the renewed threat of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Islamic invasion</a>. This was at a time when Spain was often at war with France.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> dragged the kingdom ever more deeply into the mire of religiously charged wars. The result was a country forced into ever expanding military efforts across Europe and in the Mediterranean.</p>
<p>By the middle decades of a war- and <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_Seville" title="Great Plague of Seville">plague</a>-ridden 17th-century Europe, the Spanish Habsburgs had enmeshed the country in continent-wide religious-political conflicts. These conflicts drained it of resources and undermined the economy generally. Spain managed to hold on to most of the scattered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg_empire" title="Habsburg empire">Habsburg empire</a>, and help the imperial forces of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> reverse a large part of the advances made by Protestant forces, but it was finally forced to recognise the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War" title="Portuguese Restoration War">separation of Portugal</a> (with whom it had been <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">united</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> of the crowns from 1580 to 1640) and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and eventually suffered some serious military reverses to France in the latter stages of the immensely destructive, Europe-wide <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>.</p>

<p>In the latter half of the 17th century, Spain went into a gradual decline, during which it surrendered several small territories to France and the Netherlands; however, it maintained and enlarged its vast overseas empire, which remained intact until the beginning of the 19th century.</p>
<p>The decline culminated in a controversy over succession to the throne which consumed the first years of the 18th century. The <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> was a wide-ranging international conflict combined with a civil war, and was to cost the kingdom its European possessions and its position as one of the leading powers on the Continent. During this war, a new dynasty originating in France, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbons</a>, was installed. Long united only by the Crown, a true Spanish state was established when the first Bourbon king, <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Philip V</a>, united the crowns of Castile and Aragon into a single state, abolishing many of the old regional privileges and laws.</p>
<p>The 18th century saw a gradual recovery and an increase in prosperity through much of the empire. The new Bourbon monarchy drew on the French system of modernising the administration and the economy. <a href="/wiki/Enlightenment_in_Spain" title="Enlightenment in Spain">Enlightenment</a> ideas began to gain ground among some of the kingdom's elite and monarchy. Military assistance for the rebellious British colonies in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American War of Independence</a> improved the kingdom's international standing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberalism_and_nation_state">Liberalism and nation state</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-19th-century_Spain" title="Mid-19th-century Spain">Mid-19th-century Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence" title="Spanish American wars of independence">Spanish American wars of independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_Spain" title="Anarchism in Spain">Anarchism in Spain</a></div>

<p>In 1793, Spain went to war against the revolutionary new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" title="First French Republic">French Republic</a> as a member of <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">the first Coalition</a>. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Pyrenees" title="War of the Pyrenees">War of the Pyrenees</a> polarised the country in a reaction against the <a href="/wiki/Francization" title="Francization">gallicised</a> elites and following defeat in the field, peace was made with France in 1795 at the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Basel" title="Peace of Basel">Peace of Basel</a> in which Spain lost control over two-thirds of the island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. The Prime Minister, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manuel_Godoy,_Prince_of_the_Peace" title="Manuel Godoy, Prince of the Peace">Manuel Godoy</a>, then ensured that Spain allied herself with France in the brief <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Third_Coalition" title="War of the Third Coalition">War of the Third Coalition</a> which ended with the British victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">Battle of Trafalgar</a> in 1805. In 1807, a secret treaty between Napoleon and the unpopular prime minister led to a new declaration of war against Britain and Portugal. Napoleon's troops entered the country to invade Portugal but instead occupied Spain's major fortresses. The ridiculed Spanish king abdicated in favour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon's</a> brother, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte" title="Joseph Bonaparte">Joseph Bonaparte</a>.</p>
<p>Joseph Bonaparte was seen as a <a href="/wiki/Puppet_monarch" title="Puppet monarch">puppet monarch</a> and was regarded with scorn by the Spanish. The <a href="/wiki/Dos_de_Mayo_Uprising" title="Dos de Mayo Uprising">2 May 1808 revolt</a> was one of many nationalist uprisings across the country against the Bonapartist regime. These revolts marked the beginning of a devastating <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">war of independence</a> against the Napoleonic regime. Napoleon was forced to intervene personally, defeating several Spanish armies and forcing a British army to retreat. However, further military action by Spanish armies, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerrilla" title="Guerrilla">guerrillas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Wellington's</a> British-Portuguese forces, combined with Napoleon's disastrous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon%27s_Invasion_of_Russia" title="Napoleon's Invasion of Russia">invasion of Russia</a>, led to the ousting of the French imperial armies from Spain in 1814, and the return of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">King Ferdinand VII</a>.</p>
<p>During the war, in 1810, a revolutionary body, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cortes_of_C%C3%A1diz" title="Cortes of Cádiz">Cortes of Cádiz</a>, was assembled to co-ordinate the effort against the Bonapartist regime and to prepare a constitution. It met as one body, and its members represented the entire Spanish empire. In 1812 a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1812" title="Spanish Constitution of 1812">constitution</a> for universal representation under a constitutional monarchy was declared but after the fall of the Bonapartist regime <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII" title="Ferdinand VII">Ferdinand VII</a> dismissed the <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">Cortes Generales</a> and was determined to rule as an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarch</a>. These events foreshadowed the conflict between conservatives and liberals in the 19th and early 20th centuries.</p>

<p>Spain's conquest by France benefited Latin American anti-colonialists who resented the Imperial Spanish government's policies that favoured Spanish-born citizens (<a href="/wiki/Peninsulars" title="Peninsulars">Peninsulars</a>) over those born overseas (<a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">Criollos</a>) and demanded <a href="/wiki/Retroversion_of_the_sovereignty_to_the_people" title="Retroversion of the sovereignty to the people">retroversion of the sovereignty to the people</a>. Starting in 1809 Spain's American colonies began a series of revolutions and declared independence, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence" title="Spanish American wars of independence">Spanish American wars of independence</a> that ended Spanish control over its mainland colonies in the Americas. King Ferdinand VII's attempt to <a href="/wiki/Reconquista_(Spanish_America)" title="Reconquista (Spanish America)">re-assert control</a> proved futile as he faced opposition not only in the colonies but also in Spain and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain#Revolt" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">army revolts</a> followed, led by liberal officers. By the end of 1826, the only American colonies Spain held were <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.</p>
<p>The Napoleonic War left Spain economically ruined, deeply divided and politically unstable. In the 1830s and 1840s Anti-liberal forces known as <a href="/wiki/Carlism" title="Carlism">Carlists</a> fought against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberals" title="Liberals">liberals</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Carlist_Wars" title="Carlist Wars">Carlist Wars</a>. Liberal forces won, but the conflict between progressive and conservative liberals ended in a weak early constitutional period. After the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution_(Spain)" title="Glorious Revolution (Spain)">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1868 and the short-lived <a href="/wiki/First_Spanish_Republic" title="First Spanish Republic">First Spanish Republic</a>, a more stable <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(Spain)" title="Restoration (Spain)">monarchic period began</a> characterised by the practice of <i>turnismo</i> (the rotation of government control between progressive and conservative liberals within the Spanish government).</p>


<p>In the late 19th century nationalist movements arose in the Philippines and Cuba. In 1895 and 1896 the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_War_of_Independence" title="Cuban War of Independence">Cuban War of Independence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Revolution" title="Philippine Revolution">Philippine Revolution</a> broke out and eventually the United States became involved. The <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> was fought in the spring of 1898 and resulted in Spain losing the last of its once vast colonial empire outside of North Africa. <i>El Desastre</i> (the Disaster), as the war became known in Spain, gave added impetus to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_of_98" title="Generation of 98">Generation of 98</a> who were conducting an analysis of the country.</p>
<p>Although the period around the turn of the century was one of increasing prosperity, the 20th century brought little peace; Spain played a minor part in the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">scramble for Africa</a>, with the colonisation of <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">Spanish Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>. It remained neutral during World War I (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spain_in_World_War_I" title="Spain in World War I">Spain in World War I</a>). The heavy losses suffered during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rif_War_(1920)" title="Rif War (1920)">Rif War</a> in Morocco brought discredit to the government and undermined the monarchy.</p>
<p>A period of authoritarian rule under General <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Primo_de_Rivera" title="Miguel Primo de Rivera">Miguel Primo de Rivera</a> (1923–1931) ended with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">Second Spanish Republic</a>. The Republic offered political autonomy to the linguistically distinct regions of <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and gave <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage#Spain" title="Women's suffrage">voting rights to women</a>. <a href="/wiki/Clara_Campoamor" title="Clara Campoamor">Clara Campoamor</a> (<a href="/wiki/Suffragette" title="Suffragette">suffragette</a> leader), <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Kent" title="Victoria Kent">Victoria Kent</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolores_Ibarruri" title="Dolores Ibarruri">Dolores Ibarruri</a> were some of the first female deputies and <a href="/wiki/Federica_Montseny" title="Federica Montseny">Federica Montseny</a> one of the first femlae ministers in the world. In the worsening economic situation of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression#Spain" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, Spanish politics became increasingly chaotic and violent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Civil_War_and_dictatorship">Spanish Civil War and dictatorship</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Revolution_of_1936" title="Spanish Revolution of 1936">Spanish Revolution of 1936</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francoist Spain</a></div>

<p>The Spanish Civil War broke out in 1936. For three years the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_faction_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="Nationalist faction (Spanish Civil War)">Nationalist</a> forces led by General <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a> and supported by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Fascist Italy</a> fought the <a href="/wiki/Republican_faction_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="Republican faction (Spanish Civil War)">Republican</a> side, which was supported by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, Mexico and <a href="/wiki/International_Brigades" title="International Brigades">International Brigades</a> but it was not supported by the Western powers due to the British-led policy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-Intervention" title="Non-Intervention">Non-Intervention</a>. The civil war was viciously fought and there were <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War#Atrocities" title="Spanish Civil War">many atrocities committed by all sides</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">war</a> claimed the lives of over 500,000 people and caused the flight of up to a half-million citizens from the country. In 1939, General Franco emerged victorious and became a dictator.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">state as established under Franco</a> was nominally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrality_(international_relations)" title="Neutrality (international relations)">neutral</a> in the Second World War, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spain_in_World_War_II" title="Spain in World War II">sympathetic</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" title="Axis Powers">the Axis</a>. The only legal party under Franco's post civil war regime was the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falange" title="Falange">Falange</a> Española Tradicionalista y de las JONS</i>, formed in 1937; the party emphasised <a href="/wiki/Falangism" title="Falangism">falangism</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a> that emphasised <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-communism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>. Given Franco's opposition to competing political parties, the party was renamed the National Movement (<i><a href="/wiki/Movimiento_Nacional" title="Movimiento Nacional">Movimiento Nacional</a></i>) in 1949.</p>
<p>After World War II Spain was politically and economically isolated, and was kept out of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. This changed in 1955, during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period, when it became strategically important for the US to establish a military presence on the Iberian Peninsula as a counter to any possible move by the Soviet Union into the Mediterranean basin. In the 1960s, Spain registered an <a href="/wiki/Spanish_miracle" title="Spanish miracle">unprecedented rate of economic growth</a> which was propelled by <a href="/wiki/Spanish_miracle#Industrialisation" title="Spanish miracle">industrialisation</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_miracle#Rural_exodus" title="Spanish miracle">mass internal migration</a> from rural areas to cities and the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_miracle#Mass_tourism" title="Spanish miracle">mass tourism industry</a>. Franco's rule was also characterised by <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain#Authoritarianism" title="Francoist Spain">authoritarianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain#Nationalism" title="Francoist Spain">promotion of a unitary national identity</a>, the favouring of a very conservative form of Roman Catholicism known as <a href="/wiki/National_Catholicism" title="National Catholicism">National Catholicism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Language_policies_of_Francoist_Spain" title="Language policies of Francoist Spain">discriminatory language policies</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_of_democracy">Restoration of democracy</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">Spanish transition to democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_society_after_the_democratic_transition" title="Spanish society after the democratic transition">Spanish society after the democratic transition</a></div>


<p>In 1962, <a href="/wiki/Salvador_de_Madariaga" title="Salvador de Madariaga">Salvador de Madariaga</a>, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_International" title="Liberal International">Liberal International</a> and the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Europe" title="College of Europe">College of Europe</a>, met in the congress of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Movement" title="European Movement">European Movement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> with members of the <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(politics)" title="Opposition (politics)">opposition</a> to Franco's regime inside the country and in the exile. There were 118 politicians from all factions. At the end of the meetings a resolution in favour of democracy was made.</p>
<p>With Franco's death in November 1975, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos</a> succeeded to the position of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">King of Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> in accordance with the franquist law. With the approval of the new <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Spanish Constitution of 1978</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">restoration of democracy</a>, the State <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> much authority to the regions and created an internal organisation based on <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">autonomous communities</a>. <a href="/wiki/Spanish_1977_Amnesty_Law" title="Spanish 1977 Amnesty Law">Spanish 1977 Amnesty Law</a> let people of Franco´s regime continue inside institutions without consequences, even responsibles of some crimes during transition to democracy like the <a href="/wiki/Massacre_of_3_March_1976_in_Vitoria" title="Massacre of 3 March 1976 in Vitoria">Massacre of 3 March 1976 in Vitoria</a> or <a href="/wiki/1977_Massacre_of_Atocha" title="1977 Massacre of Atocha">1977 Massacre of Atocha</a>.</p>
<p>In the Basque Country, moderate <a href="/wiki/Basque_nationalism" title="Basque nationalism">Basque nationalism</a> has coexisted with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_Conflict" title="Basque Conflict">radical nationalist movement</a> led by the armed terrorist organisation <a href="/wiki/ETA_(separatist_group)" title="ETA (separatist group)">ETA</a>. The group was formed in 1959 during Franco's rule but has continued to wage its violent campaign even after the restoration of democracy and the return of a large measure of regional autonomy. On 23 February 1981, rebel elements among the security forces seized the Cortes in an attempt to impose <a href="/wiki/23-F" title="23-F">a military backed government</a>. King Juan Carlos took personal command of the military and successfully ordered the coup plotters, via national television, to surrender.</p>
<p>During the 1980s the democratic restoration made possible a growing open society. New cultural movements based on freedom appeared, like <a href="/wiki/La_Movida_Madrile%C3%B1a" title="La Movida Madrileña">La Movida Madrileña</a> and a culture of human rights arose with <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Peces-Barba" title="Gregorio Peces-Barba">Gregorio Peces-Barba</a>. On 30 May 1982 Spain joined <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, following a referendum after a strong social opposition. That year the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Socialist_Workers_Party" title="Spanish Socialist Workers Party">Spanish Socialist Workers Party</a> (PSOE) came to power, the first left-wing government in 43 years. In 1986 Spain joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>, which later became the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The PSOE was replaced in government by the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People's Party (Spain)">Partido Popular</a> (PP) in 1996 after scandals around participation of the government of <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Felipe González">Felipe González</a> in the <a href="/wiki/GAL_(paramilitary_group)" title="GAL (paramilitary group)">Dirty war against ETA</a>; at that point the PSOE had served almost 14 consecutive years in office.</p>

<p>On 1 January 2002, Spain fully adopted the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>, and Spain experienced strong economic growth, well above the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> average during the early 2000s. However, well publicised concerns issued by many economic commentators at the height of the boom warned that extraordinary property prices and a high foreign trade deficit were likely to lead to a painful economic collapse.</p>
<p>In 2002 <a href="/wiki/Prestige_oil_spill" title="Prestige oil spill">Prestige oil spill</a> happened with big ecological consequences in the Spanish atlantic coastline. In 2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jose_Maria_Aznar" title="Jose Maria Aznar">Jose Maria Aznar</a> supported U.S. president <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Preventive_war" title="Preventive war">preventive war</a> against <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Sadam Hussein´s Iraq</a>. A strong movement against war rose in Spanish society. On 11 March 2004 a local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> terrorist group inspired by <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">Al-Qaeda</a> carried out the largest terrorist attack in Spanish history when they killed 191 people and wounded more than 1,800 others by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11_March_2004_Madrid_train_bombings" title="11 March 2004 Madrid train bombings">bombing commuter trains</a> in Madrid. Though initial suspicions focused on the Basque terrorist group <a href="/wiki/ETA_(separatist_group)" title="ETA (separatist group)">ETA</a>, evidence soon emerged indicating Islamist involvement. Because of the proximity of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_2004" title="Spanish general election, 2004">2004 election</a>, the issue of responsibility quickly became a political controversy, with the main competing parties PP and PSOE exchanging accusations over the handling of the incident. At 14 March elections, PSOE, led by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a> won the elections.</p>

<p>The proportion of <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Spain#Currently" title="Immigration to Spain">Spain's foreign born population</a> increased rapidly from around 1 in 50 in 2000 to almost 1 in 8 in 2010 but has since declined. In 2005 the Spanish government legalised <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Spain" title="Same-sex marriage in Spain">same sex marriage</a>. Decentralatation was supported with much resistance of Constitutional Court and conservative opposition, so did gender politics like cuotas or the law against gender violence. Government talks with ETA happened, and the band announced its permanent cease of violence in 2010.</p>
<p>The bursting of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_property_bubble" title="Spanish property bubble">Spanish property bubble</a> in 2008 led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9315_Spanish_financial_crisis" title="2008–15 Spanish financial crisis">2008–15 Spanish financial crisis</a> and high levels of <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a>, cuts in government spending and <a href="/wiki/Corruption" title="Corruption">corruption</a> in <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">Royal family</a> and <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People's Party (Spain)">People's Party</a> served as a backdrop to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_Spanish_protests" title="2011–12 Spanish protests">2011–12 Spanish protests</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_independentism" title="Catalan independentism">Catalan independentism</a> was also on rise. In 2011 <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rajoy" title="Mariano Rajoy">Mariano Rajoy</a>'s conservative <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People's Party (Spain)">People's Party</a> won elections with 44.6% of votes and Rajoy became the Spanish Prime Minister after having been the leader of the opposition from 2004 to 2011 with a program of cutting social spends. On 19 June 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Spain" title="Monarchy of Spain">monarch</a>, Juan Carlos, abdicated in favour of his son, who became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felipe_VI" title="Felipe VI">Felipe VI</a>. Bipartidism in Spanish politcs got to an end with the entrance of new forces in representative institutions. In 2015, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayors</a> got control of biggest cities in the country as former judge and former co-founder of the labor law office where the <a href="/wiki/1977_Massacre_of_Atocha" title="1977 Massacre of Atocha">1977 Massacre of Atocha</a> took place <a href="/wiki/Manuela_Carmena" title="Manuela Carmena">Manuela Carmena</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, cofounder and spokesperson of the <a href="/wiki/Plataforma_de_Afectados_por_la_Hipoteca" title="Plataforma de Afectados por la Hipoteca">Platform for People Affected by Mortgages</a> <a href="/wiki/Ada_Colau" title="Ada Colau">Ada Colau</a> in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a>, the first time that happens since the Spanish Second Republic. Even though, in general election of the same year, conservative People´s Party revalidated its majority in the parliament.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Spain" title="Geography of Spain">Geography of Spain</a></div>

<p>At 505,992 km (195,365 sq mi), Spain is the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_outlying_territories_by_area" title="List of countries and outlying territories by area">fifty-second largest country</a> and <a href="/wiki/Area_and_population_of_European_countries" title="Area and population of European countries">Europe's fourth largest country</a>. It is some 47,000 km (18,000 sq mi) smaller than France and 81,000 km (31,000 sq mi) larger than the US state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. Mount <a href="/wiki/Teide" title="Teide">Teide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>) is the highest mountain peak in Spain and is the third largest volcano in the world from its base.</p>
<p>Spain lies between latitudes <a href="/wiki/26th_parallel_north" title="26th parallel north">26°</a> and <a href="/wiki/44th_parallel_north" title="44th parallel north">44° N</a>, and longitudes <a href="/wiki/19th_meridian_west" title="19th meridian west">19° W</a> and <a href="/wiki/5th_meridian_east" title="5th meridian east">5° E</a>.</p>
<p>On the west, Spain is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>; on the south, it is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" title="British overseas territory">British overseas territory</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">exclaves</a> in North Africa (<a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>, and the peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera" title="Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera">Vélez de la Gomera</a>). On the northeast, along the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> mountain range, it is bordered by France and the <a href="/wiki/Principality" title="Principality">Principality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Andorra" title="Andorra">Andorra</a>. Along the Pyrenees in <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Girona" title="Province of Girona">Girona</a>, a small exclave town called <a href="/wiki/Ll%C3%ADvia" title="Llívia">Llívia</a> is surrounded by France.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands">Islands</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Spain" title="List of islands of Spain">List of islands of Spain</a></div>
<p>Spain also includes the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and a number of uninhabited islands on the Mediterranean side of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>, known as <span lang="es"><i><a href="/wiki/Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa" title="Plazas de soberanía">plazas de soberanía</a></i></span> ("places of sovereignty", or territories under [Spanish] sovereignty), such as the <a href="/wiki/Chafarinas_Islands" title="Chafarinas Islands">Chafarinas Islands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_Alhucemas" title="Peñón de Alhucemas">Alhucemas</a>. The peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera" title="Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera">Vélez de la Gomera</a> is also regarded as a <i>plaza de soberanía</i>. The isle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_de_Albor%C3%A1n" title="Isla de Alborán">Alborán</a>, located in the Mediterranean between Spain and North Africa, is also administered by Spain, specifically by the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa" title="Almería">Almería</a>, Andalusia. The little <a href="/wiki/Pheasant_Island" title="Pheasant Island">Pheasant Island</a> in the River <a href="/wiki/Bidasoa" title="Bidasoa">Bidasoa</a> is a Spanish-French <a href="/wiki/Condominium_(international_law)" title="Condominium (international law)">condominium</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains_and_rivers">Mountains and rivers</span></h3>

<p>Mainland Spain is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountainous" title="Mountainous">mountainous</a> country, dominated by high <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateaus</a> and mountain chains. After the Pyrenees, the main mountain ranges are the <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_Mountains" title="Cantabrian Mountains">Cordillera Cantábrica</a> (Cantabrian Range), <a href="/wiki/Sistema_Ib%C3%A9rico" title="Sistema Ibérico">Sistema Ibérico</a> (Iberian System), <a href="/wiki/Sistema_Central" title="Sistema Central">Sistema Central</a> (Central System), <a href="/wiki/Montes_de_Toledo" title="Montes de Toledo">Montes de Toledo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Morena" title="Sierra Morena">Sierra Morena</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sistema_B%C3%A9tico" title="Sistema Bético">Sistema Bético</a> (Baetic System) whose highest peak, the 3,478 metres (11,411 ft) <a href="/wiki/Mulhac%C3%A9n" title="Mulhacén">Mulhacén</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(Spain)" title="Sierra Nevada (Spain)">Sierra Nevada</a>, is the highest elevation in the Iberian Peninsula. The highest point in Spain is the <a href="/wiki/Teide" title="Teide">Teide</a>, a 3,718-metre (12,198 ft) active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> in the Canary Islands. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meseta_Central" title="Meseta Central">Meseta Central</a> (often translated as "Inner Plateau") is a vast plateau in the heart of peninsular Spain.</p>
<p>There are several major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivers_in_Spain" title="Rivers in Spain">rivers in Spain</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Tagus" title="Tagus">Tagus</a> (<i>Tajo</i>), <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guadiana_River" title="Guadiana River">Guadiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> (<i>Duero</i>), <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%BAcar_River" title="Júcar River">Júcar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Segura" title="Segura">Segura</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turia_River" title="Turia River">Turia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minho_River" title="Minho River">Minho</a> (<i>Miño</i>). <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_plain" title="Alluvial plain">Alluvial plains</a> are found along the coast, the largest of which is that of the Guadalquivir in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Spain" title="Climate of Spain">Climate of Spain</a></div>

<p>Three main climatic zones can be separated, according to geographical situation and <a href="/wiki/Orography" title="Orography">orographic</a> conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a>, characterised by warm/hot and dry summers. It is dominant in the peninsula, with two varieties: <i>Csa</i> and <i>Csb</i> according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>. The <i>Csb</i> Zone has warm rather than hot summers, and extends to additional cool-winter areas not typically associated with a Mediterranean climate, such as much of central and northern-central of Spain (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n,_Spain" title="León, Spain">Léon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/%C3%81vila,_Spain" title="Ávila, Spain">Ávila</a>) and into much ranier areas, notably Galicia.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid climate</a> (<i>Bsh</i>, <i>Bsk</i>), located in the southeastern quarter of the country, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">region of Murcia</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> valley. The dry season extends beyond the summer.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<i>Cfb</i>), located in the northern quarter of the country, especially in the region of <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">Cantabria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a> and partly <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>. Winter and summer temperatures are influenced by the ocean, and have no seasonal drought.</li>
</ul>
<p>Apart from these main types, other sub-types can be found, like the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine climate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(Spain)" title="Sierra Nevada (Spain)">Sierra Nevada</a>, and a typical <a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">desert climate</a> in the zone of Almería, Murcia and in most of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>. Low-lying areas of the Canary Islands average above 18.0 °C (64.4 °F) during their coldest month, thus having a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a>.</p>
<p>The below-listed list covers the average temperatures of the largest cities in three different parts of Spain; <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> (central peninsula), <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> (Mediterranean coast), and <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> (north coast). <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_de_Tenerife" title="Santa Cruz de Tenerife">Santa Cruz de Tenerife</a> which has a significantly different climates to the predominant climate in Spain. More information regarding temperature can be found in city articles and the main article about the Spanish climate.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna_and_flora">Fauna and flora</span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> presents a wide diversity that is due in large part to the geographical position of the Iberian peninsula between the Atlantic and the Mediterranean and between <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, and the great diversity of habitats and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biotopes" title="Biotopes">biotopes</a>, the result of a considerable variety of climates and well differentiated regions.</p>
<p>The vegetation of Spain is varied due to several factors including the diversity of the relief, the climate and <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. Spain includes different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytogeographic" title="Phytogeographic">phytogeographic</a> regions, each with its own floristic characteristics resulting largely from the interaction of climate, topography, soil type and fire, <a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a> factors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Spain" title="Politics of Spain">Politics of Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Spanish Constitution of 1978</a></div>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Index" title="Democracy Index">Democracy Index</a> of the EIU, Spain is one of the 20 full democracies in the world.</p>
<p>The Spanish Constitution of 1978 is the culmination of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">Spanish transition to democracy</a>. The constitutional history of Spain dates back to the constitution of 1812. Impatient with the slow pace of democratic political reforms in 1976 and 1977, Spain's new King Juan Carlos, known for his formidable personality, dismissed <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Arias_Navarro" title="Carlos Arias Navarro">Carlos Arias Navarro</a> and appointed the reformer <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Adolfo Suárez">Adolfo Suárez</a> as Prime Minister. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_1977" title="Spanish general election, 1977">general election in 1977</a> convened the <i><a href="/wiki/Constituent_Cortes" title="Constituent Cortes">Constituent Cortes</a></i> (the Spanish Parliament, in its capacity as a constitutional assembly) for the purpose of drafting and approving the constitution of 1978. After a national referendum on 6 December 1978, 88% of voters approved of the new constitution.</p>
<p>As a result, Spain is now composed of 17 <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">autonomous communities</a> and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_Communities#Autonomous_cities_and_.22plazas_de_soberan.C3.ADa.22" title="Autonomous Communities">autonomous cities</a> with varying degrees of autonomy thanks to its Constitution, which nevertheless explicitly states the indivisible unity of the Spanish nation. The constitution also specifies that Spain has no state religion and that all are free to practice and believe as they wish.</p>
<p>The Spanish administration approved legislation in 2007 aimed at furthering <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">equality between genders</a> in Spanish political and economic life (Gender Equality Act). However, in the legislative branch, as of July 2010 only 128 of the 350 members of the Congress were women (36.3%). It places Spain 13th on a list of countries ranked by proportion of women in the <a href="/wiki/Lower_house" title="Lower house">lower house</a>. In the Senate, the ratio is even lower, since there are only 79 women out of 263 (30.0%). The <a href="/wiki/Gender_Empowerment_Measure" title="Gender Empowerment Measure">Gender Empowerment Measure</a> of Spain in the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Report" title="Human Development Report">Human Development Report</a> is 0.794, 12th in the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_monarchs" title="List of Spanish monarchs">List of Spanish monarchs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monarchs_of_Spain_family_tree" title="Monarchs of Spain family tree">Monarchs of Spain family tree</a></div>
<p>Spain is a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>, with a hereditary <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicameral_parliament" title="Bicameral parliament">bicameral parliament</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">Cortes Generales</a></i> (General Courts). The executive branch consists of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_of_Spain" title="Council of Ministers of Spain">Council of Ministers of Spain</a> presided over by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Spain" title="Prime Minister of Spain">Prime Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Spain#Royal_Nomination.2C_Congressional_Confirmation" title="Prime Minister of Spain">nominated and appointed</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Spain" title="Monarchy of Spain">monarch</a> and confirmed by the Congress of Deputies following legislative elections. By political custom established by King Juan Carlos since the ratification of the 1978 Constitution, the king's nominees have all been from parties who maintain a plurality of seats in the Congress.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legislative_branch" title="Legislative branch">legislative branch</a> is made up of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Deputies_(Spain)" title="Congress of Deputies (Spain)">Congress of Deputies</a> (<i>Congreso de los Diputados</i>) with 350 members, elected by popular vote on block lists by proportional representation to serve four-year terms, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Senate" title="Spanish Senate">Senate</a> (<i>Senado</i>) with 259 seats of which 208 are directly elected by popular vote and the other 51 appointed by the regional legislatures to also serve four-year terms.</p>

<p>Spain is organisationally structured as a so-called <i>Estado de las Autonomías</i> ("State of Autonomies"); it is one of the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decentralised" title="Decentralised">decentralised</a> countries in Europe, along with Switzerland, Germany and Belgium; for example, all Autonomous Communities have their own elected parliaments, governments, public administrations, budgets, and resources. Health and education systems among others are managed regionally, and in addition, the Basque Country and Navarre also manage their own public finances based on <a href="/wiki/Fuero" title="Fuero">foral</a> provisions. In Catalonia, the Basque Country, Navarre and the Canary Islands, a full-fledged autonomous police corps replaces some of the State police functions (see <a href="/wiki/Mossos_d%27Esquadra" title="Mossos d'Esquadra">Mossos d'Esquadra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ertzaintza" title="Ertzaintza">Ertzaintza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polic%C3%ADa_Foral" title="Policía Foral">Policía Foral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polic%C3%ADa_Canaria" title="Policía Canaria">Policía Canaria</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights">Human rights</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Spain" title="Human rights in Spain">Human rights in Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Spain" title="LGBT rights in Spain">LGBT rights in Spain</a></div>

<p>The Government respects the <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> of its citizens; although there are a few problems in some areas, the law and judiciary provide effective means of addressing individual instances of abuse. There are allegations that a few members of the security forces abused detainees and mistreated foreigners and illegal immigrants. According to <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> (AI), government investigations of such alleged abuses are often lengthy and punishments were light. Violence against women was a problem, which the Government took steps to address.</p>
<p>Spain provides one of the highest degrees of liberty in the world for its <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> community. Among the countries studied by <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a> in 2013, Spain is rated first in acceptance of homosexuality, with an 88% of society supporting the gay community compared to 11% who do not.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_divisions">Administrative divisions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_divisions_of_Spain" title="Political divisions of Spain">Political divisions of Spain</a></div>
<p>The Spanish State is integrated by 17 autonomous communities and 2 autonomous cities, both groups being the highest or first-order administrative division in the country. Autonomous communities are integrated by provinces, of which there are 50 in total, and in turn, provinces are integrated by municipalities. In Catalonia, two additional divisions exist, the <i>comarques</i> (sing. <i>comarca</i>) and the <i>vegueries</i> (sing. <i>vegueria</i>) both of which have administrative powers; <i>comarques</i> being aggregations of municipalities, and the <i>vegueries</i> being aggregations of <i>comarques</i>. The concept of a <i>comarca</i> exists in all autonomous communities, however, unlike Catalonia, these are merely historical or geographical subdivisions.</p>

<p>Spain's autonomous regions are the first level administrative divisions of the country. They were created after the current constitution came into effect (in 1978) in recognition of the right to self-government of the "<a href="/wiki/Nationalities_and_regions_of_Spain" title="Nationalities and regions of Spain">nationalities and regions of Spain</a>". The autonomous regions were to be integrated into adjacent provinces with common historical, cultural, and economical traits. This territorial organisation, based on <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a>, is literally known in Spain as the "State of Autonomies".</p>
<p>The basic institutional law of each autonomous region is the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy" title="Statute of Autonomy">Statute of Autonomy</a>. The Statutes of Autonomy establish the name of the region according to its historical identity, the limits of its territories, the name and organisation of the institutions of government and the rights they enjoy according to the constitution.</p>
<p>The governments of all autonomous regions must be based on a division of powers comprising:</p>
<ul>
<li>a legislative assembly whose members must be elected by <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> according to the system of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> and in which all areas that integrate the territory are fairly represented;</li>
<li>a government council, with executive and administrative functions headed by a president, elected by the Legislative Assembly and nominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">King of Spain</a>;</li>
<li>a supreme court, under the supreme court of Spain, which heads the judiciary in the autonomous region.</li>
</ul>
<p>Catalonia, Galicia and the Basque Country, which identified themselves as "nationalities" were granted self-government through a rapid process. Andalusia also took that denomination in its first Statute of Autonomy, even though it followed the longer process stipulated in the constitution for the rest of the country. Progressively, other communities in revisions to their Statutes of Autonomy have also taken that denomination in accordance to their historical regional identity, such as the Valencian Community, the Canary Islands, the Balearic Islands, and Aragon.</p>
<p>The autonomous communities have wide legislative and executive autonomy, with their own <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliaments</a> and regional governments. The distribution of powers may be different for every community, as laid out in their Statutes of Autonomy, since devolution was intended to be asymmetrical. Only two communities—the Basque Country and Navarre—have full fiscal autonomy. Aside of fiscal autonomy, the "historical" nationalities—Andalusia, the Basque Country, Catalonia, and Galicia—were devolved more powers than the rest of the communities, among them the ability of the regional president to dissolve the parliament and call for elections at any time. In addition, the Basque Country, Catalonia and Navarre have police corps of their own: <a href="/wiki/Ertzaintza" title="Ertzaintza">Ertzaintza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mossos_d%27Esquadra" title="Mossos d'Esquadra">Mossos d'Esquadra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Polic%C3%ADa_Foral" title="Policía Foral">Policía Foral</a> respectively. Other communities have more limited forces or none at all, like the <i>Policía Autónoma Andaluza</i> in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a> or the <a href="/wiki/BESCAM" title="BESCAM">BESCAM</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>.</p>

<p>Nonetheless, recent amendments to existing Statutes of Autonomy or the promulgation of new Statutes altogether, have reduced the asymmetry between the powers originally granted to the "historical nationalities" and the rest of the regions.</p>
<p>Finally, along with the 17 autonomous communities, two autonomous cities are also part of the State of Autonomies and are first-order territorial divisions: <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>. These are two exclaves located in the northern African coast.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Provinces_and_municipalities">Provinces and municipalities</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">Provinces of Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Spain" title="Municipalities of Spain">Municipalities of Spain</a></div>
<p>Autonomous communities are subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">provinces</a>, which served as their territorial building blocks. In turn, provinces are integrated by <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Spain" title="Municipalities of Spain">municipalities</a>. The existence of both the provinces and the municipalities is guaranteed and protected by the constitution, not necessarily by the Statutes of Autonomy themselves. Municipalities are granted autonomy to manage their internal affairs, and provinces are the territorial divisions designed to carry out the activities of the State.</p>
<p>The current provincial division structure is based—with minor changes—on the <a href="/wiki/1833_territorial_division_of_Spain" title="1833 territorial division of Spain">1833 territorial division</a> by <a href="/wiki/Javier_de_Burgos" title="Javier de Burgos">Javier de Burgos</a>, and in all, the Spanish territory is divided into 50 provinces. The communities of Asturias, Cantabria, La Rioja, the Balearic Islands, Madrid, Murcia and Navarre are the only communities that are integrated by a single province, which is coextensive with the community itself. In these cases, the administrative institutions of the province are replaced by the governmental institutions of the community.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Spain" title="Foreign relations of Spain">Foreign relations of Spain</a></div>


<p>After the return of democracy following the death of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a> in 1975, Spain's <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a> priorities were to break out of the diplomatic isolation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spain_under_Franco" title="Spain under Franco">Franco years</a> and expand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diplomatic_relations" title="Diplomatic relations">diplomatic relations</a>, enter the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Community</a>, and define security relations with the West.</p>
<p>As a member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> since 1982, Spain has established itself as a participant in multilateral international security activities. Spain's EU membership represents an important part of its foreign policy. Even on many international issues beyond western Europe, Spain prefers to co-ordinate its efforts with its EU partners through the European political co-operation mechanisms.</p>
<p>Spain has maintained its special relations with <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Hispanic America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. Its policy emphasises the concept of an <a href="/wiki/Ibero-America" title="Ibero-America">Ibero-American</a> community, essentially the renewal of the historically liberal concept of <i>"<a href="/wiki/Hispanidad" title="Hispanidad">Hispanidad</a>"</i> or <i>"<a href="/wiki/Hispanism" title="Hispanism">Hispanismo</a>"</i>, as it is often referred to in English, which has sought to link the Iberian Peninsula with Hispanic America through language, commerce, history and culture.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Disputed_status_of_Gibraltar" title="Disputed status of Gibraltar">Spain claims Gibraltar</a>, a 6-square-kilometre (2.3 sq mi) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" title="British overseas territories">Overseas Territory of the United Kingdom</a> in the southernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. Then a Spanish town, it was conquered by an Anglo-Dutch force in 1704 during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Archduke Charles</a>, pretender to the Spanish throne.</p>
<p>The legal situation concerning Gibraltar was settled in 1713 by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a>, in which Spain ceded the territory in perpetuity to the British Crown stating that, should the British abandon this post, it would be offered to Spain first. Since the 1940s Spain has called for the return of Gibraltar. The overwhelming majority of <a href="/wiki/Gibraltarians" title="Gibraltarians">Gibraltarians</a> strongly oppose this, along with any proposal of shared sovereignty. UN resolutions call on the United Kingdom and Spain, both EU members, to reach an agreement over the status of Gibraltar.</p>
<p>The Spanish claim makes a distinction between the <a href="/wiki/Disputed_status_of_the_isthmus_between_Gibraltar_and_Spain" title="Disputed status of the isthmus between Gibraltar and Spain">isthmus</a> that connects the Rock to the Spanish mainland on the one hand, and the Rock and city of Gibraltar on the other. While the Rock and city were ceded by the Treaty of Utrecht, Spain asserts that the "occupation of the isthmus is illegal and against the principles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Law" title="International Law">International Law</a>". The United Kingdom relies on <i>de facto</i> arguments of possession by <a href="/wiki/Prescription_(sovereignty_transfer)" title="Prescription (sovereignty transfer)">prescription</a> in relation to the isthmus, as there has been "continuous possession [of the isthmus] over a long period".</p>

<p>Another claim by Spain is about the <a href="/wiki/Savage_Islands" title="Savage Islands">Savage Islands</a>, a claim not recognised by Portugal. Spain claims that they are rocks rather than islands, therefore claiming that there is no Portuguese territorial waters around the disputed islands. On 5 July 2013, Spain sent a letter to the UN expressing these views.</p>
<p>Spain claims the sovereignty over the <a href="/wiki/Perejil_Island" title="Perejil Island">Perejil Island</a>, a small, uninhabited rocky <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islet</a> located in the South shore of the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">Strait of Gibraltar</a>. The island lies 250 metres (820 ft) just off the coast of Morocco, 8 kilometres (5.0 mi) from <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and 13.5 kilometres (8.4 mi) from mainland Spain. Its sovereignty is disputed between Spain and Morocco. It was the subject of an armed incident between the two countries in 2002. The incident ended when both countries agreed to return to the <a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">status quo</a> ante which existed prior to the Moroccan occupation of the island. The islet is now deserted and without any sign of sovereignty.</p>
<p>Besides the Perejil Island, the Spanish-held territories claimed by other countries are two: Morocco claims the Spanish cities of Ceuta and Melilla and the <i><a href="/wiki/Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa" title="Plazas de soberanía">plazas de soberanía</a></i> islets off the northern coast of Africa; and Portugal does not recognise Spain's sovereignty over the territory of <a href="/wiki/Olivenza" title="Olivenza">Olivenza</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armed_Forces" title="Spanish Armed Forces">Spanish Armed Forces</a></div>
<p>The armed forces of Spain are known as the Spanish Armed Forces (<i>Fuerzas Armadas Españolas</i>). Their <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">Commander-in-chief</a> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">King of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felipe_VI_of_Spain" title="Felipe VI of Spain">Felipe VI</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armed_Forces" title="Spanish Armed Forces">Spanish Armed Forces</a> are divided into three branches:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Spain" title="Economy of Spain">Economy of Spain</a></div>

<p>Spain's <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Mixed_economy" title="Mixed economy">mixed economy</a> is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP)">16th largest</a> worldwide and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states_in_Europe_by_GDP_(nominal)" title="List of sovereign states in Europe by GDP (nominal)">5th largest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">Eurozone</a>'s 4th largest.</p>
<p>The centre-right government of former prime minister <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Aznar" title="José María Aznar">José María Aznar</a> worked successfully to gain admission to the group of countries launching the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> in 1999. <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_in_Spain" title="Unemployment in Spain">Unemployment</a> stood at 7.6% in October 2006, a rate that compared favourably to many other European countries, and especially with the early 1990s when it stood at over 20%. Perennial weak points of Spain's economy include high inflation, a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underground_economy" title="Underground economy">underground economy</a>, and an education system which OECD reports place among the poorest for developed countries, together with the United States and UK.</p>
<p>By the mid-1990s the economy had recommenced the growth that had been disrupted by the global recession of the early 1990s. The strong economic growth helped the government to reduce the government debt as a percentage of GDP and Spain's high unemployment began to drop steadily. With the government budget in balance and inflation under control Spain was admitted into the Eurozone in 1999.</p>

<p>Since the 1990s some Spanish companies have gained multinational status, often expanding their activities in culturally close Latin America. Spain is the second biggest foreign investor there, after the United States. Spanish companies have also expanded into Asia, especially China and India. This early global expansion is a competitive advantage over its competitors and European neighbours. The reason for this early expansion is the booming interest towards Spanish language and culture in Asia and Africa and a corporate culture that learned to take risks in unstable markets.</p>
<p>Spanish companies invested in fields like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_commercialisation" title="Renewable energy commercialisation">renewable energy commercialisation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iberdrola" title="Iberdrola">Iberdrola</a> was the world's largest renewable energy operator), technology companies like <a href="/wiki/Telef%C3%B3nica" title="Telefónica">Telefónica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abengoa" title="Abengoa">Abengoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mondragon_Corporation" title="Mondragon Corporation">Mondragon Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Movistar" title="Movistar">Movistar</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hisdesat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hisdesat (page does not exist)">Hisdesat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indra_Sistemas" title="Indra Sistemas">Indra</a>, train manufacturers like <a href="/wiki/Construcciones_y_Auxiliar_de_Ferrocarriles" title="Construcciones y Auxiliar de Ferrocarriles">CAF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talgo" title="Talgo">Talgo</a>, global corporations such as the textile company <a href="/wiki/Inditex" title="Inditex">Inditex</a>, petroleum companies like <a href="/wiki/Repsol" title="Repsol">Repsol</a> and infrastructure, with six of the ten biggest international construction firms specialising in transport being Spanish, like <a href="/wiki/Ferrovial" title="Ferrovial">Ferrovial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acciona" title="Acciona">Acciona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grupo_ACS" title="Grupo ACS">ACS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obrasc%C3%B3n_Huarte_Lain" title="Obrascón Huarte Lain">OHL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fomento_de_Construcciones_y_Contratas" title="Fomento de Construcciones y Contratas">FCC</a>.</p>

<p>In 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> survey placed Spain among the top 10 in the world. In 2013 the same survey (now called the "Where-to-be-born index"), ranked Spain 28th in the world.</p>
<p>In 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_region)" title="Basque Country (autonomous region)">Basque</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> was awarded with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Kuan_Yew_World_City_Prize" title="Lee Kuan Yew World City Prize">Lee Kuan Yew World City Prize</a>, and its mayor at the time, <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B1aki_Azkuna" title="Iñaki Azkuna">Iñaki Azkuna</a>, was awarded the <a href="/wiki/World_Mayor" title="World Mayor">World Mayor</a> Prize in 2012. The Basque capital city of <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a> received the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Capital_Award" title="European Green Capital Award">European Green Capital Award</a> in 2012.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Spain" title="Agriculture in Spain">Agriculture in Spain</a></div>


<p>Crop areas were farmed in two highly diverse manners. Areas relying on non-irrigated cultivation (<i>secano</i>), which made up 85% of the entire crop area, depended solely on rainfall as a source of water. They included the humid regions of the north and the northwest, as well as vast arid zones that had not been irrigated. The much more productive regions devoted to irrigated cultivation (<i>regadío</i>) accounted for 3 million hectares in 1986, and the government hoped that this area would eventually double, as it already had doubled since 1950. Particularly noteworthy was the development in <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Province of Almería">Almería</a>—one of the most arid and desolate provinces of Spain—of winter crops of various fruits and vegetables for export to Europe.</p>
<p>Though only about 17% of Spain's cultivated land was irrigated, it was estimated to be the source of between 40–45% of the gross value of crop production and of 50% of the value of agricultural exports. More than half of the irrigated area was planted in <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_tree" title="Fruit tree">fruit trees</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>. Other agricultural products that benefited from irrigation included <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olive_tree" title="Olive tree">olive trees</a>, mangos, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strawberries" title="Strawberries">strawberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a> grasses. Depending on the nature of the crop, it was possible to harvest two successive crops in the same year on about 10% of the country's irrigated land.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_fruit" title="Citrus fruit">Citrus fruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereal_grain" title="Cereal grain">cereal grains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>—Spain's traditional agricultural products—continued to be important in the 1980s. In 1983 they represented 12%, 12%, 8%, 6%, and 4%, respectively, of the country's agricultural production. Because of the changed diet of an increasingly affluent population, there was a notable increase in the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a>. <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">Meat</a> production for domestic consumption became the single most important agricultural activity, accounting for 30% of all farm-related production in 1983. Increased attention to livestock was the reason that Spain became a net importer of grains. Ideal growing conditions, combined with proximity to important north European markets, made citrus fruits Spain's leading export. Fresh vegetables and fruits produced through intensive irrigation farming also became important export commodities, as did <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed_oil" title="Sunflower seed oil">sunflower seed oil</a> that was produced to compete with the more expensive olive oils in oversupply throughout the Mediterranean countries of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Community" title="European Community">EC</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Spain" title="Tourism in Spain">Tourism in Spain</a></div>

<p>The climate of Spain, its geographic location, popular coastlines, diverse landscapes, historical legacy, vibrant culture and excellent infrastructure, has made Spain's international tourist industry among the largest in the world. In the last five decades, international tourism in Spain has grown to become the second largest in the world in terms of spending, worth approximately 40 billion Euros or about 5% of GDP in 2006.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Spain" title="Energy in Spain">Energy in Spain</a></div>

<p>Spain is one of the world's leading countries in the development and production of renewable energy. In 2010 Spain became the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Spain" title="Solar power in Spain">solar power</a> world leader when it overtook the United States with a massive power station plant called <a href="/wiki/Alvarado_I" title="Alvarado I">La Florida</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Badajoz" title="Badajoz">Alvarado, Badajoz</a>. Spain is also Europe's main producer of wind energy. In 2010 its wind turbines generated 42,976 GWh, which accounted for 16.4% of all electrical energy produced in Spain. On 9 November 2010, wind energy reached an instantaneous historic peak covering 53% of mainland electricity demand and generating an amount of energy that is equivalent to that of 14 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. Other renewable energies used in Spain are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power_plant" title="Hydroelectric power plant">hydroelectric</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasification_of_biomass" title="Gasification of biomass">biomass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine</a> (2 power plants under construction).</p>
<p>Non-renewable energy sources used in Spain are <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear</a> (8 operative reactors), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_power_plant" title="Natural gas power plant">gas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_power_plant" title="Coal power plant">coal</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_power_plant" title="Oil power plant">oil</a>. Fossil fuels together generated 58% of Spain's electricity in 2009, just below the OECD mean of 61%. Nuclear power generated another 19%, and wind and hydro about 12% each.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Spain" title="Transport in Spain">Transport in Spain</a></div>
<p>The Spanish road system is mainly centralised, with six highways connecting <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">Valencia</a>, West <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">Extremadura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>. Additionally, there are highways along the Atlantic (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrol,_Spain" title="Ferrol, Spain">Ferrol</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vigo" title="Vigo">Vigo</a>), Cantabrian (<a href="/wiki/Oviedo" title="Oviedo">Oviedo</a> to <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">San Sebastián</a>) and Mediterranean (<a href="/wiki/Girona" title="Girona">Girona</a> to <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">Cádiz</a>) coasts. Spain aims to put one million <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric cars</a> on the road by 2014 as part of the government's plan to save energy and boost <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>. The Minister of Industry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miguel_Sebastian" title="Miguel Sebastian">Miguel Sebastian</a> said that "the electric vehicle is the future and the engine of an industrial revolution."</p>

<p>Spain has the most extensive high-speed rail network in Europe, and the second-most extensive in the world after China. As of October 2010, Spain has a total of 3,500 km (2,174.80 mi) of high-speed tracks linking <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a>, with the trains reaching speeds up to 300 km/h (190 mph). On average, the Spanish high-speed train is the fastest one in the world, followed by the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Shinkansen" title="Shinkansen">bullet train</a> and the French <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a>. Regarding punctuality, it is second in the world (98.54% on-time arrival) after the Japanese Shinkansen (99%). Should the aims of the ambitious <a href="/wiki/AVE" title="AVE">AVE</a> programme (Spanish high speed trains) be met, by 2020 Spain will have 7,000 km (4,300 mi) of high-speed trains linking almost all provincial cities to Madrid in less than three hours and Barcelona within four hours.</p>
<p>There are 47 public airports in Spain. The busiest one is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrid_Airport" title="Madrid Airport">airport of Madrid</a> (Barajas), with 50 million passengers in 2011, being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_busiest_airports_by_passenger_traffic" title="World's busiest airports by passenger traffic">world's 15th busiest airport</a>, as well as the European Union's fourth busiest. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barcelona_Airport" title="Barcelona Airport">airport of Barcelona</a> (El Prat) is also important, with 35 million passengers in 2011, being the world's 31st-busiest airport. Other main airports are located in <a href="/wiki/Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport" title="Palma de Mallorca Airport">Majorca</a> (23 million passengers), <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga_Airport" title="Málaga Airport">Málaga</a> (13 million passengers), <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria_Airport" title="Gran Canaria Airport">Las Palmas (Gran Canaria)</a> (11 million passengers), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alicante_Airport" title="Alicante Airport">Alicante</a> (10 million passengers) and smaller, with the number of passengers between 4 and 10 million, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenerife_South_Airport" title="Tenerife South Airport">Tenerife</a> (two airports), <a href="/wiki/Valencia_Airport" title="Valencia Airport">Valencia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seville_Airport" title="Seville Airport">Seville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilbao_Airport" title="Bilbao Airport">Bilbao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibiza_Airport" title="Ibiza Airport">Ibiza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote_Airport" title="Lanzarote Airport">Lanzarote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura_Airport" title="Fuerteventura Airport">Fuerteventura</a>. Also, more than 30 airports with the number of passengers below 4 million.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_technology">Science and technology</span></h3>

<p>In the 19th and 20th centuries science in Spain was held back by severe political instability and consequent economic underdevelopment. Despite the conditions, some important scientists and engineers emerged. The most notable were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miguel_Servet" title="Miguel Servet">Miguel Servet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal" title="Santiago Ramón y Cajal">Santiago Ramón y Cajal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narc%C3%ADs_Monturiol" title="Narcís Monturiol">Narcís Monturiol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celedonio_Calatayud" title="Celedonio Calatayud">Celedonio Calatayud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_la_Cierva" title="Juan de la Cierva">Juan de la Cierva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Torres_y_Quevedo" title="Leonardo Torres y Quevedo">Leonardo Torres y Quevedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margarita_Salas" title="Margarita Salas">Margarita Salas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Severo_Ochoa" title="Severo Ochoa">Severo Ochoa</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_supply_and_sanitation">Water supply and sanitation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_and_sanitation_in_Spain" title="Water supply and sanitation in Spain">Water supply and sanitation in Spain</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">Water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">Sanitation</a> in Spain is characterised by universal access and generally good service quality, while tariffs are among the lowest in the EU. Almost half of the population is served by private or mixed private-public water companies, which operate under concession contracts with municipalities. The largest of the private water companies, with a market share of about 50% of the private concessions, is Aguas de Barcelona (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agbar" title="Agbar">Agbar</a>). However, the large cities are all served by public companies except Barcelona and Valencia. The largest public company is <a href="/wiki/Canal_de_Isabel_II" title="Canal de Isabel II">Canal de Isabel II</a>, which serves the metropolitan area of Madrid.</p>
<p>Droughts affect water supply in Southern Spain, which increasingly is turning towards seawater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_desalination" title="Water desalination">desalination</a> to meet its water needs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Spain" title="Demographics of Spain">Demographics of Spain</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_autonomous_communities_by_population" title="List of Spanish autonomous communities by population">List of Spanish autonomous communities by population</a></div>

<p>In 2008 the population of Spain officially reached 46 million people, as recorded by the <i>Padrón municipal</i> (Spain's Municipal Register). Spain's population density, at 91/km² (235/sq mi), is lower than that of most Western European countries and its distribution across the country is very unequal. With the exception of the region surrounding the capital, <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, the most populated areas lie around the coast. The population of Spain more than doubled since 1900, when it stood at 18.6 million, principally due to the spectacular demographic boom in the 1960s and early 1970s.</p>
<p>Native <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> make up 88% of the total population of Spain. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_territories_by_fertility_rate" title="List of countries and territories by fertility rate">birth rate</a> plunged in the 1980s and Spain's population growth rate dropped, the population again trended upward, based initially on the return of many Spaniards who had emigrated to other European countries during the 1970s, and more recently, fuelled by large numbers of immigrants who make up 12% of the population. The immigrants originate mainly in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> (39%), <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> (16%), <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> (15%), and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> (4%). In 2005, Spain instituted a three-month amnesty programme through which certain hitherto undocumented aliens were granted legal residency.</p>
<p>In 2008, Spain granted citizenship to 84,170 persons, mostly to people from Ecuador, Colombia and Morocco. A sizeable portion of foreign residents in Spain also comes from other Western and Central European countries. These are mostly British, French, German, Dutch, and Norwegian. They reside primarily on the Mediterranean coast and the Balearic islands, where many choose to live their retirement or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommute" title="Telecommute">telecommute</a>.</p>
<p>Substantial populations descended from Spanish colonists and immigrants exist in other parts of the world, most notably in Latin America. Beginning in the late 15th century, large numbers of Iberian colonists settled in what became Latin America and at present most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Latin_American" title="White Latin American">white Latin Americans</a> (who make up about one-third of Latin America's population) are of Spanish or Portuguese origin. Around 240,000 Spaniards emigrated in the 16th century, mostly to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Another 450,000 left in the 17th century. Between 1846 and 1932 it is estimated that nearly 5 million Spaniards emigrated to the Americas, especially to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. Approximately two million Spaniards migrated to other Western European countries between 1960 and 1975. During the same period perhaps 300,000 went to Latin America.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_areas_and_Functional_urban_areas.5B133.5D">Metropolitan areas and <a href="/wiki/Larger_urban_zone" title="Larger urban zone">Functional urban areas</a></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peoples">Peoples</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalisms_and_regionalisms_of_Spain" title="Nationalisms and regionalisms of Spain">Nationalisms and regionalisms of Spain</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Spanish Constitution of 1978</a>, in its second article, recognises historic entities—<i><a href="/wiki/Nationalities_and_regions_of_Spain" title="Nationalities and regions of Spain">nationalities</a></i> (a carefully chosen word to avoid the more politically charged "nations")—and regions, within the context of the Spanish nation. For some people, Spain's identity consists more of an overlap of different regional identities than of a sole Spanish identity. Indeed, some of the regional identities may even conflict with the Spanish one. Distinct traditional regional identities within Spain include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">Basques</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_people" title="Catalan people">Catalans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galician_people" title="Galician people">Galicians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andalusians" title="Andalusians">Andalusians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencian_people" title="Valencian people">Valencians</a>, although to some extent all of the 17 Autonomous Communities will claim a distinct historic identity.</p>
<p>It is this last feature of "shared identity" between the more local level or Autonomous Community and the Spanish level which makes the identity question in Spain complex and far from univocal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minority_groups">Minority groups</span></h3>
<p>Spain has a number of descendants of populations from former colonies, especially <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. Smaller numbers of immigrants from several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan" title="Sub-Saharan">Sub-Saharan</a> countries have recently been settling in Spain. There are also sizeable numbers of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> immigrants, most of whom are of <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Asian" title="South Asian">South Asian</a> and Chinese origin. The single largest group of immigrants are European; represented by large numbers of Britons, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a>, French and others.</p>

<p>The arrival of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roma_in_Spain" title="Roma in Spain">gitanos</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani people</a>, began in the 16th century; estimates of the Spanish Gitano population range from 750,000 to over one million. There are also the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchero" title="Merchero">mercheros</a></i> (also <i>quinquis</i>), a formerly nomadic minority group. Their origin is unclear.</p>
<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">moriscos</a> are the main minority groups originated in Spain and with a contribution to Spanish culture. The Spanish government is offering Spanish nationality to sephardi Jews.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration">Immigration</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Spain" title="Immigration to Spain">Immigration to Spain</a></div>

<p>According to the Spanish government there were 5.7 million foreign residents in Spain in 2011, or 12% of the total population. According to residence permit data for 2011, more than 860,000 were <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanian</a>, about 770,000 were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_people" title="Moroccan people">Moroccan</a>, approximately 390,000 were British, and 360,000 were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecuadorian_people" title="Ecuadorian people">Ecuadorian</a>. Other sizeable foreign communities are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_people" title="Colombian people">Colombian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolivian_people" title="Bolivian people">Bolivian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarians" title="Bulgarians">Bulgarian</a>, and Chinese. There are more than 200,000 migrants from Sub-Saharan <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> living in Spain, principally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senegalese_people" title="Senegalese people">Senegaleses</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nigerian_people" title="Nigerian people">Nigerians</a>. Since 2000, Spain has experienced high <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> as a result of immigration flows, despite a birth rate that is only half the replacement level. This sudden and ongoing inflow of immigrants, particularly those arriving illegally by sea, has caused noticeable social tension.</p>

<p>Within the EU, Spain had the 2nd highest immigration rate in percentage terms after <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, but by a great margin, the highest in absolute numbers, up to 2008. The number of immigrants in Spain had grown up from 500,000 people in 1996 to 5.2 million in 2008 out of a total population of 46 million. In 2005 alone, a regularisation programme increased the legal immigrant population by 700,000 people. There are a number of reasons for the high level of immigration, including Spain's cultural ties with <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, its geographical position, the porosity of its borders, the large size of its underground economy and the strength of the agricultural and construction sectors, which demand more low cost labour than can be offered by the national workforce.</p>
<p>Another statistically significant factor is the large number of residents of EU origin typically retiring to Spain's Mediterranean coast. In fact, Spain was Europe's largest absorber of migrants from 2002 to 2007, with its immigrant population more than doubling as 2.5 million people arrived. In 2008, prior to the onset of the economic crisis, the <i>Financial Times</i> reported that Spain was the most favoured destination for Western Europeans considering a move from their own country and seeking jobs elsewhere in the EU.</p>
<p>In 2008, the government instituted a "Plan of Voluntary Return" which encouraged unemployed immigrants from outside the EU to return to their home countries and receive several incentives, including the right to keep their unemployment benefits and transfer whatever they contributed to the Spanish Social Security. The programme had little effect; during its first two months, just 1,400 immigrants took up the offer. What the programme failed to do, the sharp and prolonged economic crisis has done from 2010 to 2011 in that tens of thousands of immigrants have left the country due to lack of jobs. In 2011 alone, more than half a million people left Spain. For the first time in decades the net migration rate was expected to be negative, and nine out of 10 emigrants were foreigners.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Spain" title="Languages of Spain">Languages of Spain</a></div>

<p>Spain is openly multilingual, and the constitution establishes that the nation will protect "all Spaniards and the peoples of Spain in the exercise of human rights, their cultures and traditions, languages and institutions.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> (<i>español</i>)—officially recognised in the constitution as <a href="/wiki/Names_given_to_the_Spanish_language" title="Names given to the Spanish language">Castilian</a> (<i>castellano</i>)—is the official language of the entire country, and it is the right and duty of every Spaniard to know the language. The constitution also establishes that "all other Spanish languages"—that is, all other languages of Spain—will also be official in their respective autonomous communities in accordance to their <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy" title="Statute of Autonomy">Statutes</a>, their organic regional legislations, and that the "richness of the distinct linguistic modalities of Spain represents a patrimony which will be the object of special respect and protection."</p>
<p>The other official languages of Spain, co-official with Spanish are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> (<i>euskara</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> (<i>català</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">Valencian Community</a>, where its distinct modality of the language is officially known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencian_language" title="Valencian language">Valencian</a> (<i>valencià</i>); and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a> (<i>galego</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a></li>
</ul>
<p>As a percentage of the general population, Basque is spoken by 2%, Catalan (or Valencian) by 17%, and Galician by 7% of all Spaniards.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aranese_language" title="Aranese language">Aranese</a> (<i>aranés</i>), a local variety of the <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan language</a>, has been declared co-official along with Catalan and Spanish since 2006. It is spoken only in the <i>comarca</i> of <i><a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Aran" title="Val d'Aran">Val d'Aran</a></i> by roughly 6,700 people. Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">Romance</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minority_languages" title="Minority languages">minority languages</a>, though not official, have special recognition, such as the Astur-Leonese group (<a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">Asturian</a> – <i>asturianu</i>, also called <i>bable</i> – in <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonese_language" title="Leonese language">Leonese</a> – <i>llionés</i> – in <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">Castile and León</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">Aragonese</a> (<i>aragonés</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> Spanish autonomous city of <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riff_language" title="Riff language">Riff Berber</a> is spoken by a significant part of the population. In the tourist areas of the Mediterranean coast and the islands, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> are widely spoken by tourists, foreign residents, and tourism workers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Spain" title="Education in Spain">Education in Spain</a></div>

<p>State education in Spain is free and compulsory from the age of six to sixteen. The current education system was established by the 2006 educational law, LOE (<i>Ley Orgánica de Educación</i>), or Fundamental Law for the Education. In 2014, the LOE was partially modified by the newer LOMCE law (<i>Ley Orgánica para la Mejora de la Calidad Educativa</i>), or Fundamental Law for the Improvement of the Education System, commonly called <i>Ley Wert</i> (Wert Law). Since 1970 to 2014, Spain has had seven different educational laws (LGE, LOECE, LODE, LOGSE, LOPEG, LOE and LOMCE).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Instituci%C3%B3n_Libre_de_Ense%C3%B1anza" title="Institución Libre de Enseñanza">Institución Libre de Enseñanza</a> was an educational project that developed in Spain for the half a century of about 1876–1936 by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Giner_de_los_R%C3%ADos" title="Francisco Giner de los Ríos">Francisco Giner de los Ríos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gumersindo_de_Azc%C3%A1rate" title="Gumersindo de Azcárate">Gumersindo de Azcárate</a>. The institute was inspired by the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Krausism" title="Krausism">Krausism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n_Arenal" title="Concepción Arenal">Concepción Arenal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal" title="Santiago Ramón y Cajal">Santiago Ramón y Cajal</a> in neuroscience were in the movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Spain" title="Religion in Spain">Religion in Spain</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Spain" title="History of the Jews in Spain">History of the Jews in Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_in_Spain" title="Bahá'í Faith in Spain">Bahá'í Faith in Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Spain" title="Hinduism in Spain">Hinduism in Spain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Spain" title="Islam in Spain">Islam in Spain</a></div>
<div class="barbox tright" style="border:1px solid silver; font-size:88%; padding:0.4em; width:auto; background: white;">

</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholicism</a> has long been the main religion of Spain, and although it no longer has official status by law, in all public schools in Spain students have to choose either a religion or ethics class, and Catholicism is the only religion officially taught. According to an April 2014 study by the Spanish Centre for Sociological Research about 69% of Spaniards self-identify as Catholics, 2% other faith, and about 26% identify with <a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">no religion</a>. Most Spaniards do not participate regularly in religious services. This same study shows that of the Spaniards who identify themselves as religious, 59% hardly ever or never go to church, 15% go to church some times a year, 8% some time per month and 14% every Sunday or multiple times per week. Recent polls and surveys have revealed that <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheists</a> comprise anywhere from 8% to 20% of the Spanish population.</p>

<p>Altogether, about 22% of the entire Spanish population attends religious services at least once per month. Though Spanish society has become considerably more secular in recent decades, the influx of Latin American immigrants, who tend to be strong Catholic practitioners, has helped the Catholic Church to recover.</p>
<p>There have been four Spanish <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Popes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Damasus_I" title="Pope Damasus I">Damasus I</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pope_Calixtus_III" title="Pope Calixtus III">Calixtus III</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Alexander VI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Benedict_XIII" title="Antipope Benedict XIII">Benedict XIII</a>. Spanish misticism was an important intellectual fight against Protestantism with <a href="/wiki/Teresa_of_%C3%81vila" title="Teresa of Ávila">Teresa of Ávila</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">reformist</a> nun, ahead. The <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Society of Jesus</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Loyola" title="Ignatius of Loyola">Ignatius of Loyola</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Javier" title="Francisco Javier">Francisco Javier</a>. In the 60´s of the XX Century, jesuits <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Arrupe" title="Pedro Arrupe">Pedro Arrupe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ignacio_Ellacuria" title="Ignacio Ellacuria">Ignacio Ellacuria</a> were inside the movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberation_Theology" title="Liberation Theology">Liberation Theology</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> churches have about 1,200,000 members. There are about 105,000 <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> has approximately 46,000 adherents in 133 congregations in all regions of the country and has a <a href="/wiki/Madrid_Spain_Temple" title="Madrid Spain Temple">temple</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Moratalaz" title="Moratalaz">Moratalaz</a> District of Madrid.</p>

<p>A study made by the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Islamic_Communities_of_Spain" title="Union of Islamic Communities of Spain">Union of Islamic Communities of Spain</a> demonstrated that there were about 1,700,000 inhabitants of Muslim background living in Spain as of 2012, accounting for 3–4% of the total population of Spain. The vast majority was composed of immigrants and descendants originating from <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and other African countries. More than 514,000 (30%) of them had Spanish nationality.</p>
<p>The recent waves of immigration have also led to an increasing number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhists" title="Buddhists">Buddhists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikhs" title="Sikhs">Sikhs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>. After the Reconquista in 1492, Muslims did not live in Spain for centuries. Late 19th-century colonial expansion in northwestern Africa gave a number of residents in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">Spanish Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> full citizenship. Their ranks have since been bolstered by recent immigration, especially from Morocco and Algeria.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> was practically non-existent in Spain from the 1492 expulsion until the 19th century, when Jews were again permitted to enter the country. Currently there are around 62,000 Jews in Spain, or 0.14% of the total population. Most are arrivals in the past century, while some are descendants of earlier Spanish Jews. Approximately 80,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a> are thought to have lived in Spain prior to its expulsion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Spain" title="Culture of Spain">Culture of Spain</a></div>
<p>Culturally, Spain is a <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western country</a>. Almost every aspect of Spanish life is permeated by its Roman heritage, making Spain one of the major <a href="/wiki/Romance-speaking_Europe" title="Romance-speaking Europe">Latin countries</a> of Europe. Spanish culture is marked by strong historic ties to Catholicism, which played a pivotal role in the country's formation and subsequent identity. Spanish art, architecture, cuisine, and music has been shaped by successive waves of foreign invaders, as well as by the country's Mediterranean climate and geography. The centuries-long colonial era globalised Spanish language and culture, with Spain also absorbing the cultural and commercial products of its diverse empire.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monuments_and_World_Heritage_Sites">Monuments and World Heritage Sites</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites_in_Spain" title="World Heritage Sites in Spain">World Heritage Sites in Spain</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castles_in_Spain" title="Castles in Spain">Castles in Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cathedrals_in_Spain" title="Cathedrals in Spain">Cathedrals in Spain</a></div>
<p>It should be noted that after <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> (49) and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (45), Spain is the third country in the world with the most <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a>. At the present time it has 44 recognised sites, including the landscape of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Perdido" title="Monte Perdido">Monte Perdido</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, which is shared with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the Prehistoric Rock Art Sites of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4a_Valley" title="Côa Valley">Côa Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siega_Verde" title="Siega Verde">Siega Verde</a>, which is shared with <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> (the Portuguese part being in the Côa Valley, <a href="/wiki/Guarda_District" title="Guarda District">Guarda</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Heritage_of_Mercury._Almad%C3%A9n_and_Idrija" title="Heritage of Mercury. Almadén and Idrija">Heritage of Mercury</a>, shared with <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>. In addition, Spain has also 14 <a href="/wiki/Intangible_cultural_heritage" title="Intangible cultural heritage">Intangible cultural heritage</a>, or "Human treasures", Spain ranks first in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> according to <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_Intangible_Cultural_Heritage_Lists" title="UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage Lists">UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage List</a>, tied with <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_literature" title="Spanish literature">Spanish literature</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Spanish_Academy" title="Royal Spanish Academy">Royal Spanish Academy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Cervantes" title="Instituto Cervantes">Instituto Cervantes</a></div>
<p>The earliest recorded examples of vernacular Romance-based literature date from the same time and location, the rich mix of Muslim, Jewish, and Christian cultures in Muslim Spain, in which Maimonides, Averroes, and others worked, the <a href="/wiki/Kharja" title="Kharja">Kharjas</a> (<i>Jarchas</i>).</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, the epic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Cantar_de_Mio_Cid" title="Cantar de Mio Cid">Cantar de Mio Cid</a></i> was written about a real man—his battles, conquests, and daily life.</p>

<p>Other major plays from the medieval times were <i><a href="/wiki/Mester_de_Juglar%C3%ADa" title="Mester de Juglaría">Mester de Juglaría</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mester_de_Clerec%C3%ADa" title="Mester de Clerecía">Mester de Clerecía</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coplas_por_la_muerte_de_su_padre" title="Coplas por la muerte de su padre">Coplas por la muerte de su padre</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Good_Love" title="The Book of Good Love">El Libro de buen amor</a></i> (The Book of Good Love).</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> the major plays are <i><a href="/wiki/La_Celestina" title="La Celestina">La Celestina</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/El_Lazarillo_de_Tormes" title="El Lazarillo de Tormes">El Lazarillo de Tormes</a></i>, while many religious literature was created with poets as <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Luis de León">Luis de León</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Juan_de_la_Cruz" title="San Juan de la Cruz">San Juan de la Cruz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Teresa_de_Jes%C3%BAs" title="Santa Teresa de Jesús">Santa Teresa de Jesús</a>, etc.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> is the most important period for Spanish culture. We are in the times of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>. The famous <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quijote de La Mancha</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a> was written in this time. Other writers from the period are: <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Quevedo" title="Francisco de Quevedo">Francisco de Quevedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Vega" title="Lope de Vega">Lope de Vega</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Calderón de la Barca">Calderón de la Barca</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tirso_de_Molina" title="Tirso de Molina">Tirso de Molina</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> we find names such as <a href="/wiki/Leandro_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Morat%C3%ADn" title="Leandro Fernández de Moratín">Leandro Fernández de Moratín</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benito_Jer%C3%B3nimo_Feij%C3%B3o" title="Benito Jerónimo Feijóo">Benito Jerónimo Feijóo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Melchor_de_Jovellanos" title="Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos">Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos</a> or <a href="/wiki/Leandro_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Morat%C3%ADn" title="Leandro Fernández de Moratín">Leandro Fernández de Moratín</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Zorrilla" title="José Zorrilla">José Zorrilla</a> created one of the most emblematic figures in European literature in <a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_Tenorio" title="Don Juan Tenorio">Don Juan Tenorio</a>. Other writers from this period are <a href="/wiki/Gustavo_Adolfo_B%C3%A9cquer" title="Gustavo Adolfo Bécquer">Gustavo Adolfo Bécquer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Espronceda" title="José de Espronceda">José de Espronceda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosal%C3%ADa_de_Castro" title="Rosalía de Castro">Rosalía de Castro</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Jos%C3%A9_de_Larra" title="Mariano José de Larra">Mariano José de Larra</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">Realism</a> we find names such as <a href="/wiki/Benito_P%C3%A9rez_Gald%C3%B3s" title="Benito Pérez Galdós">Benito Pérez Galdós</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilia_Pardo_Baz%C3%A1n" title="Emilia Pardo Bazán">Emilia Pardo Bazán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Alas" title="Leopoldo Alas">Leopoldo Alas</a> (Clarín), <a href="/wiki/Concepci%C3%B3n_Arenal" title="Concepción Arenal">Concepción Arenal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Blasco_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez" title="Vicente Blasco Ibáñez">Vicente Blasco Ibáñez</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men%C3%A9ndez_Pelayo" title="Menéndez Pelayo">Menéndez Pelayo</a>. Realism offered depictions of contemporary life and society 'as they were'. In the spirit of general "Realism", Realist authors opted for depictions of everyday and banal activities and experiences, instead of romanticised or stylised presentations.</p>
<p>The group that has become known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_of_1898" title="Generation of 1898">Generation of 1898</a> was marked by the destruction of Spain's fleet in Cuba by US gunboats in 1898, which provoked a cultural crisis in Spain. The "Disaster" of 1898 led established writers to seek practical political, economic, and social solutions in essays grouped under the literary heading of <i>Regeneracionismo</i>. For a group of younger writers, among them <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Unamuno" title="Miguel de Unamuno">Miguel de Unamuno</a>, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%ADo_Baroja" title="Pío Baroja">Pío Baroja</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mart%C3%ADnez_Ruiz" title="José Martínez Ruiz">José Martínez Ruiz</a> (Azorín), the Disaster and its cultural repercussions inspired a deeper, more radical literary shift that affected both form and content. These writers, along with <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_del_Valle-Incl%C3%A1n" title="Ramón del Valle-Inclán">Ramón del Valle-Inclán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Machado" title="Antonio Machado">Antonio Machado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramiro_de_Maeztu" title="Ramiro de Maeztu">Ramiro de Maeztu</a>, and Ángel Ganivet, came to be known as the 'Generation of 98'.</p>

<p>The Generation of 1914 or Novecentismo. The next supposed "generation" of Spanish writers following those of '98 already calls into question the value of such terminology. By the year 1914—the year of the outbreak of the First World War and of the publication of the first major work of the generation's leading voice, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ortega_y_Gasset" title="José Ortega y Gasset">José Ortega y Gasset</a>—a number of slightly younger writers had established their own place within the Spanish cultural field.</p>
<p>Leading voices include the poet <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ram%C3%B3n_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="Juan Ramón Jiménez">Juan Ramón Jiménez</a>, the academics and essayists <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Men%C3%A9ndez_Pidal" title="Ramón Menéndez Pidal">Ramón Menéndez Pidal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Mara%C3%B1%C3%B3n" title="Gregorio Marañón">Gregorio Marañón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Aza%C3%B1a" title="Manuel Azaña">Manuel Azaña</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Zambrano" title="Maria Zambrano">Maria Zambrano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugeni_d%27Ors" title="Eugeni d'Ors">Eugeni d'Ors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clara_Campoamor" title="Clara Campoamor">Clara Campoamor</a> and Ortega y Gasset, and the novelists Gabriel Miró, <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_P%C3%A9rez_de_Ayala" title="Ramón Pérez de Ayala">Ramón Pérez de Ayala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_G%C3%B3mez_de_la_Serna" title="Ramón Gómez de la Serna">Ramón Gómez de la Serna</a>. While still driven by the national and existential questions that obsessed the writers of '98, they approached these topics with a greater sense of distance and objectivity. <a href="/wiki/Salvador_de_Madariaga" title="Salvador de Madariaga">Salvador de Madariaga</a>, another prominent intellectual and writer, was one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Europe" title="College of Europe">College of Europe</a> and the composer of the constitutive manifest of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_International" title="Liberal International">Liberal International</a>.</p>
<p>The Generation of 1927, where poets Pedro Salinas, <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Guill%C3%A9n" title="Jorge Guillén">Jorge Guillén</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">Federico García Lorca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Aleixandre" title="Vicente Aleixandre">Vicente Aleixandre</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1maso_Alonso" title="Dámaso Alonso">Dámaso Alonso</a>. All were scholars of their national literary heritage, again evidence of the impact of the calls of <i>regeneracionistas</i> and the Generation of 1898 for Spanish intelligence to turn at least partially inwards.</p>

<p>The two main writers in the second half of the 20th century were the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a> laureate <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Jos%C3%A9_Cela" title="Camilo José Cela">Camilo José Cela</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Delibes" title="Miguel Delibes">Miguel Delibes</a> from <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2736" title="Generation of '36">Generation of '36</a>. Spain is one of the countries with the most number of laureates with the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>, and with <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a> laureates they made the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish language</a> literature one of the most laureates of all. The Spanish writers are: <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Echegaray" title="José Echegaray">José Echegaray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacinto_Benavente" title="Jacinto Benavente">Jacinto Benavente</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ram%C3%B3n_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="Juan Ramón Jiménez">Juan Ramón Jiménez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Aleixandre" title="Vicente Aleixandre">Vicente Aleixandre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Jos%C3%A9_Cela" title="Camilo José Cela">Camilo José Cela</a>. The Portuguese writer <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago" title="José Saramago">José Saramago</a>, also awarded with the prize, lived for many years in Spain and spoke both Portuguese and Spanish. He was also well known by his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberism" title="Iberism">Iberist</a> ideas.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2750" title="Generation of '50">Generation of '50</a> are also known as the children of the civil war. <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Chacel" title="Rosa Chacel">Rosa Chacel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Fuertes" title="Gloria Fuertes">Gloria Fuertes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Gil_de_Biedma" title="Jaime Gil de Biedma">Jaime Gil de Biedma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Goytisolo" title="Juan Goytisolo">Juan Goytisolo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmen_Martin_Gaite" title="Carmen Martin Gaite">Carmen Martin Gaite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ana_Maria_Matute" title="Ana Maria Matute">Ana Maria Matute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mars%C3%A9" title="Juan Marsé">Juan Marsé</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blas_de_Otero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blas de Otero (page does not exist)">Blas de Otero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Celaya" title="Gabriel Celaya">Gabriel Celaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gamoneda" title="Antonio Gamoneda">Antonio Gamoneda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rafael_S%C3%A1nchez_Ferlosio" title="Rafael Sánchez Ferlosio">Rafael Sánchez Ferlosio</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ignacio_Aldecoa" title="Ignacio Aldecoa">Ignacio Aldecoa</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_art" title="Spanish art">Spanish art</a></div>

<p>Artists from Spain have been highly influential in the development of various European <a href="/wiki/Art_movement" title="Art movement">artistic movements</a>. Due to historical, geographical and generational diversity, Spanish art has known a great number of influences. The Moorish heritage in Spain, especially in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, is still evident today and European influences include Italy, Germany and France, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassical</a> periods.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age" title="Golden Age">Golden Age</a> we find painters such as <a href="/wiki/El_Greco" title="El Greco">El Greco</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Ribera" title="José de Ribera">José de Ribera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Esteban_Murillo" title="Bartolomé Esteban Murillo">Bartolomé Esteban Murillo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Zurbar%C3%A1n" title="Francisco Zurbarán">Francisco Zurbarán</a>. Also inside Baroque period <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a> created some of the most famous Spanish portraits, like <a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Las_Hilanderas_(Vel%C3%A1zquez)" title="Las Hilanderas (Velázquez)">Las Hilanderas</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a> painted during a historical period that includes the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Spanish Independence War</a>, the fights between liberals and absolutists, and the raise of state-nations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Sorolla" title="Joaquín Sorolla">Joaquín Sorolla</a> is a well-known impressionist painter and there are many important Spanish painters belonging to the modernism art movement, including <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gris" title="Juan Gris">Juan Gris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Joan Miró</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">Sculpture</span></h3>

<p>The Plateresque style extended from beginnings of the 16th century until the last third of the century and its stylistic influence pervaded the works of all great Spanish artists of the time. <a href="/wiki/Alonso_Berruguete" title="Alonso Berruguete">Alonso Berruguete</a> (<a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a> School) is called the "Prince of Spanish sculpture". His main works were the upper stalls of the choir of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Toledo" title="Cathedral of Toledo">Cathedral of Toledo</a>, the tomb of Cardinal Tavera in the same Cathedral, and the altarpiece of the Visitation in the church of Santa Úrsula in the same locality. Other notable sculptors were <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Ord%C3%B3%C3%B1ez" title="Bartolomé Ordóñez">Bartolomé Ordóñez</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diego_de_Silo%C3%A9" title="Diego de Siloé">Diego de Siloé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Juni" title="Juan de Juni">Juan de Juni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A0_Forment" title="Damià Forment">Damián Forment</a>.</p>
<p>There were two Schools of special flair and talent: the <a href="/wiki/Sevillian_school_of_sculpture" title="Sevillian school of sculpture">Seville School</a>, to which <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mart%C3%ADnez_Monta%C3%B1%C3%A9s" title="Juan Martínez Montañés">Juan Martínez Montañés</a> belonged, whose most celebrated works are the Crucifix in the Cathedral of Seville, another in Vergara, and a Saint John; and the <a href="/wiki/Granadan_school_of_sculpture" title="Granadan school of sculpture">Granada School</a>, to which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alonso_Cano" title="Alonso Cano">Alonso Cano</a> belonged, to whom an Immaculate Conception and a Virgin of Rosary, are attributed.</p>
<p>Other notable Andalusian Baroque sculptors were <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Mena" title="Pedro de Mena">Pedro de Mena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Rold%C3%A1n" title="Pedro Roldán">Pedro Roldán</a> and his daughter <a href="/wiki/Luisa_Rold%C3%A1n" title="Luisa Roldán">Luisa Roldán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Mesa" title="Juan de Mesa">Juan de Mesa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro_Duque_Cornejo" title="Pedro Duque Cornejo">Pedro Duque Cornejo</a>. In the 20th century the most important Spanish sculptors were <a href="/wiki/Julio_Gonz%C3%A1lez_(sculptor)" title="Julio González (sculptor)">Julio González</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Gargallo" title="Pablo Gargallo">Pablo Gargallo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Chillida" title="Eduardo Chillida">Eduardo Chillida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Serrano" title="Pablo Serrano">Pablo Serrano</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema">Cinema</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Spain" title="Cinema of Spain">Cinema of Spain</a></div>

<p>Spanish cinema has achieved major international success including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Oscars</a> for recent films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pan%27s_Labyrinth" title="Pan's Labyrinth">Pan's Labyrinth</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Volver" title="Volver">Volver</a></i>. In the long history of Spanish cinema, the great filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a> was the first to achieve world recognition, followed by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Pedro Almodóvar">Pedro Almodóvar</a> in the 1980s (<a href="/wiki/La_Movida_Madrile%C3%B1a" title="La Movida Madrileña">La Movida Madrileña</a>). <a href="/wiki/Mario_Camus" title="Mario Camus">Mario Camus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pilar_Mir%C3%B3" title="Pilar Miró">Pilar Miró</a> worked together in <a href="/wiki/Curro_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="Curro Jiménez">Curro Jiménez</a>.</p>
<p>Spanish cinema has also seen international success over the years with films by <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">directors</a> like <a href="/wiki/Segundo_de_Chom%C3%B3n" title="Segundo de Chomón">Segundo de Chomón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flori%C3%A1n_Rey" title="Florián Rey">Florián Rey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Garc%C3%ADa_Berlanga" title="Luis García Berlanga">Luis García Berlanga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Saura" title="Carlos Saura">Carlos Saura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julio_Medem" title="Julio Medem">Julio Medem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Coixet" title="Isabel Coixet">Isabel Coixet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Amen%C3%A1bar" title="Alejandro Amenábar">Alejandro Amenábar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ic%C3%ADar_Bolla%C3%ADn" title="Icíar Bollaín">Icíar Bollaín</a> and brothers <a href="/wiki/David_Trueba" title="David Trueba">David Trueba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Trueba" title="Fernando Trueba">Fernando Trueba</a>.</p>
<p>Actresses <a href="/wiki/Sara_Montiel" title="Sara Montiel">Sara Montiel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pen%C3%A9lope_Cruz" title="Penélope Cruz">Penélope Cruz</a> or actor <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Banderas" title="Antonio Banderas">Antonio Banderas</a> are among those who have become <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> stars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_architecture" title="Spanish architecture">Spanish architecture</a></div>

<p>Due to its historical and geographical diversity, Spanish architecture has drawn from a host of influences. An important provincial city founded by the Romans and with an extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_era" title="Roman era">Roman era</a> infrastructure, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">Córdoba</a> became the cultural capital, including fine Arabic style architecture, during the time of the Islamic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad_dynasty" title="Umayyad dynasty">Umayyad dynasty</a>. Later Arab style architecture continued to be developed under successive Islamic dynasties, ending with the <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">Nasrid</a>, which built its famed palace complex in <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>.</p>
<p>Simultaneously, the Christian kingdoms gradually emerged and developed their own styles; developing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Romanesque" title="Pre-Romanesque">pre-Romanesque</a> style when for a while isolated from contemporary mainstream European architectural influences during the earlier Middle Ages, they later integrated the <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> streams. There was then an extraordinary flowering of the Gothic style that resulted in numerous instances being built throughout the entire territory. The <a href="/wiki/Mud%C3%A9jar" title="Mudéjar">Mudéjar</a> style, from the 12th to 17th centuries, was developed by introducing Arab style motifs, patterns and elements into European architecture.</p>
<p>The arrival of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a> in the academic arena produced much of the architecture of the 20th century. An influential style centred in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Modernisme" title="Modernisme">modernisme</a>, produced a number of important architects, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaud%C3%AD" title="Gaudí">Gaudí</a> is one. The <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">International style</a> was led by groups like <a href="/wiki/GATEPAC" title="GATEPAC">GATEPAC</a>. Spain is currently experiencing a revolution in <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_architecture" title="Contemporary architecture">contemporary architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Spanish_architects" title="Category:Spanish architects">Spanish architects</a> like <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Moneo" title="Rafael Moneo">Rafael Moneo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Bofill" title="Ricardo Bofill">Ricardo Bofill</a> as well as many others have gained worldwide renown.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_dance">Music and dance</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Spain" title="Music of Spain">Music of Spain</a></div>

<p>Spanish music is often considered abroad to be synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">flamenco</a>, a West Andalusian musical genre, which, contrary to popular belief, is not widespread outside that region. Various regional styles of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> abound in Aragon, Catalonia, Valencia, Castile, the Basque Country, Galicia and Asturias. Pop, rock, hip hop and heavy metal are also popular.</p>
<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>, Spain has produced a number of noted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composers" title="Composers">composers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Alb%C3%A9niz" title="Isaac Albéniz">Isaac Albéniz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_de_Falla" title="Manuel de Falla">Manuel de Falla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Granados" title="Enrique Granados">Enrique Granados</a> and singers and performers such as <a href="/wiki/Pl%C3%A1cido_Domingo" title="Plácido Domingo">Plácido Domingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Carreras" title="José Carreras">José Carreras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat_Caball%C3%A9" title="Montserrat Caballé">Montserrat Caballé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alicia_de_Larrocha" title="Alicia de Larrocha">Alicia de Larrocha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Kraus" title="Alfredo Kraus">Alfredo Kraus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Casals" title="Pablo Casals">Pablo Casals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Vi%C3%B1es" title="Ricardo Viñes">Ricardo Viñes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Iturbi" title="José Iturbi">José Iturbi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_de_Sarasate" title="Pablo de Sarasate">Pablo de Sarasate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordi_Savall" title="Jordi Savall">Jordi Savall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teresa_Berganza" title="Teresa Berganza">Teresa Berganza</a>. In Spain there are over forty professional orchestras, including the <a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Symphony_and_Catalonia_National_Orchestra" title="Barcelona Symphony and Catalonia National Orchestra">Orquestra Simfònica de Barcelona</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orquesta_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a" title="Orquesta Nacional de España">Orquesta Nacional de España</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Orquesta_Sinf%C3%B3nica_de_Madrid" title="Orquesta Sinfónica de Madrid">Orquesta Sinfónica de Madrid</a>. Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opera_houses" title="Opera houses">opera houses</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Real" title="Teatro Real">Teatro Real</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gran_Teatre_del_Liceu" title="Gran Teatre del Liceu">Gran Teatre del Liceu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Arriaga" title="Teatro Arriaga">Teatro Arriaga</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Palau_de_les_Arts_Reina_Sof%C3%ADa" title="El Palau de les Arts Reina Sofía">El Palau de les Arts Reina Sofía</a>.</p>
<p>Thousands of music fans also travel to Spain each year for internationally recognised summer music festivals <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B3nar" title="Sónar">Sónar</a> which often features the top up and coming pop and techno acts, and <a href="/wiki/Festival_Internacional_de_Benic%C3%A0ssim" title="Festival Internacional de Benicàssim">Benicàssim</a> which tends to feature alternative rock and dance acts. Both festivals mark Spain as an international music presence and reflect the tastes of young people in the country.</p>
<p>The most popular traditional <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instrument</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>, originated in Spain. Typical of the north are the traditional bag pipers or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaita_(bagpipe)" title="Gaita (bagpipe)">gaiteros</a></i>, mainly in Asturias and Galicia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_cuisine" title="Spanish cuisine">Spanish cuisine</a></div>

<p>Spanish cuisine consists of a great variety of dishes which stem from differences in geography, culture and climate. It is heavily influenced by seafood available from the waters that surround the country, and reflects the country's deep <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> roots. Spain's extensive history with many cultural influences has led to a unique cuisine. In particular, three main divisions are easily identified:</p>
<p><i><b>Mediterranean</b></i> Spain – all such coastal regions, from Catalonia to Andalusia – heavy use of seafood, such as <i>pescaíto frito</i> (fried fish); several cold soups like <i><a href="/wiki/Gazpacho" title="Gazpacho">gazpacho</a></i>; and many rice-based dishes like <i><a href="/wiki/Paella" title="Paella">paella</a></i> from Valencia and <i><a href="/wiki/Arr%C3%B2s_negre" title="Arròs negre">arròs negre</a></i> (black rice) from Catalonia.</p>
<p><i><b>Inner</b></i> Spain – Castile – hot, thick soups such as the bread and garlic-based <i>Castilian soup</i>, along with substantious stews such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cocido_madrile%C3%B1o" title="Cocido madrileño">cocido madrileño</a></i>. Food is traditionally conserved by salting, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_ham" title="Spanish ham">Spanish ham</a>, or immersed in <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchego_cheese" title="Manchego cheese">Manchego cheese</a>.</p>
<p><i><b>Atlantic</b></i> Spain – the whole Northern coast, including <a href="/wiki/Asturian_cuisine" title="Asturian cuisine">Asturian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basque_cuisine" title="Basque cuisine">Basque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_cuisine" title="Cantabrian cuisine">Cantabrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galician_cuisine" title="Galician cuisine">Galician cuisine</a> – vegetable and fish-based stews like <i><a href="/wiki/Caldo_gallego" title="Caldo gallego">caldo gallego</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marmitako" title="Marmitako">marmitako</a></i>. Also, the lightly cured <i><a href="/wiki/Lac%C3%B3n_Gallego" title="Lacón Gallego">lacón</a></i> ham. The best known cuisine of the northern countries often rely on ocean seafood, like the Basque-style <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albacore" title="Albacore">albacore</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a> or the Galician octopus-based <i><a href="/wiki/Polbo_%C3%A1_feira" title="Polbo á feira">polbo á feira</a></i> and shellfish dishes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sport_in_Spain" title="Sport in Spain">Sport in Spain</a></div>

<p>While varieties of football had been played in Spain as far back as Roman times, sport in Spain has been dominated by English style <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> since the early 20th century. <a href="/wiki/Real_Madrid_C.F." title="Real Madrid C.F.">Real Madrid C.F.</a> and <a href="/wiki/FC_Barcelona" title="FC Barcelona">FC Barcelona</a> are two of the most successful football clubs in the world. <a href="/wiki/Spain_national_football_team" title="Spain national football team">The country's national football team</a> won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UEFA_European_Football_Championship" title="UEFA European Football Championship">UEFA European Football Championship</a> in 1964, 2008 and 2012 and the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/2010_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2010 FIFA World Cup">2010</a>, and is the first team to ever win three back-to-back major international tournaments.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">Basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Team_handball" title="Team handball">handball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futsal" title="Futsal">futsal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorcycling" title="Motorcycling">motorcycling</a> and, lately, <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> are also important due to the presence of Spanish champions in all these disciplines. Today, Spain is a major world sports powerhouse, especially since the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">1992 Summer Olympics</a> that were hosted in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, which stimulated a great deal of interest in sports in the country. The tourism industry has led to an improvement in sports infrastructure, especially for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_sports" title="Water sports">water sports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rafael_Nadal" title="Rafael Nadal">Rafael Nadal</a> is the leading Spanish tennis player and has won several Grand Slam titles including the Wimbledon 2010 men's singles. In north Spain, the game of <a href="/wiki/Basque_pelota" title="Basque pelota">pelota</a> is very popular. <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Contador" title="Alberto Contador">Alberto Contador</a> is the leading Spanish cyclist and has won several Grand Tour titles including two <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> titles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_holidays_and_festivals">Public holidays and festivals</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Spain" title="Public holidays in Spain">Public holidays in Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiestas_of_International_Tourist_Interest_of_Spain" title="Fiestas of International Tourist Interest of Spain">Fiestas of International Tourist Interest of Spain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fiestas_of_National_Tourist_Interest_of_Spain" title="Fiestas of National Tourist Interest of Spain">Fiestas of National Tourist Interest of Spain</a></div>

<p>Public holidays celebrated in Spain include a mix of religious (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>), national and regional observances. Each municipality is allowed to declare a maximum of 14 <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holidays</a> per year; up to nine of these are chosen by the national government and at least two are chosen locally. Spain's National Day (<i><a href="/wiki/Fiesta_Nacional_de_Espa%C3%B1a" title="Fiesta Nacional de España">Fiesta Nacional de España</a></i>) is 12 October, the anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_America" title="Discovery of America">Discovery of America</a> and commemorate <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_the_Pillar" title="Our Lady of the Pillar">Our Lady of the Pillar</a> feast, patroness of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> and throughout Spain.</p>
<p>There are many festivals and festivities in Spain. Some of them are known worldwide, and every year millions of people from all over the world go to Spain to experience one of these festivals. One of the most famous is <a href="/wiki/San_Ferm%C3%ADn" title="San Fermín">San Fermín</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">Pamplona</a>. While its most famous event is the <i>encierro</i>, or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Running_of_the_bulls" title="Running of the bulls">running of the bulls</a>, which happens at 8:00 am from 7 to 14 July, the week-long celebration involves many other traditional and folkloric events. Its events were central to the plot of The Sun Also Rises, by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, which brought it to the general attention of English-speaking people. As a result, it has become one of the most internationally renowned fiestas in Spain, with over 1,000,000 people attending every year.</p>
<p>Other festivals include the carnivals in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falles" title="Falles">Falles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a> in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">Castile and León</a>.</p>
