For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(disambiguation)" title="Heavy metal (disambiguation)">Heavy metal (disambiguation)</a>.</div>


<p><b>Heavy metals</b> are generally defined as <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> with relatively high <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">densities</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weights</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic numbers</a>. The criteria used, and whether <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalloids" title="Metalloids">metalloids</a> are included, vary depending on the author and context. In <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, for example, a heavy metal may be defined on the basis of density, whereas in physics the distinguishing criterion might be atomic number, while a chemist would likely be more concerned with chemical behaviour. More specific definitions have been published but none of these have been widely accepted. The definitions surveyed in this article encompass up to 96 out of the 118 chemical elements; only <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> meet all of them. Despite this lack of agreement the term (plural or singular) is widely used in science. A density of more than 5 g/cm is sometimes quoted as a commonly used criterion and is used in the body of this article.</p>
<p>The earliest known metals—common metals such as <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and precious metals such as <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>—are heavy metals. From 1809 onwards, <a href="/wiki/Light_metal" title="Light metal">light metals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, were discovered, as well as less well-known heavy metals like <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>.</p>
<p>Some heavy metals are either essential nutrients (typically iron, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>), or relatively harmless (such as <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, silver, and <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>) but can be toxic in larger amounts or certain forms. Other heavy metals, like <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, mercury, and lead, are highly poisonous. Potential sources of heavy metal poisoning include <a href="/wiki/Tailings" title="Tailings">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial wastes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_runoff" title="Agricultural runoff">agricultural runoff</a>, occupational exposure, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_paint" title="Environmental impact of paint">paints</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treated_wood" title="Treated wood">treated timber</a>.</p>
<p>Physical and chemical characterisations of heavy metals need to be treated with caution as the metals involved are not always consistently defined. As well as being relatively dense, heavy metals tend to be less reactive than lighter metals and have much less soluble sulfides and hydroxides. While it is relatively easy to distinguish a heavy metal such as tungsten from a lighter metal such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, a few heavy metals such as zinc, mercury, and lead have some of the characteristics of lighter metals, and lighter metals such as <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a>, and titanium have some of the characteristics of heavier metals.</p>
<p>Heavy metals are relatively scarce in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth#Crust" title="Structure of the Earth">crust</a> but are present in many aspects of modern life. They are used in, for example, <a href="/wiki/Golf_club" title="Golf club">golf clubs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cars" title="Cars">cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-cleaning_oven" title="Self-cleaning oven">self-cleaning ovens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>

<p>There is no widely agreed criterion-based definition of a heavy metal. Different meanings may be attached to the term, depending on the context. In <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, for example, a heavy metal may be defined on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, whereas in physics the distinguishing criterion might be <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a>, and a chemist would likely be more concerned with chemical behaviour.</p>
<p>Density criteria range from above 3.5 g/cm to above 7 g/cm. Atomic weight definitions can range from greater than <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (atomic weight 22.98); greater than 40 (excluding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-block" title="S-block">s-</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-block" title="F-block">f-block</a> metals, hence starting with <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a>); or more than 200, i.e. from <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> onwards. Atomic numbers of heavy metals are generally given as greater than 20 (<a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>); sometimes this is capped at 92 (<a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>). Definitions based on atomic number have been criticised for including metals with low densities. For example, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_metals" title="Alkali metals">group (column) 1</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> has an atomic number of 37 but a density of only 1.532 g/cm, which is below the threshold figure used by other authors. The same problem may occur with atomic weight based definitions.</p>
<p>Criteria based on chemical behaviour or periodic table position have been used or suggested. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia" title="United States Pharmacopeia">United States Pharmacopeia</a> includes a test for heavy metals that involves precipitating metallic impurities as their coloured <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a>." In 1997, Stephen Hawkes, a chemistry professor writing in the context of fifty years' experience with the term, said it applied to "metals with insoluble sulfides and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> produce colored solutions in water and whose <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">complexes</a> are usually colored". On the basis of the metals he had seen referred to as heavy metals, he suggested it would useful to define them as (in general) all the metals in periodic table columns <a href="/wiki/Group_3_element" title="Group 3 element">3</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">16</a> that are in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_4" title="Period 4">row 4</a> or greater, in other words, the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metals</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> satisfy Hawkes' three-part description; the status of the <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a> is not completely settled.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, heavy metals are sometimes defined—on the basis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> (electronic pair acceptor) behaviour of their ions in aqueous solution—as class B and borderline metals. In this scheme, class A metal ions prefer <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> donors; class B ions prefer <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> donors; and borderline or ambivalent ions show either class A or B characteristics, depending on the circumstances. Class A metals, which tend to have low <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> and form bonds with large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic character</a>, are the <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth" title="Alkaline earth">alkaline earths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Group_3_element" title="Group 3 element">group 3 metals</a>, and the lanthanides and actinides. Class B metals, which tend to have higher electronegativity and form bonds with considerable <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent</a> character, are mainly the heavier transition and post-transition metals. Borderline metals largely comprise the lighter transition and post-transition metals (plus <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>). The distinction between the class A metals and the other two categories is sharp. A frequently cited proposal to use these classification categories instead of the more evocative name <i>heavy metal</i> has not been widely adopted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_heavy_metals_based_on_density">List of heavy metals based on density</span></h3>
<p>A density of more than 5 g/cm is sometimes mentioned as a common heavy metal defining factor and, in the absence of a unanimous definition, is used to populate this list and (unless otherwise stated) guide the remainder of the article. Metalloids meeting the applicable criteria–arsenic and antimony for example—are sometimes counted as heavy metals, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_chemistry" title="Environmental chemistry">environmental chemistry</a>, as is the case here. <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">Selenium</a> (density 4.8 g/cm) is also included in the list. It falls marginally short of the density criterion and is less commonly recognised as a metalloid but has a waterborne chemistry similar in some respects to that of arsenic and antimony. Other metals sometimes classified or treated as "heavy" metals, such as <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> (density 1.8 gm/cm), aluminium (2.7 gm/cm), calcium (1.55 gm/cm), and barium (3.6 gm/cm) are here treated as <a href="/wiki/Light_metal" title="Light metal">light metals</a> and, in general, are not further considered.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_use_of_the_term">Origins and use of the term</span></h2>
<p>The heaviness of <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">naturally occurring metals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Telluric_iron" title="Telluric iron">iron</a> may have been noticed in <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a> and, in light of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleability</a>, led to the first attempts to craft metal ornaments, tools, and weapons. All metals discovered from then until 1809 had relatively high densities; their heaviness was regarded as a singularly distinguishing criterion.</p>
<p>From 1809 onwards, light metals such as sodium, potassium, and <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> were isolated. Their low densities challenged conventional wisdom and it was proposed to refer to them as <i>metalloids</i> (meaning "resembling metals in form or appearance"). This suggestion was ignored; the new elements came to be recognised as metals, and the term metalloid was then used to refer to nonmetallic elements and, later, elements that were hard to describe as either metals or nonmetals.</p>
<p>An early use of the term "heavy metal" dates from 1817, when the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Gmelin" title="Leopold Gmelin">Leopold Gmelin</a> divided the elements into nonmetals, light metals, and heavy metals. Light metals had densities of 0.860–5.0 g/cm; heavy metals 5.308–22.000. The term later became associated with elements of high atomic weight or high atomic number. It is sometimes used interchangeably with the term <i>heavy element</i>. For example, in discussing the history of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chemistry" title="Nuclear chemistry">nuclear chemistry</a>, Magee notes that the actinides were once thought to represent a new heavy element transition group whereas <a href="/wiki/Glenn_T._Seaborg" title="Glenn T. Seaborg">Seaborg</a> and co-workers, "favoured ... a heavy metal <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">rare-earth</a> like series ...". In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, however, a heavy element is any element heavier than <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Scottish toxicologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Henderson_Duffus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Henderson Duffus (page does not exist)">John Duffus</a> reviewed the definitions used over the previous 60 years and concluded they were so diverse as to effectively render the term meaningless. Along with this finding, the heavy metal status of some metals is occasionally challenged on the grounds that they are too light, or are involved in biological processes, or rarely constitute environmental hazards. Examples include scandium (too light); <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> (biological processes); and <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> (too rare).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span></h3>
<p>Despite its questionable meaning, references to the term <i>heavy metal</i> appear regularly in scientific literature. A 2010 study found that it had been increasingly used and seemed to have become part of the language of science. It is said to be an acceptable term, given its convenience and familiarity, as long as it is accompanied by a strict definition. The counterparts to the heavy metals, the <i>light metals</i>, are alluded to by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Minerals,_Metals_%26_Materials_Society" title="The Minerals, Metals &amp; Materials Society">The Minerals, Metals and Materials Society</a> as including "aluminium, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a>, titanium, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, and other reactive metals." The named metals have densities of 0.534 to 4.54 g/cm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_element" title="Essential element">Essential element</a></div>
<p>Trace amounts of some heavy metals, mostly in period 4, are required for certain biological processes. These are iron and copper (<a href="/wiki/Blood#Oxygen_transport" title="Blood">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport</a>); <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">complex syntheses and cell metabolism</a>); zinc (<a href="/wiki/Hydroxylation" title="Hydroxylation">hydroxylation</a>); vanadium and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">enzyme regulation</a> or functioning); <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> utilisation); nickel (<a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">cell growth</a>); arsenic (metabolic growth in some animals and possibly in humans) and selenium (<a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> functioning and <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> production. Periods 5 and 6 contain fewer essential heavy metals, consistent with the general pattern that heavier elements tend to be less abundant and that scarcer elements are less likely to be nutritionally essential. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_5" title="Period 5">period 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> is required for the <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reactions; <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> is used by some marine <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a> for the same purpose; and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> may be required for growth in a few species. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_6" title="Period 6">period 6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> is required by some bacteria for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic_process" title="Metabolic process">metabolic processes</a>. An average 70 kg <a href="/wiki/Composition_of_the_human_body" title="Composition of the human body">human body</a> is about 0.01% heavy metals (~7 g, equivalent to the weight of two dried peas, with iron at 4 g, zinc at 2.5 g, and lead at 0.12 g comprising the three main constituents), 2% light metals (~1.4 kg, the weight of a bottle of wine) and nearly 98% nonmetals (mostly <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>).</p>
<p>A deficiency of any of these period 4–6 essential heavy metals may increase susceptibility to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_poisoning" title="Heavy metal poisoning">heavy metal poisoning</a>. A few non-essential heavy metals have also been observed to have biological effects. <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Gallium</a>, germanium (a metalloid), indium, and most lanthanides can stimulate metabolism, and titanium promotes growth in plants (though it is not always considered a heavy metal).</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h2>

<p>Heavy metals are often assumed to be highly toxic or damaging to the environment. Some are, while certain others are toxic only if taken in excess or encountered in certain forms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_heavy_metals">Environmental heavy metals</span></h3>
<p>Chromium, arsenic, cadmium, mercury, and lead have the greatest potential to cause harm on account of their extensive use, the toxicity of some of their combined or elemental forms, and their widespread distribution in the environment. <a href="/wiki/Hexavalent_chromium" title="Hexavalent chromium">Hexavalent chromium</a>, for example, is highly toxic as are mercury vapour and many mercury compounds. These five elements have a strong affinity for sulfur; in the human body they usually bind, via <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiol</a> groups (–SH), to <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> responsible for controlling the speed of metabolic reactions. The resulting sulfur-metal bonds inhibit the proper functioning of the enzymes involved; human health deteriorates, sometimes fatally. Chromium (in its hexavalent form) and arsenic are <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>; cadmium causes a <a href="/wiki/Itai-itai_disease" title="Itai-itai disease">degenerative bone disease</a>; and mercury and lead damage the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.</p>

<p>Lead is the most prevalent heavy metal contaminant. Levels in the aquatic environments of industrialised societies have been estimated to be two to three times those of pre-industrial levels. As a component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraethyl_lead" title="Tetraethyl lead">tetraethyl lead</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">(CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
3</span>CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
4</span>Pb</span>, it was used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> during the 1930s–1970s. Although the use of leaded gasoline was largely phased out in North America by 1996, soils next to roads built before this time retain high lead concentrations. Later research demonstrated a statistically significant correlation between the usage rate of leaded gasoline and violent crime in the United States; taking into account a 22-year time lag (for the average age of violent criminals), the violent crime curve virtually tracked the lead exposure curve.</p>
<p>Other heavy metals noted for their potentially hazardous nature, usually as toxic environmental pollutants, include manganese (central nervous system damage); cobalt and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> (carcinogens); copper, zinc, selenium and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> (<a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine</a> disruption, <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital disorders</a>, or general toxic effects in fish, plants, birds, or other aquatic organisms); tin, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotin" title="Organotin">organotin</a> (central nervous system damage); antimony (a suspected carcinogen); and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a> (central nervous system damage).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritionally_essential_heavy_metals">Nutritionally essential heavy metals</span></h3>
<p>Heavy metals essential for life can be toxic if taken in excess; some have notably toxic forms. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" title="Vanadium pentoxide">Vanadium pentoxide</a> (V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) is carcinogenic in animals and, when inhaled, causes <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> damage. The purple <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> ion MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">–<br/>
4</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> poison. Ingesting more than 0.5 grams of iron can induce cardiac collapse; such overdoses most commonly occur in children and may result in death within 24 hours. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl" title="Nickel carbonyl">Nickel carbonyl</a> (Ni<sub>2</sub>(CO)<sub>4</sub>), at 30 parts per million, can cause respiratory failure, brain damage and death. Imbibing a gram or more of <a href="/wiki/Copper_sulfate" title="Copper sulfate">copper sulfate</a> (Cu(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) can be fatal; survivors may be left with major organ damage. More than five milligrams of selenium is highly toxic; this is roughly ten times the 0.45 milligram recommended maximum daily intake; long-term poisoning can have paralytic effects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_heavy_metals">Other heavy metals</span></h3>
<p>A few other non-essential heavy metals have one or more toxic forms. Kidney failure and fatalities have been recorded arising from the ingestion of germanium dietary supplements (~15 to 300 g in total consumed over a period of two months to three years). Exposure to <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a> (OsO<sub>4</sub>) may cause permanent eye damage and can lead to respiratory failure and death. Indium salts are toxic if more than few milligrams are ingested and will affect the kidneys, liver, and heart. <a href="/wiki/Cisplatin" title="Cisplatin">Cisplatin</a> (PtCl<sub>2</sub>(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>), which is an important drug used to <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">kill cancer cells</a>, is also a kidney and nerve poison. <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">Bismuth</a> compounds can cause liver damage if taken in excess; insoluble uranium compounds, as well as the dangerous <a href="/wiki/Radiation_damage" title="Radiation damage">radiation</a> they emit, can cause permanent kidney damage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_sources">Exposure sources</span></h3>
<p>Heavy metals can degrade air, water, and soil quality, and subsequently cause health issues in plants, animals, and people, when they become concentrated as a result of industrial activities. Common sources of heavy metals in this context include mining and industrial wastes; vehicle emissions; <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery" title="Lead–acid battery">lead-acid batteries</a>; fertilisers; <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_paint" title="Environmental impact of paint">paints</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treated_wood" title="Treated wood">treated timber</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pipe_(fluid_conveyance)#Materials" title="Pipe (fluid conveyance)">aging water supply infrastructure</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastics</a> floating in the world's oceans. Recent examples of heavy metal contamination and health risks include the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Minamata_disease" title="Minamata disease">Minamata disease</a>, in Japan (1932–1968; lawsuits ongoing as at 2016); the <a href="/wiki/Bento_Rodrigues_dam_disaster" title="Bento Rodrigues dam disaster">Bento Rodrigues dam disaster</a> in Brazil, and high levels of lead in drinking water supplied to the residents of <a href="/wiki/Flint_water_crisis" title="Flint water crisis">Flint</a>, Michigan, in the north-east of the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation.2C_abundance.2C_occurrence.2C_and_extraction">Formation, abundance, occurrence, and extraction</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">Nucleosynthesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">Abundance of the chemical elements</a></div>

<p>Heavy metals up to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_peak" title="Iron peak">vicinity of iron</a> (in the periodic table) are largely made via <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">stellar nucleosynthesis</a>. In this process, lighter elements from hydrogen to <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> undergo successive <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> reactions inside stars, releasing light and heat and forming heavier elements with higher atomic numbers.</p>
<p>Heavier heavy metals are not usually formed this way since fusion reactions involving such nuclei would consume rather than release energy. Rather, they are largely synthesised (from elements with a lower atomic number) by <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a>, with the two main modes of this repetitive capture being the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a> and the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a>. In the s-process ("s" stands for "slow"), singular captures are separated by years or decades, allowing the less stable nuclei to <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>, while in the r-process ("rapid"), captures happen faster than nuclei can decay. Therefore, the s-process takes a more or less clear path: for example, stable cadmium-110 nuclei are successively bombarded by free neutrons inside a star until they form cadmium-115 nuclei which are unstable and decay to form indium-115 (which is nearly stable, with a half-life <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004300000000000000♠</span>30<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> times the age of the universe). These nuclei capture neutrons and form indium-116, which is unstable, and decays to form tin-116, and so on. In contrast, there is no such path in the r-process. The s-process stops at bismuth due to the short half-lives of the next two elements, polonium, and astatine, which decay to bismuth or lead. The r-process is so fast it can skip this zone of instability and go on to create heavier elements such as <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and uranium.</p>
<p>Heavy metals condense in planets as a result of stellar evolution and destruction processes. Stars lose much of their mass when it is <a href="/wiki/Stellar_mass_loss" title="Stellar mass loss">ejected</a> late in their lifetimes, and sometimes thereafter as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a> merger, thereby increasing the abundance of elements heavier than helium in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. When gravitational attraction causes this matter to coalesce and collapse <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis" title="Nebular hypothesis">new stars and planets are formed</a>.</p>
<p>The Earth's crust is made of approximately 5% of heavy metals by weight, with iron comprising 95% of this quantity. Light metals (~20%) and nonmetals (~75%) make up the other 95% of the crust. Despite their overall scarcity, heavy metals can become concentrated in economically extractable quantities as a result of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_formation" title="Mountain formation">mountain building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geological processes</a>.</p>
<p>Heavy metals are primarily found as <a href="/wiki/Goldschmidt_classification#Lithophile_elements" title="Goldschmidt classification">lithophiles</a> (rock-loving) or <a href="/wiki/Goldschmidt_classification#Chalcophile_elements" title="Goldschmidt classification">chalcophiles</a> (ore-loving). Lithophile heavy metals are mainly f-block elements and the more reactive of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-block" title="D-block">d-block</a> elements. They have a strong affinity for oxygen and mostly exist as relatively low density <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">silicate minerals</a>. Chalcophile heavy metals are mainly the less reactive d-block elements, and period 4–6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-block" title="P-block">p-block</a> metals and metalloids. They are usually found in (insoluble) <a href="/wiki/Sulfide_minerals" title="Sulfide minerals">sulfide minerals</a>. Being denser than the lithophiles, hence sinking lower into the crust at the time of its solidification, the chalcophiles tend to be less abundant than the lithophiles.</p>
<p>On the other hand, gold is a <a href="/wiki/Goldschmidt_classification#Siderophile_elements" title="Goldschmidt classification">siderophile</a>, or (iron-loving) element. It does not readily form compounds with either oxygen or sulfur. At the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_of_the_Earth" title="Formation of the Earth">Earth's formation</a>, and as the most <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Noble metal">noble</a> (inert) of metals, gold sank into the <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth#Core" title="Structure of the Earth">core</a> due to its tendency to form high-density metallic alloys. Consequently, it is a relatively rare metal. Some other (less) noble heavy metals—molybdenum, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_group_metal" title="Platinum group metal">platinum group metals</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, rhodium, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>, osmium, <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>, and platinum), germanium, and tin—can be counted as siderophiles but only in terms of their primary occurrence in the Earth (core, <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth#Mantle" title="Structure of the Earth">mantle</a> and crust), rather the crust. These metals otherwise occur in the crust, in small quantities, chiefly as chalcophiles (less so in their <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">native form</a>).</p>
<p>Concentrations of heavy metals below the crust are generally higher, with most being found in the largely iron-silicon-nickel core. <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">Platinum</a>, for example, comprises approximately 1 part per billion of the crust whereas its concentration in the core is thought to be nearly 6,000 times higher. Recent speculation suggests that uranium (and thorium) in the core may generate a substantial amount of the heat that drives <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> and (ultimately) sustains the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>.</p>
<p>The winning of heavy metals from their ores is a complex function of ore type, the chemical properties of the metals involved, and the economics of various extraction methods. Different countries and refineries may use different processes, including those that differ from the brief outlines listed here.</p>
<p>Broadly speaking, and with some exceptions, lithophile heavy metals can be extracted from their ores by <a href="/wiki/Electrowinning" title="Electrowinning">electrical</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reduction" title="Chemical reduction">chemical treatments</a>, while chalcophile heavy metals are obtained by <a href="/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy)" title="Roasting (metallurgy)">roasting</a> their sulphide ores to yield the corresponding oxides, and then heating these to obtain the raw metals. Radium occurs in quantities too small to be economically mined and is instead obtained from spent <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuels</a>. The chalcophile platinum group metals (PGM) mainly occur in small (mixed) quantities with other chalcophile ores. The ores involved need to be <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelted</a>, roasted, and then <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(metallurgy)" title="Leaching (metallurgy)">leached</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to produce a residue of PGM. This is chemically refined to obtain the individual metals in their pure forms. Compared to other metals, PGM are expensive due to their scarcity and high production costs.</p>
<p>Gold, a siderophile, is most commonly recovered by dissolving the ores in which it is found in a <a href="/wiki/Gold_cyanidation" title="Gold cyanidation">cyanide solution</a>. The gold forms a dicyanoaurate(I), for example: 2 Au + <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a> +½ O<sub>2</sub> + <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">KCN</a> → 2 K[Au(CN)<sub>2</sub>] + 2 <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">KOH</a>. Zinc is added to the mix and, being more <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_series" title="Reactivity series">reactive</a> than gold, displaces the gold: 2[Au(CN)<sub>2</sub>] + Zn → K<sub>2</sub>[Zn(CN)<sub>4</sub>] + 2 Au. The gold precipitates out of solution as a sludge, and is filtered off and melted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_compared_with_light_metals">Properties compared with light metals</span></h2>
<p>Some general physical and chemical properties of light and heavy metals are summarised in the table. The comparison should be treated with caution since the terms light metal and heavy metal are not always consistently defined. Also the physical properties of hardness and tensile strength can vary widely depending on purity, <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">grain size</a> and pre-treatment.</p>

<p>These properties make it relatively easy to distinguish a light metal like sodium from a heavy metal like tungsten, but the differences become less clear at the boundaries. Light structural metals like beryllium, scandium, and titanium have some of the characteristics of heavy metals, such as higher melting points; post-transition heavy metals like zinc, cadmium, and lead have some of the characteristics of light metals, such as being relatively soft, and having lower melting points; and forming mainly colourless complexes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<p>Heavy metals are present in nearly all aspects of modern life. Iron may be the most common as it accounts for 90% of all refined metals. Platinum may be the most ubiquitous given it is said to be found in, or used to produce, 20% of all consumer goods.</p>
<p>Some common uses of heavy metals depend on the general characteristics of metals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> or the general characteristics of heavy metals such as density, strength, and durability. Other uses depend on the characteristics of the specific element, such as their biological role as nutrients or poisons or some other specific atomic properties. Examples of such atomic properties include: partly filled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-orbital" title="D-orbital">d-</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-orbital" title="F-orbital">f- orbitals</a> (in many of the transition, lanthanide, and actinide heavy metals) that enable the formation of coloured compounds; the capacity of most heavy metal ions (such as platinum, cerium or bismuth) to exist in different <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> and therefore act as catalysts; poorly overlapping 3d or 4f orbitals (in iron, cobalt, and nickel, or the lanthanide heavy metals from <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a> through <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a>) that give rise to magnetic effects; and high atomic numbers and <a href="/wiki/Electron_density" title="Electron density">electron densities</a> that underpin their nuclear science applications. Typical uses of heavy metals can be broadly grouped into the following six categories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight-_or_density-based">Weight- or density-based</span></h3>

<p>Some uses of heavy metals, including in sport, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">military ordnance</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_science" title="Nuclear science">nuclear science</a>, take advantage of their relatively high densities. In <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a>, lead is used as a <a href="/wiki/Ballast" title="Ballast">ballast</a>; in <a href="/wiki/Handicapping#Horse_racing" title="Handicapping">handicap horse racing</a> each horse must carry a specified lead weight, based on factors including past performance, so as to equalize the chances of the various competitors. In <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, tungsten, <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>, or copper inserts in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairway_(golf)" title="Fairway (golf)">fairway</a> <a href="/wiki/Golf_club" title="Golf club">clubs and irons</a> lower the centre of gravity of the club making it easier to get the ball into the air; and golf balls with tungsten cores are claimed to have better flight characteristics. In <a href="/wiki/Fly_fishing" title="Fly fishing">fly fishing</a>, sinking fly lines have a <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> coating embedded with tungsten powder, so that they sink at the required rate. In <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> sport, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> balls used in the <a href="/wiki/Hammer_throw" title="Hammer throw">hammer throw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shot_put" title="Shot put">shot put</a> events are filled with lead in order to attain the minimum weight required under international rules. Tungsten was used in hammer throw balls at least up to 1980; the minimum size of the ball was increased in 1981 to eliminate the need for what was, at that time, an expensive metal (triple the cost of other hammers) not generally available in all countries. Tungsten hammers were so dense that they penetrated too deeply into the turf.</p>
<p>In mechanical engineering, heavy metals are used for ballast in boats, aeroplanes, and motor vehicles; or in <a href="/wiki/Tire_balance" title="Tire balance">balance weights on wheels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshafts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propellers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_clutch" title="Centrifugal clutch">centrifugal clutches</a>, in situations requiring maximum weight in minimum space (for example in <a href="/wiki/Movement_(clockwork)" title="Movement (clockwork)">watch movements</a>).</p>

<p>In military ordnance, tungsten or uranium is used in <a href="/wiki/Chobham_armour#Heavy_metal_modules" title="Chobham armour">armour plating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy_penetrator" title="Kinetic energy penetrator">armour piercing projectiles</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_design" title="Nuclear weapon design">nuclear weapons</a> to increase efficiency (by <a href="/wiki/Neutron_reflector" title="Neutron reflector">reflecting neutrons</a> and momentarily delaying the expansion of reacting materials). In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> was found to be more effective than copper in <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">shaped charge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explosively_formed_penetrator" title="Explosively formed penetrator">explosively formed anti-armour weapons</a> on account of its higher density, allowing greater force concentration, and better deformability. Less-<a href="/wiki/Toxic_heavy_metal" title="Toxic heavy metal">toxic heavy metals</a>, such as copper, tin, tungsten, and bismuth, and probably manganese (as well as <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, a metalloid), have replaced lead and antimony in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Green_bullets&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Green bullets (page does not exist)">green bullets</a> used by some armies and in some recreational shooting munitions. Doubts have been raised about the safety (or <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">green credentials</a>) of tungsten.</p>
<p>Because denser materials absorb more radioactive emissions than lighter ones, heavy metals are useful for <a href="/wiki/Radiation_protection" title="Radiation protection">radiation shielding</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Collimator#Neutron.2C_X-ray.2C_and_gamma_ray_collimators" title="Collimator">focus radiation beams</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_accelerator" title="Linear accelerator">linear accelerators</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a> applications.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strength-_or_durability-based">Strength- or durability-based</span></h3>

<p>The strength or durability of heavy metals such as chromium, iron, nickel, copper, zinc, molybdenum, tin, tungsten, and lead, as well as their alloys, makes them useful for the manufacture of artefacts such as tools, machinery, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_appliances" title="Domestic appliances">appliances</a>, utensils, pipes, railroad tracks, buildings and bridges, automobiles, locks, furniture, ships, planes, coinage and jewellery. They are also used as alloying additives for enhancing the properties of other metals. Of the two dozen elements that have been used in the world's monetised coinage only two, carbon and aluminium, are not heavy metals. Gold, silver, and platinum are used in jewellery as are (for example) nickel, copper, indium, and cobalt in <a href="/wiki/Colored_gold" title="Colored gold">colored gold</a>. <a href="/wiki/Costume_jewelry" title="Costume jewelry">Low-cost jewellery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toy#Safety_regulations" title="Toy">children's toys</a> may be made, to a significant degree, of heavy metals such as chromium, nickel, cadmium, or lead.</p>
<p>Copper, zinc, tin, and lead are mechanically weaker metals but have useful <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> prevention properties. While each of them will react with air, the resulting <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patinas</a> of either various copper salts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_carbonate" title="Zinc carbonate">zinc carbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" title="Tin dioxide">tin oxide</a>, or a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_oxide" title="Lead(II) oxide">lead oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead_carbonate" title="Lead carbonate">carbonate</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_sulfate" title="Lead sulfate">sulfate</a>, confer valuable <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">protective properties</a>. Copper and lead are therefore used, for example, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roofing_material" title="Roofing material">roofing materials</a>; zinc acts as an <a href="/wiki/Anti-corrosion" title="Anti-corrosion">anti-corrosion</a> agent in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galvanised_steel" title="Galvanised steel">galvanised steel</a>; and tin serves a similar purpose on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_can" title="Steel can">steel cans</a>.</p>
<p>The workability and corrosion resistance of iron and chromium are increased by adding <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep resistance</a> of nickel is improved with the addition of thorium. Tellurium is added to copper and steel alloys to improve their machinability; and to lead to make it harder and more acid-resistant.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_and_chemical">Biological and chemical</span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Biocide" title="Biocide">biocidal</a> effects of <a href="/wiki/Oligodynamic_effect" title="Oligodynamic effect">some heavy metals</a> have been known since antiquity. Platinum, osmium, copper, ruthenium, and other heavy metals, including arsenic, are used in anti-cancer treatments, or have shown potential. Antimony (anti-protozoal), bismuth (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiulcer_agent" title="Antiulcer agent">anti-ulcer</a>), gold (<a href="/wiki/Arthritis#Medications" title="Arthritis">anti-arthritic</a>), and iron (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-malarial_medication" title="Anti-malarial medication">anti-malarial</a>) are also important in medicine. Copper, zinc, silver, gold, or mercury are used in <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> formulations; small amounts of some heavy metals are used to control algal growth in, for example, <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling towers</a>. Depending on their intended use as fertilisers or biocides, <a href="/wiki/Agrochemical" title="Agrochemical">agrochemicals</a> may contain heavy metals such as chromium, cobalt, nickel, copper, zinc, arsenic, cadmium, mercury, or lead.</p>
<p>Selected heavy metals are used as catalysts in fuel processing (rhenium, for example), synthetic rubber and fibre production (bismuth), <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter#Construction" title="Catalytic converter">emission control devices</a> (palladium), and in <a href="/wiki/Self-cleaning_oven" title="Self-cleaning oven">self-cleaning ovens</a> (where <a href="/wiki/Cerium_oxide" title="Cerium oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a> in the walls of such ovens helps <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidise</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-based cooking residues). In soap chemistry, heavy metals form insoluble soaps that are used in <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">lubricating greases</a>, paint dryers, and <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicides</a> (apart from lithium, the alkali metals and the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ion form soluble soaps).</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colouring_and_optics">Colouring and optics</span></h3>

<p>The colours of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramic_glazes" title="Ceramic glazes">ceramic glazes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_paint" title="Environmental impact of paint">paints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pigment#Metal-based_pigments" title="Pigment">pigments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a> are commonly produced by the inclusion of heavy metals (or their compounds) such as chromium, manganese, cobalt, copper, zinc, selenium, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>, molybdenum, silver, tin, <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>, tungsten, iridium, gold, lead, or uranium. <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">Tattoo</a> inks may contain heavy metals, such as chromium, cobalt, nickel, and copper. The high reflectivity of some heavy metals is important in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, including precision <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_instrument" title="Astronomical instrument">astronomical instruments</a>. Headlight reflectors rely on the excellent reflectivity of a thin film of rhodium.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics.2C_magnets.2C_and_lighting">Electronics, magnets, and lighting</span></h3>

<p>Heavy metals or their compounds can be found in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">electronic components</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_wiring" title="Electrical wiring">wiring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> where they may be used as either conductors, semiconductors, or insulators. Molybdenum powder is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circuit_board" title="Circuit board">circuit board</a> inks. <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(IV)_oxide" title="Ruthenium(IV) oxide">Ruthenium(IV) oxide</a> coated titanium <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anodes</a> are used for the industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>. Home electrical systems, for the most part, are wired with copper wire for its good conducting properties. Silver and gold are used in electrical and electronic devices, particularly in contact <a href="/wiki/Switch" title="Switch">switches</a>, as a result of their high electrical conductivity and capacity to resist or minimise the formation of impurities on their surfaces. The semiconductors <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> are used to make solar panels. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafnium_oxide" title="Hafnium oxide">Hafnium oxide</a>, an insulator, is used as a <a href="/wiki/Voltage_controller" title="Voltage controller">voltage controller</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microchip" title="Microchip">microchips</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantalum_oxide" title="Tantalum oxide">tantalum oxide</a>, another insulator, is used in <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>. Heavy metals have been used in batteries for over 200 years, at least since <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Volta" title="Alessandro Volta">Volta</a> invented his copper and silver <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a> in 1800. <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">Promethium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>, and mercury are further examples found in, respectively, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_battery" title="Atomic battery">atomic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-metal_hydride_battery" title="Nickel-metal hydride battery">nickel-metal hydride</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Button_cell" title="Button cell">button cell</a> batteries.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">Magnets</a> are made of heavy metals such as manganese, iron, cobalt, nickel, niobium, bismuth, praseodymium, neodymium, gadolinium, and <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a>. Neodymium magnets are the strongest type of <a href="/wiki/Magnet#Types_of_permanent_magnets" title="Magnet">permanent magnet</a> commercially available. They are key components of, for example, car door locks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starter_motor" title="Starter motor">starter motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_pump" title="Fuel pump">fuel pumps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Power_window" title="Power window">power windows</a>.</p>
<p>Heavy metals are used in <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs). <a href="/wiki/Flat_panel_display" title="Flat panel display">Flat panel displays</a> incorporate a thin film of electrically conducting <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting">Fluorescent lighting</a> relies on mercury vapour for its operation. <a href="/wiki/Ruby_laser" title="Ruby laser">Ruby lasers</a> generate deep red beams by exciting chromium atoms; the lanthanides are also extensively employed in lasers. Gallium, indium, and arsenic; and copper, iridium, and platinum are used in LEDs (the latter three in <a href="/wiki/OLED" title="OLED">organic LEDs</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear">Nuclear</span></h3>

<p>Niche uses of heavy metals with high atomic numbers occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diagnostic_imaging" title="Diagnostic imaging">diagnostic imaging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">electron microscopy</a>, and nuclear science. In diagnostic imaging, heavy metals such as cobalt or tungsten make up the anode materials found in <a href="/wiki/X-ray_tube" title="X-ray tube">x-ray tubes</a>. In electron microscopy, heavy metals such as lead, gold, palladium, platinum, or uranium are used to make conductive coatings and to introduce electron density into biological specimens by <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_staining" title="Negative staining">negative staining</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Evaporation_(deposition)" title="Evaporation (deposition)">vacuum deposition</a>. In nuclear science, nuclei of heavy metals such as chromium, iron, or zinc are sometimes fired at other heavy metal targets to produce <a href="/wiki/Transuranium_element#Super-heavy_elements" title="Transuranium element">superheavy elements</a>; heavy metals are also employed as <a href="/wiki/Spallation#Nuclear_spallation" title="Spallation">spallation</a> targets for the production of <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> such as astatine (using lead, bismuth, thorium, or uranium in the latter case).</p>
