<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For current and future climatological effects of human influences, see <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. For the study of past climate change, see <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>. For temperatures on the longest time scales, see <a href="/wiki/Geologic_temperature_record" title="Geologic temperature record">geologic temperature record</a>.</div>

<p><b>Climate change</b> is a change in the statistical distribution of <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> patterns when that change lasts for an extended period of time (i.e., decades to millions of years). Climate change may refer to a change in average weather conditions, or in the time variation of weather around longer-term average conditions (i.e., more or fewer <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events). Climate change is caused by factors such as <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/biotic" title="wikt:biotic">biotic</a> processes, variations in <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">solar radiation</a> received by Earth, <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruptions" title="Volcanic eruptions">volcanic eruptions</a>. Certain human activities have also been identified as significant causes of recent climate change, often referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">Scientists</a> actively work to understand past and future <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> by using <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">observations</a> and theoretical models. A climate record—extending deep into the Earth's past—has been assembled, and continues to be built up, based on geological evidence from <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">borehole</a> temperature profiles, <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">cores removed from deep accumulations of ice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">floral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">faunal</a> records, glacial and <a href="/wiki/Periglaciation" title="Periglaciation">periglacial</a> processes, stable-isotope and other analyses of sediment layers, and records of past sea levels. More recent data are provided by the instrumental record. <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">General circulation models</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_physical_science" title="Outline of physical science">physical sciences</a>, are often used in theoretical approaches to match past climate data, make future projections, and link causes and effects in climate change.</p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming#Etymology" title="Global warming">Global warming § Etymology</a></div>
<p>The most general definition of <i>climate change</i> is a change in the statistical properties (principally its <a href="/wiki/Central_tendency" title="Central tendency">mean</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_variability" title="Statistical variability">spread</a>) of the climate system when considered over long periods of time, regardless of cause. Accordingly, fluctuations over periods shorter than a few decades, such as <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>, do not represent climate change.</p>
<p>The term sometimes is used to refer specifically to climate change caused by human activity, as opposed to changes in climate that may have resulted as part of Earth's natural processes. In this sense, especially in the context of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental policy</a>, the term <i>climate change</i> has become synonymous with <i><a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></i>. Within scientific journals, <i>global warming</i> refers to surface temperature increases while <i>climate change</i> includes global warming and everything else that increasing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> levels affect.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatic_change_versus_climate_change">Climatic change versus climate change</span></h2>
<p>In 1966, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) proposed the term climatic change to encompass all forms of climatic variability on time-scales longer than 10 years, whether the cause was natural or anthropogenic. Change was a given and climatic was used as an adjective to describe this kind of change (as opposed to political or economic change). When it was realized that human activities had a potential to drastically alter the climate, the term climate change replaced climatic change as the dominant term to reflect an anthropogenic cause. Climate change was incorporated in the title of the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="UN Framework Convention on Climate Change">UN Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC). Climate change, used as a noun, became an issue rather than the technical description of changing weather.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span></h2>
<p>On the broadest scale, the rate at which energy is received from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and the rate at which it is lost to space determine the equilibrium temperature and climate of Earth. This energy is distributed around the globe by winds, ocean currents, and other mechanisms to affect the climates of different regions.</p>
<p>Factors that can shape climate are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcings</a> or "forcing mechanisms". These include processes such as variations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>, variations in the Earth's orbit, variations in the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> or reflectivity of the continents and oceans, <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">mountain-building</a> and <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> and changes in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> concentrations. There are a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_feedback" title="Climate feedback">climate change feedbacks</a> that can either amplify or diminish the initial forcing. Some parts of the climate system, such as the oceans and ice caps, respond more slowly in reaction to climate forcings, while others respond more quickly. There are also key <a href="/wiki/Ecological_threshold" title="Ecological threshold">threshold factors</a> which when exceeded can produce rapid change.</p>
<p>Forcing mechanisms can be either "internal" or "external". Internal forcing mechanisms are natural processes within the climate system itself (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>). External forcing mechanisms can be either natural (e.g., changes in solar output) or anthropogenic (e.g., increased emissions of greenhouse gases).</p>
<p>Whether the initial forcing mechanism is internal or external, the response of the climate system might be fast (e.g., a sudden cooling due to airborne <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> reflecting sunlight), slow (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> of warming ocean water), or a combination (e.g., sudden loss of <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> in the arctic ocean as sea ice melts, followed by more gradual thermal expansion of the water). Therefore, the climate system can respond abruptly, but the full response to forcing mechanisms might not be fully developed for centuries or even longer.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_forcing_mechanisms">Internal forcing mechanisms</span></h3>
<p>Scientists generally define the five components of earth's climate system to include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_Atmosphere" title="Earth's Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> (restricted to the surface soils, rocks, and sediments), and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Natural changes in the climate system ("internal forcings") result in internal "climate variability". Examples include the type and distribution of species, and changes in ocean currents.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_variability">Ocean variability</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Thermohaline circulation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_inertia" title="Climate inertia">Climate inertia</a></div>

<p>The ocean is a fundamental part of the climate system, some changes in it occurring at longer timescales than in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, as it has hundreds of times more mass and thus very high <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_heat_capacity" title="Volumetric heat capacity">thermal inertia</a>, with effects such as the ocean depths still lagging today in temperature adjustment from effects of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> of past centuries).</p>
<p>Short-term fluctuations (years to a few decades) such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_decadal_oscillation" title="Pacific decadal oscillation">Pacific decadal oscillation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">North Atlantic oscillation</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>, represent <b>climate variability</b> rather than climate change. On longer time-scales, alterations to ocean processes such as thermohaline circulation play a key role in redistributing heat by carrying out a very slow and extremely deep movement of water and the long-term redistribution of heat in the world's oceans.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span></h4>
<p>Life affects climate through its role in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycles</a> and through such mechanisms as <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud formation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. Examples of how life may have affected past climate include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> 2.3 billion years ago triggered by the evolution of oxygenic <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, which depleted the atmosphere of the greenhouse gas carbon dioxide and introduced free oxygen.</li>
<li>another glaciation 300 million years ago ushered in by long-term burial of <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">decomposition-resistant</a> <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> of vascular land-plants (creating a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal#Formation" title="Coal">forming coal</a>)</li>
<li>termination of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> 55 million years ago by flourishing marine <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a></li>
<li>reversal of global warming 49 million years ago by <a href="/wiki/Azolla_event" title="Azolla event">800,000 years of arctic azolla blooms</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">global cooling</a> over the past 40 million years driven by the expansion of grass-grazer <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_forcing_mechanisms">External forcing mechanisms</span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_variations">Orbital variations</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a></div>
<p>Slight variations in Earth's orbit lead to changes in the seasonal distribution of sunlight reaching the Earth's surface and how it is distributed across the globe. There is very little change to the area-averaged annually averaged sunshine; but there can be strong changes in the geographical and seasonal distribution. The three types of orbital variations are variations in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a>, changes in <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">the tilt angle of Earth's axis of rotation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a> of Earth's axis. Combined together, these produce <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a> which have a large impact on climate and are notable for their correlation to <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interglacial_period" title="Interglacial period">interglacial periods</a>, their correlation with the advance and retreat of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, and for their <a href="/wiki/Cyclostratigraphy" title="Cyclostratigraphy">appearance</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_record" title="Geologic record">stratigraphic record</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> notes that Milankovitch cycles drove the ice age cycles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a> followed temperature change "with a lag of some hundreds of years," and that as a feedback amplified temperature change. The depths of the ocean have a lag time in changing temperature (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_inertia" title="Thermal inertia">thermal inertia</a> on such scale). Upon seawater temperature change, the solubility of CO<sub>2</sub> in the oceans changed, as well as other factors impacting air-sea CO<sub>2</sub> exchange.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_output">Solar output</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">Solar variation</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is the predominant source of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> input to the Earth. Other sources include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal">geothermal</a> energy from the Earth's core, and heat from the decay of radioactive compounds. Both long- and short-term variations in solar intensity are known to affect global climate.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">Three to four billion years ago</a>, the Sun emitted only 70% as much power as it does today. If the atmospheric composition had been the same as today, liquid water should not have existed on Earth. However, there is evidence for the presence of water on the early Earth, in the <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> eons, leading to what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">faint young Sun paradox</a>. Hypothesized solutions to this paradox include a vastly different atmosphere, with much higher concentrations of greenhouse gases than currently exist. Over the following approximately 4 billion years, the energy output of the Sun increased and atmospheric composition changed. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a> – oxygenation of the atmosphere around 2.4 billion years ago – was the most notable alteration. Over the next five billion years, the Sun's ultimate death as it becomes a <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> and then a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a> will have large effects on climate, with the red giant phase possibly ending any life on Earth that survives until that time.</p>
<p>Solar output also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">varies</a> on shorter time scales, including the 11-year <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a> and longer-term <a href="/wiki/Modulation" title="Modulation">modulations</a>. Solar intensity variations possibly as a result of the Wolf, <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer_Minimum" title="Spörer Minimum">Spörer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">Maunder Minimum</a> are considered to have been influential in triggering the Little Ice Age, and some of the warming observed from 1900 to 1950. The cyclical nature of the Sun's energy output is not yet fully understood; it differs from the very slow change that is happening within the Sun as it ages and evolves. Research indicates that solar variability has had effects including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maunder_minimum" title="Maunder minimum">Maunder minimum</a> from 1645 to 1715 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">A.D.</a>, part of the Little Ice Age from 1550 to 1850 A.D. that was marked by relative cooling and greater glacier extent than the centuries before and afterward. Some studies point toward solar radiation increases from cyclical sunspot activity affecting global warming, and climate may be influenced by the sum of all effects (solar variation, anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcings</a>, etc.).</p>
<p>Interestingly, a 2010 study <i>suggests</i>, “that the effects of solar variability on temperature throughout the atmosphere may be contrary to current expectations.”</p>
<p>In an Aug 2011 Press Release, <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> announced the publication in the <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a> journal the initial results from its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CLOUD" title="CLOUD">CLOUD</a> experiment. The results indicate that ionisation from cosmic rays significantly enhances aerosol formation in the presence of sulfuric acid and water, but in the lower atmosphere where ammonia is also required, this is insufficient to account for aerosol formation and additional trace vapours must be involved. The next step is to find more about these trace vapours, including whether they are of natural or human origin.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray#Postulated_role_in_climate_change" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic ray § Postulated role in climate change</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanism">Volcanism</span></h4>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">eruptions</a> considered to be large enough to affect the Earth's climate on a scale of more than 1 year are the ones that inject over 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a> into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. This is due to the optical properties of SO<sub>2</sub> and sulfate aerosols, which strongly absorb or scatter solar radiation, creating a global layer of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> haze. On average, such eruptions occur several times per century, and cause cooling (by partially blocking the transmission of solar radiation to the Earth's surface) for a period of a few years.</p>
<p>The eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in 1991, the second largest terrestrial eruption of the 20th century, affected the climate substantially, subsequently global temperatures decreased by about 0.5 °C (0.9 °F) for up to three years. Thus, the cooling over large parts of the Earth reduced surface temperatures in 1991-93, the equivalent to a reduction in net radiation of 4 watts per square meter. The <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">Mount Tambora</a> eruption in 1815 caused the <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">Year Without a Summer</a>. Much larger eruptions, known as <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous provinces</a>, occur only a few times every fifty - hundred million years - through <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a>, and caused in Earth past global warming and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinctions</a>.</p>
<p>Small eruptions, with injections of less than 0.1 Mt of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, impact the atmosphere only subtly, as temperature changes are comparable with natural variability. However, because smaller eruptions occur at a much higher frequency, they too have a significant impact on Earth's atmosphere.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Prediction_of_volcanic_activity" title="Prediction of volcanic activity">Seismic monitoring</a> maps current and future trends in volcanic activities, and tries to develop early warning systems. In <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate modelling</a> the aim is to study the physical mechanisms and feedbacks of volcanic forcing.</p>
<p>Volcanoes are also part of the extended <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>. Over very long (geological) time periods, they release carbon dioxide from the Earth's crust and mantle, counteracting the uptake by sedimentary rocks and other geological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">carbon dioxide sinks</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Geological_Survey" title="US Geological Survey">US Geological Survey</a> estimates are that volcanic emissions are at a much lower level than the effects of current human activities, which generate 100–300 times the amount of carbon dioxide emitted by volcanoes. A review of published studies indicates that annual volcanic emissions of carbon dioxide, including amounts released from mid-ocean ridges, volcanic arcs, and hot spot volcanoes, are only the equivalent of 3 to 5 days of human caused output. The annual amount put out by human activities may be greater than the amount released by <a href="/wiki/Supervolcano" title="Supervolcano">supererruptions</a>, the most recent of which was the <a href="/wiki/Toba_catastrophe_theory" title="Toba catastrophe theory">Toba eruption</a> in Indonesia 74,000 years ago.</p>
<p>Although volcanoes are technically part of the lithosphere, which itself is part of the climate system, the IPCC explicitly defines volcanism as an external forcing agent.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_tectonics">Plate tectonics</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a></div>
<p>Over the course of millions of years, the motion of tectonic plates reconfigures global land and ocean areas and generates topography. This can affect both global and local patterns of climate and atmosphere-ocean circulation.</p>
<p>The position of the continents determines the geometry of the oceans and therefore influences patterns of ocean circulation. The locations of the seas are important in controlling the transfer of heat and moisture across the globe, and therefore, in determining global climate. A recent example of tectonic control on ocean circulation is the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> about 5 million years ago, which shut off direct mixing between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> Oceans. This strongly affected the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_boundary_current" title="Western boundary current">ocean dynamics</a> of what is now the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> and may have led to Northern Hemisphere ice cover. During the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period, about 300 to 360 million years ago, plate tectonics may have triggered large-scale storage of carbon and increased <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glaciation" title="wikt:glaciation">glaciation</a>. Geologic evidence points to a "megamonsoonal" circulation pattern during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, and climate modeling suggests that the existence of the supercontinent was conducive to the establishment of monsoons.</p>
<p>The size of continents is also important. Because of the stabilizing effect of the oceans on temperature, yearly temperature variations are generally lower in coastal areas than they are inland. A larger supercontinent will therefore have more area in which climate is strongly seasonal than will several smaller continents or <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_influences">Human influences</span></h4>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>
<p>In the context of climate variation, anthropogenic factors are human activities which affect the climate. The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific consensus on climate change</a> is "that climate is changing and that these changes are in large part caused by human activities," and it "is largely irreversible."</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>“Science has made enormous inroads in understanding climate change and its causes, and is beginning to help develop a strong understanding of current and potential impacts that will affect people today and in coming decades. This understanding is crucial because it allows decision makers to place climate change in the context of other large challenges facing the nation and the world. There are still some uncertainties, and there always will be in understanding a complex system like Earth’s climate. Nevertheless, there is a strong, credible body of evidence, based on multiple lines of research, documenting that climate is changing and that these changes are in large part caused by human activities. While much remains to be learned, the core phenomenon, scientific questions, and hypotheses have been examined thoroughly and have stood firm in the face of serious scientific debate and careful evaluation of alternative explanations.”</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a>, <i>Advancing the Science of Climate Change</i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Of most concern in these anthropogenic factors is the increase in CO<sub>2</sub> levels due to emissions from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> combustion, followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">aerosols</a> (particulate matter in the atmosphere) and the CO<sub>2</sub> released by <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> manufacture. Other factors, including land use, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, animal agriculture and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, are also of concern in the roles they play – both separately and in conjunction with other factors – in affecting climate, <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a>, and measures of climate variables.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_evidence">Physical evidence</span></h2>
<div class="center">

</div>


<p>Evidence for climatic change is taken from a variety of sources that can be used to reconstruct past climates. Reasonably complete global records of surface temperature are available beginning from the mid-late 19th century. For earlier periods, most of the evidence is indirect—climatic changes are inferred from changes in <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxies</a>, indicators that reflect climate, such as <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_level_change" title="Sea level change">sea level change</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_geology" title="Glacial geology">glacial geology</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_measurements_and_proxies">Temperature measurements and proxies</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental temperature record</a> from surface stations was supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde balloons</a>, extensive atmospheric monitoring by the mid-20th century, and, from the 1970s on, with <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">global satellite data</a> as well. The O/O ratio in calcite and ice core samples <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope_ratio_cycle" title="Oxygen isotope ratio cycle">used to deduce ocean temperature in the distant past</a> is an example of a temperature proxy method, as are other climate metrics noted in subsequent categories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_and_archaeological_evidence">Historical and archaeological evidence</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Historical_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Historical impacts of climate change">Historical impacts of climate change</a></div>
<p>Climate change in the recent past may be detected by corresponding changes in settlement and agricultural patterns. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">Archaeological</a> evidence, <a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">oral history</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_documents" title="Historical documents">historical documents</a> can offer insights into past changes in the climate. Climate change effects have been linked to the collapse of various civilizations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers">Glaciers</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">Glaciers</a> are considered among the most sensitive indicators of climate change. Their size is determined by a <a href="/wiki/Mass_balance" title="Mass balance">mass balance</a> between snow input and melt output. As temperatures warm, glaciers retreat unless snow precipitation increases to make up for the additional melt; the converse is also true.</p>
<p>Glaciers grow and shrink due both to natural variability and external forcings. Variability in temperature, precipitation, and englacial and subglacial hydrology can strongly determine the evolution of a glacier in a particular season. Therefore, one must average over a decadal or longer time-scale and/or over many individual glaciers to smooth out the local short-term variability and obtain a glacier history that is related to climate.</p>
<p>A world glacier inventory has been compiled since the 1970s, initially based mainly on aerial photographs and maps but now relying more on satellites. This compilation tracks more than 100,000 glaciers covering a total area of approximately 240,000 km, and preliminary estimates indicate that the remaining ice cover is around 445,000 km. The World Glacier Monitoring Service collects data annually on <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glacier retreat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">glacier mass balance</a>. From this data, glaciers worldwide have been found to be shrinking significantly, with strong glacier retreats in the 1940s, stable or growing conditions during the 1920s and 1970s, and again retreating from the mid-1980s to present.</p>
<p>The most significant climate processes since the middle to late <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> (approximately 3 million years ago) are the glacial and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> cycles. The present interglacial period (the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>) has lasted about 11,700 years. Shaped by <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">orbital variations</a>, responses such as the rise and fall of <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental</a> ice sheets and significant sea-level changes helped create the climate. Other changes, including <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">Heinrich events</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dansgaard%E2%80%93Oeschger_event" title="Dansgaard–Oeschger event">Dansgaard–Oeschger events</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>, however, illustrate how glacial variations may also influence climate without the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a>.</p>
<p>Glaciers leave behind <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> that contain a wealth of material—including organic matter, quartz, and potassium that may be dated—recording the periods in which a glacier advanced and retreated. Similarly, by <a href="/wiki/Tephrochronology" title="Tephrochronology">tephrochronological</a> techniques, the lack of glacier cover can be identified by the presence of soil or volcanic <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">tephra</a> horizons whose date of deposit may also be ascertained.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arctic_sea_ice_loss">Arctic sea ice loss</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_Arctic" title="Climate change in the Arctic">Climate change in the Arctic</a></div>
<p>The decline in Arctic sea ice, both in extent and thickness, over the last several decades is further evidence for rapid climate change. Sea ice is frozen seawater that floats on the ocean surface. It covers millions of square miles in the polar regions, varying with the seasons. In the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, some sea ice remains year after year, whereas almost all <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> or Antarctic sea ice melts away and reforms annually. Satellite observations show that Arctic sea ice is now declining at a rate of 13.3 percent per decade, relative to the 1981 to 2010 average.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">Vegetation</span></h3>
<p>A change in the type, distribution and coverage of vegetation may occur given a change in the climate. Some changes in climate may result in increased precipitation and warmth, resulting in improved plant growth and the subsequent sequestration of airborne CO<sub>2</sub>. A gradual increase in warmth in a region will lead to earlier flowering and fruiting times, driving a change in the timing of life cycles of dependent organisms. Conversely, cold will cause plant bio-cycles to lag. Larger, faster or more radical changes, however, may result in vegetation stress, rapid plant loss and desertification in certain circumstances. An example of this occurred during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Rainforest_Collapse" title="Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse">Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse</a> (CRC), an extinction event 300 million years ago. At this time vast rainforests covered the equatorial region of Europe and America. Climate change devastated these tropical rainforests, abruptly fragmenting the habitat into isolated 'islands' and causing the extinction of many plant and animal species.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollen_analysis">Pollen analysis</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Palynology</a> is the study of contemporary and fossil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palynomorph" title="Palynomorph">palynomorphs</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>. Palynology is used to infer the geographical distribution of plant species, which vary under different climate conditions. Different groups of plants have pollen with distinctive shapes and surface textures, and since the outer surface of pollen is composed of a very resilient material, they resist decay. Changes in the type of pollen found in different layers of sediment in lakes, bogs, or river deltas indicate changes in plant communities. These changes are often a sign of a changing climate. As an example, palynological studies have been used to track changing vegetation patterns throughout the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciations</a> and especially since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_cover_and_precipitation">Cloud cover and precipitation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">Cloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">Precipitation</a></div>
<p>Past precipitation can be estimated in the modern era with the global network of precipitation gauges. Surface coverage over oceans and remote areas is relatively sparse, but, reducing reliance on <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a>, satellite clouds and precipitation data has been available since the 1970s. Quantification of climatological variation of precipitation in prior centuries and epochs is less complete but approximated using proxies such as marine sediments, ice cores, cave stalagmites, and tree rings. In July 2016 scientists published evidence of increased cloud cover over polar regions, as predicted by climate models.</p>
<p>Climatological temperatures substantially affect cloud cover and precipitation. For instance, during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> of 18,000 years ago, thermal-driven <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> from the oceans onto continental landmasses was low, causing large areas of extreme desert, including <a href="/wiki/Polar_desert" title="Polar desert">polar deserts</a> (cold but with low rates of cloud cover and precipitation). In contrast, the world's climate was cloudier and wetter than today near the start of the warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_period" title="Atlantic period">Atlantic Period</a> of 8000 years ago.</p>
<p>Estimated global land precipitation increased by approximately 2% over the course of the 20th century, though the calculated trend varies if different time endpoints are chosen, complicated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">ENSO</a> and other oscillations, including greater global land cloud cover precipitation in the 1950s and 1970s than the later 1980s and 1990s despite the positive trend over the century overall. Similar slight overall increase in global river runoff and in average soil moisture has been perceived.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dendroclimatology">Dendroclimatology</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dendroclimatology" title="Dendroclimatology">Dendroclimatology</a> is the analysis of tree ring growth patterns to determine past climate variations. Wide and thick rings indicate a fertile, well-watered growing period, whilst thin, narrow rings indicate a time of lower rainfall and less-than-ideal growing conditions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_cores">Ice cores</span></h3>
<p>Analysis of ice in a core drilled from an <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheet</a>, can be used to show a link between temperature and global sea level variations. The air trapped in bubbles in the ice can also reveal the CO<sub>2</sub> variations of the atmosphere from the distant past, well before modern environmental influences. The study of these ice cores has been a significant indicator of the changes in CO<sub>2</sub> over many millennia, and continues to provide valuable information about the differences between ancient and modern atmospheric conditions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span></h3>
<p>Remains of <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> are common in freshwater and land sediments. Different species of beetles tend to be found under different climatic conditions. Given the extensive lineage of beetles whose genetic makeup has not altered significantly over the millennia, knowledge of the present climatic range of the different species, and the age of the sediments in which remains are found, past climatic conditions may be inferred.</p>
<p>Similarly, the historical abundance of various <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> species has been found to have a substantial relationship with observed climatic conditions. Changes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_productivity" title="Primary productivity">primary productivity</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autotrophs" title="Autotrophs">autotrophs</a> in the oceans can affect marine food webs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_change">Sea level change</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">Sea level</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">Current sea level rise</a></div>
<p>Global sea level change for much of the last century has generally been estimated using <a href="/wiki/Tide_gauge" title="Tide gauge">tide gauge</a> measurements collated over long periods of time to give a long-term average. More recently, <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a> measurements — in combination with accurately determined <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> orbits — have provided an improved measurement of global sea level change. To measure sea levels prior to instrumental measurements, scientists have dated <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> that grow near the surface of the ocean, coastal sediments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_terrace" title="Marine terrace">marine terraces</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ooids" title="Ooids">ooids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a>, and nearshore archaeological remains. The predominant dating methods used are <a href="/wiki/Uranium-thorium_dating" title="Uranium-thorium dating">uranium series</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmogenic_radionuclide_dating" title="Cosmogenic radionuclide dating">cosmogenic radionuclides</a> being sometimes used to date terraces that have experienced relative sea level fall. In the early <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>, global temperatures were 1–2˚C warmer than the present temperature, yet sea level was 15–25 meters higher than today.</p>
