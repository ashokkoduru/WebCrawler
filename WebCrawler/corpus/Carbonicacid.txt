<p><b>Carbonic acid</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> (equivalently OC(OH)<sub>2</sub>). It is also a name sometimes given to <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a>), because such solutions contain small amounts of H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>. In <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, carbonic acid is described as <i>volatile acid</i> or <i>respiratory acid</i>, because it is the only acid excreted as a gas by the lungs.</p>
<p>Carbonic acid, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a>, forms two kinds of <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a>. In geology, carbonic acid causes limestone to dissolve producing <a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">calcium bicarbonate</a> which leads to many limestone features such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalactites" title="Stalactites">stalactites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalagmites" title="Stalagmites">stalagmites</a>.</p>
<p>The long-held belief that carbonic acid could not exist as a pure compound was disproved, in 1993, by the preparation of the pure substance. This was independently confirmed in 2011.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_equilibrium">Chemical equilibrium</span></h2>
<p>When carbon dioxide dissolves in water it exists in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibrium</a> producing carbonic acid:</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydration</a> <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_constant" title="Equilibrium constant">equilibrium constant</a> at 25 °C is called K<sub>h</sub>, which in the case of carbonic acid is [H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>]/[CO<sub>2</sub>] ≈ 1.7×10 in pure water and ≈ 1.2×10 in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>. Hence, the majority of the carbon dioxide is not converted into carbonic acid, remaining as CO<sub>2</sub> molecules. In the absence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, the equilibrium is reached quite slowly. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rate_constant" title="Rate constant">rate constants</a> are 0.039 s for the forward reaction (CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and 23 s for the reverse reaction (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O). Carbonic acid is used in the making of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_drinks" title="Soft drinks">soft drinks</a>, inexpensive and artificially carbonated sparkling wines, and other bubbly drinks. The addition of two molecules of water to CO<sub>2</sub> would give <i><a href="/wiki/Orthocarbonic_acid" title="Orthocarbonic acid">orthocarbonic acid</a></i>, C(OH)<sub>4</sub>, which exists only in minute amounts in aqueous solution.</p>
<p>Addition of base to an excess of carbonic acid gives <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (hydrogen carbonate). With excess base, carbonic acid reacts to give <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> salts.</p>
<p>Because carbon dioxide is non-polar, it cannot dissolve in the bloodstream to travel to the lungs. As such, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> converts carbon dioxide and water into carbonic acid, which is polar and thus can travel through the body. In the lungs, carbonic anhydrase converts carbonic acid back into water and carbon dioxide to expel carbon dioxide through the lungs without losing water.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_carbonic_acid_in_blood">Role of carbonic acid in blood</span></h2>
<p>Carbonic acid is an intermediate step on the transport of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the body via <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">respiratory gas exchange</a>. The hydration reaction of CO<sub>2</sub> is generally very slow in the absence of a catalyst, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a> contain <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a>, which both increases the reaction rate and dissociates a hydrogen ion (H) from the resulting carbonic acid, leaving <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub>) dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. This catalysed reaction is reversed in the lungs, where it converts the bicarbonate back into CO<sub>2</sub> and allows it to be expelled. This equilibration plays an important role as a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer</a> in mammalian blood. A 2016 theoretical report suggests that carbonic acid may have pivotal role in protonating various nitrogen bases in blood serum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_carbonic_acid_in_ocean_chemistry"><span id="Ocean"></span> Role of carbonic acid in ocean chemistry</span></h2>
<p>The oceans of the world have absorbed almost half of the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by humans from the burning of fossil fuels.  It has been theorized that the extra dissolved carbon dioxide has caused the ocean's average surface pH to shift by about −0.1 unit from pre-industrial levels. This theory is known as <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroscopic_studies_of_carbonic_acid">Spectroscopic studies of carbonic acid</span></h2>
<p>Theoretical calculations show that the presence of even a single molecule of water causes carbonic acid to revert to carbon dioxide and water. In the absence of water, the dissociation of gaseous carbonic acid is predicted to be very slow, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 180,000 years. This may only apply to isolated carbonic acid molecules, however, as it has been predicted to catalyze its own decomposition.</p>
<p>The longstanding belief was that pure carbonic acid cannot be obtained at room temperatures (about 20 °C, or about 70 °F) due to its catalytic destruction by water. It has been discovered that it can be generated by exposing a frozen mixture of water and carbon dioxide to high-energy radiation and then warming to remove the excess water. The carbonic acid that remains is characterized by infrared spectroscopy. The fact that the carbonic acid was prepared by irradiating a solid H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub> mixture may suggest that H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> might be found in outer space, where frozen ices of H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub> are common, as are cosmic rays and ultraviolet light, to help them react. The same carbonic acid polymorph (denoted <i>beta</i>-carbonic acid) was prepared by heating alternating layers of glassy aqueous solutions of bicarbonate and acid in vacuum, which causes protonation of bicarbonate, followed by removal of the solvent. The previously suggested <i>alpha</i>-carbonic acid, which was prepared by the same technique using methanol rather than water as a solvent was shown to be a monomethyl ester CH<sub>3</sub>OCOOH.</p>
