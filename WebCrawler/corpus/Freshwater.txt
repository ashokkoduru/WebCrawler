<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">"Freshwater" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Freshwater_(disambiguation)" title="Freshwater (disambiguation)">Freshwater (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Fresh water</b> is naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface in <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, and underground as <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and underground streams. Fresh water is generally characterized by having low <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentrations</a> of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids" title="Total dissolved solids">total dissolved solids</a>. The term specifically excludes <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a> although it does include mineral-rich waters such as <a href="/wiki/Chalybeate" title="Chalybeate">chalybeate</a> springs. The term "sweet water" (from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> "agua dulce") has been used to describe fresh water in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">salt water</a>. The term fresh water does not have the same meaning as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable water</a>. Much of the surface fresh water and ground water is unsuitable for drinking without some form of purification because of the presence of chemical or biological contaminants.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">Systems</span></h2>

<p>Fresh water habitats are divided into <a href="/wiki/Lake_ecosystem" title="Lake ecosystem">lentic systems</a>, which are the stillwaters including <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, lakes, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">mires</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotic" title="Lotic">lotic</a>, or running-water systems; and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> which flows in rocks and <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>. There is, in addition, a zone which bridges between groundwater and lotic systems, which is the <a href="/wiki/Hyporheic_zone" title="Hyporheic zone">hyporheic zone</a>, which underlies many larger rivers and can contain substantially more water than is seen in the open channel. It may also be in direct contact with the underlying underground water. The majority of fresh water on Earth is in <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span></h2>
<p>The source of almost all fresh water is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>. Fresh water falling as mist, rain or snow contains materials dissolved from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and material from the sea and land over which the rain bearing clouds have traveled. In <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrialized</a> areas rain is typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> because of dissolved oxides of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> formed from burning of fossil fuels in cars, factories, trains and aircraft and from the atmospheric emissions of industry. In some cases this <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> results in <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> of lakes and rivers.</p>
<p>In coastal areas fresh water may contain significant concentrations of salts derived from the sea if windy conditions have lifted drops of seawater into the rain-bearing clouds. This can give rise to elevated concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> as well as many other compounds in smaller concentrations.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> areas, or areas with impoverished or dusty soils, rain-bearing winds can pick up <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> and this can be deposited elsewhere in precipitation and causing the freshwater flow to be measurably contaminated both by insoluble solids but also by the soluble components of those soils. Significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> may be transported in this way including the well-documented transfer of iron-rich rainfall falling in Brazil derived from sand-storms in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_distribution">Water distribution</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_distribution_on_Earth" title="Water distribution on Earth">Water distribution on Earth</a></div>

<p>Water is a critical issue for the survival of all living organisms. Some can use salt water but many organisms including the great majority of higher plants and most <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> must have access to fresh water to live. Some terrestrial mammals, especially desert <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> appear to survive without drinking but they do generate water through the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> seeds and they also have mechanisms to conserve water to the maximum degree.</p>
<p>Out of all the water on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, saline water in <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a> and saline <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> make up about 97% of it. Only 2.5–2.75% is fresh water, including 1.75–2% frozen in <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, 0.5–0.75% as fresh <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> moisture, and less than 0.01% of it as <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>. Freshwater lakes contain about 87% of this fresh surface water, including 29% in the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a>, 20% in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in Russia, 21% in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Great_Lakes" title="North American Great Lakes">North American Great Lakes</a>, and 14% in other lakes. <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">Swamps</a> have most of the balance with only a small amount in rivers, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a>. The atmosphere contains 0.04% water. In areas with no fresh water on the ground surface, fresh water derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> may, because of its lower density, overlie saline ground water in lenses or layers. Most of the world's fresh water is frozen in <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>. Many areas suffer from lack of distribution of fresh water, such as deserts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_definition">Numerical definition</span></h2>
<p>Fresh water can be defined as water with less than 500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> (ppm) of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salts</a>.</p>

<p>Other sources give higher upper salinity limits for fresh water, e.g. 1000 ppm or 3000 ppm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquatic_organisms">Aquatic organisms</span></h2>
<p>Fresh water creates a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotonicity" title="Hypotonicity">hypotonic</a> environment for aquatic organisms. This is problematic for some organisms with pervious skins or with <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gill</a> membranes, whose cell membranes may burst if excess water is not excreted. Some <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a> accomplish this using <a href="/wiki/Contractile_vacuole" title="Contractile vacuole">contractile vacuoles</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">freshwater fish</a> excrete excess water via the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>. Although most aquatic organisms have a limited ability to regulate their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmotic" title="Osmotic">osmotic</a> balance and therefore can only live within a narrow range of salinity, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diadromous" title="Diadromous">diadromous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> have the ability to <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migrate</a> between fresh water and <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saline</a> water bodies. During these migrations they undergo changes to adapt to the surroundings of the changed salinities; these processes are hormonally controlled. The <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anguilla_anguilla" title="Anguilla anguilla">Anguilla anguilla</a></i>) uses the <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> <a href="/wiki/Prolactin" title="Prolactin">prolactin</a>, while in <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> (<i>Salmo salar</i>) the hormone <a href="/wiki/Cortisol" title="Cortisol">cortisol</a> plays a key role during this process.</p>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_bird" title="Sea bird">sea birds</a> have special glands at the base of the bill through which excess salt is excreted. Similarly the <a href="/wiki/Marine_iguana" title="Marine iguana">marine iguanas</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> excrete excess salt through a nasal gland and they sneeze out a very salty excretion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_water_as_a_resource">Fresh water as a resource</span></h2>

<p>An important concern for hydrological ecosystems is securing minimum <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">streamflow</a>, especially preserving and restoring <a href="/wiki/Instream_use" title="Instream use">instream water allocations</a>. Fresh water is an important natural resource necessary for the survival of all <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. The use of water by humans for activities such as irrigation and industrial applications can have adverse impacts on down-stream ecosystems. Chemical contamination of fresh water can also seriously damage eco-systems.</p>
<p>Pollution from human activity, including oil spills and also presents a problem for freshwater resources. The largest <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> spill that has ever occurred in fresh water was caused by a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a> tank ship in Magdalena, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, on 15 January 1999, polluting the environment, drinkable water, plants and animals.</p>
<p>Fresh and unpolluted water accounts for 0.003% of total water available globally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span></h3>
<p>Changing landscape for the use of agriculture has a great effect on the flow of fresh water. Changes in landscape by the removal of trees and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> changes the flow of fresh water in the local environment and also affects the cycle of fresh water. As a result, more fresh water is stored in the soil which benefits agriculture. However, since agriculture is the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> activity that consumes the most fresh water, this can put a severe strain on local freshwater resources resulting in the destruction of local ecosystems. In Australia, over-abstraction of fresh water for intensive <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> activities has caused 33% of the land area to be at risk of salination. With regards to agriculture, the <a class="external text" href="http://water.worldbank.org/water/publications/reengaging-agricultural-water-management-challenges-and-options/" rel="nofollow">World Bank</a> targets food production and water management as an increasingly global issue that will foster debate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_resource">Limited resource</span></h3>
<p>Fresh water is a renewable and variable, but finite natural resource. Fresh water can only be replenished through the process of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, in which water from seas, lakes, forests, land, rivers, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoirs" title="Reservoirs">reservoirs</a> evaporates, forms clouds, and returns as precipitation. Locally however, if more fresh water is consumed through human activities than is naturally restored, this may result in reduced fresh water availability from surface and underground sources and can cause serious damage to surrounding and associated environments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_water_withdrawal">Fresh water withdrawal</span></h3>
<p>Fresh water withdrawal is the quantity of water removed from available sources for use in any purpose, excluding evaporation losses. Water drawn off is not necessarily entirely consumed and some portion may be returned for further use downstream.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_limited_fresh_water">Causes of limited fresh water</span></h3>
<p>There are many causes of the apparent decrease in our fresh water supply. Principal amongst these is the increase in population through increasing life expectancy, the increase in per capita water use and the desire of many people to live in warm climates that have naturally low levels of fresh water resources. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> is also likely to change the availability and distribution of fresh water across the planet:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"If global warming continues to melt glaciers in the polar regions, as expected, the supply of fresh water may actually decrease. First, fresh water from the melting glaciers will mingle with salt water in the oceans and become too salty to drink. Second, the increased ocean volume will cause sea levels to rise, contaminating freshwater sources along coastal regions with seawater”.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a class="external text" href="http://water.worldbank.org/water/publications/water-and-climate-change-understanding-risks-and-making-climate-smart-investment-decisi" rel="nofollow">World Bank</a> adds that the response by freshwater ecosystems to a changing climate can be described in terms of three interrelated components: water quality, water quantity or volume, and water timing. A change in one often leads to shifts in the others as well. <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> also reduces the availability of fresh water.</p>
<p>Also, there is an uneven distribution of fresh water. While some countries have an abundant supply of fresh water, others do not have as much. For example, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> has 20% of the world's fresh water supply, while India has only 10% of the world's fresh water supply, even though <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s population is more than 30 times larger than that of Canada. A reason for the uneven distribution of fresh water supply may be the differences in climate. For example, in some countries in Africa, the frequent lack of rain has led to insufficient water supply for irrigation. This has affected agriculture and has led to a shortage of food for the people.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_water_in_the_future">Fresh water in the future</span></h3>
<p>Many areas of the world are already experiencing stress on water availability. Due to the accelerated pace of population growth and an increase in the amount of water a single person uses, it is expected that this situation will continue to get worse. A shortage of water in the future would be detrimental to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a> as it would affect everything from sanitation, to overall health and the production of grain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Choices_in_the_use_of_fresh_water">Choices in the use of fresh water</span></h3>
<p>With one in eight people in the world not having access to safe water it is important to use this resource in a prudent manner. Making the best use of water on a local basis probably provides the best solution. Local communities need to plan their use of fresh water and should be made aware of how certain crops and animals use water.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessing_fresh_water">Accessing fresh water</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span></h3>
<p>Canada has approximately 7% of the world’s renewable fresh water. Canadians access their water from ground water, lakes and streams; it is then cleaned and purified in water treatment plants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<p>The United States uses much more water per capita than developing countries. For example, the average American’s daily shower uses more water than a person in a developing country would use for an entire day. <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>, a city that uses an extreme amount of water to support spectacular lush greenery and golf courses, as well as huge fountains and swimming pools gets 90% of its water from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mead#Drought_and_water_usage_issues" title="Lake Mead">Lake Mead</a>, which is now at a record all-time low.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_countries">Developing countries</span></h3>
<p>In developing countries, 780 million people lack access to clean water. Half of the population of the developing world suffers from at least one disease caused by insufficient water supply and sanitation.</p>
