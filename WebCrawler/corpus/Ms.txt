<p><b>Metre per second</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>: <b>meter per second</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a> of both <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometry)" title="Vector (geometry)">vector quantity</a> which specifies both magnitude and a specific direction), defined by <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a> divided by <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> in <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">seconds</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI unit</a> symbols are m·s, m s, m/s, or <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">m</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">s</span></span>, sometimes (unofficially) abbreviated as "mps". Where metres per second are several orders of magnitude too slow to be convenient, such as in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> measurements, velocities may be given in kilometres per second, where 1 km/s is 1000 metres per second, sometimes unofficially abbreviated as "kps".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_measures">Relation to other measures</span></h2>
<p>The benz, named in honour of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a>, has been proposed as a name for one metre per second. Although it has seen some support as a practical unit, primarily from German sources, it was rejected as the SI unit of velocity and has not seen widespread use or acceptance.</p>
