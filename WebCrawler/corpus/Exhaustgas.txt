<p><b>Exhaust gas</b> or <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> is emitted as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, petrol, biodiesel blends, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>. According to the type of engine, it is discharged into the atmosphere through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exhaust_pipe" title="Exhaust pipe">exhaust pipe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_stack" title="Flue gas stack">flue gas stack</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">propelling nozzle</a>. It often disperses downwind in a pattern called an <b>exhaust plume</b>.</p>
<p>It is a major component of <b>motor vehicle emissions</b> (and from stationary internal combustion engines), which can also include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crankcase" title="Crankcase">Crankcase</a> blow-by</li>
<li>Evaporation of unused gasoline</li>
</ul>
<p>Motor vehicle emissions contribute to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and are a major ingredient in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a> in some large cities. A 2013 study by <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> indicates that 53,000 early deaths occur per year in the United States alone because of vehicle emissions. According to another study from the same university, traffic fumes alone cause the death of 5,000 people every year just in the United Kingdom.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span></h2>
<p>The largest part of most combustion gas is <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N<sub>2</sub>), water vapor (H<sub>2</sub>O) (except with pure-carbon fuels), and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) (except for fuels without carbon); these are not toxic or noxious (although carbon dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> that contributes to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>). A relatively small part of combustion gas is undesirable noxious or toxic substances, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO) from incomplete combustion, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> (properly indicated as C<sub>x</sub>H<sub>y</sub>, but typically shown simply as "HC" on emissions-test slips) from unburnt fuel, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>) from excessive combustion temperatures, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhaust_gas_temperature">Exhaust gas temperature</span></h2>
<p>Exhaust gas temperature (EGT) is important to the functioning of the <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>. It may be measured by an <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas_temperature_gauge" title="Exhaust gas temperature gauge">exhaust gas temperature gauge</a>. EGT is also a measure of engine health in gas-turbine engines (see below).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_engines">Cold engines</span></h2>

<p>During the first two minutes after starting the engine of a car that has not been operated for several hours, the amount of emissions can be very high. This occurs for two main reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Rich air-fuel ratio requirement in cold engines</b>: When a cold engine is started, the fuel does not vaporize completely, creating higher emissions of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, which diminishes only as the engine reaches operating temperature. The duration of this start-up phase has been reduced by advances in materials and technology, including <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection#Electronic_injection" title="Fuel injection">computer-controlled fuel injection</a>, shorter intake lengths, and pre-heating of fuel and/or inducted air.</li>
<li><b>Inefficient catalytic converter under cold conditions</b>: Catalytic converters are very inefficient until up to their <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>. This time has been much reduced by moving the converter closer to the exhaust manifold and even more so placing a small yet quick-to-heat-up converter directly at the exhaust manifold. The small converter handles the start-up emissions, which allows enough time for the larger main converter to heat up. Further improvements can be realised in many ways, including electric heating, thermal battery, chemical reaction preheating, flame heating and superinsulation.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passenger_car_emissions_summary">Passenger car emissions summary</span></h2>

<p>Comparable with the <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">European emission standards</a> EURO III as it was applied on October 2000</p>
<p>In 2000, the United States Environmental Protection Agency began to implement more stringent emissions standards for light duty vehicles. The requirements were phased in beginning with 2004 vehicles and all new cars and light trucks were required to meet the updated standards by the end of 2007.</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitromethane_additive">Nitromethane additive</span></h5>
<p>Exhaust gas from an internal combustion engine whose fuel includes <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a> will contain <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> vapour, which is corrosive, and when inhaled causes a muscular reaction making it impossible to breathe. People exposed to it should wear a <a href="/wiki/Gas_mask" title="Gas mask">gas mask</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diesel_engines">Diesel engines</span></h4>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas-turbine_engines">Gas-turbine engines</span></h4>
<ul>
<li>In aircraft gas turbine engines, "exhaust gas temperature" (EGT) is a primary measure of engine health. Typically the EGT is compared with a primary engine power indication called "engine pressure ratio" (EPR). For example: at full power EPR there will be a maximum permitted EGT limit. Once an engine reaches a stage in its life where it reaches this EGT limit, the engine will require specific maintenance in order to rectify the problem. The amount the EGT is below the EGT limit is called EGT margin. The EGT margin of an engine will be greatest when the engine is new, or has been overhauled. For most airlines, this information is also monitored remotely by the airline maintenance department by means of <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Communications_Addressing_and_Reporting_System" title="Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System">ACARS</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_engines_and_rocket_engines">Jet engines and rocket engines</span></h4>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a>, exhaust from <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">propelling nozzles</a> which in some applications shows <a href="/wiki/Shock_diamond" title="Shock diamond">shock diamonds</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_motor_vehicle_emissions">Main motor vehicle emissions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NOx">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></span></h3>

<p>Mono-nitrogen oxides NO and NO<sub>2</sub> (whether produced this way or naturally by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>) react with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, moisture, and other compounds to form <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> vapor and related particles. Small particles can penetrate deeply into sensitive lung tissue and damage it, causing premature death in extreme cases. Inhalation of such particles may cause or worsen respiratory diseases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>. It may also aggravate existing heart disease. In a 2005 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. EPA</a> study the largest emissions of NOx came from on road motor vehicles, with the second largest contributor being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-road_equipment" title="Non-road equipment">non-road equipment</a> which is mostly gasoline and diesel stations.</p>
<p>The resulting nitric acid may be washed into soil, where it becomes <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, which is useful to growing plants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volatile_organic_compounds">Volatile organic compounds</span></h3>

<p>When oxides of nitrogen (NOx) and <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs) react in the presence of sunlight, ground level <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> is formed, a primary ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>. A 2005 U.S. EPA report gives road vehicles as the second largest source of VOCs in the U.S. at 26% and 19% are from non road equipment which is mostly gasoline and diesel stations. 27% of VOC emissions are from solvents which are used in the manufacturer of paints and paint thinners and other uses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozone">Ozone</span></h3>
<p>Ozone is beneficial in the upper atmosphere, but at ground level, ozone irritates the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory system</a>, causing coughing, choking, and reduced lung capacity. It also has many bad effects throughout the ecosystem.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_monoxide_.28CO.29">Carbon monoxide (CO)</span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">Carbon monoxide poisoning</a> is the most common type of fatal air poisoning in many countries. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">Carbon monoxide</a> is colorless, odorless and tasteless, but highly toxic. It combines with <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">carboxyhemoglobin</a>, which is ineffective for delivering oxygen to bodily tissues. In 2011, 52% of carbon monoxide emissions were created by mobile vehicles in the U.S.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazardous_air_pollutants_.28toxics.29">Hazardous air pollutants (toxics)</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">Chronic</a> (long-term) exposure to <a href="/wiki/Benzene#Health_effects" title="Benzene">benzene</a> (<a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub></a>) damages <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>. It can also cause excessive bleeding and depress the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, increasing the chance of <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>. Benzene causes leukemia and is associated with other blood cancers and pre-cancers of the blood.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particulate_matter_.28PM10_and_PM2.5.29">Particulate matter (PM<sub>10</sub> and PM<sub>2.5</sub>)</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_Matter#Health_effects" title="Particulate Matter">health effects</a> of inhaling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">airborne particulate matter</a> have been widely studied in humans and animals and include <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, cardiovascular issues, and premature <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>. Because of the size of the particles, they can penetrate the deepest part of the lungs. A 2011 UK study estimates 90 deaths per year due to passenger vehicle PM. In a 2006 publication, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">U.S. Federal Highway Administration</a> (FHWA) state that in 2002 about 1 per-cent of all PM<sub>10</sub> and 2 per-cent of all PM<sub>2.5</sub> emissions came from the exhaust of on-road motor vehicles (mostly from <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_.28CO2.29">Carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. Motor vehicle CO<sub>2</sub> emissions are part of the anthropogenic contribution to the growth of CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations in the atmosphere which some claim is causing <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Motor vehicles are calculated to generate about 20% of the European Union's man-made CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, with passenger cars contributing about 12%. <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">European emission standards</a> limit the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions of new passenger cars and light vehicles. The European Union average new car CO<sub>2</sub> emissions figure dropped by 5.4% in the year to the first quarter of 2010, down to 145.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G/km" title="G/km">g/km</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_vapor">Water vapor</span></h3>
<p>Vehicle exhaust contains much <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_recovery">Water recovery</span></h4>
<p>There has been research into ways that troops in deserts can recover drinkable water from their vehicles' exhaust gases. </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_reduction">Pollution reduction</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emission_standard" title="Emission standard">Emission standards</a> focus on reducing <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> contained in the exhaust gases from vehicles as well as from industrial flue gas stacks and other <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> exhaust sources in various large-scale industrial facilities such as <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">petroleum refineries</a>, natural gas processing plants, <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> plants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> production plants. However, these are often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a>. <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">Catalytic converters</a> in cars intend to break down the pollution of exhaust gases using a catalyst. <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">Scrubbers</a> in ships intend to remove the sulfur dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>) of marine exhaust gases. The regulations on marine sulfur dioxide emissions are tightening, however only a small number of special areas worldwide have been designated for low sulfur diesel fuel use only.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Air_suction_valve" title="Air suction valve">Air suction valve</a></div>
<p>One of the advantages claimed for <a href="/wiki/Advanced_steam_technology" title="Advanced steam technology">advanced steam technology</a> engines is that they produce smaller quantities of toxic pollutants (e.g. oxides of nitrogen) than petrol and diesel engines of the same power. They produce larger quantities of carbon dioxide but less carbon monoxide due to more efficient combustion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_studies">Health studies</span></h2>
<p>Researchers from the University of California, Los Angeles School of Public Health say preliminary results of their statistical study of children listed in the California Cancer Registry born between 1998 and 2007 found that traffic pollution may be associated with a 5% to 15% increase in the likelihood of some cancers. A <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> study found that diesel fumes cause an increase in lung cancer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Localised_effects">Localised effects</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> (C.A.R.B.) found in studies that 50% or more of the <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> (<a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a> is due to car emissions.</p>
