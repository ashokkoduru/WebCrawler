<p><b>Willows</b>, also called <b>sallows</b>, and <b>osiers</b>, form the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Salix</b></i>, around 400 species of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>, found primarily on moist <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> in cold and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. Most species are known as willow, but some narrow-leaved shrub species are called <b>osier</b>, and some broader-leaved species are referred to as <b>sallow</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>sealh</i>, related to the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>salix</i>, willow). Some willows (particularly <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> species) are low-growing or creeping shrubs; for example, the dwarf willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_herbacea" title="Salix herbacea">Salix herbacea</a></i>) rarely exceeds 6 cm (2.4 in) in height, though it spreads widely across the ground.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>

<p>Willows all have abundant watery <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> <a href="/wiki/Sap" title="Sap">sap</a>, which is heavily charged with <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">salicylic acid</a>, soft, usually pliant, tough <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, slender branches, and large, fibrous, often <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">stoloniferous</a> <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>. The roots are remarkable for their toughness, size, and tenacity to life, and roots readily sprout from aerial parts of the plant.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are typically elongated, but may also be round to oval, frequently with serrated edges. Most species are <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>; semi<a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> willows with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriaceous" title="Coriaceous">coriaceous</a> leaves are rare, e.g. <i>Salix micans</i> and <i>S. australior</i> in the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. All the buds are lateral; no absolutely terminal bud is ever formed. The buds are covered by a single scale. Usually, the bud scale is fused into a cap-like shape, but in some species it wraps around and the edges overlap. The leaves are simple, feather-veined, and typically linear-lanceolate. Usually they are serrate, rounded at base, acute or acuminate. The leaf <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petioles</a> are short, the <a href="/wiki/Stipule" title="Stipule">stipules</a> often very conspicuous, resembling tiny, round leaves, and sometimes remaining for half the summer. On some species, however, they are small, inconspicuous, and caducous (soon falling). In color, the leaves show a great variety of greens, ranging from yellowish to bluish.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flowers">Flowers</span></h3>

<p>Willows are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioecious" title="Dioecious">dioecious</a>, with male and female <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> appearing as <a href="/wiki/Catkin" title="Catkin">catkins</a> on separate plants; the catkins are produced early in the spring, often before the leaves.</p>
<p>The staminate (male) flowers are without either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calyx_(botany)" title="Calyx (botany)">calyx</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corolla_(flower)" title="Corolla (flower)">corolla</a>; they consist simply of stamens, varying in number from two to 10, accompanied by a nectariferous gland and inserted on the base of a scale which is itself borne on the rachis of a drooping raceme called a catkin, or ament. This scale is square, entire, and very hairy. The anthers are rose-colored in the bud, but orange or purple after the flower opens; they are two-celled and the cells open longitudinally. The filaments are threadlike, usually pale brown, and often bald.</p>
<p>The pistillate (female) flowers are also without calyx or corolla, and consist of a single ovary accompanied by a small, flat nectar gland and inserted on the base of a scale which is likewise borne on the rachis of a catkin. The ovary is one-celled, the style two-lobed, and the ovules numerous.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h2>
<p>Almost all willows take root very readily from <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(plant)" title="Cutting (plant)">cuttings</a> or where broken branches lie on the ground. The few exceptions include the goat willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_caprea" title="Salix caprea">Salix caprea</a></i>) and peachleaf willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_amygdaloides" title="Salix amygdaloides">Salix amygdaloides</a></i>). One famous example of such growth from cuttings involves the poet <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>, who begged a twig from a parcel tied with twigs sent from Spain to <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Howard,_Countess_of_Suffolk" title="Henrietta Howard, Countess of Suffolk">Lady Suffolk</a>. This twig was planted and thrived, and legend has it that all of England's weeping willows are descended from this first one.</p>
<p>Willows are often planted on the borders of streams so their interlacing roots may protect the bank against the action of the water. Frequently, the roots are much larger than the stem which grows from them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrids">Hybrids</span></h2>
<p>Willows are very cross-compatible, and numerous <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> occur, both naturally and in cultivation. A well-known <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental</a> example is the <a href="/wiki/Salix_babylonica#Horticultural_selections_and_related_hybrids" title="Salix babylonica">weeping willow</a> (<i>Salix × sepulcralis</i>), which is a hybrid of Peking willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_babylonica" title="Salix babylonica">Salix babylonica</a></i>) from China and white willow (<i><a href="/wiki/Salix_alba" title="Salix alba">Salix alba</a></i>) from Europe.</p>
<p>The hybrid <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> 'Boydii' has gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_issues">Ecological issues</span></h2>

<p>Willows are used as food plants by the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of some <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lepidoptera_that_feed_on_willows" title="List of Lepidoptera that feed on willows">species</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nymphalis_antiopa" title="Nymphalis antiopa">mourning cloak butterfly</a>. Ants, such as <a href="/wiki/Formica_rufa_group" title="Formica rufa group">wood ants</a>, are common on willows inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, coming to collect aphid <a href="/wiki/Honeydew_(secretion)" title="Honeydew (secretion)">honeydew</a>, as sometimes do <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a>.</p>
<p>A small number of willow species were widely planted in Australia, notably as <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>-control measures along watercourses. They are now regarded as invasive <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a>, and many catchment management authorities are removing and replacing them with native trees.</p>
<p>Willow roots spread widely and are very aggressive in seeking out moisture; for this reason, they can become problematic when planted in residential areas, where the roots are notorious for clogging <a href="/wiki/French_drain" title="French drain">French drains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(agriculture)" title="Drainage system (agriculture)">drainage systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weeping_tile" title="Weeping tile">weeping tiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onsite_sewage_facility" title="Onsite sewage facility">septic systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewer systems</a>, particularly older, <a href="/wiki/Pipe_(fluid_conveyance)#Materials" title="Pipe (fluid conveyance)">tile, concrete, or ceramic pipes</a>. Newer, <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pipework" title="Plastic pipework">PVC sewer pipes</a> are much less leaky at the joints, and are therefore less susceptible to problems from willow roots; the same is true of <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> piping.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pests_and_diseases">Pests and diseases</span></h2>
<p>Willow species are hosts to more than a hundred <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphid</a> species, belonging to <i>Chaitophorus</i> and other genera, forming large colonies to feed on plant juices, on the underside of leaves in particular. <i><a href="/wiki/Corythucha_elegans" title="Corythucha elegans">Corythucha elegans</a></i>, the willow lace bug, is a bug species in the family Tingidae found on willows in North America.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">Rust</a>, caused by fungi of genus <i><a href="/wiki/Melampsora" title="Melampsora">Melampsora</a></i>, is known to damage leaves of willows, covering them with orange spots.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span></h3>
<p>The leaves and bark of the willow tree have been mentioned in ancient texts from <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> as a remedy for aches and <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> the physician <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> wrote about its medicinal properties in the fifth century BC. <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> relied on it as a staple of their medical treatments. It provides temporary pain relief. Salicin is metabolized into <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">salicylic acid</a> in the human body, and is a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>. In 1763, its medicinal properties were observed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reverend_Edward_Stone" title="Reverend Edward Stone">Reverend Edward Stone</a> in England. He notified the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>, which published his findings. The active extract of the bark, called salicin, was isolated to its crystalline form in 1828 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henri_Leroux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henri Leroux (page does not exist)">Henri Leroux</a>, a French pharmacist, and <a href="/wiki/Raffaele_Piria" title="Raffaele Piria">Raffaele Piria</a>, an Italian chemist, who then succeeded in separating out the compound in its pure state. In 1897, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Hoffmann" title="Felix Hoffmann">Felix Hoffmann</a> created a synthetically altered version of salicin (in his case derived from the <i><a href="/wiki/Spiraea" title="Spiraea">Spiraea</a></i> plant), which caused less digestive upset than pure salicylic acid. The new drug, formally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetylsalicylic_acid" title="Acetylsalicylic acid">acetylsalicylic acid</a>, was named <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">Aspirin</a> by Hoffmann's employer <a href="/wiki/Bayer" title="Bayer">Bayer AG</a>. This gave rise to the hugely important class of drugs known as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSAID" title="NSAID">NSAIDs</a>).</p>
<p>Willow has been <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bach_flower_remedies" title="List of Bach flower remedies">listed</a> as one of the 38 substances used to prepare <a href="/wiki/Bach_flower_remedies" title="Bach flower remedies">Bach flower remedies</a>, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span></h3>
<p>Some of humans' earliest manufactured items may have been made from willow. A fishing net made from willow dates back to 8300 BC. Basic crafts, such as <a href="/wiki/Basket_weaving" title="Basket weaving">baskets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> traps, wattle <a href="/wiki/Fence" title="Fence">fences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">wattle and daub</a> house walls, were often woven from osiers or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Withies" title="Withies">withies</a> (rod-like willow shoots, often grown in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coppice" title="Coppice">coppices</a>). One of the forms of Welsh <a href="/wiki/Coracle" title="Coracle">coracle</a> boat traditionally uses willow in the framework. Thin or split willow rods can be woven into <a href="/wiki/Wicker" title="Wicker">wicker</a>, which also has a long history. The relatively pliable willow is less likely to split while being woven than many other woods, and can be bent around sharp corners in basketry. Willow wood is also used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Box" title="Box">boxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broom" title="Broom">brooms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket_bat" title="Cricket bat">cricket bats</a>, cradle boards, <a href="/wiki/Chair" title="Chair">chairs</a> and other furniture, <a href="/wiki/Doll" title="Doll">dolls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willow_flute" title="Willow flute">flutes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flagpole_(structure)" title="Flagpole (structure)">poles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweat_lodge" title="Sweat lodge">sweat lodges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toy" title="Toy">toys</a>, turnery, tool handles, <a href="/wiki/Wood_veneer" title="Wood veneer">veneer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wand" title="Wand">wands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whistle" title="Whistle">whistles</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibre" title="Fibre">fibre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">rope</a> and string can be produced from the wood. Willow is also used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double basses</a> for backs, sides and linings, and in making splines and blocks for bass repair.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span></h3>





<ul>
<li><b>Agriculture</b>: Willows produce a modest amount of nectar from which bees can make honey, and are especially valued as a source of early pollen for bees. Poor people at one time often ate willow catkins that had been cooked to form a mash.</li>
<li><b>Art</b>: Willow is used to make <a href="/wiki/Charcoal#Art" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> (for drawing) and in living <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptures</a>. Living sculptures are created from live willow rods planted in the ground and woven into shapes such as domes and tunnels. Willow stems are used to weave baskets and three-dimensional sculptures, such as animals and figures. Willow stems are also used to create garden features, such as decorative panels and obelisks.</li>
<li><b>Energy</b>: Willow is grown for <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Energy_forestry" title="Energy forestry">energy forestry</a> systems, as a consequence of its high energy in-energy out ratio, large carbon mitigation potential and fast growth. Large-scale projects to support willow as an <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crop</a> are already at commercial scale in Sweden. Programs in other countries are being developed through initiatives such as the <a href="/wiki/Willow_Biomass_Project" title="Willow Biomass Project">Willow Biomass Project</a> in the US, and the Energy Coppice Project in the UK. Willow may also be grown to produce <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>.</li>
<li><b>Environment</b>: As a plant, willow is used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofiltration" title="Biofiltration">biofiltration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">constructed wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> treatment systems, <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">land reclamation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscaping" title="Landscaping">landscaping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">phytoremediation</a>, streambank stabilisation (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioengineering" title="Bioengineering">bioengineering</a>), <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slope</a> stabilisation, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> control, shelterbelt and <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">windbreak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> building, soil reclamation, <a href="/wiki/Tree_bog" title="Tree bog">tree bog</a> compost toilet, and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>.</li>
<li><b>Religion</b>: Willow is one of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_Species" title="Four Species">Four Species</a>" used ritually during the <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> holiday of <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">Sukkot</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, a willow branch is one of the chief attributes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kwan_Yin" title="Kwan Yin">Kwan Yin</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a></i> of compassion. Christian churches in northwestern Europe and Ukraine often used willow branches in place of palms in the ceremonies on <a href="/wiki/Palm_Sunday" title="Palm Sunday">Palm Sunday</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span></h2>
<p>In China, some people carry willow branches with them on the day of their Tomb Sweeping or <a href="/wiki/Qingming_Festival" title="Qingming Festival">Qingming Festival</a>. Willow branches are also put up on gates and/or front doors, which they believe help ward off the evil spirits that wander on Qingming. Legend states that on Qingming Festival, the ruler of the underworld allows the spirits of the dead to return to earth. Since their presence may not always be welcome, willow branches keep them away. In traditional pictures of the Goddess of Mercy <a href="/wiki/Guanyin" title="Guanyin">Guanyin</a>, she is often shown seated on a rock with a willow branch in a vase of water at her side. The Goddess employs this mysterious water and the branch for putting demons to flight. <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witch" title="Witch">witches</a> also use a small carving made from willow wood for communicating with the spirits of the dead. The image is sent to the nether world, where the disembodied spirit is deemed to enter it, and give the desired information to surviving relatives on its return. The willow is a famous subject in many East Asian nations' cultures, particularly in pen and ink paintings from China and Japan.</p>
<p>A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gisaeng" title="Gisaeng">gisaeng</a></i> (Korean <a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">geisha</a>) named Hongrang, who lived in the middle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a>, wrote the poem "By the willow in the rain in the evening", which she gave to her parting lover (Choi Gyeong-chang). Hongrang wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"...I will be the willow on your bedside."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In Japanese tradition, the willow is associated with ghosts. It is popularly supposed that a ghost will appear where a willow grows. Willow trees are also quite prevalent in folklore and myths.</p>
<p>In English folklore, a willow tree is believed to be quite sinister, capable of uprooting itself and stalking travellers. The Viminal Hill, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_hills_of_rome" title="Seven hills of rome">Seven Hills Of Rome</a>, derives its name from the Latin word for osier, <i>viminia</i> (pl.).</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a> wrote a story called "Under the Willow Tree" (1853) in which children ask questions of a tree they call "willow-father", paired with another entity called "<a href="/wiki/Elder_Mother" title="Elder Mother">elder-mother</a>".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Old_Man_Willow" title="Old Man Willow">Old Man Willow</a> in <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendarium" title="Legendarium">legendarium</a>, appearing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>.</li>
<li>"Green Willow" is a <a href="/wiki/Kaidan" title="Kaidan">Japanese ghost story</a> in which a young samurai falls in love with a woman called Green Willow who has a close spiritual connection with a willow tree. "The Willow Wife" is another, not dissimilar tale. "Wisdom of the Willow Tree" is an <a href="/wiki/Osage_Nation" title="Osage Nation">Osage Nation</a> story in which a young man seeks answers from a willow tree, addressing the tree in conversation as 'Grandfather'.</li>
<li>In "The Secret of Salix Babylonicus," set in the Babylonian exile, the willow is portrayed benignly as a symbol of sympathy in grief and perseverance in suffering, an upright character who shows solidarity with the exiles and comes to express the spirit of the psalms.</li>
<li>The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whomping_Willow" title="Whomping Willow">Whomping Willow</a>" is a feature of the grounds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hogwarts_School_for_Witchcraft_and_Wizardry" title="Hogwarts School for Witchcraft and Wizardry">Hogwarts School for Witchcraft and Wizardry</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> stories.</li>
</ul>
<p>In Central Europe a "hollow willow" is a common figure of speech, alluding to a person one can confide secrets in. The metaphor was used e.g. in the poem <i>Král Lávra</i> (King Lear) by Czech poet <a href="/wiki/Karel_Havl%C3%AD%C4%8Dek_Borovsk%C3%BD" title="Karel Havlíček Borovský">Karel Havlíček Borovský</a> (1854).</p>
