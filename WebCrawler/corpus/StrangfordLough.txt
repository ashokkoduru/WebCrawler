<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">


<p><b>Strangford Lough</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i>Strangr Fjörðr</i>, meaning "strong sea-inlet") is a large sea <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">loch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlet</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, in the east of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. It is the largest inlet in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, covering 150 km. The loch is almost totally enclosed by the <a href="/wiki/Ards_Peninsula" title="Ards Peninsula">Ards Peninsula</a> and is linked to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> by a long narrow channel or strait. The main body of the loch has at least seventy islands along with many islets (pladdies), bays, coves, headlands and mudflats. Strangford Lough was designated as Northern Ireland's first <a href="/wiki/Marine_Conservation_Zone" title="Marine Conservation Zone">Marine Conservation Zone</a> (MCZ) under the introduction of the Marine Act (Northern Ireland) 2013. It has also been designated a <a href="/wiki/Special_Area_of_Conservation" title="Special Area of Conservation">Special Area of Conservation</a> under the EU <a href="/wiki/Habitats_Directive" title="Habitats Directive">Habitats Directive</a>, and its abundant wildlife is recognised internationally for its importance.</p>
<p>In the medieval and early modern period Strangford Lough was known in Irish as <b>Loch Cuan</b>, meaning "sea-inlet of bays/havens".</p>
<p>Strangford Lough is a popular tourist destination noted for its fishing and scenery. Towns and villages around the loch include <a href="/wiki/Killyleagh" title="Killyleagh">Killyleagh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comber" title="Comber">Comber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newtownards" title="Newtownards">Newtownards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portaferry" title="Portaferry">Portaferry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strangford" title="Strangford">Strangford</a>. The latter two are connected by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RORO" title="RORO">car ferry</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span></h2>
<p>The name <i>Strangford</i> comes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> <i>Strangr-fjörðr</i>, meaning "strong sea-inlet". The <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> were active in the area during the Middle Ages. Originally, this name referred only to the narrow channel linking the loch to the sea (between the villages of <a href="/wiki/Strangford" title="Strangford">Strangford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portaferry" title="Portaferry">Portaferry</a>). Up until about the 18th century, the main body of the loch was better known by the (older) <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> name <i>Loch Cuan</i>, meaning "loch of the bays/havens". This name was anglicized as <i>Lough Coan</i>, <i>Lough Cone</i>, <i>Lough Coyn</i>, <i>Lough Coin</i>, or similar.</p>
<p>The narrow channel may have been known in Latin as the <i>fretum Brene</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">Ulster-Scots</a> the loch's name is spelt <i>Strangfurd</i> or <i>Strangﬁrt Loch</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span></h3>
<p>Common cord-grass (<i><a href="/wiki/Spartina_anglica" title="Spartina anglica">Spartina anglica</a></i>) C.E. Hubbard was introduced in the mid-1940s is now abundant. <a href="/wiki/Maerl" title="Maerl">Maerl</a> is a calcareous deposit, in the main, of two species, of <a href="/wiki/Calcareous" title="Calcareous">calcareous</a> <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> <i>Phymatolithon calcareum</i> and <i>Lithothamnion glaciale</i> which form free-living beds of unattached, branched <a href="/wiki/Coralline_algae" title="Coralline algae">corallines</a>, living or dead, in Strangford Lough.</p>
<p>The rocky and boulder shores toward the south of the lough are dominated by the seaweed knotted wrack <i>Ascophyllum nodosum</i>. The usual zonation of weeds on these shore is, at the top channel wrack (<i><a href="/wiki/Pelvetia" title="Pelvetia">Pelvetia</a> canaliculata</i> (L.) Dcne. et Rhur.), followed by spiral wrack (<i><a href="/wiki/Fucus_spiralis" title="Fucus spiralis">Fucus spiralis</a></i> L.), then knotted wrack (<i>Ascophyllum nodosum</i> (L.) Le Jol) with some admixture of bladder wrack (<i><a href="/wiki/Fucus_vesiculosus" title="Fucus vesiculosus">Fucus vesiculosus</a></i>) L. and then serrated wrack (<i><a href="/wiki/Fucus_serratus" title="Fucus serratus">Fucus serratus</a></i> L.) before coming to the low water kelps.</p>
<p>A brown <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> named <i><a href="/wiki/Sargassum" title="Sargassum">Sargassum</a> muticum</i>, originally from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> (<a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>) was discovered on 15 March 1995 in Strangford Lough at Paddy's Point. The plants were well established on mesh bags containing oysters. The bags had been put out in 1987 containing Pacific oysters (<i>Crassostrea gigas</i>) imported from <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>. This <i>Sargassum</i> is known to be a highly invasive species.</p>
<p>Other algae include: <i>Apoglossum rusciofolium</i> (Turn.) J. Ag.; <i>Spyridia filamentosa</i> (Wulf.) Harv.; <i>Sphondylothamnion multifium</i> (Huds.) Näg.; <i>Griffithsia corallinoides</i> (L.) Batt.; <i>Compsothamnion gracillimum</i> de Toni; <i>Compsothamnion thuyoides</i> (Sm.) Schmitz; <i>Calliothamnion corymbosum</i> (Sm.) Lyngb; <i>Rhodymenia delicatula</i> P.Dang.; <i>Haemescharia hennedyi</i> (Harv.) Vinogradova ; <i>Rhodophyllis divaricate</i> (Stackh.) Papenf.; <i>Calliblepharis jubata</i> (Good. et Woodw.) Kütz.; <i>Calliblepharis ciliata</i> (Huds.) Kütz; <i>Peyssonnelia dubyi</i> P. et H. Crouan; <i>Plagiospora gracilis</i> Kuck; <i>Gloiosiphonia capillaris</i> (Huds.) Carm.; <i>Dudresnaya verticillata</i> (With.) Le Jol.: <i>Scinaia pseudocrispa</i> (Clem.)<i>Cremades/S.turgida</i> Chemin; <i>Porphyropsis coccinea</i> (J.Ag. ex Aresch) Rosenv.; <i>Pelvetia canaliculata</i> (L.) Dcne. et Thur.; <i>Fucus vesiculosus</i> L. var <i>volubilis</i> Turn.; <i>Fucus cottonii</i> Wynne et Magne; <i>Colpomenia peregrine</i> (Sauv.) Hame; <i>Asperococcus compressus</i> Griff. ex Hook.; <i>Striaria attenuate</i> (Grev.) Grev.; <i>Myriotrichia clavaeformis</i> Harv.; <i>Tilopteris mertensii</i> (Turn.) Kütz.; <i>Chordaria flagelliformis</i> (O.F.Müll.) C.Ag.; <i>Spermatochnus paradoxus</i> (Roth) Kütz.; <i>Pseudolithoderma extensum</i> (P. et H. Crouan) Lund.; <i>Enteromorpha ralfsii</i> Harv.; <i>Chlorochytrium</i> sp.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span></h3>
<p>Strangford Lough and Islands is an <a href="/wiki/Important_Bird_Area" title="Important Bird Area">Important Bird Area</a>. Strangford Lough is an important winter <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migration</a> destination for many wading and sea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a>. <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animals</a> commonly found in the lough include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_seal" title="Common seal">common seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basking_shark" title="Basking shark">basking sharks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brent_goose" title="Brent goose">brent geese</a>. Three quarters of the world population of pale bellied brent geese spend winter in the lough area.</p>
<p>The invasive carpet sea squirt, <i>Didemnum vexillum</i>, was found in the Lough in 2012.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_electricity">Tidal electricity</span></h2>

<p>In 2007 Strangford Lough became home to the world's first commercial <a href="/wiki/Marine_science" title="Marine science">tidal stream</a> power station, <a href="/wiki/SeaGen" title="SeaGen">SeaGen</a>. The 1.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> underwater tidal electricity generator, part of Northern Ireland's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Environment_and_Renewable_Energy_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Environment and Renewable Energy Fund (page does not exist)">Environment and Renewable Energy Fund</a> scheme, takes advantage of the fast tidal flow in the lough which can be up to 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M/s" title="M/s">m/s</a>. Although the generator is powerful enough to power up to a thousand homes, the <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> has a minimal <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental impact</a>, as it is almost entirely submerged, and the rotors turn slowly enough that they pose no danger to <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>.</p>
<p>Since June 2008 a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_energy" title="Tidal energy">tidal energy</a> device called <a href="/wiki/Evopod" title="Evopod">Evopod</a> has been tested in Strangford Lough near the Portaferry Ferry landing. The device is a 1/10 scale prototype and is being monitored by <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_Belfast" title="Queen's University Belfast">Queen's University Belfast</a>. The device is a semi submerged floating tidal turbine and is moored to the seabed via a buoy mounted swivel so that it always maintains optimum heading into the direction of the tidal flow. The scale device is not grid connected and dissipates the small amount of power it generates as heat into the sea. Malik Priestley (executive creator of SeaGen) said "tidal power will provide sustainable amounts of green, renewable energy and pose no threats to surrounding wild life".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activity_on_Strangford_Lough">Activity on Strangford Lough</span></h2>

<p>Strangford Lough is a place of natural beauty that the locals put to great use. From using it as a national highway (Portaferry-Strangford ferry) to using it for activities of leisure - sailing, kayaking, bird watching, diving and much more. There are a number of companies based in the local area encouraging visitors to explore the area including: Exploris Aquarium - where you can learn more about the lough and its wildlife; Clearsky Adventure Centre - kayaking, canoeing etc. and DV diving. There are also canoe trails in place and a 'Strangford Lough Activity Map' has been launched as a part of a series of maps produced by the Ordnance Survey of Northern Ireland.</p>
<p>In July 2016 the Strangford Lough and Lecale Partnership, Scottish Coastal Rowing Association, <a href="/wiki/Newry,_Mourne_and_Down_District_Council" title="Newry, Mourne and Down District Council">Newry, Mourne and Down District Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ards_and_North_Down_Borough_Council" title="Ards and North Down Borough Council">Ards and North Down Borough Council</a>, together with local rowing clubs based on Strangford Lough and the <a href="/wiki/Ards_Peninsula" title="Ards Peninsula">Ards Peninsula</a> hosted the world community, coastal rowing championships “Skiffie Worlds 2016”. The event was attended by 50 clubs from Scotland, England, Northern Ireland, the Netherlands, The United States, Canada and Tasmania. Racing was held over a 2km course on Strangford Lough at <a href="/wiki/Delamont_Country_Park" title="Delamont Country Park">Delamont Country Park</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ferry">Ferry</span></h2>

<p>There has been a ferry service between <a href="/wiki/Portaferry" title="Portaferry">Portaferry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strangford" title="Strangford">Strangford</a>, without a break, for almost four centuries. The alternative road journey is 47 miles and takes about an hour and a half, while the ferry crosses the 0.6 nautical miles (0.69 mi) in 8 minutes. The subsidised public service operates at a loss of more than £1m per year but is viewed as an important transport link to the Ards Peninsula.</p>
<p>MV <i>Portaferry</i> (1) (ex-<i>Cleddau King</i>) was bought from a company in Wales in 1975 after completion of the <a href="/wiki/Cleddau_Bridge" title="Cleddau Bridge">Cleddau Bridge</a>. She was modified by <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">Harland and Wolff</a> of Belfast and operated until sold in May 2002.</p>
<p>MV <i>Strangford</i> was built by the Verolme Shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> and launched on 6 September 1969.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/MV_Portaferry_II" title="MV Portaferry II">MV <i>Portaferry II</i></a>, a £2.7 million vessel built by <a href="/wiki/Remontowa" title="Remontowa">Gdańska Stocznia Remontowa</a> and <a href="/wiki/McTay_Marine" title="McTay Marine">McTay Marine</a> of Merseyside, came into service on 18 December 2001, relegating MV <i>Strangford</i> to a support role.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span></h2>
<p>Strangford Lough has a substantial archaeological heritage. Intertidal archaeological surveys in recent years have brought hundreds of sites to light, including <a href="/wiki/Fish_trap" title="Fish trap">fish traps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tide_mill" title="Tide mill">tidal mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> walls and harbours and landing places.</p>
