<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, an <b>organism</b> is any contiguous <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>, such as an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaeon</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>. All known types of organisms are capable of some degree of <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">response</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">growth and development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. An organism consists of one or more <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>; when it has one cell it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular organism</a>; and when it has more than one it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular organism</a>. Most unicellular organisms are of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">microscopic</a> size and are thus classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a>. Humans are multicellular organisms composed of many trillions of cells grouped into specialized <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>.</p>
<p>An organism may be either a <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a>. Prokaryotes are represented by two separate <a href="/wiki/Three-domain_system" title="Three-domain system">domains</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a>. Eukaryotic organisms are characterized by the presence of a membrane-bound <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a> and contain additional membrane-bound compartments called <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> in animals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a> in plants and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, all generally considered to be derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory" title="Endosymbiotic theory">endosymbiotic</a> bacteria). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">Fungi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animals" title="Animals">animals</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a> are examples of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdoms</a> of organisms within the eukaryotes.</p>
<p>Estimates on the number of Earth's current <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> range from 10 million to 14 million, of which only about 1.2 million have been documented. More than 99% of all species, amounting to over five billion species, that ever lived on Earth are estimated to be <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>. In July 2016, scientists reported identifying a set of 355 <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Universal_Common_Ancestor" title="Last Universal Common Ancestor">Last Universal Common Ancestor</a> (LUCA) of all organisms living on Earth.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The term "organism" (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ὀργανισμός, <i>organismos</i>, from ὄργανον, <i>organon</i>, i.e. "instrument, implement, tool, organ of sense or apprehension") first appeared in the English language in 1703 and took on its current definition by 1834 (<a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a>). It is directly related to the term "organization". There is a long tradition of defining organisms as self-organizing beings.</p>
<p>There has been controversy about the best way to define the organism and indeed about whether or not such a definition is necessary. Several contributions are responses to the suggestion that the category of "organism" may well not be adequate in biology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantics">Semantics</span></h2>
<p>The word <i>organism</i> may broadly be defined as an assembly of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> functioning as a more or less stable whole that exhibits the <a href="/wiki/Life#Biology" title="Life">properties of life</a>. However, many sources propose definitions that exclude <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> and theoretically possible man-made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_biochemistry" title="Alternative biochemistry">non-organic life</a> forms. Viruses are dependent on the biochemical machinery of a host cell for reproduction.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chambers_Online_Reference" title="Chambers Online Reference">Chambers Online Reference</a> provides a broad definition: "any living structure, such as a plant, animal, fungus or bacterium, capable of growth and reproduction".</p>
<p>In multicellular terms, "organism" usually describes the whole hierarchical assemblage of systems (for example <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system" title="Reproductive system">reproductive</a>) themselves collections of <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>; these are, in turn, collections of tissues, which are themselves made of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>. In some plants and the <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">Caenorhabditis elegans</a></i>, individual cells are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totipotent" title="Totipotent">totipotent</a>.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Superorganism" title="Superorganism">superorganism</a> is an organism consisting of many individuals working together as a single functional or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_unit" title="Social unit">social unit</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-cellular_life">Non-cellular life</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-cellular_life" title="Non-cellular life">Non-cellular life</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">Viruses</a> are not typically considered to be organisms because they are incapable of autonomous <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, growth or <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. This controversy is problematic because some cellular organisms are also incapable of independent survival (but are capable of independent metabolism and procreation) and live as obligatory intracellular parasites. Although viruses have a few <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and molecules characteristic of living organisms, they have no metabolism of their own; they cannot synthesize and organize the organic compounds from which they are formed. Naturally, this rules out autonomous reproduction: they can only be passively replicated by the machinery of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">host cell</a>. In this sense they are similar to inanimate matter. While viruses sustain no independent metabolism, and thus are usually not classified as organisms, they do have their own <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, and they do <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolve</a> by mechanisms similar to the evolutionary mechanisms of organisms.</p>
<p>The most common argument in support of viruses as living organisms is their ability to undergo evolution and replicate through self-assembly. Some scientists argue that viruses neither evolve, nor self- reproduce. In fact, viruses are evolved by their host cells, meaning that there was co-evolution of viruses and host cells. If host cells did not exist, viral evolution would be impossible. This is not true for cells. If viruses did not exist, the direction of cellular evolution could be different, but cells would nevertheless be able to evolve. As for the reproduction, viruses totally rely on hosts' machinery to replicate. The discovery of viral megagenomes with genes coding for energy metabolism and protein synthesis fueled the debate about whether viruses belong in the <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_life" title="Tree of life">tree of life</a>. The presence of these genes suggested that viruses were once able to metabolize. However, it was found later that the genes coding for energy and protein metabolism have cellular origin. Most likely, these genes were acquired through <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a> from viral hosts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizational_terminology">Organizational terminology</span></h2>

<p>All organisms are classified by the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_taxonomy" title="Alpha taxonomy">alpha taxonomy</a> into either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxa" title="Taxa">taxa</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clades" title="Clades">clades</a>.</p>
<p>Taxa are ranked groups of organisms, which run from the general (<a href="/wiki/Domain_(biology)" title="Domain (biology)">domain</a>) to the specific (<a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>). A broad scheme of ranks in hierarchical order is:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Domain_(biology)" title="Domain (biology)">Domain</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">Kingdom</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">Phylum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">Class</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">Order</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">Family</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">Genus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">Species</a></li>
</ol>
</div>
<p>To give an example, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_binomial" title="Latin binomial">Latin binomial</a> equating to modern "humans". All members of the species <i>sapiens</i> are, at least in theory, genetically able to interbreed. Several species may belong to a genus, but the members of different species within a genus are usually unable to interbreed to produce fertile offspring. (The <a href="/wiki/Red_wolf" title="Red wolf">red wolf</a> <i>Canis lupus rufus</i>, however, may be a result of interbreeding between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grey_wolf" title="Grey wolf">grey wolf</a> <i>Canis lupus</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyote</a> <i>Canis latrans</i>.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_(genus)" title="Homo (genus)">Homo</a> only has one surviving species (<i>sapiens</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis" title="Homo neanderthalensis">Homo neanderthalensis</a></i>, etc. having become extinct thousands of years ago; some scientists argue for interbreeding between <i>H. sapiens</i> and <i>H. neanderthalensis</i> with fertile progeny. Several genera belong to the same family and so on up the hierarchy. Eventually, the relevant kingdom (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animalia" title="Animalia">Animalia</a>, in the case of humans) is placed into one of the three domains depending upon certain genetic and structural characteristics.</p>
<p>All living organisms known to science are given classification by this system such that the species within a particular family are more closely related and genetically similar than the species within a particular phylum.</p>
<p>Since viruses are not living organisms, their classification is a challenging task. At first, viruses were classified according to their hosts: plant viruses, animal viruses, bacteriophages. Later, they were classified by the disease that they cause. For example, respiratory viruses, enterics. Now, viruses are classified based on the nucleic acid content, capsid symmetry and the presence or absence of the envelope.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h2>
<p>Organisms are complex chemical systems, organized in ways that promote reproduction and some measure of sustainability or survival. The same laws that govern non-living chemistry govern <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">the chemical processes of life</a>. It is generally the phenomena of entire organisms that determine their fitness to an environment and therefore the survivability of their <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>-based genes.</p>
<p>Organisms clearly owe their origin, metabolism, and many other internal functions to chemical phenomena, especially the chemistry of large organic molecules. Organisms are complex systems of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> that, through interaction and environment, play a wide variety of roles.</p>
<p>Organisms are semi-closed chemical systems. Although they are individual units of life (as the definition requires), they are not closed to the environment around them. To operate they constantly take in and release energy. <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">Autotrophs</a> produce usable energy (in the form of organic compounds) using light from the sun or inorganic compounds while <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a> take in organic compounds from the environment.</p>
<p>The primary <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> in these compounds is <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>. The chemical properties of this element such as its great affinity for bonding with other small atoms, including other carbon atoms, and its small size making it capable of forming multiple bonds, make it ideal as the basis of organic life. It is able to form small three-atom compounds (such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>), as well as large chains of many thousands of atoms that can store data (<a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>), hold cells together, and transmit information (protein).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macromolecules">Macromolecules</span></h3>
<p>Compounds that make up organisms may be divided into <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a> and other, smaller molecules. The four groups of macromolecule are <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>. Nucleic acids (specifically deoxyribonucleic acid, or DNA) store genetic data as a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>. The particular sequence of the four different types of nucleotides (<a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>) dictate many characteristics that constitute the organism. The sequence is divided up into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codon" title="Codon">codons</a>, each of which is a particular sequence of three nucleotides and corresponds to a particular <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a>. Thus a sequence of DNA codes for a particular protein that, due to the chemical properties of the amino acids it is made from, <a href="/wiki/Protein_folding" title="Protein folding">folds</a> in a particular manner and so performs a particular function.</p>
<p>These protein functions have been recognized:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzymes</a>, which catalyze all of the reactions of metabolism</li>
<li>Structural proteins, such as <a href="/wiki/Tubulin" title="Tubulin">tubulin</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a></li>
<li>Regulatory proteins, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcription_factors" title="Transcription factors">transcription factors</a> or cyclins that regulate the cell cycle</li>
<li>Signaling molecules or their receptors such as some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormones" title="Hormones">hormones</a> and their receptors</li>
<li>Defensive proteins, which can include everything from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibodies" title="Antibodies">antibodies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, to toxins (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Dendrotoxin" title="Dendrotoxin">dendrotoxins</a> of snakes), to proteins that include unusual amino acids like <a href="/wiki/Canavanine" title="Canavanine">canavanine</a></li>
</ol>
<p>A bilayer of <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> makes up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phospholipid_membrane" title="Phospholipid membrane">membrane</a> of cells that constitutes a barrier, containing everything within the cell and preventing compounds from freely passing into, and out of, the cell. Due to the selective permeability of the phospholipid membraine only specific compounds can pass through it. In some multicellular organisms they serve as a storage of energy and mediate communication between cells. Carbohydrates are more easily broken down than lipids and yield more energy to compare to lipids and proteins. In fact, carbohydrates are the number one source of energy for all living organisms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h2>
<p>All organisms consist of monomeric units called <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>; some contain a single cell (unicellular) and others contain many units (multicellular). Multicellular organisms are able to specialize cells to perform specific functions. A group of such cells is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissue</a>, and in animals these occur as four basic types, namely <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nervous_tissue" title="Nervous tissue">nervous tissue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle tissue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>. Several types of tissue work together in the form of an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> to produce a particular function (such as the pumping of the blood by the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, or as a barrier to the environment as the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>). This pattern continues to a higher level with several organs functioning as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_system" title="Organ system">organ system</a> to allow for <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system" title="Reproductive system">reproduction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestion</a>, etc. Many multicellular organisms consist of several organ systems, which coordinate to allow for life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell">Cell</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">cell theory</a>, first developed in 1839 by <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Jakob_Schleiden" title="Matthias Jakob Schleiden">Schleiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Schwann</a>, states that all organisms are composed of one or more cells; all cells come from preexisting cells; all vital functions of an organism occur within cells, and cells contain the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">hereditary information</a> necessary for regulating cell functions and for transmitting information to the next generation of cells.</p>
<p>There are two types of cells, eukaryotic and prokaryotic. Prokaryotic cells are usually singletons, while eukaryotic cells are usually found in multicellular organisms. Prokaryotic cells lack a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_membrane" title="Nuclear membrane">nuclear membrane</a> so <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is unbound within the cell; eukaryotic cells have nuclear membranes.</p>
<p>All cells, whether prokaryotic or eukaryotic, have a <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">membrane</a>, which envelops the cell, separates its interior from its environment, regulates what moves in and out, and maintains the <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">electric potential of the cell</a>. Inside the membrane, a <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salty</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> takes up most of the cell volume. All cells possess DNA, the hereditary material of <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, containing the information necessary to <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">build</a> various <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, the cell's primary machinery. There are also other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a> in cells.</p>
<p>All cells share several similar characteristics of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reproduction by <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_fission" title="Binary fission">binary fission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>).</li>
<li>Use of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and other proteins <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">coded</a> by DNA genes and made via <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> intermediates and <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosomes</a>.</li>
<li>Metabolism, including taking in raw materials, building cell components, converting energy, <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and releasing <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a>. The functioning of a cell depends upon its ability to extract and use chemical energy stored in organic molecules. This energy is derived from <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a>.</li>
<li>Response to external and internal <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">stimuli</a> such as changes in temperature, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> or nutrient levels.</li>
<li>Cell contents are contained within a <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell surface membrane</a> that contains proteins and a <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_span">Life span</span></h2>
<p>One of the basic parameters of an organism is its <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life span</a>. Some organisms live as short as one day, while some plants and fungi can live thousands of years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">Common descent</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">Origin of life</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_universal_common_ancestor">Last universal common ancestor</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor" title="Last universal common ancestor">Last universal common ancestor</a></div>
<p>The last universal common ancestor (LUCA) is the most recent organism from which all organisms now living on Earth <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">descend</a>. Thus it is the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> of all current life on Earth. The LUCA is estimated to have lived some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_evolution" title="Timeline of evolution">3.5 to 3.8 billion years ago</a> (sometime in the <a href="/wiki/Paleoarchean" title="Paleoarchean">Paleoarchean</a> <a href="/wiki/Era" title="Era">era</a>). The earliest evidences for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> are <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> found to be <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic</a> in 3.7 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary rocks</a> discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossils" title="Fossils">fossils</a> found in 3.48 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> discovered in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Although more than 99 percent of all species that ever lived on the planet are estimated to be extinct, there are currently 10–14 million species of life on Earth.</p>
<p>Information about the early development of life includes input from many different fields, including <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>. These sciences provide information about the history of the Earth and the changes produced by life. However, a great deal of information about the early Earth has been destroyed by geological processes over the course of time.</p>
<p>All organisms on Earth are descended from a common ancestor or ancestral gene pool. Evidence for common descent may be found in traits shared between all living organisms. In Darwin's day, the evidence of shared traits was based solely on visible observation of morphologic similarities, such as the fact that all birds have wings, even those that do not fly.</p>
<p>Today, there is strong evidence from genetics that all organisms have a common ancestor. For example, every living cell makes use of <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> as its genetic material, and uses the same twenty <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> as the building blocks for <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. All organisms use the same <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> (with some extremely rare and minor deviations) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translation_(genetics)" title="Translation (genetics)">translate</a> nucleic acid sequences into proteins. The universality of these traits strongly suggests common ancestry, because the selection of many of these traits seems arbitrary.</p>
<p>In 2000, W. Ford Doolittle discussed variability in the genetic code (see: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code#Variations_to_the_standard_genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code variation</a>) and suggested that <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a> might make it difficult to study the last universal ancestor. In 2010, a formal mathematical test calculated from the genetic record (and in particular the universal use of the same genetic code, same nucleotides, and same amino acids), that the factor in favor of a common ancestor's existence is 10^2489.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<p>Sexual reproduction (<a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">sex</a>) is widespread among current eukaryotes. Dacks and Rogers proposed, on the basis of a phylogenetic analysis, that sex was present in the common ancestor of all eukaryotes. The finding of a core set of genes necessary for meiosis in the descendants of lineages that diverged early form the eukaryotic evolutionary tree also led Ramesh et al. and Malik et al. to suggest that sex was present in a common ancestor of all eukaryotes. Furthermore, this view is supported by evidence that eukaryotes previously regarded as "ancient asexuals", such as amoeba, were likely sexual in the past, and that most present day asexual amoeboid lineages likely arose recently and independently.</p>
<p>In prokaryotes, <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)#Natural_transformation" title="Transformation (genetics)">natural bacterial transformation</a> involves the transfer of DNA from one bacterium to another and integration of the donor DNA into the recipient chromosome by recombination. <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)#Transformation.2C_as_an_adaptation_for_DNA_repair" title="Transformation (genetics)">Natural bacterial transformation</a> is considered to be a primitive sexual process and occurs in both bacteria and archaea, although it has been studied mainly in bacteria. Transformation is clearly a bacterial adaptation and not an accidental occurrence, because it depends on numerous gene products that specifically interact with each other to enter a state of <a href="/wiki/Natural_competence" title="Natural competence">natural competence</a> to perform this complex process. Transformation is a common mode of DNA transfer, and over 60 prokaryotic species are known to be naturally competent for transformation.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_evolution" title="Timeline of evolution">Timeline of evolution</a></div>
<p>The chemical evolution from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">self-catalytic chemical reactions</a> to life (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">Origin of life</a>) is not a part of biological evolution, but it is unclear at which point such increasingly complex sets of reactions became what we would consider, today, to be living organisms.</p>
<p>Little is known about the earliest developments in life. However, all existing organisms share certain traits, including cellular structure and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>. Most scientists interpret this to mean all existing organisms share a common ancestor, which had already developed the most fundamental cellular processes, but there is no <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> on the relationship of the three domains of life (Archaea, Bacteria, Eukaryota) or the origin of life. Attempts to shed light on the earliest history of life generally focus on the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a>, particularly RNA, and the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex systems</a>.</p>

<p>The emergence of oxygenic <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> (around 3 billion years ago) and the subsequent emergence of an oxygen-rich, non-reducing atmosphere can be traced through the formation of <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron</a> deposits, and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_bed" title="Red bed">red beds</a> of iron oxides. This was a necessary prerequisite for the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, believed to have emerged around 2 billion years ago.</p>
<p>In the last billion years, simple multicellular plants and animals began to appear in the oceans. Soon after the emergence of the first animals, the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a> (a period of unrivaled and remarkable, but brief, organismal diversity documented in the fossils found at the <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a>) saw the creation of all the major body plans, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylum_(biology)" title="Phylum (biology)">phyla</a>, of modern animals. This event is now believed to have been triggered by the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hox_genes" title="Hox genes">Hox genes</a>. About 500 million years ago, plants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> colonized the land, and were soon followed by <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> and other animals, leading to the development of today's land <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.</p>
<p>The evolutionary process may be exceedingly slow. Fossil evidence indicates that the diversity and complexity of modern life has developed over much of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">history of the earth</a>. Geological evidence indicates that the Earth is approximately <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">4.6 billion years old</a>. Studies on guppies by David Reznick at the University of California, Riverside, however, have shown that the rate of evolution through natural selection can proceed 10 thousand to 10 million times faster than what is indicated in the fossil record. Such comparative studies however are invariably biased by disparities in the time scales over which evolutionary change is measured in the laboratory, field experiments, and the fossil record.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_gene_transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</a></div>
<p>The ancestry of living organisms has traditionally been reconstructed from morphology, but is increasingly supplemented with phylogenetics—the reconstruction of phylogenies by the comparison of genetic (DNA) sequence.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Sequence comparisons suggest recent <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal transfer</a> of many genes among diverse <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> including across the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> "domains". Thus determining the phylogenetic history of a species can not be done conclusively by determining evolutionary trees for single genes.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Biologist Gogarten suggests "the original metaphor of a tree no longer fits the data from recent genome research", therefore "biologists (should) use the metaphor of a mosaic to describe the different histories combined in individual genomes and use (the) metaphor of a net to visualize the rich exchange and cooperative effects of HGT among microbes."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_life_.28cloning_and_synthetic_organisms.29">Future of life (cloning and synthetic organisms)</span></h3>
<p>In modern terms, the category of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organism_cloning" title="Organism cloning">organism cloning</a> refers to the procedure of creating a new multicellular organism, genetically identical to another. However, the techniques used in cloning have the potential of creating entirely new species of organisms. Organism cloning is the subject of much ethical debate. (see <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">Bioethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_cloning" title="Ethics of cloning">Ethics of cloning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Designer_baby" title="Designer baby">Designer baby</a> articles)</p>
<p>In 2008 the <a href="/wiki/J._Craig_Venter_Institute" title="J. Craig Venter Institute">J. Craig Venter Institute</a> assembled a synthetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">bacterial</a> <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_genitalium" title="Mycoplasma genitalium">Mycoplasma genitalium</a></i>, by using recombination in yeast of 25 overlapping DNA fragments in a single step. <i>The use of yeast recombination greatly simplifies the assembly of large DNA molecules from both synthetic and natural fragments.</i> Other companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Genomics" title="Synthetic Genomics">Synthetic Genomics</a>, have already been formed to take advantage of the many commercial uses of custom designed genomes.</p>
