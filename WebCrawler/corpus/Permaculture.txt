<p><b>Permaculture</b> is a system of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> and social design principles centered on simulating or directly utilizing the patterns and features observed in natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. Permaculture was developed, and the term coined by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Mollison" title="Bill Mollison">Bill Mollison</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Holmgren" title="David Holmgren">David Holmgren</a> in 1978.</p>
<p>It has many branches that include but are not limited to <a href="/wiki/Ecological_design" title="Ecological design">ecological design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_engineering" title="Ecological engineering">ecological engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_design" title="Environmental design">environmental design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_water_resources_management" title="Integrated water resources management">integrated water resources management</a> that develops <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a>, and regenerative and self-maintained <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> and agricultural systems modeled from natural ecosystems.</p>
<p>Mollison has said: "Permaculture is a philosophy of working with, rather than against nature; of protracted and thoughtful observation rather than protracted and thoughtless labor; and of looking at plants and animals in all their functions, rather than treating any area as a <a href="/wiki/Monocropping" title="Monocropping">single product system</a>."</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>In 1929, Joseph Russell Smith took up an antecedent term as the subtitle for <i>Tree Crops: A Permanent Agriculture</i>, a book in which he summed up his long experience experimenting with fruits and nuts as crops for human food and animal feed. Smith saw the world as an inter-related whole and suggested mixed systems of trees and crops underneath. This book inspired many individuals intent on making agriculture more sustainable, such as <a href="/wiki/Toyohiko_Kagawa" title="Toyohiko Kagawa">Toyohiko Kagawa</a> who pioneered <a href="/wiki/Forest_farming" title="Forest farming">forest farming</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in the 1930s.</p>
<p>The definition of permanent agriculture as that which can be sustained indefinitely was supported by Australian <a href="/wiki/P._A._Yeomans" title="P. A. Yeomans">P. A. Yeomans</a> in his 1964 book <i>Water for Every Farm</i>. Yeomans introduced an observation-based approach to land use in Australia in the 1940s, and the <a href="/wiki/Keyline_design" title="Keyline design">keyline design</a> as a way of managing the supply and distribution of water in the 1950s.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stewart_Brand" title="Stewart Brand">Stewart Brand</a>’s works were an early influence noted by Holmgren. Other early influences include <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Stout" title="Ruth Stout">Ruth Stout</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esther_Deans" title="Esther Deans">Esther Deans</a>, who pioneered <a href="/wiki/No-dig_gardening" title="No-dig gardening">no-dig gardening</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Masanobu_Fukuoka" title="Masanobu Fukuoka">Masanobu Fukuoka</a> who, in the late 1930s in Japan, began advocating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No-till" title="No-till">no-till</a> orchards, gardens, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_farming" title="Natural farming">natural farming</a>.</p>

<p>In the late 1960s, Bill Mollison and David Holmgren started developing ideas about stable agricultural systems on the southern Australian island state of <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>. This was a result of the danger of the rapidly growing use of industrial-agricultural methods. In their view,  these methods were highly dependent on non-renewable resources, and were additionally poisoning land and water, reducing <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and removing billions of tons of <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a> from previously fertile landscapes. A design approach called <i>permaculture</i> was their response and was first made public with the publication of their book <i>Permaculture One</i> in 1978.</p>
<p>By the early 1980s, the concept had broadened from agricultural systems design towards <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_habitat" title="Sustainable habitat">sustainable human habitats</a>. After <i>Permaculture One</i>, Mollison further refined and developed the ideas by designing hundreds of permaculture sites and writing more detailed books, notably <i>Permaculture: A Designers Manual</i>. Mollison lectured in over 80 countries and taught his two-week Permaculture Design Course (PDC) to many hundreds of students. Mollison "encouraged graduates to become teachers themselves and set up their own institutes and demonstration sites. This multiplier effect was critical to permaculture’s rapid expansion."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_tenets_and_principles_of_design">Core tenets and principles of design</span></h2>
<p>The three core tenets of permaculture are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Care for the earth</b></i>: Provision for all life systems to continue and multiply. This is the first principle, because without a healthy earth, <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> cannot flourish.</li>
<li><i><b>Care for the people</b></i>: Provision for people to access those resources necessary for their existence.</li>
<li><i><b>Return of surplus</b></i>: Reinvesting surpluses back into the system to provide for the first two ethics. This includes returning waste back into the system to recycle into usefulness. The third ethic is sometimes referred to as Fair Share to reflect that each of us should take no more than what we need before we reinvest the surplus.</li>
</ul>
<p>Permaculture design emphasizes patterns of <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a>, function, and species assemblies. It determines where these elements should be placed so they can provide maximum benefit to the local environment. The central concept of permaculture is maximizing useful connections between components and <a href="/wiki/Synergy" title="Synergy">synergy</a> of the final design. The focus of permaculture, therefore, is not on each separate element, but rather on the relationships created among elements by the way they are placed together; the whole becoming <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">greater than the sum of its parts</a>. Permaculture design therefore seeks to minimize <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>, human labor, and energy input by building systems with maximal benefits between design elements to achieve a high level of <a href="/wiki/Synergy" title="Synergy">synergy</a>. Permaculture designs evolve over time by taking into account these relationships and elements and can become extremely complex systems that produce a high density of food and materials with minimal input.</p>
<p>The design principles which are the conceptual foundation of permaculture were derived from the science of <a href="/wiki/Systems_ecology" title="Systems ecology">systems ecology</a> and study of pre-industrial examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> land use. Permaculture draws from several disciplines including <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_farming" title="Integrated farming">integrated farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Applied_ecology" title="Applied ecology">applied ecology</a>. Permaculture has been applied most commonly to the design of housing and landscaping, integrating techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_building" title="Natural building">natural building</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">rainwater harvesting</a> within the context of permaculture design principles and theory.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twelve_design_principles">Twelve design principles</span></h3>
<p>Twelve Permaculture design principles articulated by David Holmgren in his <i>Permaculture: Principles and Pathways Beyond Sustainability</i>:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Observe and interact</i>: By taking time to engage with nature we can design solutions that suit our particular situation.</li>
<li><i>Catch and store energy</i>: By developing systems that collect resources at peak abundance, we can use them in times of need.</li>
<li><i>Obtain a yield</i>: Ensure that you are getting truly useful rewards as part of the work that you are doing.</li>
<li><i>Apply self-regulation and accept feedback</i>: We need to discourage inappropriate activity to ensure that systems can continue to function well.</li>
<li><i>Use and value <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable" title="Renewable">renewable</a> resources and services</i>: Make the best use of nature's abundance to reduce our consumptive behavior and dependence on non-renewable resources.</li>
<li><i>Produce no waste</i>: By valuing and making use of all the resources that are available to us, nothing goes to waste.</li>
<li><i>Design from patterns to details</i>: By stepping back, we can observe patterns in nature and society. These can form the backbone of our designs, with the details filled in as we go.</li>
<li><i>Integrate rather than segregate</i>: By putting the right things in the right place, relationships develop between those things and they work together to support each other.</li>
<li><i>Use small and slow solutions</i>: Small and slow systems are easier to maintain than big ones, making better use of local resources and producing more sustainable outcomes.</li>
<li><i>Use and value diversity</i>: Diversity reduces vulnerability to a variety of threats and takes advantage of the unique nature of the environment in which it resides.</li>
<li><i>Use edges and value the marginal</i>: The interface between things is where the most interesting events take place. These are often the most valuable, diverse and productive elements in the system.</li>
<li><i>Creatively use and respond to change</i>: We can have a positive impact on inevitable change by carefully observing, and then intervening at the right time.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers">Layers</span></h3>

<p>Layers are one of the tools used to design functional ecosystems that are both sustainable and of direct benefit to humans. A mature ecosystem has a huge number of relationships between its component parts: trees, <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">understory</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_cover" title="Ground cover">ground cover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, insects, and animals. Because plants grow to different heights, a diverse community of life is able to grow in a relatively small space, as the vegetation occupies different layers. There are generally seven recognized layers in a <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">food forest</a>, although some practitioners also include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> as an eighth layer.</p>
<ol>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopy</a>: the tallest trees in the system. Large trees dominate but typically do not saturate the area, i.e. there exist patches barren of trees.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">Understory</a> layer: trees that revel in the dappled light under the canopy.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrub_layer" title="Shrub layer">Shrub layer</a>: a diverse layer of woody perennials of limited height. includes most berry bushes.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous_layer" title="Herbaceous layer">Herbaceous layer</a>: Plants in this layer die back to the ground every winter (if winters are cold enough, that is). They do not produce woody stems as the Shrub layer does. Many culinary and medicinal herbs are in this layer. A large variety of beneficial plants fall into this layer. May be annuals, biennials or perennials.</li>
<li>Soil surface/<a href="/wiki/Groundcover" title="Groundcover">Groundcover</a>: There is some overlap with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous_layer" title="Herbaceous layer">Herbaceous layer</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groundcover_layer" title="Groundcover layer">Groundcover layer</a>; however plants in this layer grow much closer to the ground, grow densely to fill bare patches of soil, and often can tolerate some foot traffic. <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">Cover crops</a> retain soil and lessen <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manures</a> that add nutrients and <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> to the soil, especially <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhizosphere_(ecology)" title="Rhizosphere (ecology)">Rhizosphere</a>: Root layers within the soil. The major components of this layer are the soil and the organisms that live within it such as plant roots (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_crop" title="Root crop">root crops</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> and other edible <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>), fungi, insects, nematodes, worms, etc.</li>
<li>Vertical layer: climbers or <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runner_bean" title="Runner bean">runner beans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lima_bean" title="Lima bean">lima beans</a> (vine varieties).</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guilds">Guilds</span></h3>
<p>There are many forms of guilds, including guilds of plants with similar functions (that could interchange within an ecosystem), but the most common perception is that of a mutual support guild. Such a <a href="/wiki/Guild_(ecology)" title="Guild (ecology)">guild</a> is a group of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> where each provides a unique set of diverse functions that work in conjunction, or harmony. Mutual support guilds are groups of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, etc. that work well together. Some plants may be grown for food production, some have tap roots that draw nutrients up from deep in the soil, some are nitrogen-fixing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legumes" title="Legumes">legumes</a>, some attract <a href="/wiki/Beneficial_insects" title="Beneficial insects">beneficial insects</a>, and others repel harmful insects. When grouped together in a mutually beneficial arrangement, these plants form a guild. See Dave Jacke's work on edible forest gardens for more information on other guilds, specifically resource-partitioning and community-function guilds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edge_effect">Edge effect</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_effect" title="Edge effect">edge effect</a> in ecology is the effect of the juxtaposition or placing side by side of contrasting <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environments</a> on an ecosystem. Permaculturists argue that, where vastly differing systems meet, there is an intense area of productivity and useful connections. An example of this is the coast; where the land and the sea meet there is a particularly rich area that meets a disproportionate percentage of human and animal needs. So this idea is played out in permacultural designs by using spirals in the herb garden or creating ponds that have wavy undulating shorelines rather than a simple circle or oval (thereby increasing the amount of edge for a given area).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zones">Zones</span></h3>

<p>Zones are a way of intelligently organizing design elements in a human environment on the basis of the frequency of human use and plant or animal needs. Frequently manipulated or harvested elements of the design are located close to the house in zones 1 and 2. Less frequently used or manipulated elements, and elements that benefit from isolation (such as wild species) are farther away. Zones are about positioning things appropriately, and are numbered from 0 to 5.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="People_and_permaculture">People and permaculture</span></h3>
<p>Permaculture uses observation of nature to create regenerative systems, and the place where this has been most visible has been on the landscape. There has been a growing awareness though that firstly, there is the need to pay more attention to the peoplecare ethic, as it is often the dynamics of people that can interfere with projects, and secondly that the principles of permaculture can be used as effectively to create vibrant, healthy and productive people and communities as they have been in landscapes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domesticated_animals">Domesticated animals</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">Domesticated animals</a> are often incorporated into site design.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_practices">Common practices</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agroforestry">Agroforestry</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">Agroforestry</a> is an integrated approach of using the interactive benefits from combining trees and shrubs with crops and/or livestock. It combines agricultural and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> technologies to create more diverse, productive, profitable, healthy and sustainable land-use systems. In agroforestry systems, trees or shrubs are intentionally used within agricultural systems, or <a href="/wiki/Non-timber_forest_product" title="Non-timber forest product">non-timber forest products</a> are cultured in forest settings.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">Forest gardening</a> is a term permaculturalists use to describe systems designed to mimic natural forests. Forest gardens, like other permaculture designs, incorporate processes and relationships that the designers understand to be valuable in natural ecosystems. The terms forest garden and food forest are used interchangeably in the permaculture literature. Numerous permaculturists are proponents of forest gardens, such as Graham Bell, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Whitefield" title="Patrick Whitefield">Patrick Whitefield</a>, Dave Jacke, Eric Toensmeier and <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Lawton" title="Geoff Lawton">Geoff Lawton</a>. Bell started building his forest garden in 1991 and wrote the book <i>The Permaculture Garden</i> in 1995, Whitefield wrote the book <i>How to Make a Forest Garden</i> in 2002, Jacke and Toensmeier co-authored the two volume book set <i>Edible Forest Gardening</i> in 2005, and Lawton presented the film <i>Establishing a Food Forest</i> in 2008.</p>
<p>Tree Gardens, such as Kandyan tree gardens, in South and Southeast Asia, are often hundreds of years old. Whether they derived initially from experiences of cultivation and forestry, as is the case in agroforestry, or whether they derived from an understanding of forest ecosystems, as is the case for permaculture systems, is not self-evident. Many studies of these systems, especially those that predate the term permaculture, consider these systems to be forms of agroforestry. Permaculturalists who include existing and ancient systems of polycropping with woody species as examples of food forests may obscure the distinction between permaculture and agroforestry.</p>
<p>Food forests and agroforestry are parallel approaches that sometimes lead to similar designs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="H.C3.BCgelkultur">Hügelkultur</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCgelkultur" title="Hügelkultur">Hügelkultur</a> is the practice of burying large volumes of wood to increase soil water retention. The porous structure of wood acts as a sponge when decomposing underground. During the rainy season, masses of buried wood can absorb enough water to sustain crops through the dry season. This technique has been used by permaculturalists <a href="/wiki/Sepp_Holzer" title="Sepp Holzer">Sepp Holzer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toby_Hemenway" title="Toby Hemenway">Toby Hemenway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wheaton" title="Paul Wheaton">Paul Wheaton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Masanobu_Fukuoka" title="Masanobu Fukuoka">Masanobu Fukuoka</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_building">Natural building</span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Natural_building" title="Natural building">natural building</a> involves a range of <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> systems and materials that place major emphasis on sustainability. Ways of achieving sustainability through natural building focus on durability and the use of minimally processed, plentiful or <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a>, as well as those that, while <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycled</a> or salvaged, produce healthy living environments and maintain indoor air quality.</p>
<p>The basis of natural building is the need to lessen the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> impact of buildings and other supporting systems, without sacrificing comfort, health, or <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>. To be more sustainable, natural building uses primarily abundantly available, renewable, reused, or recycled materials. In addition to relying on natural building materials, the emphasis on the architectural design is heightened. The orientation of a building, the utilization of local climate and site conditions, the emphasis on natural ventilation through design, fundamentally lessen operational costs and positively impact the environment. Building compactly and minimizing the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> is common, as are on-site handling of energy acquisition, on-site water capture, alternate sewage treatment, and water reuse.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainwater_harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a> is the accumulating and storing of rainwater for reuse before it reaches the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>. It has been used to provide <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, water for <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, water for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, as well as other typical uses. Rainwater collected from the roofs of houses and local institutions can make an important contribution to the availability of drinking water. It can supplement the subsoil water level and increase urban greenery. Water collected from the ground, sometimes from areas which are especially prepared for this purpose, is called <a href="/wiki/Stormwater_harvesting" title="Stormwater harvesting">stormwater harvesting</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">Greywater</a> is <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> generated from domestic activities such as laundry, dishwashing, and bathing, which can be recycled on-site for uses such as landscape irrigation and <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">constructed wetlands</a>. Greywater is largely sterile, but not potable (drinkable). Greywater differs from water from the toilets, which is designated <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_(waste)" title="Blackwater (waste)">blackwater</a> to indicate it contains <a href="/wiki/Human_waste" title="Human waste">human waste</a>. Blackwater is septic or otherwise toxic and cannot easily be reused. There are, however, continuing efforts to make use of blackwater or human waste. The most notable is for composting through a process known as humanure; a combination of the words human and manure. Additionally, the methane in humanure can be collected and used similar to natural gas as a fuel, such as for heating or cooking, and is commonly referred to as biogas. Biogas can be harvested from the human waste and the remainder still used as humanure. Some of the simplest forms of humanure use include a composting toilet or an outhouse or dry bog surrounded by trees that are heavy feeders which can be coppiced for wood fuel. This process eliminates the use of a standard toilet with plumbing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sheet_mulching">Sheet mulching</span></h3>
<p>In agriculture and <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a>, mulch is a protective cover placed over the soil. Any material or combination can be used as mulch, such as stones, leaves, cardboard, wood chips, gravel, etc., though in permaculture mulches of organic material are the most common because they perform more functions. These include absorbing rainfall, reducing evaporation, providing nutrients, increasing organic matter in the soil, feeding and creating habitat for soil organisms, suppressing weed growth and seed germination, moderating diurnal temperature swings, protecting against frost, and reducing erosion. <a href="/wiki/Sheet_mulching" title="Sheet mulching">Sheet mulching</a> is an agricultural no-dig gardening technique that attempts to mimic natural processes occurring within <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>. Sheet mulching mimics the leaf cover that is found on forest floors. When deployed properly and in combination with other Permacultural principles, it can generate healthy, productive and low maintenance ecosystems.</p>
<p>Sheet mulch serves as a "nutrient bank," storing the nutrients contained in organic matter and slowly making these nutrients available to plants as the organic matter slowly and naturally breaks down. It also improves the soil by attracting and feeding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthworms" title="Earthworms">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">slaters</a> and many other soil micro-organisms, as well as adding <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>. Earthworms "till" the soil, and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worm_castings" title="Worm castings">worm castings</a> are among the best fertilizers and <a href="/wiki/Soil_conditioner" title="Soil conditioner">soil conditioners</a>. Sheet mulching can be used to reduce or eliminate undesirable plants by starving them of light, and can be more advantageous than using <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a> or other methods of control.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensive_rotational_grazing">Intensive rotational grazing</span></h3>
<p>Grazing has long been blamed for much of the destruction we see in the environment. However, it has been shown that when grazing is modeled after nature, the opposite effect can be seen. Also known as cell grazing, <a href="/wiki/Managed_intensive_rotational_grazing" title="Managed intensive rotational grazing">managed intensive rotational grazing</a> (MIRG) is a system of grazing in which ruminant and non-ruminant herds and/or flocks are regularly and systematically moved to fresh pasture, range, or forest with the intent to maximize the quality and quantity of forage growth. This disturbance is then followed by a period of rest which allows new growth. MIRG can be used with cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, chickens, rabbits, geese, turkeys, ducks, and other animals depending on the natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_community" title="Ecological community">ecological community</a> that is being mimicked. <a href="/wiki/Sepp_Holzer" title="Sepp Holzer">Sepp Holzer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joel_Salatin" title="Joel Salatin">Joel Salatin</a> have shown how the disturbance caused by the animals can be the spark needed to start <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">ecological succession</a> or prepare ground for planting. <a href="/wiki/Allan_Savory" title="Allan Savory">Allan Savory</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic_management" title="Holistic management">holistic management</a> technique has been likened to "a permaculture approach to <a href="/wiki/Rangeland_management" title="Rangeland management">rangeland management</a>". One variation on MIRG that is gaining rapid popularity is called eco-grazing. Often used to either control invasives or re-establish native species, in eco-grazing the primary purpose of the animals is to benefit the environment and the animals can be, but are not necessarily, used for meat, milk or fiber.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keyline_design">Keyline design</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Keyline_design" title="Keyline design">Keyline design</a> is a technique for maximizing beneficial use of water resources of a piece of land developed in Australia by farmer and engineer <a href="/wiki/P._A._Yeomans" title="P. A. Yeomans">P. A. Yeomans</a>. The <i>Keyline</i> refers to a specific <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographic feature</a> linked to water flow which is used in designing the drainage system of the site.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fruit_tree_management">Fruit tree management</span></h3>
<p>Some proponents of permaculture advocate no, or limited, pruning. One advocate of this approach is <a href="/wiki/Sepp_Holzer" title="Sepp Holzer">Sepp Holzer</a> who used the method in connection with <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCgelkultur" title="Hügelkultur">Hügelkultur</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berms" title="Berms">berms</a>. He has successfully grown several varieties of fruiting trees at altitudes (approximately 9,000 feet (2,700 m)) far above their normal altitude, temperature, and snow load ranges. He notes that the Hügelkultur berms kept and/or generated enough heat to allow the roots to survive during alpine winter conditions. The point of having unpruned branches, he notes, was that the longer (more naturally formed) branches bend over under the snow load until they touched the ground, thus forming a natural arch against snow loads that would break a shorter, pruned, branch.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Masanobu_Fukuoka" title="Masanobu Fukuoka">Masanobu Fukuoka</a>, as part of early experiments on his family farm in Japan, experimented with no-pruning methods, noting that he ended up killing many fruit trees by simply letting them go, which made them become convoluted and tangled, and thus unhealthy. Then he realised this is the difference between natural-form fruit trees and the process of change of tree form that results from abandoning previously-pruned unnatural fruit trees. He concluded that the trees should be raised all their lives without pruning, so they form healthy and efficient branch patterns that follow their natural inclination. This is part of his implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">Tao</a>-philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Wu_wei" title="Wu wei">Wú wéi</a> translated in part as no-action (against nature), and he described it as no unnecessary pruning, nature farming or "do-nothing" farming, of fruit trees, distinct from non-intervention or literal no-pruning. He ultimately achieved yields comparable to or exceeding standard/intensive practices of using pruning and chemical fertilisation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trademark_and_copyright_issues">Trademark and copyright issues</span></h2>
<p>There has been contention over who, if anyone, controls legal rights to the word <i>permaculture</i>: is it <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarked</a> or <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyrighted</a>? and if so, who holds the legal rights to the use of the word? For a long time Bill Mollison claimed to have copyrighted the word, and his books said on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copyright_page" title="Copyright page">copyright page</a>, "The contents of this book and the word PERMACULTURE are copyright." These statements were largely accepted at face-value within the permaculture community. However, copyright law does not protect names, ideas, concepts, systems, or methods of doing something; it only protects the expression or the description of an idea, not the idea itself. Eventually Mollison acknowledged that he was mistaken and that no copyright protection existed for the word <i>permaculture</i>.</p>
<p>In 2000, Mollison's US based Permaculture Institute sought a <a href="/wiki/Service_mark" title="Service mark">service mark</a> (a form of trademark) for the word <i>permaculture</i> when used in educational services such as conducting classes, seminars, or workshops. The service mark would have allowed Mollison and his two Permaculture Institutes (one in the US and one in Australia) to set enforceable guidelines regarding how permaculture could be taught and who could teach it, particularly with relation to the PDC, despite the fact that he had instituted a system of certification of teachers to teach the PDC in 1993. The service mark failed and was abandoned in 2001. Also in 2001 Mollison applied for trademarks in Australia for the terms "Permaculture Design Course" and "Permaculture Design". These applications were both withdrawn in 2003. In 2009 he sought a trademark for "Permaculture: A Designers’ Manual" and "Introduction to Permaculture", the names of two of his books. These applications were withdrawn in 2011. There has never been a trademark for the word <i>permaculture</i> in Australia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_criticisms">General criticisms</span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Owen Hablutzel argued that "permaculture has yet to gain a large amount of specific mainstream scientific acceptance," and that "the sensitiveness to being perceived and accepted on scientific terms is motivated in part by a desire for permaculture to expand and become increasingly relevant."</p>
<p>In his books <i>Sustainable Freshwater Aquaculture</i> and <i>Farming in Ponds and Dams</i>, Nick Romanowski expresses the view that the presentation of aquaculture in Bill Mollison's books is unrealistic and misleading.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agroforestry_2">Agroforestry</span></h3>
<p>Greg Williams argues that forests cannot be more productive than farmland because the net productivity of forests decline as they mature due to <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">ecological succession</a>. Proponents of permaculture respond that this is true only if one compares data between woodland forest and climax vegetation, but not when comparing farmland vegetation with woodland forest. For example, ecological succession generally results in a forest's productivity rising after its establishment only until it reaches the <i>woodland state</i> (67% tree cover), before declining until <i>full maturity</i>.</p>
