<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Biosphere_(disambiguation)" title="Biosphere (disambiguation)">Biosphere (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p>The <b>biosphere</b> is the global sum of all <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. The two joined words are "bio" and "sphere". It can also be termed as the zone of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, a closed system (apart from solar and cosmic radiation and heat from the interior of the Earth), and largely self-regulating. By the most general <a href="/wiki/Geophysiology" title="Geophysiology">biophysiological</a> definition, the biosphere is the global <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> system integrating all living beings and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. The biosphere is postulated to have <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a>, beginning with a process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">biopoesis</a> (life created naturally from non-living matter, such as simple organic compounds) or <a href="/wiki/Biogenesis" title="Biogenesis">biogenesis</a> (life created from living matter), at least some 3.5 billion years ago. The earliest evidence for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> found in 3.7 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary rocks</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossils" title="Fossils">fossils</a> found in 3.48 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. More recently, in 2015, "remains of <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic life</a>" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia. According to one of the researchers, "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth ... then it could be common in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>."</p>
<p>In a general sense, biospheres are any closed, self-regulating systems containing ecosystems. This includes artificial biospheres such as <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_2" title="Biosphere 2">Biosphere 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/BIOS-3" title="BIOS-3">BIOS-3</a>, and potentially ones on other planets or moons.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_use_of_the_term">Origin and use of the term</span></h2>

<p>The term "biosphere" was coined by geologist <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">Eduard Suess</a> in 1875, which he defined as the place on Earth's surface where life dwells.</p>
<p>While the concept has a geological origin, it is an indication of the effect of both <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew_F._Maury" title="Matthew F. Maury">Matthew F. Maury</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_sciences" title="Earth sciences">Earth sciences</a>. The biosphere's ecological context comes from the 1920s (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_I._Vernadsky" title="Vladimir I. Vernadsky">Vladimir I. Vernadsky</a>), preceding the 1935 introduction of the term "<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>" by Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tansley" title="Arthur Tansley">Arthur Tansley</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecology_(history)" title="Ecology (history)">ecology history</a>). Vernadsky defined <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> as the science of the biosphere. It is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> concept for integrating <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> and, generally speaking, all life and Earth sciences.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrow_definition">Narrow definition</span></h3>
<p>Geochemists define the biosphere as being the total sum of living organisms (the "<a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>" as referred to by biologists and ecologists). In this sense, the biosphere is but one of four separate components of the geochemical model, the other three being <i><a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a></i>. When these four component spheres are combined into one system, it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Ecosphere_(planetary)" title="Ecosphere (planetary)">Ecosphere</a>. This term was coined during the 1960s and encompasses both biological and physical components of the planet.</p>
<p>The Second International Conference on Closed Life Systems defined <i>biospherics</i> as the science and technology of analogs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_(abstract)" title="Model (abstract)">models</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s biosphere; i.e., artificial Earth-like biospheres. Others may include the creation of artificial non-Earth biospheres—for example, human-centered biospheres or a native <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Martian</a> biosphere—as part of the topic of biospherics.</p>

<p>Every part of the planet, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar</a> ice caps to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, features life of some kind. Recent advances in <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a> have demonstrated that microbes live deep beneath the Earth's terrestrial surface, and that the total mass of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> life in so-called "uninhabitable zones" may, in <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>, exceed all animal and plant life on the surface. The actual thickness of the biosphere on earth is difficult to measure. Birds typically fly at altitudes as high as 1,800 m (5,900 ft; 1.1 mi) and fish live as much as 8,372 m (27,467 ft; 5.202 mi) underwater in the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a>.</p>
<p>There are more extreme examples for life on the planet: <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_vulture" title="Rüppell's vulture">Rüppell's vulture</a> has been found at <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> of 11,300 m (37,100 ft; 7.0 mi); <a href="/wiki/Bar-headed_goose" title="Bar-headed goose">bar-headed geese</a> migrate at altitudes of at least 8,300 m (27,200 ft; 5.2 mi); <a href="/wiki/Yak" title="Yak">yaks</a> live at elevations as high as 5,400 m (17,700 ft; 3.4 mi) above sea level; <a href="/wiki/Mountain_goat" title="Mountain goat">mountain goats</a> live up to 3,050 m (10,010 ft; 1.90 mi). Herbivorous animals at these elevations depend on lichens, grasses, and herbs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microscopic organisms</a> live in every part of the biosphere, including <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a>, "seven miles deep" in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, "40 miles high" in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and inside rocks far down within the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Endolith" title="Endolith">endolith</a>). Microorganisms, under certain test conditions, have been observed to <a href="/wiki/Panspermia#Research_in_outer_space" title="Panspermia">thrive in the vacuum of outer space</a>. The total amount of soil and subsurface bacterial <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is estimated as 5 x 10 g, or the "weight of the United Kingdom". The mass of <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> microorganisms — which includes bacteria and archaea, but not the nucleated <a href="/wiki/Microorganism#Eukaryotes" title="Microorganism">eukaryote microorganisms</a> — may be as much as 0.8 trillion tons of carbon (of the total biosphere <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">mass</a>, estimated at between 1 and 4 trillion tons). <a href="/wiki/Piezophile" title="Piezophile">Barophilic</a> marine microbes have been found at more than a depth of 10,000 m (33,000 ft; 6.2 mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, the deepest spot in the Earth's oceans. In fact, single-celled life forms have been found in the deepest part of the Mariana Trench, by the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>, at depths of 11,034 m (36,201 ft; 6.856 mi). Other researchers reported related studies that microorganisms thrive inside rocks up to 580 m (1,900 ft; 0.36 mi) below the sea floor under 2,590 m (8,500 ft; 1.61 mi) of ocean off the coast of the northwestern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, as well as 2,400 m (7,900 ft; 1.5 mi) beneath the seabed off Japan. Culturable thermophilic microbes have been extracted from cores drilled more than 5,000 m (16,000 ft; 3.1 mi) into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> in Sweden, from rocks between 65–75 °C (149–167 °F). Temperature <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">increases with increasing depth</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>. The rate at which the temperature increases depends on many factors, including type of crust (continental vs. oceanic), rock type, geographic location, etc. The greatest known temperature at which microbial life can exist is 122 °C (252 °F) (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanopyrus_kandleri" title="Methanopyrus kandleri">Methanopyrus kandleri</a></i> Strain 116), and it is likely that the limit of life in the "deep biosphere" is defined by temperature rather than absolute depth. On 20 August 2014, scientists confirmed the existence of microorganisms living 800 m (2,600 ft; 0.50 mi) below the ice of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. According to one researcher, "You can find microbes everywhere — they're extremely adaptable to conditions, and survive wherever they are."</p>
<p>Our biosphere is divided into a number of <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a>, inhabited by fairly similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fauna_(animals)" title="Fauna (animals)">fauna</a>. On land, biomes are separated primarily by <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. Terrestrial biomes lying within the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circles</a> are relatively barren of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life, while most of the more populous biomes lie near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_biospheres">Specific biospheres</span></h2>
<p>For this list, if a word is followed by a number, it is usually referring to a specific system or number. Thus:</p>
<ul>
<li>Biosphere 1, the planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biosphere_2" title="Biosphere 2">Biosphere 2</a>, laboratory in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, United States, which contains 3.15 acres (13,000 m) of closed ecosystem.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BIOS-3" title="BIOS-3">BIOS-3</a>, a closed ecosystem at the Institute of Biophysics in <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk" title="Krasnoyarsk">Krasnoyarsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, in what was then the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.</li>
<li>Biosphere J (CEEF, Closed Ecology Experiment Facilities), an experiment in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_biospheres">Extraterrestrial biospheres</span></h2>
<p>No biospheres have been detected beyond the Earth; therefore, the existence of extraterrestrial biospheres remains hypothetical. The <a href="/wiki/Rare_Earth_hypothesis" title="Rare Earth hypothesis">rare Earth hypothesis</a> suggests they should be very rare, save ones composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbial</a> life only. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Earth_analog" title="Earth analog">Earth analogs</a> may be quite numerous, at least in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy. Given limited understanding of <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>, it is currently unknown what percentage of these planets actually develop biospheres.</p>
<p>It is also possible that artificial biospheres will be created during the future, for example on <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars" title="Terraforming of Mars">Mars</a>. The process of creating an uncontained system that mimics the function of Earth's biosphere is called <a href="/wiki/Terraforming" title="Terraforming">terraforming</a>.</p>
