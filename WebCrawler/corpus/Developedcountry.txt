<p>A <b>developed country</b>, <b>industrialized country</b>, or "<b>more economically developed country</b>" (<b>MEDC</b>), is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> that has a highly developed economy and advanced technological infrastructure relative to other less industrialized nations. Most commonly, the criteria for evaluating the degree of economic development are <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP), <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" title="Gross national product">gross national product</a> (GNP), the <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a>, level of industrialization, amount of widespread infrastructure and general standard of living. Which criteria are to be used and which countries can be classified as being developed are subjects of debate.</p>
<p>Developed countries have <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial</a> economies, meaning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_sector" title="Service sector">service sector</a> provides more wealth than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_sector" title="Industrial sector">industrial sector</a>. They are contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, which are in the process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, or undeveloped countries, which are pre-industrial and almost entirely <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agrarian</a>. As of 2015, advanced economies comprise 60.8% of global GDP based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominal_value" title="Nominal value">nominal values</a> and 42.9% of global GDP based on <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing-power parity</a> (PPP) according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>. In 2015, the ten largest advanced economies by GDP in both nominal and PPP terms were <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_terms">Similar terms</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/North%E2%80%93South_divide" title="North–South divide">North–South divide</a></div>
<p>Terms similar to <i>developed country</i> include "advanced country", "industrialized country", "'more developed country" (MDC), "more economically developed country" (MEDC), "Global North country", "first world country", and "post-industrial country". The term industrialized country may be somewhat ambiguous, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> is an ongoing process that is hard to define. The first industrialized country was the United Kingdom, followed by Belgium. Later it spread further to Germany, United States, France and other <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western European</a> countries. According to some <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Sachs" title="Jeffrey Sachs">Jeffrey Sachs</a>, however, the current divide between the developed and developing world is largely a phenomenon of the 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_criteria">Definition and criteria</span></h2>
<p>Economic criteria have tended to dominate discussions. One such criterion is income per capita; countries with high <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) per capita would thus be described as developed countries. Another economic criterion is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>; countries in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_industry" title="Tertiary sector of industry">tertiary</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_industry" title="Quaternary sector of industry">quaternary sectors of industry</a> dominate would thus be described as developed. More recently another measure, the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI), which combines an economic measure, national income, with other measures, indices for life expectancy and education has become prominent. This criterion would define developed countries as those with a very high (HDI) rating.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_Development_Index_.28HDI.29">Human Development Index (HDI)</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_Human_Development_Index" title="List of countries by Human Development Index">List of countries by Human Development Index</a></div>
<p>The UN HDI is a statistical measure that gauges a country's level of human development. While there is a strong correlation between having a high HDI score and a prosperous economy, the UN points out that the HDI accounts for more than income or productivity. Unlike GDP per capita or per capita income, the HDI takes into account how income is turned "into education and health opportunities and therefore into higher levels of human development."</p>
<p>Since 1990, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> (2001–2006, 2009–2013), <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (1990–91 and 1993), <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (1992 and 1994–2000) and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> (2007–2008) have had the highest HDI score. The top 47 countries have scores ranging from 0.793 in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> to 0.955 in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.</p>
<p>Many countries listed by IMF or CIA as "advanced" (as of 2009), possess an HDI over 0.788 (as of 2010). Many countries possessing an HDI of 0.788 and over (as of 2010) are also listed by IMF or CIA as "advanced" (as of 2009). Thus, many "advanced economies" (as of 2009) are characterized by an HDI score of 0.9 or higher (as of 2007). Since April 2016, the IMF classifies Macau as an advanced economy.</p>

<p>As a non-UN member, the government of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> calculates its own HDI, which had a value of 0.882 in 2011. Additionally, while the HDI for the Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_administrative_region" title="Special administrative region">special administrative region</a> of Hong Kong is calculated by the UN, it is not for <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>. The Macanese government calculated the territory's HDI to be 0.868 in 2011. These values place both Taiwan and Macau well within the list of countries with "Very high human development". Furthermore, in 2009 a United Nations project calculated the HDI for all of its members, as well as Taiwan, Macau, and many <a href="/wiki/Dependent_territory" title="Dependent territory">dependent territories</a>. The HDI values for the countries of <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">San Marino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, which have not been included in official annual HDI reports, were found to be at 0.961 and 0.956 respectively. This places both countries firmly within the category of countries with "Very high human development" as well. The dependent territories with HDI values equivalent to "Very high human development" were: <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>. Of note, the HDI values in the 2009 report were calculated using the old HDI formula, while HDI values after the year 2010 are calculated with a different formula.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Net_take-home_pay_of_OECD_members">Net take-home pay of OECD members</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_net_take-home_pay" title="List of countries by net take-home pay">List of countries by net take-home pay</a></div>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita">GDP per capita</a> is often used to measure how developed a country is, it includes components that do not contribute to a citizen's standard of living. GDP per capita may increase while real incomes for the majority decline. However, measuring only <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_net_take-home_pay" title="List of countries by net take-home pay">net take-home pay</a> gives a more accurate picture of a country's standard of living. The tables show net income figures published annually in the OECD Tax Database. They reflect the annual augmented total net take-home pay, by family-type, deducting taxes and non-tax compulsory payments. Since <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">PPP</a> conversion is a widely accepted way to compare income, the OECD publishes the data in USD PPPs. The two major family-types, married with 2 children and single with no child at 100% average wage have been selected to give an accurate picture of the wage levels.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_lists_of_developed_countries">Other lists of developed countries</span></h2>
<p>Some institutions have produced lists of developed countries: the UN (list shown above), the CIA, and some providers of stock market indices(the FTSE Group, MSCI, S&amp;P, Dow Jones, etc.). The latter is not included here because its association of developed countries with countries with both high incomes and <a href="/wiki/Developed_market" title="Developed market">developed markets</a> is not deemed as directly relevant.</p>
<p>However many other institutions have created more general lists referred to when discussing developed countries. For example, the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> (IMF) identifies 39 "advanced economies". The <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">OECD</a>'s 35 members are known as the "developed countries club" The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> identifies 79 "high income countries".</p>
