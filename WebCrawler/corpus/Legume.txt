<p>A <b>legume</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>/<a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a> (or Leguminosae). Legumes are grown agriculturally, primarily for their <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> seed called <b>pulse</b>, for <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a>, and as soil-enhancing <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a>. Well-known legumes include <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lupin_bean" title="Lupin bean">lupin bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesquite" title="Mesquite">mesquite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carob" title="Carob">carob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a>.</p>
<p>A legume fruit is a <a href="/wiki/Fruit#Simple_fruit" title="Fruit">simple dry fruit</a> that develops from a simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpel" title="Carpel">carpel</a> and usually <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)" title="Dehiscence (botany)">dehisces</a> (opens along a seam) on two sides. A common name for this type of fruit is a <i>pod</i>, although the term "pod" is also applied to a few other fruit types, such as that of <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Capsule_(botany)" title="Capsule (botany)">capsule</a>) and of <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radish</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Silique" title="Silique">silique</a>).</p>
<p>Legumes are notable in that most of them have symbiotic <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing</a> bacteria in structures called <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">root nodules</a>. For that reason, they play a key role in <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bean#Terminology" title="Bean">Bean § Terminology</a></div>
<p>The term "pulse", as used by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), is reserved for <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> harvested solely for the dry seed. This excludes <a href="/wiki/Green_bean" title="Green bean">green beans</a> and green peas, which are considered <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetable</a> crops. Also excluded are seeds that are mainly grown for <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">oil</a> extraction (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oilseed" title="Oilseed">oilseeds</a> like <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>), and seeds which are used exclusively for sowing <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> (<a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clovers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a>). However, in common <a href="/wiki/Usage" title="Usage">usage</a>, these distinctions are not always clearly made, and many of the varieties used for dried pulses are also used for green vegetables, with their beans in pods while young.</p>
<p>Some Fabaceae, such as <a href="/wiki/Cytisus_scoparius" title="Cytisus scoparius">Scotch broom</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Genisteae" title="Genisteae">Genisteae</a>, are leguminous but are usually not called legumes by farmers, who tend to restrict that term to food crops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<p>Farmed legumes can belong to many agricultural classes, including <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grain</a>, blooms, pharmaceutical/industrial, fallow/green manure, and timber species. Most commercially farmed species fill two or more roles simultaneously, depending upon their degree of maturity when harvested.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_consumption">Human consumption</span></h2>

<p>Grain legumes are cultivated for their seeds. The seeds are used for human and animal consumption or for the production of oils for industrial uses. Grain legumes include <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupin" title="Lupin">lupins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_value">Nutritional value</span></h3>
<p>Legumes are a significant source of <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_minerals" title="Dietary minerals">dietary minerals</a>; for example, a 100 gram serving of cooked <a href="/wiki/Chickpea" title="Chickpea">chickpeas</a> contains 18% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Value" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a> (DV) for protein, 30% DV for dietary fiber, 43% DV for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folate" title="Folate">folate</a> and 52% DV for <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>. Like other plant-based foods, pulses contain no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_cholesterol" title="Dietary cholesterol">cholesterol</a> and little fat or sodium.</p>
<p>Legumes are also an excellent source of <a href="/wiki/Resistant_starch" title="Resistant starch">resistant starch</a> which is broken down by <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">bacteria</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">short-chain fatty acids</a> used by intestinal cells for <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a>.</p>
<p>Preliminary studies in humans include the potential for regular consumption of legumes in a <a href="/wiki/Plant-based_diet" title="Plant-based diet">vegetarian diet</a> to affect <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_syndrome" title="Metabolic syndrome">metabolic syndrome</a>. There is evidence that a portion of pulses (roughly one cup daily) in a diet may help lower blood pressure and reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDL_cholesterol" title="LDL cholesterol">LDL cholesterol</a> levels, though there is a concern about the quality of the supporting data.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forage">Forage</span></h2>

<p>Forage legumes are of two broad types. Some, like <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>, vetch (<i><a href="/wiki/Vicia" title="Vicia">Vicia</a></i>), stylo (<i><a href="/wiki/Stylosanthes" title="Stylosanthes">Stylosanthes</a></i>), or <i><a href="/wiki/Arachis" title="Arachis">Arachis</a></i>, are sown in <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> and grazed by livestock. Other forage legumes such as <i><a href="/wiki/Leucaena" title="Leucaena">Leucaena</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Albizia" title="Albizia">Albizia</a></i> are woody shrub or tree species that are either broken down by livestock or regularly cut by humans to provide livestock feed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h2>

<p>Legume species grown for their flowers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupin" title="Lupin">lupins</a>, which are farmed commercially for their blooms as well as being popular in gardens worldwide. Industrially farmed legumes include <i><a href="/wiki/Indigofera" title="Indigofera">Indigofera</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i> species, which are cultivated for <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gum" title="Natural gum">natural gum</a> production, respectively. Fallow/green manure legume species are cultivated to be tilled back into the soil in order to exploit the high levels of captured atmospheric nitrogen found in the roots of most legumes. Numerous legumes farmed for this purpose include <i>Leucaena</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cyamopsis" title="Cyamopsis">Cyamopsis</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sesbania" title="Sesbania">Sesbania</a></i> species. Various legume species are farmed for timber production worldwide, including numerous <i>Acacia</i> species and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castanospermum_australe" title="Castanospermum australe">Castanospermum australe</a></i>.</p>
<p>Legume trees like the locust trees (<i><a href="/wiki/Gleditsia" title="Gleditsia">Gleditsia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Robinia" title="Robinia">Robinia</a></i>) or the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_coffeetree" title="Kentucky coffeetree">Kentucky coffeetree</a> (<i>Gymnocladus dioicus</i>) can be used in <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_forest" title="Food forest">food forests</a>. Other legume trees like <a href="/wiki/Laburnum#Description" title="Laburnum">laburnum</a> and the woody climbing vine <a href="/wiki/Wisteria#Description" title="Wisteria">wisteria</a> are <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisonous</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_fixation">Nitrogen fixation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">Nitrogen fixation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">Green manure</a></div>

<p>Many legumes contain <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> bacteria called <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizobia" title="Rhizobia">Rhizobia</a></i> within <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">root nodules</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root systems</a>. <p>This arrangement means that the root nodules are sources of nitrogen for legumes, making them relatively rich in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_proteins" title="Plant proteins">plant proteins</a>. All proteins contain nitrogenous <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>. Nitrogen is therefore a necessary <a href="/wiki/Ingredient" title="Ingredient">ingredient</a> in the production of proteins. Hence, legumes are among the best sources of plant protein.</p>
<p>When a legume plant dies in the field, for example following the <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a>, all of its remaining nitrogen, incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> inside the remaining plant parts, is released back into the soil. In the soil, the amino acids are converted to nitrate (NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span>), making the nitrogen available to other plants, thereby serving as fertilizer for future crops.</p>
<p>In many traditional and organic farming practices, <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a> involving legumes is common. By alternating between legumes and nonlegumes, sometimes planting nonlegumes two times in a row and then a legume, the field usually receives a sufficient amount of nitrogenous compounds to produce a good result, even when the crop is nonleguminous. Legumes are sometimes referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Archaeologists have discovered traces of pulse production around <a href="/wiki/Ravi_River" title="Ravi River">Ravi River</a> (<a href="/wiki/Punjab_(region)" title="Punjab (region)">Punjab</a>), the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation" title="Indus Valley Civilisation">Indus Valley Civilisation</a>, dating circa 3300 BC. Meanwhile, evidence of lentil cultivation has also been found in Egyptian pyramids and dry pea seeds have been discovered in a Swiss village that are believed to date back to the Stone Age. Archaeological evidence suggests that these peas must have been grown in the eastern Mediterranean and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> regions at least 5,000 years ago and in Britain as early as the 11th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_economy">World economy</span></h2>
<p>India is the world's largest producer and the largest consumer of pulses. Pakistan, Canada, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, Australia and the United States, in that order, are significant exporters and are India's most significant suppliers. The global pulse market is estimated at 60 million tonnes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Year_of_Pulses">International Year of Pulses</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Year_of_Pulses" title="International Year of Pulses">International Year of Pulses</a></div>

<p>The International Year of Pulses 2016 (IYP 2016) was declared by the <a href="/wiki/Sixty-eighth_session_of_the_United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="Sixty-eighth session of the United Nations General Assembly">Sixty-eighth session of the United Nations General Assembly</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization_of_the_United_Nations" title="Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations">Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations</a> has been nominated to facilitate the implementation of IYP 2016 in collaboration with governments, relevant organizations, non-governmental organizations and other relevant stakeholders. Its aim is to heighten public awareness of the nutritional benefits of pulses as part of sustainable food production aimed towards <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>. IYP 2016 will create an opportunity to encourage connections throughout the food chain that would better utilize pulse-based proteins, further global production of pulses, better utilize crop rotations and address challenges in the global trade of pulses.</p>
