<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the type of forest. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Woodland_(disambiguation)" title="Woodland (disambiguation)">Woodland (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Woodland</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/En-us-woodland.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a low-density <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> forming open <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> with plenty of sunlight and limited shade. Woodlands may support an understory of <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous plants</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grasses</a>. Woodland may form a transition to <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a> under drier conditions or during early stages of <a href="/wiki/Primary_succession" title="Primary succession">primary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Secondary_succession" title="Secondary succession">secondary succession</a>. Higher density areas of trees with a largely closed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(forest)" title="Canopy (forest)">canopy</a> that provides extensive and nearly continuous shade are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>.</p>
<p>Conservationists have worked hard to preserve woodlands, because people are destroying animals habitats when building homes and other buildings. For example, the woodlands in Northwest Indiana have been preserved as part of the Indiana Dunes.</p>
<p><i>Woodland</i> is used in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodland_management" title="Woodland management">woodland management</a> to mean tree-covered areas which arose naturally and which are then managed, while <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> is usually used in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> to describe <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>, usually more extensive, or hunting <a href="/wiki/Royal_forest" title="Royal forest">Forests</a>, which are a land use with a legal definition and may not be wooded at all. The term "<a href="/wiki/Ancient_woodland" title="Ancient woodland">ancient woodland</a>" is used in British <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">nature conservation</a> to refer to any wooded land that has existed since 1600, and often (though not always) for thousands of years, since the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last Ice Age</a> (equivalent to the American term <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">old-growth forest</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Woodlot" title="Woodlot">Woodlot</a> is a closely related <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> term, which refers to a stand of trees generally used for firewood. While woodlots often technically have closed canopies, they are so small that light <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_effect" title="Edge effect">penetration</a> from the edge makes them ecologically closer to woodland than forest.</p>
