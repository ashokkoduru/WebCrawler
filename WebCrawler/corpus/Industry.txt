<p><b>Industry</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> or related <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> within an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>. The major source of revenue of a group or company is the indicator of its relevant industry. When a large group has multiple sources of revenue generation, it is considered to be working in different industries. Manufacturing industry became a key sector of production and labour in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> countries during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, upsetting previous <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> economies. This came through many successive rapid advances in technology, such as the production of <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, possibly a third of the world's economic output are derived that is from <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> industries. Many <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a> and many developing/semi-developed countries (People's Republic of China, India etc.) depend significantly on manufacturing industry. Industries, the countries they reside in, and the economies of those countries are interlinked in a complex web of interdependence.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">economic sector</a></div>
<p>Industries can be classified in a variety of ways. At the top level, industry is often classified according to the <a href="/wiki/Three-sector_theory" title="Three-sector theory">three-sector theory</a> into <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">sectors</a>: <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary or extractive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Secondary sector of the economy">secondary or manufacturing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary or services</a>. Some authors add <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Quaternary sector of the economy">quaternary (knowledge)</a> or even <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quinary_sector_of_the_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quinary sector of the economy (page does not exist)">quinary (culture and research)</a> sectors. Over time, the fraction of a society's industry within each sector changes.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industry_classification" title="Industry classification">industry classification</a></div>
<p>Below the economic sectors there are many other more detailed <a href="/wiki/Industry_classification" title="Industry classification">industry classifications</a>. These classification systems commonly divide industries according to similar functions and markets and identify businesses producing related products.</p>
<p>Industries can also be identified by product, such as: <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemical industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">electronic industry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meatpacking_industry" title="Meatpacking industry">meatpacking industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hospitality_industry" title="Hospitality industry">hospitality industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_industry" title="Fish industry">fish industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_industry" title="Software industry">software industry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_industry" title="Paper industry">paper industry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entertainment_industry" title="Entertainment industry">entertainment industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_industry" title="Semiconductor industry">semiconductor industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_industry" title="Cultural industry">cultural industry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poverty_industry" title="Poverty industry">poverty industry</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">Market</a>-based classification systems such as the <a href="/wiki/Global_Industry_Classification_Standard" title="Global Industry Classification Standard">Global Industry Classification Standard</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Industry_Classification_Benchmark" title="Industry Classification Benchmark">Industry Classification Benchmark</a> are used in <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_research" title="Market research">market research</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ISIC">ISIC</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Industrial_Classification" title="International Standard Industrial Classification">International Standard Industrial Classification</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Industrial_Classification" title="International Standard Industrial Classification">International Standard Industrial Classification</a> (ISIC) of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_activity" title="Economic activity">economic activities</a> is the most complete and systematic industrial classification made by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Statistics_Division" title="United Nations Statistics Division">United Nations Statistics Division</a>.</p>
<p>ISIC is a standard classification of economic activities arranged so that entities can be classified according to the activity they carry out. The categories of ISIC at the most detailed level (classes) are delineated according to what is, in most countries, the customary combination of activities described in statistical units, considering the relative importance of the activities included in these classes.</p>
<p>While ISIC Rev.4 continues to use criteria such as input, output and use of the products produced, more emphasis has been given to the character of the production process in defining and delineating ISIC classes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_development">Industrial development</span></h2>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> led to the development of factories for large-scale production, with consequent changes in society. Originally the factories were steam-powered, but later transitioned to <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> once an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> was developed. The mechanized <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> was introduced to assemble parts in a repeatable fashion, with individual workers performing specific steps during the process. This led to significant increases in efficiency, lowering the cost of the end process. Later <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a> was increasingly used to replace human operators. This process has accelerated with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robot</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deindustrialisation">Deindustrialisation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deindustrialisation" title="Deindustrialisation">Deindustrialisation</a></div>

<p>Historically certain manufacturing industries have gone into a decline due to various economic factors, including the development of replacement technology or the loss of competitive advantage. An example of the former is the decline in <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a> manufacturing when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> was mass-produced.</p>
<p>A recent trend has been the migration of prosperous, industrialized nations towards a <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a>. This is manifested by an increase in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_sector" title="Service sector">service sector</a> at the expense of manufacturing, and the development of an information-based economy, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informational_revolution" title="Informational revolution">informational revolution</a>. In a post-industrial society, manufacturing is relocated to economically more favourable locations through a process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off-shoring" title="Off-shoring">off-shoring</a>.</p>
<p>The difficulty for people looking to measure manufacturing industries outputs and economic effect is finding a measurement which is stable historically. Traditionally, success has been measured in the number of jobs created. The lowering of employee numbers in the manufacturing sector has been assumed to be caused by a decline in the competitiveness of the sector although much has been caused by the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Lean_manufacturing" title="Lean manufacturing">lean manufacturing</a> process. Eventually, this will lead to competing product lines being managed by one or two people, as is already the case in the cigarette manufacturing industry.</p>
<p>Related to this change is the upgrading of the <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> of the product being manufactured. While it is easy to produce a low tech, low skill product, the ability to manufacture high quality products is limited to companies with a highly skilled staff.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">Industrial society</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">industrial society</a> can be defined in many ways. Today, industry is an important part of most societies and nations. A government must have some kind of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_policy" title="Industrial policy">industrial policy</a>, regulating industrial placement, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">industrial pollution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financing" title="Financing">financing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_labour" title="Industrial labour">industrial labour</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_labour">Industrial labour</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_labour" title="Industrial labour">Industrial labour</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_sociology" title="Industrial sociology">industrial sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_and_organizational_psychology" title="Industrial and organizational psychology">industrial and organizational psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_district" title="Industrial district">industrial district</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_park" title="Industrial park">industrial park</a></div>
<p>In an industrial society, industry employs a major part of the population. This occurs typically in the manufacturing sector. A labour union is an organization of workers who have banded together to achieve common goals in key areas such as wages, hours, and other working conditions. The trade union, through its leadership, bargains with the employer on behalf of union members (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rank_and_file" title="wikt:rank and file">rank and file</a> members) and negotiates labour contracts with employers. This <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">movement</a> first rose among industrial workers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War">War</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_warfare" title="Industrial warfare">Industrial warfare</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> changed warfare, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass-produced" title="Mass-produced">mass-produced</a> weaponry and supplies, machine-powered transportation, <a href="/wiki/Mobilization" title="Mobilization">mobilization</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Total_war" title="Total war">total war</a> concept and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>. Early instances of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_warfare" title="Industrial warfare">industrial warfare</a> were the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, but its full potential showed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_wars" title="World wars">world wars</a>. See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military-industrial_complex" title="Military-industrial complex">military-industrial complex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arms_industry" title="Arms industry">arms industry</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_industry" title="Military industry">military industry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_warfare" title="Modern warfare">modern warfare</a>.</p>
