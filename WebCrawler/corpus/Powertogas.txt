<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Power to gas</b> (also power-to-gas) (often abbreviated P2G) is a technology that converts <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> power to a gas <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>. When using surplus power from wind generation, the concept is sometimes called <b>windgas</b>. There are currently three methods in use; all use electricity to <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">split water</a> into <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.</p>
<p>In the first method, the resulting hydrogen is injected into the natural gas grid or is used in transport or industry. The second method is to combine the hydrogen with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and convert the two gases to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) using a methanation reaction such as the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>, or biological methanation resulting in an extra energy conversion loss of 8%. The methane may then be fed into the natural gas grid. The third method uses the output gas of a <a href="/wiki/Wood_gas_generator" title="Wood gas generator">wood gas generator</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> plant, after the <a href="/wiki/Biogas_upgrader" title="Biogas upgrader">biogas upgrader</a> is mixed with the produced hydrogen from the electrolyzer, to upgrade the quality of the biogas.</p>
<p>Impurities, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">particulates</a>, must be removed from the biogas if the gas is used for pipeline storage to prevent damage.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage_function">Storage function</span></h2>
<p>Power-to-gas systems may be deployed as adjuncts to wind parks or solar-electric generation. The excess power or off-peak power generated by wind generators or solar arrays may then be used at a later time for load balancing in the energy grid. Before switching to <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, the German gas networks were operated using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Towngas" title="Towngas">towngas</a>, which for 50–60 % consisted of hydrogen. The storage capacity of the German natural gas network is more than 200,000 GWh which is enough for several months of energy requirement. By comparison, the capacity of all German pumped storage power plants amounts to only about 40 GWh. The storage requirement in Germany is estimated at 16GW in 2023, 80GW in 2033 and 130GW in 2050. The transport of energy through a gas network is done with much less loss (&lt;0.1%) than in a power network (8%). The storage costs per kilowatt hour are estimated at €0.10 for hydrogen and €0.15 for methane. The use of the existing <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_gas_pipelines" title="List of natural gas pipelines">natural gas pipelines</a> for hydrogen was studied by the EU NaturalHy project and US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">DOE</a>. The blending technology is also used in <a href="/wiki/HCNG" title="HCNG">HCNG</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">Efficiency</span></h3>
<p>In 2013 the round-trip efficiency of power-to-gas-storage was well below 50%, with the hydrogen path being able to reach an maximum efficiency of ~ 43% and methane of ~ 39% by using combined-cycle powerplants. If <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plants are used that produce both electricity and heat, efficiency can be above 60%, but is still less than pumped hydro or battery storage. However, there is potential to increase efficieny of power-to-gas storage. In 2015 a study published in <a href="/wiki/Energy_and_Environmental_Science" title="Energy and Environmental Science">Energy and Environmental Science</a> found that by using <a href="/wiki/Unitized_regenerative_fuel_cell" title="Unitized regenerative fuel cell">reversible solid oxide electrochemical cells</a> and recycling waste heat in the storage process a round-trip efficiency electricity to electricity of more than 70% can be reached at low cost.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_hydrogen">Power to hydrogen</span></h2>
<p>In this method, electricity is used to <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">split water</a> into hydrogen and oxygen by means of electrolysis. The resulting hydrogen is injected into the natural gas grid or is used in transport or industry.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/ITM_Power" title="ITM Power">ITM Power</a> won a tender in March 2013 for a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th%C3%BCga_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thüga Group (page does not exist)">Thüga Group</a> project, to supply a 360 kW self-pressurising <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_electrolysis" title="High-pressure electrolysis">high pressure electrolysis</a> rapid response <a href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">PEM</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolyser</a> <i>Rapid Response Electrolysis</i> Power-to-Gas energy storage plant. The unit produces 125 kg/day of hydrogen gas and incorporates <a href="/wiki/AEG" title="AEG">AEG</a> power electronics. It will be situated at a <a href="/wiki/Mainova" title="Mainova">Mainova</a> AG site in the Schielestraße, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> in the state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hessen" title="Hessen">Hessen</a>. The operational data will be shared by the whole Thüga group – the largest network of energy companies in Germany with around 100 municipal utility members. The project partners include: badenova AG &amp; Co. kg, Erdgas Mittelsachsen GmbH, Energieversorgung Mittelrhein GmbH, erdgas schwaben GmbH, Gasversorgung Westerwald GmbH, <a href="/wiki/Mainova" title="Mainova">Mainova</a> Aktiengesellschaft, Stadtwerke Ansbach GmbH, Stadtwerke Bad Hersfeld GmbH, Thüga Energienetze GmbH, WEMAG AG, e-rp GmbH, ESWE Versorgungs AG with Thüga Aktiengesellschaft as project coordinator. Scientific partners will participate in the operational phase. It can produce 60 cubic metres of hydrogen per hour and feed 3,000 cubic metres of natural gas enriched with hydrogen into the grid per hour. An expansion of the pilot plant is planned from 2016, facilitating the full conversion of the hydrogen produced into methane to be directly injected into the natural gas grid.</p>

<p>In December 2013, <a href="/wiki/ITM_Power" title="ITM Power">ITM Power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mainova" title="Mainova">Mainova</a>, and NRM Netzdienste Rhein-Main GmbH began injecting hydrogen into the German gas distribution network using <a href="/wiki/ITM_Power" title="ITM Power">ITM Power</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HGas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HGas (page does not exist)">HGas</a>, which is a rapid response <a href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">proton exchange membrane</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyser" title="Electrolyser">electrolyser</a> plant. The power consumption of the electrolyser is 315 kilowatts. It produces about 60 cubic meters per hour of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and thus in one hour can feed 3,000 cubic meters of hydrogen-enriched natural gas into the network.</p>
<p>On August 28, 2013, <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON Hanse</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvay_(company)" title="Solvay (company)">Solvicore</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swissgas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swissgas (page does not exist)">Swissgas</a> inaugurated a commercial power-to-gas unit in <a href="/wiki/Falkenhagen" title="Falkenhagen">Falkenhagen</a>, Germany. The unit, which has a capacity of two megawatts, can produce 360 cubic meters of hydrogen per hour. The plant uses wind power and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenics" title="Hydrogenics">Hydrogenics</a> electrolysis equipment to transform water into hydrogen, which is then injected into the existing regional natural gas transmission system. Swissgas, which represents over 100 local natural gas utilities, is a partner in the project with a 20 percent capital stake and an agreement to purchase a portion of the gas produced. A second 800 kW power-to-gas project has been started in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>/Reitbrook district and is expected to open in 2015.</p>
<p>In August 2013, a 140 MW wind park in <a href="/wiki/Grapzow" title="Grapzow">Grapzow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a> ,owned by <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a> received an electrolyser. The hydrogen produced can be used in an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> or can be injected into the local gas grid. The hydrogen compression and storage system stores up to 27 MWh of energy and increases the overall efficiency of the wind park by tapping into wind energy that otherwise would be wasted. The electrolyser produces 210 Nm/h of hydrogen and is operated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RH2-WKA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RH2-WKA (page does not exist)">RH2-WKA</a>.</p>
<p>In January 2011, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Secure_Supplies_Hydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Secure Supplies Hydrogen (page does not exist)">Secure Supplies Hydrogen</a>, an internationally certified engineering and warrantied power to gas vendor of quality Australian and USA made Hydrogen Fueling equipment, Hydrogen Fueled Engines and Hydrogen Home /Business Solar fuel cell Kits entered the market. Project's deploying (2015-2020) are focused on renewable power investments. Value adding Sites to produce a Hydrogen fuel and transfer storage gas. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Secure_Supplies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Secure Supplies (page does not exist)">Secure Supplies</a> value adds all solar wind, geo thermal and hydropower projects. Enabling owners to achieve a higher ROI, to produce hydrogen gas grids to fuel industry and a variety of applications emission free. End users include Port Operators, Farms, Green Community residential development or and Renewable power operators. Gas is sold to gas grid or transported and used to fuel fixed engines or fuel cell that run 24r to pump water or make power for business and communities projects. Hydrogen Equipment along with Engineering and Service provision makes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Secure_Supplies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Secure Supplies (page does not exist)">Secure Supplies</a> is well positioned to supply key markets globally.</p>
<p>The INGRID project started in 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a>, Italy. It is a four-year project with 39 MWh storage and a 1.2 MW electrolyser for smart grid monitoring and control. The hydrogen is used for grid balancing, transport, industry, and injection into the gas network.</p>
<p>The surplus energy from the 12 MW <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prenzlau_Windpark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prenzlau Windpark (page does not exist)">Prenzlau Windpark</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, Germany will be injected into the gas grid from 2014 on.</p>
<p>The 6 MW Energiepark Mainz from Stadtwerke Mainz, <a href="/wiki/RheinMain_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="RheinMain University of Applied Sciences">RheinMain University of Applied Sciences</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linde_AG" title="Linde AG">Linde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> (Germany) will open in 2015.</p>
<p>Power to gas and other <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">energy storage</a> schemes to store and utilize <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> are part of Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energiewende" title="Energiewende">Energiewende</a> (energy transition program).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_injection_without_compression">Grid injection without compression</span></h3>
<p>The core of the system is a <a href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">proton exchange membrane</a> (PEM) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyser" title="Electrolyser">electrolyser</a>. The electrolyser converts electrical energy into chemical energy, which in turn facilitates the storage of electricity. A gas mixing plant ensures that the proportion of hydrogen in the natural gas stream does not exceed two per cent by volume, the technically permissible maximum value when a natural gas filling station is situated in the local distribution network. The electrolyser supplies the hydrogen-methane mixture at the same pressure as the gas distribution network, namely 3.5 bar. </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_methane">Power to methane</span></h2>

<p>The Power to Gas Methane method is to combine hydrogen from an electrolyzer with carbon dioxide and convert the two gases to methane (see natural gas) using a methanation reaction such as the Sabatier reaction or biological methanation resulting in an extra energy conversion loss of 8%, the methane may then be fed into the natural gas grid if the purity requirement is reached.</p>
<p>ZSW (Center for Solar Energy and Hydrogen Research) and SolarFuel GmbH (now ETOGAS GmbH) realized a demonstration project with 250 kW electrical input power in Stuttgart, Germany. The plant was put into operation on October 30, 2012.</p>
<p>The first industry-scale Power-to-Methane plant was realized by ETOGAS for Audi AG in Werlte, Germany. The plant with 6 MW electrical input power is using CO<sub>2</sub> from a waste-biogas plant and intermittent renewable power to produce synthetic natural gas (SNG) which is directly fed into the local gas grid (which is operated by EWE). The plant is part of the Audi e-fuels program. The produced synthetic natural gas, named Audi e-gas, enables CO<sub>2</sub>-neutral mobility with standard CNG vehicles. Currently it is available to customers of Audi's first CNG car, the Audi A3 g-tron.</p>
<p>In April 2014 the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union’s</a> co-financed and from the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">KIT</a> coordinated HELMETH (Integrated <b>H</b>igh-Temperature <b>EL</b>ectrolysis and <b>METH</b>anation for Effective Power to Gas Conversion) research project started. The objective of the project is the proof of concept of a highly efficient Power-to-Gas technology by thermally integrating high temperature electrolysis (<a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_electrolyser_cell" title="Solid oxide electrolyser cell">SOEC</a> technology) with CO<sub>2</sub>-methanation. Through the thermal integration of exothermal methanation and steam generation for the high temperature steam electrolysis a conversion efficiency &gt; 85% is expected (<a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">higher heating value</a> of produced methane per used electrical energy). The process consists of a pressurized high-temperature steam <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis</a> and a pressurized CO<sub>2</sub>-methanation module which are planned to be coupled in 2016. A methane output of approximately 30 kW (higher heating value) is targeted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_methanation">Microbial methanation</span></h3>
<p>The biological methanation combines both processes, the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and the subsequent CO<sub>2</sub> reduction to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> using this <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. During this process, methane forming microorganisms (methanogenic <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogens" title="Methanogens">methanogens</a>) release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> that reduce the <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> of a non-catalytic <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>) so that it can produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. This microbial power-to-gas reaction occurs at ambient conditions, i.e. room temperature and pH 7, at efficiencies that routinely reach 80-100%. However, methane is formed more slowly than in the Sabatier reaction due to the lower temperatures. A direct conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> has also been postulated, circumventing the need for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> production. Microorganisms involved in the microbial power-to-gas reaction are typically members of the order <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobacteriales" title="Methanobacteriales">Methanobacteriales</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">Genera</a> that were shown to catalyze this reaction are <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobacterium" title="Methanobacterium">Methanobacterium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobrevibacter" title="Methanobrevibacter">Methanobrevibacter</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Methanothermobacter" title="Methanothermobacter">Methanothermobacter</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">thermophile</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas-upgrading_to_biomethane">Biogas-upgrading to biomethane</span></h2>
<p>In the third method the carbon dioxide in the output of a wood gas generator or a biogas plant after the biogas upgrader is mixed with the produced hydrogen from the electrolyzer to produce methane. The free heat coming from the electrolyzer is used to cut heating costs in the biogas plant. The impurities carbon dioxide, water, hydrogen sulfide, and particulates must be removed from the biogas if the gas is used for pipeline storage to prevent damage.</p>
<p>2014-Avedøre wastewater Services in <a href="/wiki/Aved%C3%B8re" title="Avedøre">Avedøre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kopenhagen" title="Kopenhagen">Kopenhagen</a> (Denmark) is adding a 1 MW electrolyzer plant to upgrade the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> biogas from sewage sludge. The produced hydrogen is used with the carbon dioxide from the biogas in a Sabatier reaction to produce methane. Electrochaea is testing another project outside P2G BioCat with biocatalytic methanation. The company uses an adapted strain of the thermophilic methanogen Methanothermobacter thermautotrophicus and has demonstrated its technology at laboratory-scale in an industrial environment. A pre-commercial demonstration project with a 10,000-liter reactor vessel was executed between January and November 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Foulum" title="Foulum">Foulum</a>, Denmark.</p>
<p>In 2016 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Torrgas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Torrgas (page does not exist)">Torrgas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stedin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stedin (page does not exist)">Stedin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasunie" title="Gasunie">Gasunie</a>, A.Hak, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanze_University_of_Applied_Sciences,_Groningen" title="Hanze University of Applied Sciences, Groningen">Hanzehogeschool</a>/EnTranCe and Energy Valley intend to open a 12 MW Power to Gas facility in <a href="/wiki/Delfzijl" title="Delfzijl">Delfzijl</a> (The Netherlands) where biogas from Torrgas (<a href="/wiki/Torrefaction" title="Torrefaction">biocoal</a>) will be upgraded with hydrogen from electrolysis and delivered to nearby industrial consumers.</p>
