<p>An <b>ocean</b> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%A8%CE%BA%CE%B5%CE%B1%CE%BD%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:Ὠκεανός">Ὠκεανός</a>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek" title="Romanization of Greek">transc.</a></small><i> <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Okeanós</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>) is a body of <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline water</a> that composes much of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, an ocean is one of the major conventional divisions of the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a>, which covers almost 71% of its surface. These are, in descending order by area, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern</a> (Antarctic), and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic</a> Oceans. The word <i><a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a></i> is often used interchangeably with "ocean" in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> but, strictly speaking, a sea is a body of saline water (generally a division of the world ocean) partly or fully enclosed by land.</p>
<p>Saline water covers approximately 72% of the planet's surface (~3.6<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10 km) and is customarily divided into several principal oceans and smaller seas, with the ocean covering approximately 71% of Earth's surface and 90% of the Earth's biosphere. The ocean contains 97% of Earth's water, and <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographers</a> have stated that less than 5% of the World Ocean has been explored. The total volume is approximately 1.35 billion cubic kilometers (320 million cu mi) with an average depth of nearly 3,700 meters (12,100 ft).</p>
<p>As it is the principal component of Earth's hydrosphere, the world ocean is integral to <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">all known life</a>, forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, and influences <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> patterns. It is the <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> of 230,000 known <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, although much of the oceans depths remain unexplored, and over two million <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> species are estimated to exist. The <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth" title="Origin of water on Earth">origin of Earth's oceans</a> remains unknown; oceans are thought to have formed in the <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a> period and may have been the impetus for the <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">emergence of life</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_oceans" title="Extraterrestrial oceans">Extraterrestrial oceans</a> may be composed of water or other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a>. The only confirmed large stable bodies of extraterrestrial surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan" title="Lakes of Titan">lakes of Titan</a>, although there is evidence for the existence of oceans elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. Early in their geologic histories, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> are theorized to have had large water oceans. The <a href="/wiki/Mars_ocean_hypothesis" title="Mars ocean hypothesis">Mars ocean hypothesis</a> suggests that nearly a third of the surface of Mars was once covered by water, and a <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a> may have boiled away the global ocean of Venus. Compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> dissolved in water lower its freezing point, so that water might exist in large quantities in extraterrestrial environments as brine or convecting <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>. Unconfirmed oceans are speculated beneath the surface of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwarf_planets" title="Dwarf planets">dwarf planets</a> and natural satellites; notably, the ocean of <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> is estimated to have over twice the water volume of Earth. The Solar System's <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a> are also thought to have liquid <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> layers of yet to be confirmed compositions. Oceans may also exist on <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exomoon" title="Exomoon">exomoons</a>, including surface oceans of liquid water within a <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_habitable_zone" title="Circumstellar habitable zone">circumstellar habitable zone</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ocean_planet" title="Ocean planet">Ocean planets</a> are a hypothetical type of planet with a surface completely covered with liquid.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word « ocean » comes from the figure in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%A8%CE%BA%CE%B5%CE%B1%CE%BD%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:Ὠκεανός">Ὠκεανός</a></span> <i>Ōkeanós</i>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">[ɔːkeanós]</a></span>), the elder of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a> in classical <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, believed by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> to be the divine personification of the <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>, an enormous <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> encircling the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_global_ocean">Earth's global ocean</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_divisions">Oceanic divisions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Borders_of_the_oceans" title="Borders of the oceans">Borders of the oceans</a></div>

<p>Though generally described as several separate oceans, these waters comprise one global, interconnected body of salt water sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean or global ocean</a>. This concept of a continuous body of water with relatively free interchange among its parts is of fundamental importance to <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>.</p>
<p>The major oceanic divisions – listed below in descending order of area and volume – are defined in part by the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a>, and other criteria.</p>

<div style="padding:0.1em 0;line-height:1.2em;line-height: 1.4em; margin: 1em 0 1.4em 1em; font-size: 0.9em;">NB: Volume, area, and average depth figures include <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> ETOPO1 figures for marginal <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>.<br/>
Sources: <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Earth" title="Encyclopedia of Earth">Encyclopedia of Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a>, <i>Regional Oceanography: an Introduction</i> (Tomczak, 2005), <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a>.</div>
<p>Oceans are fringed by smaller, adjoining bodies of water such as <a href="/wiki/List_of_seas" title="List of seas">seas</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_gulfs" title="List of gulfs">gulfs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bight_(geography)" title="Bight (geography)">bights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">straits</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_system">Global system</span></h3>


<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">Mid-Oceanic Ridge</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">World</a> are connected and form <i>the</i> Ocean Ridge, a single global mid-oceanic ridge system that is part of every ocean, making it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountain_ranges#By_length" title="List of mountain ranges">longest</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a> in the world. The continuous mountain range is 65,000 km (40,400 mi) long (several times longer than the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, the longest continental mountain range), and the total length of the oceanic ridge system is 80,000 km (49,700 mi) long.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">Seawater</a></div>
<p>The total mass of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a> is about 1.4 quintillion metric tons (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018140000000000000♠</span>1.4<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10</span> long tons or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018150000000000000♠</span>1.5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10</span> short tons), which is about 0.023% of Earth's total mass. Less than 3% is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>; the rest is <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saltwater</a>, almost all of which is in the ocean. The area of the World Ocean is about 361.9 million square kilometers (139.7 million square miles), which covers about 70.9% of Earth's surface, and its volume is approximately 1.335 billion cubic kilometers (320.3 million cubic miles). This can be thought of as a cube of water with an edge length of 1,101 kilometers (684 mi). Its average depth is about 3,688 meters (12,100 ft), and its maximum depth is 10,994 meters (6.831 mi) at the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>. Nearly half of the world's marine waters are over 3,000 meters (9,800 ft) deep. The vast expanses of deep ocean (anything below 200 meters or 660 feet) cover about 66% of Earth's surface. This does not include seas not connected to the World Ocean, such as the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>.</p>
<p>The bluish <a href="/wiki/Color_of_water" title="Color of water">color of water</a> is a composite of several contributing agents. Prominent contributors include dissolved organic matter and <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>.</p>
<p>Mariners and other seafarers have reported that the ocean <a href="/wiki/Milky_seas_effect" title="Milky seas effect">often emits a visible glow</a> which extends for miles at night. In 2005, scientists announced that for the first time, they had obtained photographic evidence of this glow. It is most likely caused by <a href="/wiki/Bioluminescence" title="Bioluminescence">bioluminescence</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_zones">Oceanic zones</span></h3>

<p>Oceanographers divide the ocean into different <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_zone" title="Oceanic zone">zones</a> by physical and biological conditions. The <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic zone</a> includes all open ocean regions, and can be divided into further regions categorized by depth and light abundance. The <a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">photic zone</a> includes the oceans from the surface to a depth of 200 m; it is the region where photosynthesis can occur and is, therefore, the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiverse" title="Biodiverse">biodiverse</a>. Because plants require <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, life found deeper than the photic zone must either rely on material sinking from above (see <a href="/wiki/Marine_snow" title="Marine snow">marine snow</a>) or find another energy source. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">Hydrothermal vents</a> are the primary source of energy in what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Aphotic_zone" title="Aphotic zone">aphotic zone</a> (depths exceeding 200 m). The pelagic part of the photic zone is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epipelagic" title="Epipelagic">epipelagic</a>.</p>
<p>The pelagic part of the aphotic zone can be further divided into vertical regions according to temperature. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopelagic" title="Mesopelagic">mesopelagic</a> is the uppermost region. Its lowermost boundary is at a <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> of 12 °C (54 °F), which, in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> generally lies at 700–1,000 meters (2,300–3,300 ft). Next is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathypelagic" title="Bathypelagic">bathypelagic</a> lying between 10 and 4 °C (50 and 39 °F), typically between 700–1,000 meters (2,300–3,300 ft) and 2,000–4,000 meters (6,600–13,100 ft) Lying along the top of the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_zone" title="Abyssal zone">abyssopelagic</a>, whose lower boundary lies at about 6,000 meters (20,000 ft). The last zone includes the deep <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trench</a>, and is known as the <a href="/wiki/Hadal_zone" title="Hadal zone">hadalpelagic</a>. This lies between 6,000–11,000 meters (20,000–36,000 ft) and is the deepest oceanic zone.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> zones are aphotic and correspond to the three deepest zones of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep-sea" title="Deep-sea">deep-sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bathyal_zone" title="Bathyal zone">bathyal zone</a> covers the continental slope down to about 4,000 meters (13,000 ft). The abyssal zone covers the abyssal plains between 4,000 and 6,000 m. Lastly, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadal" title="Hadal">hadal</a> zone corresponds to the hadalpelagic zone, which is found in oceanic trenches.</p>
<p>The pelagic zone can be further subdivided into two subregions: the <a href="/wiki/Neritic_zone" title="Neritic zone">neritic zone</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_zone" title="Oceanic zone">oceanic zone</a>. The neritic zone encompasses the water mass directly above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a> whereas the oceanic zone includes all the completely open water.</p>
<p>In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">littoral zone</a> covers the region between low and high tide and represents the transitional area between marine and terrestrial conditions. It is also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertidal" title="Intertidal">intertidal</a> zone because it is the area where tide level affects the conditions of the region.</p>
<p>The ocean can be divided into three density zones: the surface zone, the <a href="/wiki/Pycnocline" title="Pycnocline">pycnocline</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep zone</a>. The surface zone, also called the mixed layer, refers to the uppermost density zone of the ocean. Temperature and salinity are relatively constant with depth in this zone due to currents and wave action. The surface zone contains ocean water that is in contact with the atmosphere and within the photic zone. The surface zone has the ocean's least dense water and represents approximately 2% of the total volume of ocean water. The surface zone usually ranges between depths of 500 feet to 3,300 feet below ocean surface, but this can vary a great deal. In some cases, the surface zone can be entirely non-existent. The surface zone is typically thicker in the tropics than in regions of higher latitude. The transition to colder, denser water is more abrupt in the tropics than in regions of higher latitudes. The pycnocline refers to a zone wherein density substantially increases with depth due primarily to decreases in temperature. The pycnocline effectively separates the lower-density surface zone above from the higher-density deep zone below. The pycnocline represents approximately 18% of the total volume of ocean water. The deep zone refers to the lowermost density zone of the ocean. The deep zone usually begins at depths below 3,300 feet in mid-latitudes. The deep zone undergoes negligible changes in water density with depth. The deep zone represents approximately 80% of the total volume of ocean water. The deep zone contains relatively colder and stable water.</p>
<p>If a zone undergoes dramatic changes in temperature with depth, it contains a <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>. The tropical thermocline is typically deeper than the thermocline at higher latitudes. Polar waters, which receive relatively little solar energy, are not stratified by temperature and generally lack a thermocline because surface water at polar latitudes are nearly as cold as water at greater depths. Below the thermocline, water is very cold, ranging from −1 °C to 3 °C. Because this deep and cold layer contains the bulk of ocean water, the average temperature of the world ocean is 3.9 °C If a zone undergoes dramatic changes in salinity with depth, it contains a <a href="/wiki/Halocline" title="Halocline">halocline</a>. If a zone undergoes a strong, vertical chemistry gradient with depth, it contains a <a href="/wiki/Chemocline" title="Chemocline">chemocline</a>.</p>
<p>The halocline often coincides with the thermocline, and the combination produces a pronounced pycnocline.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span></h3>

<p>Ocean travel by boat dates back to prehistoric times, but only in modern times has extensive underwater travel become possible.</p>
<p>The deepest point in the ocean is the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, located in the Pacific Ocean near the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a>. Its maximum depth has been estimated to be 10,971 meters (35,994 ft) (plus or minus 11 meters; see the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a> article for discussion of the various estimates of the maximum depth.) The British naval vessel <i>Challenger II</i> surveyed the trench in 1951 and named the deepest part of the trench the "<a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>". In 1960, the <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe_Trieste" title="Bathyscaphe Trieste">Trieste</a> successfully reached the bottom of the trench, manned by a crew of two men.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_maritime_currents">Oceanic maritime currents</span></h3>


<p>Oceanic maritime currents have different origins. Tidal currents are in phase with the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a>, hence are <a href="/wiki/Quasiperiodicity" title="Quasiperiodicity">quasiperiodic</a>, they may fomulate various knots in certain places, most notably around <a href="/wiki/Cape_(geography)" title="Cape (geography)">headlands</a>. Non periodic currents have for origin the waves, wind and different densities.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> create surface currents (designated as « drift currents »). These currents can decompose in one quasi permanent current (which varies within the hourly scale) and one movement of <a href="/wiki/Stokes_drift" title="Stokes drift">Stokes drift</a> under the effect of rapid waves movement (at the echelon of a couple of seconds).). The quasi permanent current is accelerated by the breaking of waves, and in a lesser governing effect, by the friction of the wind on the surface.</p>
<p>This acceleration of the current takes place in the direction of waves and dominant wind. Accordingly, when the sea depth increases, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">rotation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> changes the direction of currents, in proportion with the increase of depth while friction lowers their speed. At a certain sea depth, the current changes direction and is seen inverted in the opposite direction with speed current becoming nul: known as the <a href="/wiki/Ekman_spiral" title="Ekman spiral">Ekman spiral</a>. The influence of these currents is mainly experienced at the mixed layer of the ocean surface, often from 400 to 800 meters of maximum depth. These currents can considerably alter, change and are dependent on the various yearly seasons. If the mixed layer is less thick (10 to 20 meters), the quasi permanent current at the surface adopts an extreme oblique direction in relation to the direction of the wind, becoming virtually homogeneous, until the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">Thermocline</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep</a> however, maritime currents are caused by the temperature gradients and the <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> between water density masses.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">Littoral zones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breaking_wave" title="Breaking wave">Breaking wave</a> is so intense and the depth measurement so low, that maritime currents reach often 1 to 2 knots.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">Ocean currents</a> greatly affect Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> by transferring heat from the tropics to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_regions" title="Polar regions">polar regions</a>. Transferring warm or cold air and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> to coastal regions, winds may carry them inland. Surface heat and freshwater <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">fluxes</a> create global <a href="/wiki/Density_gradient" title="Density gradient">density gradients</a> that drive the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> part of large-scale ocean circulation. It plays an important role in supplying heat to the polar regions, and thus in sea ice regulation. Changes in the thermohaline circulation are thought to have significant impacts on <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Earth's energy budget</a>. In so far as the thermohaline circulation governs the rate at which deep waters reach the surface, it may also significantly influence atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> concentrations.</p>
<p>For a discussion of the possibilities of changes to the thermohaline circulation under <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a>.</p>
<p>It is often stated that the thermohaline circulation is the primary reason that the climate of Western Europe is so temperate. An alternate hypothesis claims that this is largely incorrect, and that Europe is warm mostly because it lies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downwind" title="Downwind">downwind</a> of an ocean basin, and because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_waves" title="Atmospheric waves">atmospheric waves</a> bring warm air north from the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a> encircles that continent, influencing the area's climate and connecting currents in several oceans.</p>
<p>One of the most dramatic forms of <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> occurs over the oceans: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> (also called "typhoons" and "hurricanes" depending upon where the system forms).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">Marine biology</a></div>
<p>The ocean has a significant effect on the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Oceanic <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, as a phase of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, is the source of most rainfall, and ocean temperatures determine <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> patterns that affect life on land. Life within the ocean <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> 3 billion years prior to life on land. Both the depth and the distance from shore strongly influence the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> of the plants and animals present in each region.</p>
<p>Lifeforms native to the ocean include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radiata" title="Radiata">Radiata</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">Cnidaria</a>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">Cetacea</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porpoise" title="Porpoise">porpoises</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">Cephalopods</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">Crustaceans</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marine_worm" title="Marine worm">Marine worms</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">Plankton</a>; and</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echinoderms" title="Echinoderms">Echinoderms</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Brittle_star" title="Brittle star">brittle stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_cucumber" title="Sea cucumber">sea cucumbers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sand_dollar" title="Sand dollar">sand dollars</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, many land animals have adapted to living a major part of their life on the oceans. For instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seabirds" title="Seabirds">seabirds</a> are a diverse group of birds that have adapted to a life mainly on the oceans. They feed on marine animals and spend most of their lifetime on water, many only going on land for breeding. Other birds that have adapted to oceans as their living space are <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">seagulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelecaniformes" title="Pelecaniformes">pelicans</a>. Seven species of turtles, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, also spend most of their time in the oceans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gases">Gases</span></h3>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface">Surface</span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixing_time">Mixing time</span></h3>
<p>The <i>residence time</i> is the amount of an element in the ocean divided by the rate at which that element is added to (or removed from) the ocean.</p>
<p>The mean oceanic <a href="/wiki/Mixing_(mathematics)" title="Mixing (mathematics)">mixing time</a> is thought to be approximately 1,600 years. If a given element in the ocean stays in the ocean, on average, longer than the oceanic mixing time, then that element is assumed to be homogeneously spread throughout the ocean. As a result, because the major salts have a <a href="/wiki/Residence_time_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Residence time (fluid dynamics)">residence time</a> that is longer than 1,600 years, the ratio of major salts is thought to be unchanging across the ocean. This constant ratio is often referred to as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Forchhammer%27s_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Forchhammer's principle (page does not exist)">Forchhammer's principle</a> or the principle of constant proportions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salinity">Salinity</span></h3>
<p>A zone of rapid salinity increase with depth is called a <a href="/wiki/Halocline" title="Halocline">halocline</a>. The temperature of maximum density of <a href="/wiki/Salinity#Seawater" title="Salinity">seawater</a> decreases as its salt content increases. Freezing temperature of water decreases with salinity, and boiling temperature of water increases with <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>. Typical seawater freezes at around −1.9 °C at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>. If precipitation exceeds evaporation, as is the case in polar and temperate regions, salinity will be lower. If evaporation exceeds precipitation, as is the case in tropical regions, salinity will be higher. Thus, oceanic waters in polar regions have lower salinity content than oceanic waters in temperate and tropical regions.</p>
<p>Salinity can be calculated using the chlorinity, which is a measure of the total mass of halogen ions (includes fluorine, chlorine, bromine, and iodine) in seawater. By international agreement, the following formula is used to determine salinity:</p>
<p><b>Salinity (in ‰)=1.80655 x Chlorinity (in ‰)</b></p>
<p>The average chlorinity is about 19.2‰, and, thus, the average salinity is around 34.7‰ </p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_of_light">Absorption of light</span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_value">Economic value</span></h3>
<p>The oceans are essential to transportation. This is because most of the world's goods move by <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> between the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport" title="Seaport">seaports</a>. Oceans are also the major supply source for the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a>. Some of the major harvests are <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crabs" title="Crabs">crabs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waves">Waves</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">Wind wave</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sea#Waves" title="Sea">Sea § Waves</a></div>
<p>The motions of the ocean surface, known as undulations or <i><a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">waves</a></i>, are the partial and alternate rising and falling of the ocean surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_oceans">Extraterrestrial oceans</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_liquid_water" title="Extraterrestrial liquid water">Extraterrestrial liquid water</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_lakes_and_seas_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of largest lakes and seas in the Solar System">List of largest lakes and seas in the Solar System</a></div>


<p>Although Earth is the only known <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> with large stable bodies of liquid water on its surface and the only one in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, other celestial bodies are thought to have large oceans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">Planets</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, are thought to lack surfaces and instead have a stratum of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>, however their <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">planetary geology</a> is not well understood. The possibility of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">ice giants</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> having hot, highly compressed, <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> water under their thick atmospheres has been hypothesised. Although their composition is still not fully understood, a 2006 study by Wiktorowicz and Ingersall ruled out the possibility of such a water "ocean" existing on Neptune, though some studies have suggested that exotic oceans of liquid <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> are possible.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mars_ocean_hypothesis" title="Mars ocean hypothesis">Mars ocean hypothesis</a> suggests that nearly a third of the surface of Mars was once covered by water, though the water on Mars is no longer oceanic (much of it residing in the <a href="/wiki/Martian_polar_ice_caps" title="Martian polar ice caps">ice caps</a>). The possibility continues to be studied along with reasons for their apparent disappearance. Astronomers think that <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">Venus</a> had liquid water and perhaps oceans in its very early history. If they existed, all later vanished via <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Venus" title="Geology of Venus">resurfacing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_satellites">Natural satellites</span></h3>
<p>A global layer of liquid water thick enough to decouple the crust from the mantle is thought to be present on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural satellites</a> <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> and, with less certainty, <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> ocean is thought to be present on <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">Geysers</a> have been found on Saturn's moon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">Enceladus</a>, possibly originating from about 10 kilometers (6.2 mi) deep ocean beneath an ice shell. Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icy_moons" title="Icy moons">icy moons</a> may also have internal oceans, or may once have had internal oceans that have now frozen.</p>
<p>Large bodies of liquid hydrocarbons are thought to be present on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, although they are not large enough to be considered oceans and are sometimes referred to as <i>lakes</i> or seas. The <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> space mission initially discovered only what appeared to be dry lakebeds and empty river channels, suggesting that Titan had lost what surface liquids it might have had. Cassini's more recent fly-by of Titan offers radar images that strongly suggest hydrocarbon lakes exist near the colder polar regions. Titan is thought to have a subsurface liquid-water ocean under the ice and hydrocarbon mix that forms its outer crust.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarf_planets_and_trans-Neptunian_objects">Dwarf planets and trans-Neptunian objects</span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a> appears to be <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">differentiated</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocky</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">core</a> and icy <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and may harbour a liquid-water ocean under its surface.</p>
<p>Not enough is known of the larger <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian objects</a> to determine whether they are differentiated bodies capable of supporting oceans, although models of radioactive decay suggest that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluto_(dwarf_planet)" title="Pluto (dwarf planet)">Pluto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a>, <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">Sedna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/90482_Orcus" title="90482 Orcus">Orcus</a> have oceans beneath solid icy crusts approximately <span class="nowrap">100 to 180 km</span> thick.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extrasolar">Extrasolar</span></h3>

<p>Some planets and natural satellites outside the Solar System are likely to have oceans, including possible water ocean planets similar to Earth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> or "liquid-water belt". The detection of oceans, even through the <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> method, however is likely extremely difficult and inconclusive.</p>
<p>Theoretical models have been used to predict with high probability that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GJ_1214_b" title="GJ 1214 b">GJ 1214 b</a>, detected by transit, is composed of exotic form of <a href="/wiki/Ice_VII" title="Ice VII">ice VII</a>, making up 75% of its mass, making it an <a href="/wiki/Ocean_planet" title="Ocean planet">ocean planet</a>.</p>
<p>Other possible candidates are merely speculated based on their mass and position in the habitable zone include planet though little is actually known of their composition. Some scientists speculate <a href="/wiki/Kepler-22b" title="Kepler-22b">Kepler-22b</a> may be an "ocean-like" planet. Models have been proposed for <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581_d" title="Gliese 581 d">Gliese 581 d</a> that could include surface oceans. <a href="/wiki/Gliese_436_b" title="Gliese 436 b">Gliese 436 b</a> is speculated to have an ocean of "hot ice". <a href="/wiki/Exomoon" title="Exomoon">Exomoons</a> orbiting planets, particularly gas giants within their parent star's habitable zone may theoretically have surface oceans.</p>
<p>Terrestrial planets will acquire water during their accretion, some of which will be buried in the magma ocean but most of it will go into a steam atmosphere, and when the atmosphere cools it will collapse on to the surface forming an ocean. There will also be outgassing of water from the mantle as the magma solidifies—this will happen even for planets with a low percentage of their mass composed of water, so "super-Earth exoplanets may be expected to commonly produce water oceans within tens to hundreds of millions of years of their last major accretionary impact."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-water_surface_liquids">Non-water surface liquids</span></h3>
<p>Oceans, seas, lakes and other bodies of liquids can be composed of liquids other than water, for example the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> lakes on <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. The possibility of seas of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> on <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a> was also considered but ruled out. There is evidence that the icy surfaces of the moons <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>, <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, Titan and <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> are shells floating on oceans of very dense liquid water or water–ammonia. Earth is often called <i>the</i> ocean planet because it is 70% covered in water. Extrasolar <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a> that are extremely close to their parent star will be tidally locked and so one half of the planet will be a magma ocean. It is also possible that terrestrial planets had magma oceans at some point during their formation as a result of giant impacts. <a href="/wiki/Hot_Neptune" title="Hot Neptune">Hot Neptunes</a> close to their star could lose their atmospheres via <a href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic_escape" title="Hydrodynamic escape">hydrodynamic escape</a>, leaving behind their cores with various liquids on the surface. Where there are suitable temperatures and pressures, volatile chemicals that might exist as liquids in abundant quantities on planets include <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">phosphine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silane" title="Silane">silane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.</p>
<p>Supercritical fluids, although not liquids, do share various properties with liquids. Underneath the thick atmospheres of the planets <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>, it is expected that these planets are composed of oceans of hot high-density fluid mixtures of water, ammonia and other volatiles. The gaseous outer layers of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> transition smoothly into oceans of <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">atmosphere of Venus</a> is 96.5% <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, which is a supercritical fluid at its surface.</p>
