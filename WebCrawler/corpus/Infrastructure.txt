<p><b>Infrastructure</b> refers to structures, systems, and facilities serving the economy of a business, industry, country, city, town, or area, including the services and facilities necessary for its <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> to function. It is typically a term to characterize the existence or condition of <i>costly 'technical structures'</i> such as <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a>, or other constructed facilities such as loading docks, cold storage chambers, electrical capacity, fuel tanks, cranes, overhead clearances, or components of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_supplies" title="Water supplies">water supplies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a>, and so forth. Infrastructure thus consists of improvements with significant cost to develop or install that return an important value over time.</p>
<p>Infrastructure can be defined as "the physical components of interrelated systems providing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodities" title="Commodities">commodities</a> and services essential to enable, sustain, or enhance societal <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">living conditions</a>." the word infrastructure has been used in English since at least 1887 and in French since at least 1875, originally meaning "The installations that form the basis for any operation or system".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_infrastructure" title="History of infrastructure">History of infrastructure</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>The word was imported from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, where it means <i><a href="/wiki/Subgrade" title="Subgrade">subgrade</a></i>, the native material underneath a constructed pavement or railway. The word is a combination of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> prefix "infra", meaning "below", and "structure". The military use of the term achieved currency in the United States after the formation of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> in the 1940s, and was then adopted by <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planners</a> in its modern civilian sense by 1970.</p>
<p>The term came to prominence in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the 1980s following the publication of <i>America in Ruins</i>, which initiated a public-policy discussion of the nation's "infrastructure crisis", purported to be caused by decades of inadequate investment and poor maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a>. This crisis discussion has contributed to the increase in infrastructure <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a> and maintenance planning in the US.</p>
<p>That public-policy discussion was hampered by lack of a precise definition for infrastructure. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> panel sought to clarify the situation by adopting the term "public works infrastructure", referring to:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"... both specific functional modes – highways, streets, roads, and bridges; mass transit; airports and airways; <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">management</a>; <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">solid waste</a> treatment and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">disposal</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_power_generation" title="Electric power generation">electric power generation</a> and transmission; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> management – and the combined system these modal elements comprise. A comprehension of infrastructure spans not only these public works facilities, but also the operating procedures, management practices, and development policies that interact together with societal demand and the physical world to facilitate the <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> of people and goods, provision of water for drinking and a variety of other uses, safe disposal of society's waste products, provision of energy where it is needed, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_transmission" title="Information transmission">transmission of information</a> within and between communities."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>, the word <i>infrastructure</i> was exclusively used to describe public assets that facilitate production, but not private assets of the same purpose. In post-Keynesian times, however, the word has grown in popularity. It has been applied with increasing generality to suggest the internal framework discernible in any technology system or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_organisation" title="Business organisation">business organisation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_and_economics" title="Infrastructure and economics">Infrastructure and economics</a></div>
<p>Infrastructure may be owned and managed by <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> or by private companies, such as sole <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">public utility</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway</a> companies. Generally, most roads, major ports and airports, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_distribution" title="Water distribution">water distribution</a> systems and <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> networks are publicly owned, whereas most energy and telecommunications networks are privately owned. Publicly owned infrastructure may be paid for from taxes, tolls, or metered user fees, whereas private infrastructure is generally paid for by metered user fees. Major investment projects are generally financed by the issuance of long-term <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a>.</p>
<p>Hence, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> owned and operated infrastructure may be developed and operated in the <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a> or in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public-private_partnership" title="Public-private partnership">public-private partnerships</a>, in addition to in the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a>. In the United States for example, public spending on infrastructure has varied between 2.3% and 3.6% of GDP since 1950. Many <a href="/wiki/List_of_financial_institutions_that_invest_in_infrastructure" title="List of financial institutions that invest in infrastructure">financial institutions invest in infrastructure</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Hard.22_and_.22soft.22_infrastructure">"Hard" and "soft" infrastructure</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hard_infrastructure" title="Hard infrastructure">Hard infrastructure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soft_infrastructure" title="Soft infrastructure">Soft infrastructure</a></div>
<p>"Hard" infrastructure refers to the large physical networks necessary for the functioning of a modern industrial nation, whereas "soft" infrastructure refers to all the <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> which are required to maintain the economy, health, and cultural and social standards of a country, such as the <a href="/wiki/Financial_system" title="Financial system">financial system</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_system" title="Education system">education system</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_system" title="Health care system">health care system</a>, the system of government, and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement" title="Law enforcement">law enforcement</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_services" title="Emergency services">emergency services</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_the_term">Uses of the term</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_and_construction">Engineering and construction</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineers" title="Engineers">Engineers</a> generally limit the use of the term "infrastructure" to describe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_assets" title="Fixed assets">fixed assets</a> that are in the form of a large network, in other words, "hard" infrastructure. Recent efforts to devise more generic definitions of infrastructures have typically referred to the network aspects of most of the structures, and to the accumulated value of investments in the networks as assets. One such effort defines infrastructure as the network of assets "where the system as a whole is intended to be maintained indefinitely at a specified standard of service by the continuing replacement and refurbishment of its components".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_defense_and_economic_development">Civil defense and economic development</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense_by_country" title="Civil defense by country">Civil defense by country</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Civil_defense" title="Civil defense">Civil defense</a> planners and <a href="/wiki/Development_economics" title="Development economics">developmental economists</a> generally refer to both hard and soft infrastructure, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_services" title="Public services">public services</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schools" title="Schools">schools</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hospitals" title="Hospitals">hospitals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_services" title="Emergency services">emergency services</a> such as police and fire fighting, and basic <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a>. The notion of <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure-based_development" title="Infrastructure-based development">Infrastructure-based development</a> combining long-term infrastructure investments by government agencies at central and regional levels with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_private_partnership" title="Public private partnership">public private partnerships</a> has proven popular among Asian- notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singaporean" title="Singaporean">Singaporean</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a>, Mainland European and Latin American economists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">Military</a> strategists use the term infrastructure to refer to all building and permanent installations necessary for the support of military forces, whether they are stationed in bases, being deployed or engaged in operations, such as barracks, headquarters, airfields, communications facilities, stores of military equipment, port installations, and maintenance stations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_infrastructure">Critical infrastructure</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Critical_infrastructure" title="Critical infrastructure">Critical infrastructure</a></div>
<p>The term <i>critical infrastructure</i> has been widely adopted to distinguish those infrastructure elements that, if significantly damaged or destroyed, would cause serious disruption of the dependent system or organization. <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">Storm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> damage leading to loss of certain transportation routes in a city, for example bridges crossing a river, could make it impossible for people to evacuate, and for <a href="/wiki/Emergency_service" title="Emergency service">emergency services</a> to operate; these routes would be deemed critical infrastructure. Similarly, an on-line booking system might be critical infrastructure for an <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a>. These elements of infrastructure are often the focus of recovery efforts in the aftermath of natural disasters. Damage to critical infrastructure could also result in a public safety hazard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_infrastructure">Urban infrastructure</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_infrastructure" title="Municipal infrastructure">Municipal infrastructure</a></div>
<p><i>Urban</i> or <i>municipal infrastructure</i> refers to hard infrastructure systems generally owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>, such as streets, water distribution, and sewers. It may also include some of the facilities associated with soft infrastructure, such as parks, public pools, schools, hospitals and libraries. From 2016, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPFL" title="EPFL">EPFL</a>, one of the top universities in the world, is offering an <a href="/wiki/Massive_open_online_course" title="Massive open online course">open online course</a> on Management of Urban Infrastructures which covers the main characteristics of urban infrastructures and basic principles of urban infrastructure management.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_infrastructure">Green infrastructure</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">Green infrastructure</a></div>
<p><i>Green infrastructure</i> is a concept that highlights the importance of the natural environment in decisions about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning">land use planning</a>. In particular there is an emphasis on the "life support" functions provided by a network of natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Interconnectivity" title="Interconnectivity">interconnectivity</a> to support long-term <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. Examples include clean <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and healthy soils, as well as the more <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentric</a> functions such as <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a> and providing shade and shelter in and around towns and cities. The concept can be extended to apply to the management of <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> runoff at the local level through the use of natural systems, or engineered systems that mimic natural systems, to treat polluted <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">runoff</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism">Marxism</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, the term infrastructure is sometimes used as a synonym for "base" in the <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectic</a> synthetic pair <i><a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">base and superstructure</a></i>. However the Marxist notion of base is broader than the non-Marxist use of the term infrastructure, and some soft infrastructure, such as laws, governance, regulations and standards, would be considered by Marxists to be part of the superstructure, not the base.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h3>
<p>In other applications, the term infrastructure may refer to <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, informal and formal channels of communication, software development tools, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political" title="Political">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networks</a>, or beliefs held by members of particular groups. Still underlying these more conceptual uses is the idea that infrastructure provides organizing structure and support for the system or organization it serves, whether it is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>, or a collection of people with common interests. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_management" title="Information technology management">IT infrastructure</a>, research infrastructure, terrorist infrastructure, employment infrastructure and tourism infrastructure. the transportation of crude oil and petroleum products is being taken care by ONGC</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span></h2>
<p>The term <i>infrastructure</i> is often confused with the following overlapping or related concepts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_improvement_and_land_development">Land improvement and land development</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_improvement" title="Land improvement">Land improvement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">Land development</a></div>
<p>The terms <i>land improvement</i> and <i>land development</i> are general terms that in some contexts may include infrastructure, but in the context of a discussion of infrastructure would refer only to smaller scale systems or works that are not included in infrastructure because they are typically limited to a single <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">parcel of land</a>, and are owned and operated by the land owner. For example, an irrigation canal that serves a region or district would be included with infrastructure, but the private irrigation systems on individual land parcels would be considered land improvements, not infrastructure. Service connections to municipal service and public utility networks would also be considered land improvements, not infrastructure.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_works_and_public_services">Public works and public services</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">Public works</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_services" title="Public services">Public services</a></div>
<p>The term <i>public works</i> includes government owned and operated infrastructure as well as public buildings such as schools and court houses. Public works generally refers to physical assets needed to deliver <i>public services</i>. Public services include both infrastructure and services generally provided by government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure_in_the_developing_world">Infrastructure in the developing world</span></h2>
<p>According to researchers at the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a>, the lack of infrastructure in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> represents one of the most significant limitations to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> and achievement of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals (MDGs)</a>. Infrastructure investments and maintenance can be very expensive, especially in such as areas as landlocked, rural and sparsely populated countries in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It has been argued that infrastructure investments contributed to more than half of Africa's improved growth performance between 1990 and 2005, and increased investment is necessary to maintain growth and tackle <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. The returns to investment in infrastructure are very significant, with on average thirty to forty percent returns for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> (ICT) investments, over forty percent for <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> generation, and eighty percent for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roads" title="Roads">roads</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_differences">Regional differences</span></h3>
<p>The demand for infrastructure, both by consumers and by companies is much higher than the amount invested. There are severe constraints on the supply side of the provision of infrastructure in Asia. The infrastructure financing gap between what is invested in <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> (around US$48 billion) and what is needed (US$228 billion) is around US$180 billion every year.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, three percent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> (around US$71 billion) would need to be invested in infrastructure in order to satisfy demand, yet in 2005, for example, only around two percent was invested leaving a financing gap of approximately US$24 billion.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, in order to reach the seven percent annual growth calculated to be required to meet the MDGs by 2015 would require infrastructure investments of about fifteen percent of GDP, or around US$93 billion a year. In <a href="/wiki/Fragile_state" title="Fragile state">fragile states</a>, over thirty-seven percent of GDP would be required.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_funding">Sources of funding</span></h3>
<p>Currently, the source of financing varies significantly across sectors. Some sectors are dominated by government spending, others by <a href="/wiki/Development_aid" title="Development aid">overseas development aid (ODA)</a>, and yet others by private investors.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, governments spend around US$9.4 billion out of a total of US$24.9 billion. In <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, governments represent almost all spending. In <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> a majority of investment is government spending. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_and_communication_technologies" title="Information and communication technologies">ICT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>, the private sector represents the majority of capital expenditure. Overall, between them aid, the private sector, and non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> financiers exceed government spending. The private sector spending alone equals state capital expenditure, though the majority is focused on ICT infrastructure investments. External financing increased in the 2000s (decade) and in Africa alone external infrastructure investments increased from US$7 billion in 2002 to US$27 billion in 2009. <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, in particular, has emerged as an important investor.</p>
