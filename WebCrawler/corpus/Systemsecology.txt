<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p><b>Systems ecology</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> field of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, a subset of <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_science" title="Earth system science">Earth system science</a>, that takes a <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> approach to the study of ecological systems, especially <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. Systems ecology can be seen as an application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_systems_theory" title="General systems theory">general systems theory</a> to ecology. Central to the systems ecology approach is the idea that an ecosystem is a <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex system</a> exhibiting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergent_properties" title="Emergent properties">emergent properties</a>. Systems ecology focuses on interactions and transactions within and between biological and ecological systems, and is especially concerned with the way the functioning of ecosystems can be influenced by human interventions. It uses and extends concepts from <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> and develops other macroscopic descriptions of complex systems.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<p>Systems ecology seeks a <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> view of the interactions and transactions within and between biological and ecological systems. Systems ecologists realise that the function of any ecosystem can be influenced by human economics in fundamental ways. They have therefore taken an additional transdisciplinary step by including economics in the consideration of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological-economic</a> systems. In the words of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R.L._Kitching&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="R.L. Kitching (page does not exist)">R.L. Kitching</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Systems ecology can be defined as the approach to the study of ecology of organisms using the techniques and philosophy of systems analysis: that is, the methods and tools developed, largely in engineering, for studying, characteriszing and making predictions about complex entities, that is, systems..</i></li>
<li><i>In any study of an ecological system, an essential early procedure is to draw a diagram of the system of interest ... diagrams indicate the system's <b>boundaries</b> by a solid line. Within these boundaries, series of components are isolated which have been chosen to represent that portion of the world in which the systems analyst is interested ... If there are no connections across the systems' boundaries with the surrounding <b>systems environments</b>, the systems are described as</i> closed<i>. Ecological work, however, deals almost exclusively with</i> open <i>systems.</i></li>
</ul>
<p>As a mode of scientific enquiry, a central feature of Systems Ecology is the general application of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_of_energetics" title="Principles of energetics">principles of energetics</a> to all systems at any scale. Perhaps the most notable proponent of this view was <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Howard T. Odum</a> - sometimes considered the father of ecosystems ecology. In this approach the principles of energetics constitute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystem_principles" title="Ecosystem principles">ecosystem principles</a>. Reasoning by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Formal_analogy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Formal analogy (page does not exist)">formal analogy</a> from one system to another enables the Systems Ecologist to see principles functioning in an analogous manner across system-scale boundaries. H.T. Odum commonly used the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Systems_Language" title="Energy Systems Language">Energy Systems Language</a> as a tool for making systems diagrams and flow charts.</p>
<p>The fourth of these principles, the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_power_principle" title="Maximum power principle">principle of maximum power efficiency</a>, takes central place in the analysis and synthesis of ecological systems. The fourth principle suggests that the most evolutionarily advantageous system function occurs when the environmental load matches the internal resistance of the system. The further the environmental load is from matching the internal resistance, the further the system is away from its sustainable steady state. Therefore, the systems ecologist engages in a task of resistance and impedance matching in <a href="/wiki/Ecological_engineering" title="Ecological engineering">ecological engineering</a>, just as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_engineer" title="Electronic engineer">electronic engineer</a> would do.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closely_related_fields">Closely related fields</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_ecology">Deep ecology</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology</a></div>
<p>Deep ecology is an ideology whose metaphysical underpinnings are deeply concerned with the science of ecology. The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Naess</a>, a Norwegian philosopher, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gandhi" title="Gandhi">Gandhian</a> scholar, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_activist" title="Environmental activist">environmental activist</a>. He argues that the prevailing approach to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_management" title="Environmental management">environmental management</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropocentric" title="Anthropocentric">anthropocentric</a>, and that the natural environment is not only "more complex than we imagine, it is more complex than we can imagine." Naess formulated deep ecology in 1973 at an environmental conference in Budapest.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joanna_Macy" title="Joanna Macy">Joanna Macy</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Seed" title="John Seed">John Seed</a>, and others developed Naess' thesis into a branch they called <i>experiential deep ecology</i>. Their efforts were motivated by a need they perceived for the development of an "<a href="/wiki/Ecological_self" title="Ecological self">ecological self</a>", which views the human ego as an integrated part of a living system that encompasses the individual. They sought to transcend <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a> with a deeper self-interest based on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biospherical_equality&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biospherical equality (page does not exist)">biospherical equality</a> beyond <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Human_chauvinism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Human chauvinism (page does not exist)">human chauvinism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_systems_engineering_and_management">Earth systems engineering and management</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth_systems_engineering_and_management" title="Earth systems engineering and management">Earth systems engineering and management</a></div>
<p>Earth systems engineering and management (ESEM) is a discipline used to analyze, design, engineer and manage <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complex</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a>. It entails a wide range of subject areas including anthropology, engineering, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a>, ethics and philosophy. At its core, ESEM looks to "rationally design and manage coupled human-natural systems in a highly integrated and ethical fashion"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_economics">Ecological economics</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a></div>
<p>Ecological economics is a transdisciplinary field of academic research that addresses the dynamic and spatial interdependence between human economies and natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. Ecological economics brings together and connects different disciplines, within the natural and social sciences but especially between these broad areas. As the name suggests, the field is made up of researchers with a background in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>. An important motivation for the emergence of ecological economics has been criticism on the assumptions and approaches of traditional (mainstream) <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental and resource economics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_energetics">Ecological energetics</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_energetics" title="Ecological energetics">Ecological energetics</a></div>
<p>Ecological energetics is the quantitative study of the flow of energy through ecological systems. It aims to uncover the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_law" title="Scientific law">principles</a> which describe the propensity of such energy flows through the trophic, or 'energy availing' levels of ecological networks. In systems ecology the principles of ecosystem energy flows or "ecosystem laws" (i.e. principles of ecological energetics) are considered formally analogous to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_of_energetics" title="Principles of energetics">principles of energetics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_humanities">Ecological humanities</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_humanities" title="Ecological humanities">Ecological humanities</a></div>
<p>Ecological humanities aims to bridge the divides between the sciences and the humanities, and between <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_culture" title="Eastern culture">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous</a> ways of knowing nature. Like ecocentric political theory, the ecological humanities are characterised by a connectivity ontology and a commitment to two fundamental axioms relating to the need to submit to <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> laws and to see humanity as part of a larger living system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_ecology">Ecosystem ecology</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_ecology" title="Ecosystem ecology">Ecosystem ecology</a></div>
<p>Ecosystem ecology is the integrated study of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">biotic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abiotic" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> components of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and their interactions within an ecosystem framework. This <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> examines how ecosystems work and relates this to their components such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. Ecosystem ecology examines physical and biological structure and examines how these ecosystem characteristics interact.</p>
<p>The relationship between systems ecology and ecosystem ecology is complex. Much of systems ecology can be considered a subset of ecosystem ecology. Ecosystem ecology also utilizes methods that have little to do with the holistic approach of systems ecology. However, systems ecology more actively considers external influences such as economics that usually fall outside the bounds of ecosystem ecology. Whereas ecosystem ecology can be defined as the scientific study of ecosystems, systems ecology is more of a particular approach to the study of ecological systems and phenomena that interact with these systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_ecology">Industrial ecology</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">Industrial ecology</a></div>
<p>Industrial ecology is the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_process" title="Industrial process">industrial processes</a> as linear (open loop) systems, in which resource and capital investments move through the system to become waste, to a closed loop system where wastes become inputs for new processes.</p>
