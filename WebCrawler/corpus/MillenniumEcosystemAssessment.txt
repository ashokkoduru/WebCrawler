<p>The <b>Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</b> (MEA) is a major assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on the environment</a>, called for by the United Nations Secretary-General Kofi Annan in 2000, launched in 2001 and published in 2005 with more than $14 million of grants. It popularized the term <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, the benefits gained by humans from <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>During the 1990s, international conventions such as the UNEP <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_to_Combat_Desertification" title="Convention to Combat Desertification">Convention to Combat Desertification</a> identified the need for a global scientific ecosystem assessment. There had been advances in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_economics" title="Resource economics">resource economics</a> with little effect on <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental policy</a>. In November 1998, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> published a study called "Protecting our Planet, Securing our Future: Linkages Among Global Environmental Issues and Human Needs". In 2001, the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA) was launched with work over a period of four years. Over 1300 contributors from 95 countries were involved as authors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span></h2>
<p>In May 2000 the <a href="/wiki/Global_Environment_Facility" title="Global Environment Facility">Global Environment Facility</a> approved a $7 million grant, followed in July 2000 by a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Foundation" title="United Nations Foundation">United Nations Foundation</a> $4 million grant and financial support from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Norway" title="Government of Norway">government of Norway</a> for the first meeting of the Board of the MA in <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>, and in December 2000 a $2.4 million grant by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packard_Foundation" title="Packard Foundation">Packard Foundation</a> for a total of more than $13.4 million, considered "75% of the full budget".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Findings">Findings</span></h2>
<p>The MA was published in 2005 and made four main assessments:</p>
<ul>
<li>Over the past 50 years, humans have changed ecosystems more rapidly and extensively than in any comparable period of time in human history, largely to meet rapidly growing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_demand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Resource demand (page does not exist)">demands</a> for food, fresh water, timber, fiber and fuel. This has resulted in a substantial and largely irreversible loss in the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">diversity of life</a> on Earth.</li>
<li>The changes to ecosystems have contributed to substantial net gains in human <a href="/wiki/Well-being" title="Well-being">well-being</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>, but these gains have been achieved at growing costs in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">degradation</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, increased risks of nonlinear changes, and the exacerbation of <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> for some groups of people. These problems, unless addressed, will substantially diminish the benefits that future generations obtain from ecosystems.</li>
<li>The degradation of ecosystem services could grow significantly worse during the first half of this century and is a barrier to achieving the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>.</li>
<li>The challenge of reversing the degradation of ecosystem while meeting increasing demands for services can be partially met under some scenarios considered by the MA, but will involve significant changes in policies, institutions and practices that are not currently under way. Many options exist to conserve or enhance specific ecosystem services in ways that reduce negative trade-offs or that provide positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synergies" title="Synergies">synergies</a> with other ecosystem services.</li>
</ul>
<p>The bottom line of the MA findings has been that human actions are <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depleting</a> Earth’s natural capital, putting such strain on the environment that the ability of the planet’s ecosystems to sustain future generations can no longer be taken for granted. At the same time, the assessment shows that with appropriate actions it is possible to reverse the degradation of many ecosystem services over the next 50 years, but the changes in policy and practice required are substantial and not currently underway.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<p>In 2008, a report calculated that the world's richest countries caused environmental damage to developing nations at more than the entire third world debt of $1.8 trillion.</p>
