<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Bioaccumulation</b> refers to the accumulation of substances, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a>, or other chemicals in an organism. Bioaccumulation occurs when an organism absorbs a - possibly <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">toxic</a> - substance at a rate faster than that at which the substance is lost by <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Excretion" title="Excretion">excretion</a>. Thus, the longer the <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> of a toxic substance the greater the risk of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chronic_poisoning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chronic poisoning (page does not exist)">chronic poisoning</a>, even if <a href="/wiki/Measured_environmental_concentration" title="Measured environmental concentration">environmental levels</a> of the toxin are not very high. Bioaccumulation, for example in fish, can be predicted by models. Hypotheses for molecular size cutoff criteria for use as bioaccumulation potential indicators are not supported by data. <a href="/wiki/Biotransformation" title="Biotransformation">Biotransformation</a> can strongly modify bioaccumulation of chemicals in an organism.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bioconcentration" title="Bioconcentration">Bioconcentration</a> is a related but more specific term, referring to uptake and accumulation of a substance from water alone. By contrast, bioaccumulation refers to uptake from all sources combined (e.g. water, food, air, etc.)</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span></h2>
<p>An example of poisoning in the workplace can be seen from the phrase "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/As_mad_as_a_hatter" title="As mad as a hatter">as mad as a hatter</a>" (18th and 19th century England). The process for stiffening the felt used in making hats more than a hundred years ago involved <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, which forms organic species such as <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>, which is lipid-soluble, and tends to accumulate in the brain, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a>. Other lipid-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble">soluble</a> (fat-soluble) poisons include <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyllead" title="Tetraethyllead">tetraethyllead</a> compounds (the <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in leaded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">petrol</a>), and <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>. These compounds are stored in the body's <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>, and when the <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">fatty tissues</a> are used for energy, the compounds are released and cause acute poisoning.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90">Strontium-90</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">fallout</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bombs</a>, is chemically similar enough to calcium that it is utilized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osteogenesis" title="Osteogenesis">osteogenesis</a>, where its radiation can cause damage for a long time.</p>
<p>Naturally produced toxins can also bioaccumulate. The marine <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal blooms</a> known as "<a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">red tides</a>" can result in local filter feeding organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a> becoming toxic; coral fish can be responsible for the poisoning known as <a href="/wiki/Ciguatera" title="Ciguatera">ciguatera</a> when they accumulate a toxin called <a href="/wiki/Ciguatoxin" title="Ciguatoxin">ciguatoxin</a> from reef algae.</p>
<p>Some animal species exhibit bioaccumulation as a mode of defense; by consuming toxic plants or animal prey, a species may accumulate the toxin, which then presents a deterrent to a potential predator. One example is the <a href="/wiki/Manduca_sexta" title="Manduca sexta">tobacco hornworm</a>, which concentrates <a href="/wiki/Nicotine" title="Nicotine">nicotine</a> to a toxic level in its body as it consumes <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> plants. Poisoning of small consumers can be passed along the food chain to affect the consumers later on. Other compounds that are not normally considered toxic can be accumulated to toxic levels in organisms. The classic example is of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">Vitamin A</a>, which becomes concentrated in <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivore</a> <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">livers</a> of e.g. <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a>: as a pure carnivore that feeds on other carnivores (seals), they accumulate extremely large amounts of Vitamin A in their livers. It was known by the native peoples of the Arctic that the livers of carnivores should not be eaten, but Arctic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorers</a> have suffered <a href="/wiki/Hypervitaminosis_A" title="Hypervitaminosis A">Hypervitaminosis A</a> from eating the bear livers (and there has been at least one example of similar poisoning of <a href="/wiki/Xavier_Mertz" title="Xavier Mertz">Antarctic explorers</a> eating <a href="/wiki/Husky" title="Husky">husky</a> <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> livers). One notable example of this is the expedition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mawson" title="Mawson">Sir Douglas Mawson</a>, where his exploration companion died from eating the liver of one of their dogs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coastal_fish" title="Coastal fish">Coastal fish</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_toadfish" title="Smooth toadfish">smooth toadfish</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_puffin" title="Atlantic puffin">Atlantic puffin</a>) are often monitored for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metal</a> bioaccumulation.</p>
<p>In some eutrophic aquatic systems, <a href="/wiki/Biodilution" title="Biodilution">biodilution</a> can occur. This trend is a decrease in a contaminant with an increase in trophic level and is due to higher concentrations of algae and bacteria to "dilute" the concentration of the pollutant.</p>
