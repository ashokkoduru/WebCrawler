<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p><b>Nevada Solar One</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> plant, with a nominal capacity of 64 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">MW</a> and maximum steam turbine power output up to 72 MW net (75 MW gross), spread over an area of 400 acres (160 ha). The projected <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> emissions avoided is equivalent to taking approximately 20,000 cars off the road annually. The project required an investment of $266 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>, and the project officially went to operation in June 2007. Electricity production is estimated to be 134 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hours" title="Kilowatt hours">kilowatt hours</a> per year.</p>
<p>It is the second <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">solar thermal energy</a> (STE) power plant built in the United States in more than 16 years, and the largest STE plant built in the world since 1991. It is located in <a href="/wiki/Eldorado_Valley" title="Eldorado Valley">Eldorado Valley</a> in the southwest fringe of <a href="/wiki/Boulder_City,_Nevada" title="Boulder City, Nevada">Boulder City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, and was built in that city's Energy Resource Zone, which requires renewable generation as part of plant development permits; Nevada Solar One was approved as part of <a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy" title="Duke Energy">Duke Energy</a>'s larger El Dorado Energy project that built 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GigaWatt" title="GigaWatt">GW</a> of electrical generation capacity. The solar trough generation was built by Acciona Solar Power, a partially owned subsidiary of Spanish conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Acciona_Energy" title="Acciona Energy">Acciona Energy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Engineers_%26_Constructors" title="Lauren Engineers &amp; Constructors">Lauren Engineers &amp; Constructors</a> (Abilene, TX) was the EPC contractor for the project. Acciona purchased a 55 percent stake in Solargenix (formerly Duke Solar) and Acciona owns 95 percent of the project. Nevada Solar One is unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/The_Solar_Project#Solar_One" title="The Solar Project">Solar One</a> power plant in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>In 2006, located 30 miles north of Tucson, <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Public_Service" title="Arizona Public Service">Arizona Public Service's</a> Saguaro Solar Facility opened, with 1 MW of electrical generation capacity. Nevada Solar One went online for commercial use on June 27, 2007. It uses similar technology and was constructed over a period of 16 months. The total project site is approximately 400 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a> (0.6 mi² / 1.6 km²), while the solar collectors cover 300 acres (1.2 km).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span></h2>
<p>Nevada Solar One uses proprietary technology to track the sun’s location and concentrate its rays during peak demand hours. The plant uses 760 <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic trough</a> concentrators with more than 182,000 mirrors that concentrate the sun’s rays onto more than 18,240 receiver tubes placed at the focal axis of the troughs and containing a heat transfer fluid (solar receivers). Fluid that heats up to 735 °F (391 °C) flows through these tubes and is used to produce steam that drives a <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> SST-700 <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a>, adapted to the specific requirements of the CSP technology, which is connected to a generator to produce electricity.</p>
<p>The mirrors are manufactured by Flabeg AG in Germany. In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">power tower concentrator</a> concept that California's original Solar One project uses. The specially coated tubes, made of glass and steel, were designed and produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solel_Solar_Systems" title="Solel Solar Systems">Solel Solar Systems</a> as well as by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schott_Glass" title="Schott Glass">Schott Glass</a> in Germany. Motion control was supplied by <a href="/wiki/Parker_Hannifin" title="Parker Hannifin">Parker Hannifin</a>, from components by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ansco_Machine_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ansco Machine Company (page does not exist)">Ansco Machine Company</a>.</p>
<p>Solar thermal power plants designed for solar-only generation are well matched to summer noon peak loads in areas with significant cooling demands, such as the southwestern United States. Using thermal energy storage systems, solar thermal operating periods can be extended to meet base load needs. Given Nevada's land and sun resources the state has the theoretical ability to have more than 600 GW of electrical generation capacity using solar thermal concentrators like those used by Nevada Solar One. It has been proposed that massive expansion of solar plants such as Nevada Solar One has the potential to provide sufficient electricity to power the entire United States.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trough_concentrator" title="Trough concentrator">Parabolic concentrator</a> facilities have been successfully operating in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s Mojave Desert commercially since 1984 with a combined generating capacity of 354MW from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems" title="Solar Energy Generating Systems">Solar Energy Generating Systems</a>. About 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough_power" title="Parabolic trough power">parabolic trough power</a> plants are operating in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Spain" title="Solar power in Spain">Solar power in Spain</a>) and more are in construction or proposed, and two 110 MW plants in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.</p>
