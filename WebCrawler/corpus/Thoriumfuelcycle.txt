<p>The <b>thorium fuel cycle</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">nuclear fuel cycle</a> that uses the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium-232" title="Thorium-232"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, as the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_material" title="Fertile material">fertile material</a>. In the reactor, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_transmutation" title="Nuclear transmutation">transmuted</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> artificial <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> isotope <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-233" title="Uranium-233"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> which is the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Natural_uranium" title="Natural uranium">natural uranium</a>, natural thorium contains only trace amounts of fissile material (such as <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium-231" title="Thorium-231"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">231<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>), which are insufficient to initiate a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction" title="Nuclear chain reaction">nuclear chain reaction</a>. Additional fissile material or another neutron source are necessary to initiate the fuel cycle. In a thorium-fuelled reactor, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> absorbs <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> eventually to produce <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>. This parallels the process in uranium <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactors</a> whereby fertile <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">238<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> absorbs neutrons to form fissile <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Plutonium-239" title="Plutonium-239"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. Depending on the design of the reactor and fuel cycle, the generated <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> either fissions <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a> or is chemically separated from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Used_nuclear_fuel" title="Used nuclear fuel">used nuclear fuel</a> and formed into new nuclear fuel.</p>
<p>The thorium fuel cycle claims several potential advantages over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_fuel_cycle" title="Uranium fuel cycle">uranium fuel cycle</a>, including thorium's <a href="/wiki/Thorium#Occurrence" title="Thorium">greater abundance</a>, superior physical and nuclear properties, better resistance to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear weapons proliferation</a> and reduced <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> production.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Concerns about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_depletion" title="Uranium depletion">limits of worldwide uranium resources</a> motivated initial interest in the thorium fuel cycle. It was envisioned that as uranium reserves were depleted, thorium would supplement uranium as a fertile material. However, for most countries uranium was relatively abundant and research in thorium fuel cycles waned. A notable exception was <a href="/wiki/India%27s_three-stage_nuclear_power_programme" title="India's three-stage nuclear power programme">India's three-stage nuclear power programme</a>. In the twenty-first century thorium's potential for improving proliferation resistance and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" title="Nuclear waste">waste</a> characteristics led to renewed interest in the thorium fuel cycle.</p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> in the 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/Molten-Salt_Reactor_Experiment" title="Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment">Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment</a> used <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> as the fissile fuel as an experiment to demonstrate a part of the Molten Salt Breeder Reactor that was designed to operate on the thorium fuel cycle. <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">Molten salt reactor</a> (MSR) experiments assessed thorium's feasibility, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium(IV)_fluoride" title="Thorium(IV) fluoride">thorium(IV) fluoride</a> dissolved in a <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">molten salt</a> fluid that eliminated the need to fabricate fuel elements. The MSR program was defunded in 1976 after its patron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alvin_Weinberg" title="Alvin Weinberg">Alvin Weinberg</a> was fired.</p>
<p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Rubbia" title="Carlo Rubbia">Carlo Rubbia</a> proposed the concept of an <a href="/wiki/Energy_amplifier" title="Energy amplifier">energy amplifier</a> or "accelerator driven system" (ADS), which he saw as a novel and safe way to produce nuclear energy that exploited existing accelerator technologies. Rubbia's proposal offered the potential to incinerate high-activity nuclear waste and produce energy from natural <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and depleted <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirk_Sorensen" title="Kirk Sorensen">Kirk Sorensen</a>, former NASA scientist and Chief Nuclear Technologist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teledyne_Brown_Engineering" title="Teledyne Brown Engineering">Teledyne Brown Engineering</a>, has been a long-time promoter of thorium fuel cycle and particularly <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fluoride_thorium_reactor" title="Liquid fluoride thorium reactor">liquid fluoride thorium reactors</a> (LFTRs). He first researched thorium reactors while working at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, while evaluating power plant designs suitable for lunar colonies. In 2006 Sorensen started "energyfromthorium.com" to promote and make information available about this technology.</p>
<p>A 2011 MIT study concluded that although there is little in the way of barriers to a thorium fuel cycle, with current or near term light-water reactor designs there is also little incentive for any significant market penetration to occur. As such they conclude there is little chance of thorium cycles replacing conventional uranium cycles in the current nuclear power market, despite the potential benefits.</p>

<p>In the thorium cycle, fuel is formed when <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">captures</a> a <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> (whether in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_reactor" title="Fast reactor">fast reactor</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_reactor" title="Thermal reactor">thermal reactor</a>) to become <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium-233" title="Thorium-233"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. This normally emits an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antineutrino" title="Antineutrino">anti-neutrino</a> (<span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span><span style="text-decoration:overline;">ν</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>) by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>β<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span> decay</a> to become <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protactinium-233" title="Protactinium-233"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. This then emits another electron and anti-neutrino by a second <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><br/></span>β<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/></span></span> decay to become <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, the fuel:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fission_product_wastes">Fission product wastes</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">Nuclear fission</a> produces radioactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission products</a> which can have half-lives from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LLFP#Short-term" title="LLFP">days</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LLFP" title="LLFP">greater than 200,000 years</a>. According to some toxicity studies, the thorium cycle can fully recycle actinide wastes and only emit fission product wastes, and after a few hundred years, the waste from a thorium reactor can be less toxic than the <a href="/wiki/Uranium_ore" title="Uranium ore">uranium ore</a> that would have been used to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_enriched_uranium" title="Low enriched uranium">low enriched uranium</a> fuel for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" title="Light water reactor">light water reactor</a> of the same power. Other studies assume some actinide losses and find that actinide wastes dominate thorium cycle waste radioactivity at some future periods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Actinide_wastes">Actinide wastes</span></h3>
<p>In a reactor, when a neutron hits a fissile atom (such as certain isotopes of uranium), it either splits the nucleus or is captured and transmutes the atom. In the case of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, the transmutations tend to produce useful nuclear fuels rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transuranic" title="Transuranic">transuranic</a> wastes. When <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> absorbs a neutron, it either fissions or becomes <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-234" title="Uranium-234"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">234<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. The chance of fissioning on absorption of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutron</a> is about 92%; the capture-to-fission ratio of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, therefore, is about 1:12 — which is better than the corresponding capture vs. fission ratios of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> (about 1:6), or <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> or <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Plutonium-241" title="Plutonium-241"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">241<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> (both about 1:3). The result is less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transuranic" title="Transuranic">transuranic</a> waste than in a reactor using the uranium-plutonium fuel cycle.</p>

<p><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">234<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, like most <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a> with an even number of neutrons, is not fissile, but neutron capture produces fissile <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>. If the fissile isotope fails to fission on neutron capture, it produces <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-236" title="Uranium-236"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">236<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptunium-237" title="Neptunium-237"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">237<br/></span>Np<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Plutonium-238" title="Plutonium-238"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">238<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, and eventually fissile <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> and heavier <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_plutonium" title="Isotopes of plutonium">isotopes of plutonium</a>. The <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">237<br/></span>Np<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> can be removed and stored as waste or retained and transmuted to plutonium, where more of it fissions, while the remainder becomes <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Plutonium-242" title="Plutonium-242"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">242<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, then <a href="/wiki/Americium" title="Americium">americium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Curium" title="Curium">curium</a>, which in turn can be removed as waste or returned to reactors for further transmutation and fission.</p>
<p>However, the <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protactinium-231" title="Protactinium-231"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">231<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> (with a half-life of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7012103193352000000♠</span>3.27<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10 years</span>) formed via (<i>n</i>,2<i>n</i>) reactions with <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> (yielding <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">231<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> that decays to <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protactinium-231" title="Protactinium-231"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">231<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>), while not a transuranic waste, is a major contributor to the long-term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotoxic" title="Radiotoxic">radiotoxicity</a> of spent nuclear fuel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uranium-232_contamination">Uranium-232 contamination</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Uranium-232" title="Uranium-232">Uranium-232</a> is also formed in this process, via (<i>n</i>,2<i>n</i>) reactions between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_neutron" title="Fast neutron">fast neutrons</a> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protactinium-233" title="Protactinium-233"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>:</p>

<p>Uranium-232 has a relatively short half-life (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009217431864000000♠</span>68.9 years</span>), and some <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> emit high energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a>, such as <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radon-224" title="Radon-224"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">224<br/></span>Rn<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth-212" title="Bismuth-212"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">212<br/></span>Bi<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> and particularly <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thallium-208" title="Thallium-208"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">208<br/></span>Tl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium_series" title="Thorium series">full decay chain</a>, along with half-lives and relevant gamma energies, is:</p>

<p><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Uranium-232" title="Uranium-232"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> decays to <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium-228" title="Thorium-228"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">228<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span> where it joins the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium_series" title="Thorium series">decay chain of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span></a></p>

<p>Thorium-cycle fuels produce hard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_emission" title="Gamma emission">gamma emissions</a>, which damage electronics, limiting their use in military bomb triggers. <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> cannot be chemically separated from <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Used_nuclear_fuel" title="Used nuclear fuel">used nuclear fuel</a>; however, chemical separation of thorium from uranium removes the decay product <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">228<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> and the radiation from the rest of the decay chain, which gradually build up as <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">228<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> reaccumulates. The hard gamma emissions also create a radiological hazard which requires remote handling during reprocessing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fuel">Nuclear fuel</span></h2>
<p>As a fertile material thorium is similar to <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">238<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, the major part of natural and depleted uranium. The thermal neutron absorption <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_cross_section" title="Nuclear cross section">cross section</a> (σ<sub>a</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonance_integral" title="Resonance integral">resonance integral</a> (average of neutron cross sections over intermediate neutron energies) for <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> are about three and one third times those of the respective values for <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">238<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span></h3>
<p>Thorium is estimated to be about three to four times more abundant than uranium in Earth's crust, although present knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Thorium#Distribution" title="Thorium">reserves</a> is limited. Current demand for thorium has been satisfied as a by-product of <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare-earth</a> extraction from <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> sands.</p>
<p>Although the thermal neutron fission cross section (σ<sub>f</sub>) of the resulting <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is comparable to <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, it has a much lower capture cross section (σ<sub>γ</sub>) than the latter two fissile isotopes, providing fewer non-fissile neutron absorptions and improved <a href="/wiki/Neutron_economy" title="Neutron economy">neutron economy</a>. Finally, the ratio of neutrons released per neutron absorbed (η) in <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is greater than two over a wide range of energies, including the thermal spectrum; as a result, thorium-based fuels can be the basis for a <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor#Thermal_breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">thermal breeder reactor</a>. A breeding reactor in the uranium - plutonium cycle needs to use a fast neutron spectrum, because in the thermal spectrum one neutron absorbed by <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> on average leads to less than two neutrons.</p>
<p>Thorium-based fuels also display favorable physical and chemical properties that improve reactor and <a href="/wiki/Deep_geological_repository" title="Deep geological repository">repository</a> performance. Compared to the predominant reactor fuel, <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">ThO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>) has a higher <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>, higher <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a>, and lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a>. Thorium dioxide also exhibits greater <a href="/wiki/Chemical_stability" title="Chemical stability">chemical stability</a> and, unlike uranium dioxide, does not further <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a>.</p>
<p>Because the <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> produced in thorium fuels is significantly contaminated with <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> in proposed power reactor designs, thorium-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Used_nuclear_fuel" title="Used nuclear fuel">used nuclear fuel</a> possesses inherent <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">proliferation</a> resistance. <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> cannot be <a href="/wiki/Separation_process" title="Separation process">chemically separated</a> from <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> and has several <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> that emit high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a>. These high-energy photons are a <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">radiological hazard</a> that necessitate the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remote_handling" title="Remote handling">remote handling</a> of separated uranium and aid in the passive <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_detection" title="Nuclear detection">detection</a> of such materials.</p>
<p>The long-term (on the order of roughly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100000000000000♠</span>10</span> to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7013315576000000000♠</span>10 years</span>) radiological hazard of conventional uranium-based used nuclear fuel is dominated by plutonium and other <a href="/wiki/Minor_actinide" title="Minor actinide">minor actinides</a>, after which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-lived_fission_products" title="Long-lived fission products">long-lived fission products</a> become significant contributors again. A single neutron capture in <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">238<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is sufficient to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transuranic_elements" title="Transuranic elements">transuranic elements</a>, whereas five captures are generally necessary to do so from <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>. 98–99% of thorium-cycle fuel nuclei would fission at either <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> or <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, so fewer long-lived transuranics are produced. Because of this, thorium is a potentially attractive alternative to uranium in <a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">mixed oxide (MOX) fuels</a> to minimize the generation of transuranics and maximize the destruction of plutonium.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span></h3>
<p>There are several challenges to the application of thorium as a nuclear fuel, particularly for solid fuel reactors:</p>
<p>In contrast to uranium, naturally occurring thorium is effectively <a href="/wiki/Mononuclidic_element" title="Mononuclidic element">mononuclidic</a> and contains no fissile isotopes; fissile material, generally <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> or plutonium, must be added to achieve <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction" title="Nuclear chain reaction">criticality</a>. This, along with the high <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a> temperature necessary to make thorium-dioxide fuel, complicates fuel fabrication. <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> experimented with <a href="/wiki/Thorium_tetrafluoride" title="Thorium tetrafluoride">thorium tetrafluoride</a> as fuel in a <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactor</a> from 1964–1969, which was expected to be easier to process and separate from contaminants that slow or stop the chain reaction.</p>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle#Once-through_nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">open fuel cycle</a> (i.e. utilizing <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> in situ), higher <a href="/wiki/Burnup" title="Burnup">burnup</a> is necessary to achieve a favorable <a href="/wiki/Neutron_economy" title="Neutron economy">neutron economy</a>. Although thorium dioxide performed well at burnups of 170,000 MWd/t and 150,000 MWd/t at <a href="/wiki/Fort_St._Vrain_Generating_Station" title="Fort St. Vrain Generating Station">Fort St. Vrain Generating Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/AVR_reactor" title="AVR reactor">AVR</a> respectively, challenges complicate achieving this in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a> (LWR), which compose the vast majority of existing power reactors.</p>
<p>In a once-through thorium fuel cycle the residual <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is a long-lived radioactive isotope in the waste.</p>
<p>Another challenge associated with the thorium fuel cycle is the comparatively long interval over which <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> breeds to <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>. The <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is about 27 days, which is an order of magnitude longer than the half-life of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptunium-239" title="Neptunium-239"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">239<br/></span>Np<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></a></span>. As a result, substantial <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> develops in thorium-based fuels. <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_absorber" title="Neutron absorber">neutron absorber</a> and, although it eventually <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeds</a> into fissile <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">235<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, this requires two more neutron absorptions, which degrades <a href="/wiki/Neutron_economy" title="Neutron economy">neutron economy</a> and increases the likelihood of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transuranic_element" title="Transuranic element">transuranic</a> production.</p>
<p>Alternatively, if solid thorium is used in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle#Plutonium_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">closed fuel cycle</a> in which <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> is <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">recycled</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remote_handling" title="Remote handling">remote handling</a> is necessary for fuel fabrication because of the high radiation levels resulting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_products" title="Decay products">decay products</a> of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>. This is also true of recycled thorium because of the presence of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">228<br/></span>Th<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>, which is part of the <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> decay sequence. Further, unlike proven uranium fuel recycling technology (e.g. <a href="/wiki/PUREX" title="PUREX">PUREX</a>), recycling technology for thorium (e.g. THOREX) is only under development.</p>
<p>Although the presence of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">232<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> complicates matters, there are public documents showing that <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span> has been used once in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapon</a> test. The United States tested a composite <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">233<br/></span>U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>-plutonium bomb core in the MET (Military Effects Test) blast during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Teapot" title="Operation Teapot">Operation Teapot</a> in 1955, though with much lower yield than expected.</p>
<p>Though thorium-based fuels produce far less long-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transuranic_elements" title="Transuranic elements">transuranics</a> than uranium-based fuels, some long-lived <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> products constitute a long-term radiological impact, especially <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right">231<br/></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/></span></span>.</p>
<p>Advocates for liquid core and <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fluoride_thorium_reactor" title="Liquid fluoride thorium reactor">LFTRs</a> claim that these technologies negate thorium's disadvantages present in solid fueled reactors. As only two liquid-core fluoride salt reactors have been built (the ORNL <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_Reactor_Experiment#Direct_Air_Cycle" title="Aircraft Reactor Experiment">ARE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molten-Salt_Reactor_Experiment" title="Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment">MSRE</a>) and neither have used thorium, it is hard to validate the exact benefits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactors">Reactors</span></h2>
<p>Thorium fuels have fuelled several different reactor types, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_water_reactor" title="Heavy water reactor">heavy water reactors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HTGR" title="HTGR">high temperature gas reactors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium-cooled_fast_reactor" title="Sodium-cooled fast reactor">sodium-cooled fast reactors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactors</a>.</p>
