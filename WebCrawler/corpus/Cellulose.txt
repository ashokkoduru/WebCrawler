<p><b>Cellulose</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">(<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
6</span><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
10</span><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
5</span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
n</span></span>, a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a> consisting of a linear chain of several hundred to many thousands of <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">β(1→4) linked</a> <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose"><small>D</small>-glucose</a> units. Cellulose is an important structural component of the primary <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_plants" title="Green plants">green plants</a>, many forms of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oomycete" title="Oomycete">oomycetes</a>. Some species of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> secrete it to form <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilms</a>. Cellulose is the most abundant organic polymer on Earth. The cellulose content of <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> fiber is 90%, that of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> is 40–50% and that of dried <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> is approximately 57%.</p>
<p>Cellulose is mainly used to produce <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">paperboard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>. Smaller quantities are converted into a wide variety of derivative products such as <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a>. Conversion of cellulose from <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a> into <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> is under investigation as an alternative fuel source. Cellulose for industrial use is mainly obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>.</p>
<p>Some animals, particularly <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, can <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digest</a> cellulose with the help of <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> micro-organisms that live in their guts, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Trichonympha" title="Trichonympha">Trichonympha</a></i>. In humans, cellulose acts as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> bulking agent for <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> and is often referred to as a "<a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Cellulose was discovered in 1838 by the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Anselme_Payen" title="Anselme Payen">Anselme Payen</a>, who isolated it from plant matter and determined its chemical formula. Cellulose was used to produce the first successful <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic polymer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a>, by Hyatt Manufacturing Company in 1870. Production of <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a> ("artificial <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>") from cellulose began in the 1890s and <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a> was invented in 1912. <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Staudinger" title="Hermann Staudinger">Hermann Staudinger</a> determined the polymer structure of cellulose in 1920. The compound was first chemically synthesized (without the use of any biologically derived <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>) in 1992, by Kobayashi and Shoda.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_properties">Structure and properties</span></h2>
<p>Cellulose has no taste, is odorless, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Contact_angle" title="Contact angle">contact angle</a> of 20–30 degrees, is insoluble in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and most organic <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradable</a>. It was shown to melt at 467 °C in 2016. It can be broken down chemically into its glucose units by treating it with concentrated mineral acids at high temperature.</p>
<p>Cellulose is derived from <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose"><small>D</small>-glucose</a> units, which <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condense</a> through β(1→4)-<a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">glycosidic bonds</a>. This linkage motif contrasts with that for α(1→4)-glycosidic bonds present in <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a>. Cellulose is a straight chain polymer: unlike starch, no coiling or branching occurs, and the molecule adopts an extended and rather stiff rod-like conformation, aided by the equatorial conformation of the glucose residues. The multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl groups</a> on the glucose from one chain form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> with oxygen atoms on the same or on a neighbor chain, holding the chains firmly together side-by-side and forming <i>microfibrils</i> with high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a>. This confers tensile strength in <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a>, where cellulose microfibrils are meshed into a polysaccharide <i>matrix</i>.</p>


<p>Compared to starch, cellulose is also much more <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystalline</a>. Whereas starch undergoes a crystalline to <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous</a> transition when heated beyond 60–70 °C in water (as in cooking), cellulose requires a temperature of 320 °C and pressure of 25 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> to become amorphous in water.</p>
<p>Several different crystalline structures of cellulose are known, corresponding to the location of hydrogen bonds between and within strands. Natural cellulose is cellulose I, with structures I<sub>α</sub> and I<sub>β</sub>. Cellulose produced by bacteria and algae is enriched in I<sub>α</sub> while cellulose of higher plants consists mainly of I<sub>β</sub>. Cellulose in <a href="/wiki/Regenerated_cellulose" title="Regenerated cellulose">regenerated cellulose</a> fibers is cellulose II. The conversion of cellulose I to cellulose II is irreversible, suggesting that cellulose I is <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a> and cellulose II is stable. With various chemical treatments it is possible to produce the structures cellulose III and cellulose IV.</p>
<p>Many properties of cellulose depend on its chain length or <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_polymerization" title="Degree of polymerization">degree of polymerization</a>, the number of glucose units that make up one polymer molecule. Cellulose from wood pulp has typical chain lengths between 300 and 1700 units; cotton and other plant fibers as well as bacterial cellulose have chain lengths ranging from 800 to 10,000 units. Molecules with very small chain length resulting from the breakdown of cellulose are known as <a href="/wiki/Cellodextrin" title="Cellodextrin">cellodextrins</a>; in contrast to long-chain cellulose, cellodextrins are typically soluble in water and organic solvents.</p>
<p>Plant-derived cellulose is usually found in a mixture with <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a> and other substances, while <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_cellulose" title="Bacterial cellulose">bacterial cellulose</a> is quite pure, has a much higher water content and higher tensile strength due to higher chain lengths.</p>
<p>Cellulose is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Schweizer%27s_reagent" title="Schweizer's reagent">Schweizer's reagent</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cupriethylenediamine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cupriethylenediamine (page does not exist)">cupriethylenediamine</a> (CED), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmiumethylenediamine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cadmiumethylenediamine (page does not exist)">cadmiumethylenediamine</a> (Cadoxen), <a href="/wiki/N-Methylmorpholine_N-oxide" title="N-Methylmorpholine N-oxide"><i>N</i>-methylmorpholine <i>N</i>-oxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lithium_chloride" title="Lithium chloride">lithium chloride</a> / <a href="/wiki/Dimethylacetamide" title="Dimethylacetamide">dimethylacetamide</a>. This is used in the production of regenerated celluloses (such as <a href="/wiki/Viscose" title="Viscose">viscose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Dissolving_pulp" title="Dissolving pulp">dissolving pulp</a>. Cellulose is also soluble in many kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_liquids" title="Ionic liquids">ionic liquids</a>.</p>
<p>Cellulose consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous</a> regions. By treating it with strong acid, the amorphous regions can be broken up, thereby producing <a href="/wiki/Nanocellulose" title="Nanocellulose">nanocrystalline cellulose</a>, a novel material with many desirable properties. Recently, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanocrystalline_cellulose" title="Nanocrystalline cellulose">nanocrystalline cellulose</a> was used as the filler phase in bio-based <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> matrices to produce <a href="/wiki/Nanocomposite" title="Nanocomposite">nanocomposites</a> with superior thermal and mechanical properties.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing">Processing</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assay">Assay</span></h3>
<p>Given a cellulose-containing material, the carbohydrate portion that does not dissolve in a 17.5% solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> at 20 °C is <i>α cellulose</i>, which is true cellulose. Acidification of the extract precipitates <i>β cellulose</i>. The portion that dissolves in base but does not precipitate with acid is <i>γ cellulose</i>.</p>
<p>Cellulose can be <a href="/wiki/Assay" title="Assay">assayed</a> using a method described by Updegraff in 1969, where the fiber is dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xylosan" title="Xylosan">xylosans</a>. The resulting cellulose is allowed to react with <a href="/wiki/Anthrone" title="Anthrone">anthrone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. The resulting coloured compound is assayed <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">spectrophotometrically</a> at a wavelength of approximately 635 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E-9_m" title="1 E-9 m">nm</a>.</p>
<p>In addition, cellulose is represented by the difference between acid detergent fiber (ADF) and acid detergent lignin (ADL).</p>
<p>Luminescent conjugated oligothiophenes can also be used to detect cellulose using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescence_microscopy" title="Fluorescence microscopy">fluorescence microscopy</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrofluorimetry" title="Spectrofluorimetry">spectrofluorometric</a> methods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">vascular plants</a> cellulose is synthesized at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membrane</a> by rosette terminal complexes (RTCs). The RTCs are <a href="/wiki/Oligomer" title="Oligomer">hexameric</a> protein structures, approximately 25 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> in diameter, that contain the cellulose <a href="/wiki/Synthase" title="Synthase">synthase</a> enzymes that synthesise the individual cellulose chains. Each RTC floats in the cell's plasma membrane and "spins" a microfibril into the <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a>.</p>
<p>RTCs contain at least three different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellulose_synthase" title="Cellulose synthase">cellulose synthases</a>, encoded by <i>CesA</i> genes, in an unknown <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a>. Separate sets of <i>CesA</i> genes are involved in primary and secondary cell wall biosynthesis. There are known to be about seven subfamilies in the <i>CesA</i> superfamily. These cellulose synthases use UDP-glucose to form the β(1→4)-linked cellulose.</p>
<p>Cellulose synthesis requires chain initiation and elongation, and the two processes are separate. <i>CesA</i> <a href="/wiki/Glucosyltransferase" title="Glucosyltransferase">glucosyltransferase</a> initiates cellulose polymerization using a <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroid</a> primer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-sitosterol" title="Beta-sitosterol">sitosterol</a>-beta-<a href="/wiki/Glucoside" title="Glucoside">glucoside</a>, and UDP-glucose. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_synthase_(UDP-forming)" title="Cellulose synthase (UDP-forming)">Cellulose synthase</a> utilizes <a href="/wiki/Uridine_diphosphate" title="Uridine diphosphate">UDP</a>-D-glucose precursors to elongate the growing cellulose chain. A <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulase</a> may function to cleave the primer from the mature chain.</p>
<p>Cellulose is also synthesised by animals, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">tests</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascidian" title="Ascidian">ascidians</a> (where the cellulose was historically termed "tunicine") although it is also a minor component of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakdown_.28cellulolysis.29">Breakdown (cellulolysis)</span></h3>
<p>Cellulolysis is the process of breaking down cellulose into smaller polysaccharides called <a href="/wiki/Cellodextrin" title="Cellodextrin">cellodextrins</a> or completely into glucose units; this is a <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> reaction. Because cellulose molecules bind strongly to each other, cellulolysis is relatively difficult compared to the breakdown of other polysaccharides. However, this process can be significantly intensified in a proper solvent, e.g. in an ionic liquid.</p>
<p>Most mammals have limited ability to digest dietary fiber such as cellulose. Some <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> like cows and sheep contain certain <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> bacteria (like <i><a href="/wiki/Cellulomonas" title="Cellulomonas">Cellulomonas</a></i>) in the flora of the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>, and these bacteria produce <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> called <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulases</a> that help the microorganism to digest cellulose; the breakdown products are then used by the bacteria for proliferation. The bacterial mass is later digested by the ruminant in its digestive system (stomach and small intestine). Horses use cellulose in their diet by <a href="/wiki/Hindgut_fermentation" title="Hindgut fermentation">fermentation in their hindgut</a> via symbiotic bacteria which produce <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulase</a> to digest cellulose. Similarly, some <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a> contain in their <a href="/wiki/Hindgut" title="Hindgut">hindguts</a> certain <a href="/wiki/Flagellate" title="Flagellate">flagellate</a> <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> producing such enzymes, whereas others contain bacteria or may produce cellulase.</p>
<p>The enzymes used to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cleave" title="wikt:cleave">cleave</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycosidic_linkage" title="Glycosidic linkage">glycosidic linkage</a> in cellulose are <a href="/wiki/Glycoside_hydrolase" title="Glycoside hydrolase">glycoside hydrolases</a> including endo-acting <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulases</a> and exo-acting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucosidase" title="Glucosidase">glucosidases</a>. Such enzymes are usually secreted as part of multienzyme complexes that may include <a href="/wiki/Dockerin" title="Dockerin">dockerins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate-binding_module" title="Carbohydrate-binding module">carbohydrate-binding modules</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakdown_.28thermolysis.29">Breakdown (thermolysis)</span></h3>
<p>At temperatures above 350 °C, cellulose undergoes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermolysis" title="Thermolysis">thermolysis</a> (also called ‘<a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a>’), decomposing into solid <a href="/wiki/Char" title="Char">char</a>, vapors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, and gases such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Maximum yield of vapors which condense to a liquid called ‘<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-oil" title="Bio-oil">bio-oil</a>’ is obtained at 500 °C.</p>
<p>Semi-crystalline cellulose polymers react at pyrolysis temperatures (350 – 600 °C) in a few seconds; this transformation has been shown to occur via a solid-to-liquid-to-vapor transition, with the liquid (called ‘intermediate liquid cellulose’ or 'molten cellulose') existing for only a fraction of a second. Glycosidic bond cleavage produces short cellulose chains of two-to-seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomers" title="Monomers">monomers</a> comprising the melt. Vapor bubbling of intermediate liquid cellulose produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, which consist of short chain anhydro-oligomers derived from the melt.</p>
<p>Continuing decomposition of molten cellulose produces volatile compounds including <a href="/wiki/Levoglucosan" title="Levoglucosan">levoglucosan</a>, furans, pyrans, light oxygenates and gases via primary reactions. Within thick cellulose samples, volatile compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Levoglucosan" title="Levoglucosan">levoglucosan</a> undergo ‘secondary reactions’ to volatile products including pyrans and light oxygenates such as <a href="/wiki/Glycolaldehyde" title="Glycolaldehyde">glycolaldehyde</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hemicellulose">Hemicellulose</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">Hemicellulose</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">Hemicellulose</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a> related to cellulose that comprises about 20% of the biomass of most plants. In contrast to cellulose, hemicellulose is derived from several sugars in addition to <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Xylose" title="Xylose">xylose</a> but also including <a href="/wiki/Mannose" title="Mannose">mannose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhamnose" title="Rhamnose">rhamnose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arabinose" title="Arabinose">arabinose</a>. Hemicellulose consists of shorter chains – between 500 and 3000 sugar units. Furthermore, hemicellulose is branched, whereas cellulose is unbranched.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dissolving_pulp" title="Dissolving pulp">dissolving pulp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">pulp (paper)</a></div>

<p>Cellulose for industrial use is mainly obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">kraft process</a> is used to separate cellulose from <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, another major component of plant matter.</p>
<ul>
<li>Paper products: Cellulose is the major constituent of <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paperboard" title="Paperboard">paperboard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Card_stock" title="Card stock">card stock</a>.</li>
<li>Fibers: Cellulose is the main ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_fiber" title="Plant fiber">plant fibers</a>. It can be turned into <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a>, an important fiber that has been used for textiles since the beginning of the 20th century. Both <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a> and rayon are known as "regenerated <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_fiber" title="Cellulose fiber">cellulose fibers</a>"; they are identical to cellulose in chemical structure and are usually made from <a href="/wiki/Dissolving_pulp" title="Dissolving pulp">dissolving pulp</a> via <a href="/wiki/Viscose" title="Viscose">viscose</a>. A more recent and environmentally friendly method to produce a form of rayon is the <a href="/wiki/Lyocell" title="Lyocell">Lyocell</a> process.</li>
<li>Consumables: <a href="/wiki/Microcrystalline_cellulose" title="Microcrystalline cellulose">Microcrystalline cellulose</a> (<a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E460i</a>) and powdered cellulose (E460ii) are used as inactive <a href="/wiki/Excipient#Fillers_and_diluents" title="Excipient">fillers</a> in drug tablets and as thickeners and stabilizers in processed foods. Cellulose powder is, for example, used in <a href="/wiki/Parmigiano-Reggiano" title="Parmigiano-Reggiano">Parmesan cheese</a> to prevent caking inside the package.</li>
<li>Science: Cellulose is used in the laboratory as a stationary phase for <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">thin layer chromatography</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_fiber" title="Cellulose fiber">Cellulose fibers</a> are also used in liquid <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a>, sometimes in combination with <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a> or other filtration media, to create a filter bed of inert material.</li>
<li>Energy crops:
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">Energy crop</a></div>
The major <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustible</a> component of non-food <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a> is cellulose, with <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a> second. Non-food energy crops produce more usable energy than edible energy crops (which have a large <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> component), but still compete with food crops for agricultural land and water resources. Typical non-food energy crops include <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">industrial hemp</a> (though outlawed in some countries), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switchgrass" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Miscanthus" title="Miscanthus">Miscanthus</a></i>, <i>Salix</i> (<a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a>), and <i>Populus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a>) species.</li>
<li>Biofuel: TU-103, a strain of <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium" title="Clostridium">Clostridium</a></i> bacteria found in zebra waste, can convert nearly any form of cellulose into <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a> fuel.</li>
<li>Building material: Hydroxyl bonding of cellulose in water produces a sprayable, moldable material as an alternative to the use of plastics and resins. The recyclable material can be made water- and fire-resistant. It provides sufficient strength for use as a building material. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_insulation" title="Cellulose insulation">Cellulose insulation</a> made from recycled paper is becoming popular as an environmentally preferable material for <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">building insulation</a>. It can be treated with <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Fire_retardant" title="Fire retardant">fire retardant</a>.</li>
<li>Miscellaneous: Cellulose can be converted into <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a>, a thin transparent film. It is the base material for the <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a> that was used for photographic and movie films until the mid-1930s. Cellulose is used to make water-soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adhesives" title="Adhesives">adhesives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Binder_(material)" title="Binder (material)">binders</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methyl_cellulose" title="Methyl cellulose">methyl cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carboxymethyl_cellulose" title="Carboxymethyl cellulose">carboxymethyl cellulose</a> which are used in <a href="/wiki/Wallpaper_paste" title="Wallpaper paste">wallpaper paste</a>. Cellulose is further used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> and highly absorbent sponges. Cellulose is the raw material in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> (cellulose nitrate) which is used in <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless gunpowder</a>.</li>
</ul>
