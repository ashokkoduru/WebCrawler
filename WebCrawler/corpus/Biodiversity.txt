<p><b>Biodiversity</b>, a contraction of "biological diversity," generally refers to the variety and variability of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> on Earth. One of the most widely used definitions defines it in terms of the variability within species, between species and between ecosystems. It is a measure of the variety of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> present in different <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. This can refer to <a href="/wiki/Genetic_variation" title="Genetic variation">genetic variation</a>, ecosystem variation, or <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> variation (number of species) within an area, <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>. Terrestrial biodiversity tends to be greater near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, which seems to be the result of the warm <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_productivity" title="Primary productivity">primary productivity</a>. Biodiversity is not distributed evenly on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. It is richest in the tropics. <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">Marine biodiversity</a> tends to be highest along coasts in the Western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> is highest and in the mid-latitudinal band in all oceans. There are <a href="/wiki/Latitudinal_gradients_in_species_diversity" title="Latitudinal gradients in species diversity">latitudinal gradients in species diversity</a>. Biodiversity generally tends to cluster in hotspots, and has been increasing through time, but will be likely to slow in the future.</p>
<p>The number and variety of plants, animals and other organisms that exist is known as biodiversity. It is an essential component of nature and it ensures the survival of human species by providing food, fuel, shelter, medicines and other resources to mankind. The richness of biodiversity depends on the climatic conditions and area of the region. All species of plants taken together are known as flora and about 70,000 species of plants are known to date. All species of animals taken together are known as fauna which includes birds, mammals, fish, reptiles, insects, crustaceans, molluscs, etc.</p>
<p>Rapid <a href="/wiki/Environmental_change" title="Environmental change">environmental changes</a> typically cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinctions</a>. More than 99 percent of all species, amounting to over five billion species, that ever lived on Earth are estimated to be <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>. Estimates on the number of Earth's current <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> range from 10 million to 14 million, of which about 1.2 million have been documented and over 86 percent have not yet been described. More recently, in May 2016, scientists reported that 1 trillion species are estimated to be on Earth currently with only one-thousandth of one percent described. The total amount of related <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairs</a> on Earth is estimated at 5.0 x 10 and weighs 50 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. In comparison, the total <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">mass</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> has been estimated to be as much as 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes#Derived_units" title="Tonnes">TtC</a> (trillion tons of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>). In July 2016, scientists reported identifying a set of 355 <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Universal_Common_Ancestor" title="Last Universal Common Ancestor">Last Universal Common Ancestor</a> (LUCA) of all <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> living on Earth.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">age of the Earth</a> is about 4.54 billion years old. The earliest undisputed evidence of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> dates at least from 3.5 billion years ago, during the <a href="/wiki/Eoarchean" title="Eoarchean">Eoarchean</a> Era after a geological <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> started to solidify following the earlier molten <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a> Eon. There are <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossils" title="Fossils">fossils</a> found in 3.48 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> discovered in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Other early physical evidence of a <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic substance</a> is <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> in 3.7 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" title="Metasediment">meta-sedimentary rocks</a> discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a>. More recently, in 2015, "remains of <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic life</a>" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia. According to one of the researchers, "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth .. then it could be common in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>."</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">life began on Earth</a>, five major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" title="Mass extinctions">mass extinctions</a> and several minor events have led to large and sudden drops in biodiversity. The <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> eon (the last 540 million years) marked a rapid growth in biodiversity via the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a>—a period during which the majority of <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phyla</a> first appeared. The next 400 million years included repeated, massive biodiversity losses classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinction</a> events. In the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainforest_collapse" title="Rainforest collapse">rainforest collapse</a> led to a great loss of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life. The <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, 251 million years ago, was the worst; vertebrate recovery took 30 million years. The most recent, the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>, occurred 65 million years ago and has often attracted more attention than others because it resulted in the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>.</p>
<p>The period since the emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">humans</a> has displayed an ongoing biodiversity reduction and an accompanying loss of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a>. Named the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a>, the reduction is caused primarily by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impacts</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> destruction. Conversely, biodiversity impacts <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">human health</a> in a number of ways, both positively and negatively.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> designated 2011–2020 as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Decade_on_Biodiversity" title="United Nations Decade on Biodiversity">United Nations Decade on Biodiversity</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The term biological diversity was used first by wildlife scientist and conservationist Raymond F. Dasmann in the year 1968 lay book <i>A Different Kind of Country</i> advocating conservation. The term was widely adopted only after more than a decade, when in the 1980s it came into common usage in science and environmental policy. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lovejoy" title="Thomas Lovejoy">Thomas Lovejoy</a>, in the foreword to the book <i>Conservation Biology</i>, introduced the term to the scientific community. Until then the term "natural diversity" was common, introduced by The Science Division of <a href="/wiki/The_Nature_Conservancy" title="The Nature Conservancy">The Nature Conservancy</a> in an important 1975 study, "The Preservation of Natural Diversity." By the early 1980s TNC's Science program and its head, Robert E. Jenkins, Lovejoy and other leading conservation scientists at the time in America advocated the use of the term "biological diversity".</p>
<p>The term's contracted form <i>biodiversity</i> may have been coined by W.G. Rosen in 1985 while planning the 1986 <i>National Forum on Biological Diversity</i> organized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a> (NRC). It first appeared in a publication in 1988 when <a href="/wiki/Sociobiology" title="Sociobiology">sociobiologist</a> <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a> used it as the title of the <a href="/wiki/Proceedings" title="Proceedings">proceedings</a> of that forum.</p>
<p>Since this period the term has achieved widespread use among biologists, environmentalists, political leaders and concerned citizens.</p>
<p>A similar term in the United States is <i>"natural heritage."</i> It pre-dates the others and is more accepted by the wider audience interested in conservation. Broader than biodiversity, it includes geology and landforms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>

<p>"Biodiversity" is most commonly used to replace the more clearly defined and long established terms, <a href="/wiki/Species_diversity" title="Species diversity">species diversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">species richness</a>. Biologists most often define biodiversity as the "totality of genes, species and ecosystems of a region". An advantage of this definition is that it seems to describe most circumstances and presents a unified view of the traditional types of biological variety previously identified:</p>
<ul>
<li>taxonomic diversity (usually measured at the <a href="/wiki/Species_diversity" title="Species diversity">species diversity</a> level)</li>
<li>ecological diversity often viewed from the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_diversity" title="Ecosystem diversity">ecosystem diversity</a></li>
<li>morphological diversity which stems from <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a></li>
<li>functional diversity which is a measure of the number of functionally disparate species within a population (e.g. different feeding mechanism, different motility, predator vs prey, etc.)</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2003 Professor Anthony Campbell at <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_University" title="Cardiff University">Cardiff University</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> and the Darwin Centre, <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>, defined a fourth level: Molecular Diversity.</p>
<p>This multilevel construct is consistent with Datman and Lovejoy. An explicit definition consistent with this interpretation was first given in a paper by Bruce A. Wilcox commissioned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature_and_Natural_Resources" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources">International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources</a> (IUCN) for the 1982 World National Parks Conference. Wilcox's definition was "Biological diversity is the variety of life forms...at all levels of biological systems (i.e., molecular, organismic, population, species and ecosystem)...". The 1992 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a> defined "biological diversity" as "the variability among living organisms from all sources, including, 'inter alia', <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregion" title="Terrestrial ecoregion">terrestrial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">aquatic ecosystems</a> and the ecological complexes of which they are part: this includes diversity within species, between species and of ecosystems". This definition is used in the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>.</p>
<p>One textbook's definition is "variation of life at all levels of biological organization".</p>
<p>Genetically biodiversity can be defined as the diversity of alleles, genes and <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>. They study processes such as <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_transfer" title="Gene transfer">gene transfer</a> that drive evolution.</p>
<p>Measuring diversity at one level in a group of organisms may not precisely correspond to diversity at other levels. However, <a href="/wiki/Tetrapod" title="Tetrapod">tetrapod</a> (terrestrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>) taxonomic and ecological diversity shows a very close correlation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span></h2>

<p>Biodiversity is not evenly distributed, rather it varies greatly across the globe as well as within regions. Among other factors, the diversity of all living things (<a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>) depends on temperature, precipitation, altitude, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, geography and the presence of other species. The study of the spatial distribution of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>, species and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, is the science of <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>.</p>
<p>Diversity consistently measures higher in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> and in other localized regions such as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Floristic_Region" title="Cape Floristic Region">Cape Floristic Region</a> and lower in polar regions generally. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forests" title="Rain forests">Rain forests</a> that have had wet climates for a long time, such as <a href="/wiki/Yasuni_National_Park" title="Yasuni National Park">Yasuní National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, have particularly high biodiversity.</p>
<p>Terrestrial biodiversity is thought to be up to 25 times greater than ocean biodiversity. A recently discovered method put the total number of species on Earth at 8.7 million, of which 2.1 million were estimated to live in the ocean. However, this estimate seems to under-represent the diversity of microorganisms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latitudinal_gradients">Latitudinal gradients</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latitudinal_gradients_in_species_diversity" title="Latitudinal gradients in species diversity">Latitudinal gradients in species diversity</a></div>
<p>Generally, there is an increase in biodiversity from the <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">poles</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. Thus localities at lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a> have more species than localities at higher latitudes. This is often referred to as the latitudinal gradient in species diversity. Several ecological mechanisms may contribute to the gradient, but the ultimate factor behind many of them is the greater mean temperature at the equator compared to that of the poles.</p>
<p>Even though terrestrial biodiversity declines from the equator to the poles, some studies claim that this characteristic is unverified in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystems" title="Aquatic ecosystems">aquatic ecosystems</a>, especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystems" title="Marine ecosystems">marine ecosystems</a>. The latitudinal distribution of parasites does not appear to follow this rule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hotspots">Hotspots</span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">biodiversity hotspot</a> is a region with a high level of <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> species that has experienced great <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat loss</a>. The term hotspot was introduced in 1988 by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Myers" title="Norman Myers">Norman Myers</a>. While hotspots are spread all over the world, the majority are forest areas and most are located in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Forest" title="Atlantic Forest">Atlantic Forest</a> is considered one such hotspot, containing roughly 20,000 plant species, 1,350 vertebrates and millions of insects, about half of which occur nowhere else. The island of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and India are also particularly notable. <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> is characterized by high biodiversity, with the highest rate of species by area unit worldwide and it has the largest number of endemics (species that are not found naturally anywhere else) of any country. About 10% of the species of the Earth can be found in Colombia, including over 1,900 species of bird, more than in Europe and North America combined, Colombia has 10% of the world's mammals species, 14% of the amphibian species and 18% of the bird species of the world. <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_dry_deciduous_forests" title="Madagascar dry deciduous forests">Madagascar dry deciduous forests</a> and lowland rainforests possess a high ratio of <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a>. Since the island separated from mainland <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> 66 million years ago, many species and ecosystems have evolved independently. <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>'s 17,000 islands cover 735,355 square miles (1,904,560 km) and contain 10% of the world's flowering plants, 12% of mammals and 17% of <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>—along with nearly 240 million people. Many regions of high biodiversity and/or endemism arise from specialized <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> which require unusual adaptations, for example, <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> environments in high <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern European</a> peat <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>.</p>
<p>Accurately measuring differences in biodiversity can be difficult. <a href="/wiki/Selection_bias" title="Selection bias">Selection bias</a> amongst researchers may contribute to biased empirical research for modern estimates of biodiversity. In 1768, Rev. <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_White" title="Gilbert White">Gilbert White</a> succinctly observed of his <a href="/wiki/Selborne" title="Selborne">Selborne, Hampshire</a> <i>"all nature is so full, that that district produces the most variety which is the most examined."</i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_and_history">Evolution and history</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">Evolution</a></div>


<p>Biodiversity is the result of 3.5 billion years of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a> has not been definitely established by science, however some evidence suggests that life may already have been well-established only a few hundred million years after the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">formation of the Earth</a>. Until approximately 600 million years ago, all life consisted of <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protozoan" title="Protozoan">protozoans</a> and similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-celled_organism" title="Single-celled organism">single-celled organisms</a>.</p>
<p>The history of biodiversity during the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> (the last 540 million years), starts with rapid growth during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a>—a period during which nearly every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylum_(biology)" title="Phylum (biology)">phylum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular organisms</a> first appeared. Over the next 400 million years or so, invertebrate diversity showed little overall trend and vertebrate diversity shows an overall exponential trend. This dramatic rise in diversity was marked by periodic, massive losses of diversity classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinction</a> events. A significant loss occurred when rainforests collapsed in the carboniferous. The worst was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic extinction event</a>, 251 million years ago. Vertebrates took 30 million years to recover from this event.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> suggests that the last few million years featured the greatest biodiversity in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">history</a>. However, not all scientists support this view, since there is uncertainty as to how strongly the fossil record is biased by the greater availability and preservation of recent <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologic</a> sections. Some scientists believe that corrected for sampling artifacts, modern biodiversity may not be much different from biodiversity 300 million years ago., whereas others consider the fossil record reasonably reflective of the diversification of life. Estimates of the present global macroscopic species diversity vary from 2 million to 100 million, with a best estimate of somewhere near 9 million, the vast majority <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>. Diversity appears to increase continually in the absence of natural selection.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_diversification">Evolutionary diversification</span></h3>
<p>The existence of a "global carrying capacity", limiting the amount of life that can live at once, is debated, as is the question of whether such a limit would also cap the number of species. While records of life in the sea shows a <a href="/wiki/Logistic_function" title="Logistic function">logistic</a> pattern of growth, life on land (insects, plants and tetrapods)shows an <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential</a> rise in diversity. As one author states, "Tetrapods have not yet invaded 64 per cent of potentially habitable modes and it could be that without human influence the ecological and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxonomic" title="Taxonomic">taxonomic</a> diversity of tetrapods would continue to increase in an exponential fashion until most or all of the available ecospace is filled."</p>
<p>On the other hand, changes through the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> correlate much better with the <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth" title="Hyperbolic growth">hyperbolic</a> model (widely used in <a href="/wiki/Population_biology" title="Population biology">population biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macrosociology" title="Macrosociology">macrosociology</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> biodiversity) than with exponential and logistic models. The latter models imply that changes in diversity are guided by a first-order <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> (more ancestors, more descendants) and/or a <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> arising from resource limitation. Hyperbolic model implies a second-order positive feedback. The hyperbolic pattern of the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> growth arises from a second-order positive feedback between the population size and the rate of technological growth. The hyperbolic character of biodiversity growth can be similarly accounted for by a feedback between diversity and community structure complexity. The similarity between the curves of biodiversity and human population probably comes from the fact that both are derived from the interference of the hyperbolic trend with cyclical and <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">stochastic</a> dynamics.</p>
<p>Most biologists agree however that the period since human emergence is part of a new mass extinction, named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction_event" title="Holocene extinction event">Holocene extinction event</a>, caused primarily by the impact humans are having on the environment. It has been argued that the present rate of extinction is sufficient to eliminate most species on the planet Earth within 100 years.</p>
<p>New species are regularly discovered (on average between 5–10,000 new species each year, most of them <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>) and many, though discovered, are not yet classified (estimates are that nearly 90% of all <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> are not yet classified). Most of the terrestrial diversity is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a> and in general, land has more species than the ocean; some 8.7 million species may exists on Earth, of which some 2.1 million live in the ocean</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_and_ecosystem_services">Biodiversity and ecosystem services</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_balance_of_evidence">The balance of evidence</span></h3>
<p>"Ecosystem services are the suite of benefits that ecosystems provide to humanity."</p>
<p>These services come in three flavors:</p>
<ol>
<li>Provisioning services which involve the production of renewable resources (e.g.: food, wood, fresh water)</li>
<li>Regulating services which are those that lessen environmental change (e.g.: climate regulation, pest/disease control)</li>
<li>Cultural services represent human value and enjoyment (e.g.: landscape aesthetics, cultural heritage, outdoor recreation and spiritual significance)</li>
</ol>
<p>There have been many claims about biodiversity's effect on these ecosystem services, especially provisioning and regulating services. After an exhaustive survey through peer-reviewed literature to evaluate 36 different claims about biodiversity's effect on ecosystem services, 14 of those claims have been validated, 6 demonstrate mixed support or are unsupported, 3 are incorrect and 13 lack enough evidence to draw definitive conclusions.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Services_enhanced_by_biodiversity">Services enhanced by biodiversity</span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisioning_services">Provisioning services</span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Greater species diversity of plants increases fodder yield (synthesis of 271 experimental studies).</li>
<li>Greater genetic diversity of plants (i.e.: diversity within a single species) increases overall crop yield (synthesis of 575 experimental studies). Although another review of 100 experimental studies reports mixed evidence.</li>
<li>Greater species diversity of trees increases overall wood production (Synthesis of 53 experimental studies). However, there is not enough data to draw a conclusion about the effect of tree trait diversity on wood production.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulating_services">Regulating services</span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Greater species diversity of fish increases the stability of fisheries yield (Synthesis of 8 observational studies)</li>
<li>Greater species diversity of natural pest enemies decreases herbivorous pest populations (Data from two separate reviews; Synthesis of 266 experimental and observational studies; Synthesis of 18 observational studies. Although another review of 38 experimental studies found mixed support for this claim, suggesting that in cases where mutual intraguild predation occurs, a single predatory species is often more effective</li>
<li>Greater species diversity of plants decreases disease prevalence on plants (Synthesis of 107 experimental studies)</li>
<li>Greater species diversity of plants increases resistance to plant invasion (Data from two separate reviews; Synthesis of 105 experimental studies; Synthesis of 15 experimental studies)</li>

<p>Since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_age" title="Stone age">stone age</a>, species loss has accelerated above the average basal rate, driven by human activity. Estimates of species losses are at a rate 100-10,000 times as fast as is typical in the fossil record. Biodiversity also affords many non-material benefits including spiritual and aesthetic values, knowledge systems and education.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_and_agriculture">Biodiversity and agriculture</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_biodiversity" title="Agricultural biodiversity">Agricultural biodiversity</a></div>

<p>Agricultural diversity can be divided into two categories: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">intraspecific diversity</a>, which includes the genetic variety within a single species, like the potato (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solanum_tuberosum" title="Solanum tuberosum">Solanum tuberosum</a></i>) that is composed of many different forms and types (e.g.: in the U.S. we might compare russet potatoes with new potatoes or purple potatoes, all different, but all part of the same species, <i>S. tuberosum</i>).</p>
<p>The other category of agricultural diversity is called <a href="/wiki/Species_diversity" title="Species diversity">interspecific diversity</a> and refers to the number and types of different species. Thinking about this diversity we might note that many small vegetable farmers grow many different crops like potatoes and also carrots, peppers, lettuce etc.</p>
<p>Agricultural diversity can also be divided by whether it is ‘planned’ diversity or ‘associated’ diversity. This is a functional classification that we impose and not an intrinsic feature of life or diversity. Planned diversity includes the crops which a farmer has encouraged, planted or raised (e.g.: crops, covers, symbionts and livestock, among others), which can be contrasted with the associated diversity that arrives among the crops, uninvited (e.g.: herbivores, weed species and pathogens, among others).</p>
<p>The control of associated biodiversity is one of the great agricultural challenges that farmers face. On <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> farms, the approach is generally to eradicate associated diversity using a suite of biologically destructive <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, mechanized tools and <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">transgenic engineering techniques</a>, then to <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">rotate crops</a>. Although some <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a> farmers use the same techniques, they also employ <a href="/wiki/Integrated_pest_management" title="Integrated pest management">integrated pest management</a> strategies as well as strategies that are more labor-intensive, but generally less dependent on capital, biotechnology and energy.</p>
<p>Interspecific crop diversity is, in part, responsible for offering variety in what we eat. Intraspecific diversity, the variety of alleles within a single species, also offers us choice in our diets. If a crop fails in a monoculture, we rely on agricultural diversity to replant the land with something new. If a wheat crop is destroyed by a pest we may plant a hardier variety of wheat the next year, relying on intraspecific diversity. We may forgo wheat production in that area and plant a different species altogether, relying on interspecific diversity. Even an agricultural society which primarily grows monocultures, relies on biodiversity at some point.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Irish potato blight</a> of 1846 was a major factor in the deaths of one million people and the emigration of about two million. It was the result of planting only two potato varieties, both vulnerable to the blight, <i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_infestans" title="Phytophthora infestans">Phytophthora infestans</a></i>, which arrived in 1845</li>
<li>When <a href="/wiki/Rice_grassy_stunt_virus" title="Rice grassy stunt virus">rice grassy stunt virus</a> struck rice fields from Indonesia to India in the 1970s, 6,273 varieties were tested for resistance. Only one was resistant, an Indian variety and known to science only since 1966. This variety formed a hybrid with other varieties and is now widely grown.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_rust" title="Coffee rust">Coffee rust</a> attacked coffee plantations in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and Central America in 1970. A resistant variety was found in Ethiopia. The diseases are themselves a form of biodiversity.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">Monoculture</a> was a contributing factor to several agricultural disasters, including the European wine industry collapse in the late 19th century and the US <a href="/wiki/Southern_corn_leaf_blight" title="Southern corn leaf blight">southern corn leaf blight</a> epidemic of 1970.</p>
<p>Although about 80 percent of humans' food supply comes from just 20 kinds of plants, humans use at least 40,000 species. Many people depend on these species for food, shelter and clothing. Earth's surviving biodiversity provides resources for increasing the range of food and other products suitable for human use, although the present extinction rate shrinks that potential.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_and_human_health">Biodiversity and human health</span></h3>

<p>Biodiversity's relevance to human health is becoming an international political issue, as scientific evidence builds on the global health implications of biodiversity loss.  This issue is closely linked with the issue of climate change, as many of the anticipated health risks of climate change are associated with changes in biodiversity (e.g. changes in populations and distribution of disease vectors, scarcity of fresh water, impacts on agricultural biodiversity and food resources etc.) This is because the species most likely to disappear are those that buffer against infectious disease transmission, while surviving species tend to be the ones that increase disease transmission, such as that of West Nile Virus, Lyme disease and Hantavirus, according to a study done co-authored by Felicia Keesing, an ecologist at Bard College and Drew Harvell, associate director for Environment of the <a href="/wiki/Atkinson_Center_for_a_Sustainable_Future" title="Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future">Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future</a> (ACSF) at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>.</p>
<p>The growing demand and lack of drinkable water on the planet presents an additional challenge to the future of human health. Partly, the problem lies in the success of water suppliers to increase supplies and failure of groups promoting preservation of water resources. While the distribution of clean water increases, in some parts of the world it remains unequal. According to <i>2008 World Population Data Sheet</i>, only 62% of least developed countries are able to access clean water.</p>
<p>Some of the health issues influenced by biodiversity include dietary health and nutrition security, infectious disease, medical science and medicinal resources, social and psychological health. Biodiversity is also known to have an important role in reducing disaster risk and in post-disaster relief and recovery efforts.</p>
<p>Biodiversity provides critical support for drug discovery and the availability of medicinal resources. A significant proportion of drugs are derived, directly or indirectly, from biological sources: at least 50% of the pharmaceutical compounds on the US market are derived from plants, animals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro-organisms" title="Micro-organisms">micro-organisms</a>, while about 80% of the world population depends on medicines from nature (used in either modern or traditional medical practice) for primary healthcare. Only a tiny fraction of wild species has been investigated for medical potential. Biodiversity has been critical to advances throughout the field of <a href="/wiki/Bionics" title="Bionics">bionics</a>. Evidence from market analysis and biodiversity science indicates that the decline in output from the pharmaceutical sector since the mid-1980s can be attributed to a move away from natural product exploration ("bioprospecting") in favor of genomics and synthetic chemistry, indeed claims about the value of undiscovered pharmaceuticals may not provide enough incentive for companies in free markets to search for them because of the high cost of development; meanwhile, natural products have a long history of supporting significant economic and health innovation. Marine ecosystems are particularly important, although inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Bioprospecting" title="Bioprospecting">bioprospecting</a> can increase biodiversity loss, as well as violating the laws of the communities and states from which the resources are taken.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity.2C_business_and_industry">Biodiversity, business and industry</span></h3>

<p>Many industrial materials derive directly from biological sources. These include building materials, fibers, dyes, rubber and oil. Biodiversity is also important to the security of resources such as water, timber, paper, fiber and food. As a result, biodiversity loss is a significant risk factor in business development and a threat to long term economic sustainability.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity.2C_leisure.2C_cultural_and_aesthetic_value">Biodiversity, leisure, cultural and aesthetic value</span></h3>
<p>Biodiversity enriches leisure activities such as <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birdwatching" title="Birdwatching">birdwatching</a> or natural history study. Biodiversity inspires <a href="/wiki/Musician" title="Musician">musicians</a>, painters, sculptors, writers and other artists. Many cultures view themselves as an integral part of the natural world which requires them to respect other living organisms.</p>
<p>Popular activities such as <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishkeeping" title="Fishkeeping">fishkeeping</a> and specimen collecting strongly depend on biodiversity. The number of species involved in such pursuits is in the tens of thousands, though the majority do not enter commerce.</p>
<p>The relationships between the original natural areas of these often exotic animals and plants and commercial collectors, suppliers, breeders, propagators and those who promote their understanding and enjoyment are complex and poorly understood. The general public responds well to exposure to rare and unusual organisms, reflecting their inherent value.</p>
<p>Philosophically it could be argued that biodiversity has intrinsic aesthetic and spiritual value to <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">mankind</a> <i>in and of itself</i>. This idea can be used as a counterweight to the notion that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a> and other ecological realms are only worthy of conservation because of the services they provide.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_and_ecological_services">Biodiversity and ecological services</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_effects_of_biodiversity" title="Ecological effects of biodiversity">Ecological effects of biodiversity</a></div>

<p>Biodiversity supports many <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"There is now unequivocal evidence that biodiversity loss reduces the efficiency by which ecological communities capture biologically essential resources, produce biomass, decompose and recycle biologically essential nutrients... There is mounting evidence that biodiversity increases the stability of ecosystem functions through time... Diverse communities are more productive because they contain key species that have a large influence on productivity and differences in functional traits among organisms increase total resource capture... The impacts of diversity loss on ecological processes might be sufficiently large to rival the impacts of many other global drivers of environmental change... Maintaining multiple ecosystem processes at multiple places and times requires higher levels of biodiversity than does a single process at a single place and time."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It plays a part in regulating the chemistry of our <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>. Biodiversity is directly involved in <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a>, recycling <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> and providing fertile soils. Experiments with controlled environments have shown that humans cannot easily build ecosystems to support human needs; for example <a href="/wiki/Entomophily" title="Entomophily">insect pollination</a> cannot be mimicked, and that activity alone represented between $2.1-14.6 billions in 2003.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_species">Number of species</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_biodiversity" title="Global biodiversity">Global biodiversity</a></div>
<div class="center">

</div>
<p>According to Mora and colleagues, the total number of terrestrial species is estimated to be around 8.7 million while the number of oceanic species is much lower, estimated at 2.2 million. The authors note that these estimates are strongest for eukaryotic organisms and likely represent the lower bound of prokaryote diversity. Other estimates include:</p>
<ul>
<li>220,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascular_plants" title="Vascular plants">vascular plants</a>, estimated using the species-area relation method</li>
<li>0.7-1 million marine species</li>
<li>10–30 million <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>; (of some 0.9 million we know today)</li>
<li>5–10 million <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>;</li>
<li>1.5-3 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, estimates based on data from the tropics, long-term non-tropical sites and molecular studies that have revealed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptic_species" title="Cryptic species">cryptic speciation</a>. Some 0.075 million species of fungi had been documented by 2001)</li>
<li>1 million <a href="/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">mites</a></li>
<li>The number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> species is not reliably known, but the <a href="/wiki/Global_Ocean_Sampling_Expedition" title="Global Ocean Sampling Expedition">Global Ocean Sampling Expedition</a> dramatically increased the estimates of genetic diversity by identifying an enormous number of new genes from near-surface <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> samples at various marine locations, initially over the 2004-2006 period. The findings may eventually cause a significant change in the way science defines <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and other taxonomic categories.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since the rate of extinction has increased, many extant species may become extinct before they are described.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species_loss_rates">Species loss rates</span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>No longer do we have to justify the existence of humid tropical forests on the feeble grounds that they might carry plants with drugs that cure human disease. <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis"><i>Gaia theory</i></a> forces us to see that they offer much more than this. Through their capacity to evapotranspirate vast volumes of water vapor, they serve to keep the planet cool by wearing a sunshade of white reflecting cloud. Their replacement by cropland could precipitate a disaster that is global in scale.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>During the last century, decreases in biodiversity have been increasingly observed. In 2007, German Federal Environment Minister Sigmar Gabriel cited estimates that up to 30% of all species will be extinct by 2050. Of these, about one eighth of known plant species are threatened with <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>. Estimates reach as high as 140,000 species per year (based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species-area_curve" title="Species-area curve">Species-area theory</a>). This figure indicates <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">unsustainable</a> ecological practices, because few species emerge each year. Almost all scientists acknowledge that the rate of species loss is greater now than at any time in human history, with extinctions occurring at rates hundreds of times higher than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Background_extinction" title="Background extinction">background extinction</a> rates. As of 2012, some studies suggest that 25% of all mammal species could be extinct in 20 years.</p>
<p>In absolute terms, the planet has lost 52% of its biodiversity since 1970 according to a 2014 study by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>. The Living Planet Report 2014 claims that "the number of mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians and fish across the globe is, on average, about half the size it was 40 years ago". Of that number, 39% accounts for the terrestrial wildlife gone, 39% for the marine wildlife gone and 76% for the freshwater wildlife gone. Biodiversity took the biggest hit in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, plummeting 83 percent. High-income countries showed a 10% increase in biodiversity, which was canceled out by a loss in low-income countries. This is despite the fact that high-income countries use five times the ecological resources of low-income countries, which was explained as a result of process whereby wealthy nations are outsourcing resource depletion to poorer nations, which are suffering the greatest ecosystem losses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span></h2>
<p>In 2006 many species were formally classified as <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> or <a href="/wiki/Threatened_species" title="Threatened species">threatened</a>; moreover, scientists have estimated that millions more species are at risk which have not been formally recognized. About 40 percent of the 40,177 species assessed using the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> criteria are now listed as threatened with <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>—a total of 16,119.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jared_Diamond" title="Jared Diamond">Jared Diamond</a> describes an "Evil Quartet" of habitat destruction, overkill, introduced species and secondary extinctions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_O._Wilson" title="Edward O. Wilson">Edward O. Wilson</a> prefers the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> HIPPO, standing for <b>H</b>abitat destruction, <b>I</b>nvasive species, <b>P</b>ollution, human over-<b>P</b>opulation and <b>O</b>ver-harvesting. The most authoritative classification in use today is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>'s Classification of Direct Threats which has been adopted by major international conservation organizations such as the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_Conservancy" title="Nature Conservancy">Nature Conservancy</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a> and <a href="/wiki/BirdLife_International" title="BirdLife International">BirdLife International</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_destruction">Habitat destruction</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">Habitat destruction</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">Habitat</a> destruction has played a key role in extinctions, especially related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forest</a> destruction. Factors contributing to habitat loss are: <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_use,_land-use_change_and_forestry" title="Land use, land-use change and forestry">land use change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> (<a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> or climate change.</p>
<p>Habitat size and numbers of species are systematically related. Physically larger species and those living at lower latitudes or in forests or oceans are more sensitive to reduction in habitat area. Conversion to "trivial" standardized ecosystems (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> following <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>) effectively destroys habitat for the more diverse species that preceded the conversion. In some countries lack of property rights or lax law/regulatory enforcement necessarily leads to biodiversity loss (degradation costs having to be supported by the community).</p>
<p>A 2007 study conducted by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> found that biodiversity and genetic diversity are codependent—that diversity among species requires diversity within a species and vice versa. "If any one type is removed from the system, the cycle can break down and the community becomes dominated by a single species." At present, the most threatened ecosystems are found in <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, according to the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> 2005, which was confirmed by the "Freshwater Animal Diversity Assessment", organised by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBIF" title="GBIF">biodiversity platform</a> and the French <i><a href="/wiki/Institut_de_recherche_pour_le_d%C3%A9veloppement" title="Institut de recherche pour le développement">Institut de recherche pour le développement</a></i> (MNHNP).</p>
<p>Co-extinctions are a form of habitat destruction. Co-extinction occurs when the extinction or decline in one accompanies the other, such as in plants and beetles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduced_and_invasive_species">Introduced and invasive species</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">Introduced species</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">Invasive species</a></div>

<p>Barriers such as large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivers" title="Rivers">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountains" title="Mountains">mountains</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">deserts</a> encourage diversity by enabling independent evolution on either side of the barrier, via the process of <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">allopatric speciation</a>. The term <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> is applied to species that breach the natural barriers that would normally keep them constrained. Without barriers, such species occupy new territory, often supplanting native species by occupying their niches, or by using resources that would normally sustain native species.</p>
<p>The number of species invasions has been on the rise at least since the beginning of the 1900s. Species are increasingly being moved by humans (on purpose and accidentally). In some cases the invaders are causing drastic changes and damage to their new habitats (e.g.: zebra mussels and the emerald ash borer in the Great Lakes region and the lion fish along the North American Atlantic coast). Some evidence suggests that invasive species are competitive in their new habitats because they are subject to less pathogen disturbance. Others report confounding evidence that occasionally suggest that species-rich communities harbor many native and exotic species simultaneously while some say that diverse ecosystems are more resilient and resist invasive plants and animals. An important question is, "do invasive species cause extinctions?" Many studies cite effects of invasive species on natives, but not extinctions. Invasive species seem to increase local (i.e.: <a href="/wiki/Alpha_diversity" title="Alpha diversity">alpha diversity</a>) diversity, which decreases turnover of diversity (i.e.: <a href="/wiki/Beta_diversity" title="Beta diversity">beta diversity</a>). Overall <a href="/wiki/Gamma_diversity" title="Gamma diversity">gamma diversity</a> may be lowered because species are going extinct because of other causes, but even some of the most insidious invaders (e.g.: Dutch elm disease, emerald ash borer, chestnut blight in North America) have not caused their host species to become extinct. <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">Extirpation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_decline" title="Population decline">population decline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Species_homogeneity" title="Species homogeneity">homogenization</a> of regional biodiversity are much more common. Human activities have frequently been the cause of invasive species circumventing their barriers, by introducing them for food and other purposes. Human activities therefore allow species to migrate to new areas (and thus become invasive) occurred on time scales much shorter than historically have been required for a species to extend its range.</p>
<p>Not all introduced species are invasive, nor all invasive species deliberately introduced. In cases such as the <a href="/wiki/Zebra_mussel" title="Zebra mussel">zebra mussel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasion</a> of US waterways was unintentional. In other cases, such as <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongooses</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, the introduction is deliberate but ineffective (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nocturnal" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> were not vulnerable to the <a href="/wiki/Diurnality" title="Diurnality">diurnal</a> mongoose). In other cases, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">oil palms</a> in Indonesia and Malaysia, the introduction produces substantial economic benefits, but the benefits are accompanied by costly <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequences</a>.</p>
<p>Finally, an introduced species may unintentionally injure a species that depends on the species it replaces. In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prunus_spinosa" title="Prunus spinosa">Prunus spinosa</a> from Eastern Europe leafs much sooner than its West European counterparts, disrupting the feeding habits of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thecla_betulae" title="Thecla betulae">Thecla betulae</a></i> butterfly (which feeds on the leaves). Introducing new species often leaves endemic and other local species unable to compete with the exotic species and unable to survive. The exotic organisms may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a>, or may simply outcompete indigenous species for nutrients, water and light.</p>
<p>At present, several countries have already imported so many exotic species, particularly agricultural and ornamental plants, that their own indigenous fauna/flora may be outnumbered. For example, the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Kudzu" title="Kudzu">kudzu</a> from Southeast Asia to Canada and the United States has threatened biodiversity in certain areas.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_pollution">Genetic pollution</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_pollution" title="Genetic pollution">Genetic pollution</a></div>
<p>Endemic species can be threatened with <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> through the process of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_pollution" title="Genetic pollution">genetic pollution</a>, i.e. uncontrolled <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Introgression" title="Introgression">introgression</a> and genetic swamping. Genetic pollution leads to homogenization or replacement of local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genomes" title="Genomes">genomes</a> as a result of either a numerical and/or <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> advantage of an introduced species. Hybridization and introgression are side-effects of introduction and invasion. These phenomena can be especially detrimental to <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare species</a> that come into contact with more abundant ones. The abundant species can interbreed with the rare species, swamping its <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a>. This problem is not always apparent from <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> (outward appearance) observations alone. Some degree of <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> is normal adaptation and not all <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a> constellations can be preserved. However, hybridization with or without introgression may, nevertheless, threaten a rare species' existence.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overexploitation">Overexploitation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">Overexploitation</a></div>
<p>Overexploitation occurs when a resource is consumed at an unsustainable rate. This occurs on land in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overhunting" title="Overhunting">overhunting</a>, excessive <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>, poor <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a> in agriculture and the illegal <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_trade" title="Wildlife trade">wildlife trade</a>.</p>
<p>About 25% of world <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> are now overfished to the point where their current biomass is less than the level that maximizes their sustainable yield.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overkill_hypothesis" title="Overkill hypothesis">overkill hypothesis</a>, a pattern of large animal extinctions connected with <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">human migration</a> patterns, can be used explain why <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafaunal</a> extinctions can occur within a relatively short time period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybridization.2C_genetic_pollution.2Ferosion_and_food_security">Hybridization, genetic pollution/erosion and food security</span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_Security" title="Food Security">Food Security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_erosion" title="Genetic erosion">Genetic erosion</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a> popularized the use of conventional <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> to increase yield. Often hybridized breeds originated in developed countries and were further hybridized with local varieties in the developing world to create high yield strains resistant to local climate and diseases. Local governments and industry have been pushing hybridization. Formerly huge gene pools of various wild and indigenous breeds have collapsed causing widespread <a href="/wiki/Genetic_erosion" title="Genetic erosion">genetic erosion</a> and genetic pollution. This has resulted in loss of genetic diversity and biodiversity as a whole.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">(GM organisms)</a> have genetic material altered by <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> procedures such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA_technology" title="Recombinant DNA technology">recombinant DNA technology</a>. GM crops have become a common source for genetic pollution, not only of wild varieties but also of domesticated varieties derived from classical hybridization.</p>
<p>Genetic erosion coupled with genetic pollution may be destroying unique genotypes, thereby creating a hidden crisis which could result in a severe threat to our <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>. Diverse genetic material could cease to exist which would impact our ability to further hybridize food crops and livestock against more resistant diseases and climatic changes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_climate_change_on_plant_biodiversity" title="Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity">Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity</a></div>

<p>Global warming is also considered to be a major potential threat to global biodiversity in the future. For example, coral reefs - which are biodiversity hotspots - will be lost within the century if global warming continues at the current trend.</p>
<p>Climate change has seen many claims about potential to affect biodiversity but evidence supporting the statement is tenuous. Increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide certainly affects plant morphology and is acidifying oceans, and temperature affects species ranges, phenology, and weather, but the major impacts that have been predicted are still just <i>potential</i> impacts. We have not documented major extinctions yet, even as climate change drastically alters the biology of many species.</p>
<p>In 2004, an international collaborative study on four continents estimated that 10 percent of species would become extinct by 2050 because of global warming. "We need to limit climate change or we wind up with a lot of species in trouble, possibly extinct," said Dr. Lee Hannah, a co-author of the paper and chief climate change biologist at the Center for Applied Biodiversity Science at Conservation International.</p>
<p>A recent study predicts that up to 35% of the world terrestrial carnivores and ungulates will be at higher risk of extinction by 2050 because of the joint effects of predicted climate and land-use change under business-as-usual human development scenarios.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_overpopulation">Human overpopulation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Human overpopulation</a></div>
<p>From 1950 to 2011, world population increased from 2.5 billion to 7 billion and is forecast to reach a plateau of more than 9 billion during the 21st century. Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_King_(scientist)" title="David King (scientist)">David King</a>, former chief scientific adviser to the UK government, told a parliamentary inquiry: <i>"It is self-evident that the massive growth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a> through the 20th century has had more impact on biodiversity than any other single factor."</i> At least until the middle of the 21st century, worldwide losses of pristine biodiverse land will probably depend much on the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">human birth rate</a>.</p>
<p>According to a 2014 study by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>, the global human population already exceeds planet's <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> - it would take the equivalent of 1.5 Earths of biocapacity to meet our current demands. The report further points that if everyone on the planet had the Footprint of the average resident of Qatar, we would need 4.8 Earths and if we lived the lifestyle of a typical resident of the USA, we would need 3.9 Earths.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Holocene_extinction">The Holocene extinction</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a></div>
<p>Rates of decline in biodiversity in this sixth mass extinction match or exceed rates of loss in the five previous <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinction events</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>. Loss of biodiversity results in the loss of <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> that supplies <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem goods and services</a>. From the perspective of the method known as Natural Economy the economic value of 17 ecosystem services for Earth's <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> (calculated in 1997) has an estimated value of US$33 trillion (3.3x10) per year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">Conservation biology</a></div>


<p><a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">Conservation biology</a> matured in the mid-20th century as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologists" title="Ecologists">ecologists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalists" title="Naturalists">naturalists</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientists" title="Scientists">scientists</a> began to research and address issues pertaining to global biodiversity declines.</p>
<p>The conservation ethic advocates management of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> for the purpose of sustaining biodiversity in <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary process</a> and human culture and society.</p>
<p>Conservation biology is reforming around strategic plans to protect biodiversity. Preserving global biodiversity is a priority in strategic conservation plans that are designed to engage public policy and concerns affecting local, regional and global scales of communities, ecosystems and cultures. Action plans identify <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics#Methodology" title="Ecological economics">ways</a> of sustaining human well-being, employing <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_market" title="Capital market">market capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EU_Directive_1999/22/EC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EU Directive 1999/22/EC (page does not exist)">EU Directive 1999/22/EC</a> zoos are described as having a role in the preservation of the biodiversity of wildlife animals by conducting research or participation in <a href="/wiki/Breeding_program" title="Breeding program">breeding programs</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection_and_restoration_techniques">Protection and restoration techniques</span></h3>
<p>Removal of exotic species will allow the species that they have negatively impacted to recover their ecological niches. Exotic species that have become pests can be identified taxonomically (e.g., with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Automated_Identification_SYstem" title="Digital Automated Identification SYstem">Digital Automated Identification SYstem</a> (DAISY), using the <a href="/wiki/Consortium_for_the_Barcode_of_Life" title="Consortium for the Barcode of Life">barcode of life</a>). Removal is practical only given large groups of individuals due to the economic cost.</p>
<p>As sustainable populations of the remaining native species in an area become assured, "missing" species that are candidates for reintroduction can be identified using databases such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Life#Resources_and_collaborations" title="Encyclopedia of Life">Encyclopedia of Life</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Biodiversity_Information_Facility" title="Global Biodiversity Information Facility">Global Biodiversity Information Facility</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_banking" title="Biodiversity banking">Biodiversity banking</a> places a monetary value on biodiversity. One example is the Australian <a href="/wiki/Native_Vegetation_Management_Framework" title="Native Vegetation Management Framework">Native Vegetation Management Framework</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gene_bank" title="Gene bank">Gene banks</a> are collections of specimens and genetic material. Some banks intend to reintroduce banked species to the ecosystem (e.g., via tree nurseries).</li>
<li>Reduction of and better targeting of pesticides allows more species to survive in agricultural and urbanized areas.</li>
<li>Location-specific approaches may be less useful for protecting migratory species. One approach is to create <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">wildlife corridors</a> that correspond to the animals' movements. National and other boundaries can complicate corridor creation.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_areas">Protected areas</span></h2>

<p>Protected areas is meant for affording protection to wild animals and their habitat which also includes forest reserves and biosphere reserves. Protected areas have been set up all over the world with the specific aim of protecting and conserving plants and animals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_parks">National parks</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">National park</a></div>
<p>National park and nature reserve is the area selected by governments or private organizations for special protection against damage or degradation with the objective of biodiversity and landscape conservation. National parks are usually owned and managed by national or state governments. A limit is placed on the number of visitors permitted to enter certain fragile areas. Designated trails or roads are created. The visitors are allowed to enter only for study, cultural and recreation purposes. Forestry operations, grazing of animals and hunting of animals are regulated. Exploitation of habitat or wildlife is banned.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_sanctuary">Wildlife sanctuary</span></h3>
<p>Wildlife sanctuary aims only at conservation of species and have the following features:</p>
<ol>
<li>The boundaries of the sanctuaries are not limited by state legislation.</li>
<li>The killing, hunting or capturing of any species is prohibited except by or under the control of the highest authority in the department which is responsible for the management of the sanctuary.</li>
<li>Private ownership may be allowed.</li>
<li>Forestry and other usages can also be permitted.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_reserves">Forest reserves</span></h3>
<p>The forests play a vital role in harbouring more than 45,000 floral and 81,000 faunal species of which 5150 floral and 1837 faunal species are endemic. Plant and animal species confined to a specific geographical area are called endemic species. In reserved forests, rights to activities like hunting and grazing are sometimes given to communities living on the fringes of the forest, who sustain their livelihood partially or wholly from forest resources or products. The unclassed forests covers 6.4 percent of the total forest area and they are marked by the following characteristics:</p>
<ol>
<li>They are large inaccessible forests.</li>
<li>Many of these are unoccupied.</li>
<li>They are ecologically and economically less important.</li>
</ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps_to_conserve_the_forest_cover">Steps to conserve the forest cover</span></h4>
<ol>
<li>An extensive reforestation/afforestation program should be followed.</li>
<li>Alternative environment-friendly sources of fuel energy such as biogas other than wood should be used.</li>
<li>Loss of biodiversity due to forest fire is a major problem, immediate steps to prevent forest fire need to be taken.</li>
<li>Overgrazing by cattle can damage a forest seriously. Therefore, certain steps should be taken to prevent overgrazing by cattle.</li>
<li>Hunting and poaching should be banned.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zoological_parks">Zoological parks</span></h3>
<p>In zoological parks or zoos, live animals are kept for public recreation, education and conservation purposes. Modern zoos offer veterinary facilities, provide opportunities for threatened species to breed in captivity and usually build environments that simulate the native habitats of the animals in their care. Zoos play a major role in creating awareness among common people about the need to conserve nature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_gardens">Botanical gardens</span></h3>
<p>Botanical garden is a garden in which plants are grown and displayed primarily for scientific and educational purposes. It consists of a collection of living plants, grown outdoors or under glass in greenhouses and conservatories. In addition, it includes a collection of dried plants or herbarium and such facilities as lecture rooms, laboratories, libraries, museums and experimental or research plantings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_allocation">Resource allocation</span></h2>
<p>Focusing on limited areas of higher potential biodiversity promises greater immediate return on investment than spreading resources evenly or focusing on areas of little diversity but greater interest in biodiversity.</p>
<p>A second strategy focuses on areas that retain most of their original diversity, which typically require little or no restoration. These are typically non-urbanized, non-agricultural areas. Tropical areas often fit both criteria, given their natively high diversity and relative lack of development.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>United Nations <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (1992) and <a href="/wiki/Cartagena_Protocol_on_Biosafety" title="Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety">Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety</a>;</li>
<li>Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (<a href="/wiki/CITES" title="CITES">CITES</a>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention" title="Ramsar Convention">Ramsar Convention</a> (Wetlands);</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Bonn Convention</a> on Migratory Species;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Convention" title="World Heritage Convention">World Heritage Convention</a> (indirectly by protecting biodiversity habitats)</li>
<li>Regional Conventions such as the Apia Convention</li>
<li>Bilateral agreements such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan-Australia_Migratory_Bird_Agreement" title="Japan-Australia Migratory Bird Agreement">Japan-Australia Migratory Bird Agreement</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Global agreements such as the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>, give "sovereign national rights over biological resources" (not property). The agreements commit countries to "conserve biodiversity", "develop resources for sustainability" and "share the benefits" resulting from their use. Biodiverse countries that allow <a href="/wiki/Bioprospecting" title="Bioprospecting">bioprospecting</a> or collection of natural products, expect a share of the benefits rather than allowing the individual or institution that discovers/exploits the resource to capture them privately. Bioprospecting can become a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biopiracy" title="Biopiracy">biopiracy</a> when such principles are not respected.</p>
<p>Sovereignty principles can rely upon what is better known as <a href="/wiki/International_Treaty_on_Plant_Genetic_Resources_for_Food_and_Agriculture" title="International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture">Access and Benefit Sharing Agreements</a> (ABAs). The Convention on Biodiversity implies <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> between the source country and the collector, to establish which resource will be used and for what and to settle on a <a href="/wiki/International_Treaty_on_Plant_Genetic_Resources_for_Food_and_Agriculture" title="International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture">fair agreement on benefit sharing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_level_laws">National level laws</span></h3>
<p>Biodiversity is taken into account in some political and judicial decisions:</p>
<ul>
<li>The relationship between law and ecosystems is very ancient and has consequences for biodiversity. It is related to private and public property rights. It can define protection for threatened ecosystems, but also some rights and duties (for example, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> and hunting rights).</li>
<li>Law regarding species is more recent. It defines species that must be protected because they may be threatened by extinction. The U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a> is an example of an attempt to address the "law and species" issue.</li>
<li>Laws regarding gene pools are only about a century old. Domestication and plant breeding methods are not new, but advances in genetic engineering have led to tighter laws covering distribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically modified organisms</a>, gene <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> and process patents. Governments struggle to decide whether to focus on for example, genes, genomes, or organisms and species.</li>
</ul>
<p>Uniform approval for use of biodiversity as a legal standard has not been achieved, however. Bosselman argues that biodiversity should not be used as a legal standard, claiming that the remaining areas of scientific uncertainty cause unacceptable administrative waste and increase litigation without promoting preservation goals.</p>
<p>India passed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biological_Diversity_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biological Diversity Act (page does not exist)">Biological Diversity Act</a> in 2002 for the conservation of biological diversity in India. The Act also provides mechanisms for equitable sharing of benefits from the use of traditional biological resources and knowledge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytical_limits">Analytical limits</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomic_and_size_relationships">Taxonomic and size relationships</span></h3>
<p>Less than 1% of all species that have been described have been studied beyond simply noting their existence. The vast majority of Earth's species are microbial. Contemporary biodiversity physics is "firmly fixated on the visible [macroscopic] world". For example, microbial life is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolically" title="Metabolically">metabolically</a> and environmentally more diverse than multicellular life (see e.g., <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">extremophile</a>). "On the tree of life, based on analyses of small-subunit <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">ribosomal RNA</a>, visible life consists of barely noticeable twigs. The inverse relationship of size and population recurs higher on the evolutionary ladder—"to a first approximation, all multicellular species on Earth are insects". Insect extinction rates are high—supporting the Holocene extinction hypothesis. </p>
