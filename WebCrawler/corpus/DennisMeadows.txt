<p><b>Dennis L. Meadows</b> (born June 7, 1942) is an American scientist and Emeritus Professor of Systems Management, and former director of the Institute for Policy and Social Science Research at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Hampshire" title="University of New Hampshire">University of New Hampshire</a>. He is President of the Laboratory for Interactive Learning and widely known as the co-author of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limits_to_Growth" title="Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span></h2>
<p>Dennis Meadows received a BA from <a href="/wiki/Carleton_College" title="Carleton College">Carleton College</a>, a Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">Management</a> from the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Sloan_School_of_Management" title="MIT Sloan School of Management">MIT Sloan School of Management</a>, and holds four honorary doctorates.</p>
<p>He started working at the faculty of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> in the late 1960s. From 1970 to 1972 at MIT he was director of the "Club of Rome Project on the Predicament of Mankind". Further on Meadows has been a tenured professor in faculties of management, engineering, and social sciences. For many years he was the director of a graduate program based in business and engineering. He has facilitated workshops and developed innovative and complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_game" title="Strategic game">strategic games</a> all over the world for decades. In addition, Dr. Meadows has lectured in over 50 countries.</p>
<p>He has been the Director of three university research institutes: at MIT, Dartmouth College and the University of New Hampshire. He is the Past President of the International <a href="/wiki/System_Dynamics_Society" title="System Dynamics Society">System Dynamics Society</a> and the International Simulation and Games Association.</p>
<p>He has been a corporate board member and a consultant for government, industry and non-profit groups in the U.S. and many countries abroad. He co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Balaton_Group" title="Balaton Group">Balaton Group</a>, an international network of over 300 professionals in over 30 nations involved in systems science, public policy and sustainable development. In the year 2008 he supported the project <a href="/wiki/GPSO" title="GPSO">GPSO</a>.</p>
<p>He has received numerous international awards for his work, including the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Prize" title="Japan Prize">Japan Prize</a> in April 2009.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_of_Rome">Club of Rome</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> is a global <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> that deals with a variety of international political issues. It was founded in April 1968 and raised considerable public attention in 1972 with its report <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>. From 1970 to 1972 at MIT Meadows was director of the "Club of Rome Project on Predicament of mankind at MIT" which constructed the world model underlying that publication.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Limits_to_Growth">The Limits to Growth</span></h3>
<p><i>The Limits to Growth</i> is a 1972 book modeling the consequences of a rapidly growing <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> and finite resource supplies, commissioned by the Club of Rome. Meadows coauthored the book with <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella H. Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rgen_Randers" title="Jørgen Randers">Jørgen Randers</a>, and William W. Behrens III.</p>
<p>The book used the <a href="/wiki/World3" title="World3">World3</a> model to simulate the consequence of interactions between the Earth's and human systems. The book echoes some of the concerns and predictions of the Reverend <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus" title="Thomas Robert Malthus">Thomas Robert Malthus</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_on_the_Principle_of_Population" title="An Essay on the Principle of Population">An Essay on the Principle of Population</a></i> (1798).</p>
<p>The eventual purpose of <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was not to make specific predictions, but to explore how exponential growth interacts with finite resources. Because the size of resources is not known, only the general behavior can be explored.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_30-year_update">The 30-year update</span></h3>
<p>There has been a major cultural shift in the thinking about global processes in the last three decades of the 20th century. In an 2004 interview Meadows explained:</p>

<p>In their 1972 publication <i>Limits to Growth</i> their recommendations were focused on "how to slow growth". In the 2004 <i>Limits to growth : the 30-year update</i> the message has changed. Meadows explained: "Now we must tell people how to manage an orderly reduction of their activities back down below the limits of the earth's resources.." </p>
<p>In 2014, research at the University of Melbourne confirmed that the predictions from the book Limits to Growth were largely correct. Presently we are very close to tracking “business-as-usual” scenario from the book.</p>
