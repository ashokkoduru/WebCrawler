<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p>The <b>density of air</b>, (Greek: rho) (air density), is the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> per unit <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>. Air density, like air pressure, decreases with increasing altitude. It also changes with variation in temperature or <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> and at 15 °C air has a density of approximately 1.225 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram_per_cubic_metre" title="Kilogram per cubic metre">kg/m</a> (0.001225 g/cm, 0.0023769 <a href="/wiki/Slug_(mass)" title="Slug (mass)">slug</a>/ft, 0.0765 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lbm</a>/ft) according to ISA (<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a>).</p>
<p>The air density is a property used in many branches of science as <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>; <a href="/wiki/Gravimetric_analysis" title="Gravimetric analysis">gravimetric analysis</a>; the air-conditioning industry; atmospheric research and meteorology; the agricultural engineering in their modeling and tracking of Soil-Vegetation-Atmosphere-Transfer (SVAT) models; and the engineering community that deals with compressed air from industry utility, heating, drying and cooling processes in industries like cooling towers, vacuum and deep vacuum processes, high pressure processes, gas and light oil combustion processes that power turbine-powered airplanes, gas turbine-powered generators and heating furnaces, and air conditioning from deep mines to space capsules.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Density_of_air_calculations">Density of air calculations</span></h2>
<p>Depending on the measuring instruments, use, area of expertise and necessary rigor of the result different calculation criteria and sets of equations for the calculation of the density of air are used. This topic are some examples of calculations with the main variables involved, the amounts presented throughout these examples are properly referenced usual values, different values can be found in other references depending on the criteria used for the calculation . Furthermore we must pay attention to the fact that air is a mixture of gases and the calculation always simplify, to a greater or lesser extent, the properties of the mixture and the values for the composition according to the criteria of calculation.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity_.28water_vapor.29">Humidity (water vapor)</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">Humidity</a></div>
<p>The addition of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> to air (making the air humid) reduces the density of the air, which may at first appear counter-intuitive. This occurs because the molar mass of water (18 g/mol) is less than the molar mass of dry air (around 29 g/mol). For any gas, at a given temperature and pressure, the number of molecules present is constant for a particular volume (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_Law" title="Avogadro's Law">Avogadro's Law</a>). So when water molecules (water vapor) are added to a given volume of air, the dry air molecules must decrease by the same number, to keep the pressure or temperature from increasing. Hence the mass per unit volume of the gas (its density) decreases.</p>
