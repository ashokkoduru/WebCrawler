<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
<p>A conventional idea of a <b>sustainable fishery</b> is that it is one that is harvested at a sustainable rate, where the fish population does not decline over time because of fishing practices. <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">Sustainability</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> combines theoretical disciplines, such as the <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">population dynamics of fisheries</a>, with practical strategies, such as avoiding <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> through techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFQ" title="IFQ">individual fishing quotas</a>, curtailing <a href="/wiki/Destructive_fishing_practices" title="Destructive fishing practices">destructive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illegal,_unreported_and_unregulated_fishing" title="Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing">illegal fishing practices</a> by lobbying for appropriate law and policy, setting up protected areas, restoring collapsed fisheries, incorporating all externalities involved in harvesting marine ecosystems into fishery economics, educating stakeholders and the wider public, and developing independent certification programs.</p>
<p>Some primary concerns around sustainability are that heavy fishing pressures, such as <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Growth_overfishing" title="Growth overfishing">growth</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recruitment_overfishing" title="Recruitment overfishing">recruitment overfishing</a>, will result in the loss of significant <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_yield" title="Sustainable yield">potential yield</a>; that <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stock</a> structure will erode to the point where it loses diversity and resilience to environmental fluctuations; that ecosystems and their economic infrastructures will cycle between collapse and recovery; with each cycle less productive than its predecessor; and that changes will occur in the <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic</a> balance (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_down_marine_food_webs" title="Fishing down marine food webs">fishing down marine food webs</a>).</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>

<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fisheries</a> are believed to have peaked and begun a decline, with valuable habitats, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>, in critical condition. Current <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">farming</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piscivorous" title="Piscivorous">piscivorous</a> fish, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, does not solve the problem because farmed piscivores are fed products from wild fish, such as <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon" title="Aquaculture of salmon">Salmon farming</a> also has major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon#Issues" title="Aquaculture of salmon">negative impacts</a> on wild salmon. Fish that occupy the higher <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic levels</a> are less efficient sources of food energy.</p>
<p>Fishery ecosystems are an important subset of the wider marine environment. This article documents the views of <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries scientists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">marine conservationists</a> about innovative approaches towards sustainable fisheries.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>

<p>In his 1883 inaugural address to the International Fisheries Exhibition in London, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Huxley" title="Thomas Huxley">Thomas Huxley</a> asserted that overfishing or "permanent exhaustion" was scientifically impossible, and stated that probably "all the great sea fisheries are inexhaustible". In reality, by 1883 marine fisheries were already collapsing. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_Commission" title="United States Fish Commission">United States Fish Commission</a> was established 12 years earlier for the purpose of finding why fisheries in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> were declining. At the time of Huxley's address, the Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Halibut" title="Halibut">halibut</a> fishery had already collapsed (and has never recovered).</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_management_of_fisheries">Traditional management of fisheries</span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a> and the science underpinning it was distorted by its "narrow focus on <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">target populations</a> and the corresponding failure to account for ecosystem effects leading to declines of species abundance and diversity" and by perceiving the fishing industry as "the sole legitimate user, in effect the owner, of marine living resources." Historically, stock assessment scientists usually worked in government laboratories and considered their work to be providing services to the fishing industry. These scientists dismissed conservation issues and distanced themselves from the scientists and the science that raised the issues. This happened even as commercial fish stocks deteriorated, and even though many governments were signatories to binding conservation agreements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_sustainability">Defining sustainability</span></h2>
<p>The notion of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> is sometimes regarded as an unattainable, even illogical notion because development inevitably depletes and degrades the environment.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ray_Hilborn" title="Ray Hilborn">Ray Hilborn</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>, distinguishes three ways of defining a sustainable fishery:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Long term constant yield</i> is the idea that undisturbed nature establishes a steady state that changes little over time. Properly done, fishing at up to <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">maximum sustainable yield</a> allows nature to adjust to a new steady state, without compromising future harvests. However, this view is naive, because constancy is not an attribute of marine ecosystems, which dooms this approach. Stock abundance fluctuates naturally, changing the potential yield over short and long term periods.</li>
<li><i>Preserving intergenerational equity</i> acknowledges natural fluctuations and regards as unsustainable only practices which damage the <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetic</a> structure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss">destroy habitat</a>, or deplete <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stock levels</a> to the point where rebuilding requires more than a single generation. Providing rebuilding takes only one generation, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> may be economically foolish, but it is not unsustainable. This definition is widely accepted.</li>
<li><i>Maintaining a biological, social and economic system</i> considers the health of the human ecosystem as well as the marine ecosystem. A fishery which rotates among multiple species can deplete individual stocks and still be sustainable so long as the ecosystem retains its intrinsic integrity. Such a definition might consider as sustainable fishing practices that lead to the reduction and possible extinction of some species.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_sustainability">Social sustainability</span></h3>
<p>Fisheries and aquaculture are, directly or indirectly, a source of livelihood for over 500 million people, mostly in developing countries.</p>
<p>Social sustainability can conflict with biodiversity. A fishery is socially sustainable if the fishery ecosystem maintains the ability to deliver products the society can use. Major species shifts within the ecosystem could be acceptable as long as the flow of such products continues. Humans have been operating such regimes for thousands of years, transforming many ecosystems, depleting or driving to extinction many species.</p>

<p>According to Hilborn, the "loss of some species, and indeed transformation of the ecosystem is not incompatible with sustainable harvests." For example, in recent years, <a href="/wiki/Barndoor_skate" title="Barndoor skate">barndoor skates</a> have been caught as <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> in the western Atlantic. Their numbers have severely declined and they will probably go extinct if these catch rates continue. Even if the barndoor skate goes extinct, changing the ecosystem, there could still be sustainable fishing of other commercial species.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconciling_fisheries_with_conservation">Reconciling fisheries with conservation</span></h2>

<p>At the Fourth World Fisheries Congress in 2004, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a> asked, "How can fisheries science and conservation biology achieve a reconciliation?", then answered his own question, "By accepting each other’s essentials: that fishing should remain a viable occupation; and that aquatic ecosystems and their biodiversity are allowed to persist."</p>
<p>A relatively new concept is relationship farming. This is a way of operating farms so they restore the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> in their area. Re-establishing a healthy food chain can result in the farm automatically filtering out impurities from feed water and air, feeding its own food chain, and additionally producing high net yields for harvesting. An example is the large cattle ranch <a href="/wiki/Veta_La_Palma" title="Veta La Palma">Veta La Palma</a> in southern Spain. Relationship farming was first made popular by <a href="/wiki/Joel_Salatin" title="Joel Salatin">Joel Salatin</a> who created a 220 hectare relationship farm featured prominently in <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pollan" title="Michael Pollan">Michael Pollan's</a> book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Omnivore%27s_Dilemma" title="The Omnivore's Dilemma">The Omnivore's Dilemma</a></i> (2006) and the documentary films, <a href="/wiki/Food,_Inc." title="Food, Inc.">Food, Inc.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fresh_(2009_film)" title="Fresh (2009 film)">Fresh</a>. The basic concept of relationship farming is to put effort into building a healthy food chain, and then the food chain does the hard work.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Obstacles">Obstacles</span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a></div>
<p>Overfishing can be sustainable. According to Hilborn, overfishing can be "a misallocation of societies' resources", but it does not necessarily threaten conservation or sustainability".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a> is traditionally defined as harvesting so many fish that the yield is less than it would be if fishing were reduced. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_salmon" title="Pacific salmon">Pacific salmon</a> are usually managed by trying to determine how many spawning salmon, called the "<a href="/wiki/Escapement" title="Escapement">escapement</a>", are needed each generation to produce the maximum harvestable surplus. The optimum escapement is that needed to reach that surplus. If the escapement is half the optimum, then normal fishing looks like overfishing. But this is still sustainable fishing, which could continue indefinitely at its reduced stock numbers and yield. There is a wide range of escapement sizes that present no threat that the stock might collapse or that the stock structure might erode.</p>
<p>On the other hand, overfishing can precede severe stock depletion and fishery collapse. Hilborn points out that continuing to exert fishing pressure while production decreases, stock collapses and the fishery fails, is largely "the product of institutional failure."</p>
<p>Today over 70% of fish species are either fully exploited, overexploited, depleted, or recovering from depletion. If overfishing does not decrease, it is predicted that stocks of all species currently commercially fished for will collapse by 2048.”</p>
<p>A Hubbert linearization (<a href="/wiki/Hubbert_curve" title="Hubbert curve">Hubbert curve</a>) has been applied to the <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> industry, as well as charting the price of <a href="/wiki/Caviar" title="Caviar">caviar</a>, which depends on <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon" title="Sturgeon">sturgeon</a> stocks. Another example is <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>. Comparing fisheries and mineral extraction tells us that human pressure on the environment is causing a wide range of resources to go through a Hubbert depletion cycle.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_modification">Habitat modification</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fishing" title="Environmental effects of fishing">Environmental effects of fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Destructive_fishing_practices" title="Destructive fishing practices">Destructive fishing practices</a></div>
<p>Nearly all the world’s <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelves</a>, and large areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slopes</a>, underwater ridges, and <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a>, have had heavy <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">bottom trawls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_dredge" title="Fishing dredge">dredges</a> repeatedly dragged over their surfaces. For fifty years, governments and organizations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank" title="Asian Development Bank">Asian Development Bank</a>, have encouraged the fishing industry to develop trawler fleets. Repeated bottom trawling and dredging literally flattens diversity in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> habitat, radically changing the associated communities.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_and_climate_change" title="Fisheries and climate change">Fisheries and climate change</a></div>
<p>Rising ocean temperatures and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> are radically altering <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystems</a>. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> is modifying fish distribution and the productivity of marine and freshwater species. This reduces sustainable catch levels across many habitats, puts pressure on resources needed for <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, on the communities that depend on fisheries, and on the oceans' ability to capture and store carbon (<a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological pump</a>). <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">Sea level rise</a> puts coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_community" title="Fishing community">fishing communities</a> at risk, while changing rainfall patterns and water use impact on inland (freshwater) fisheries and aquaculture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_pollution">Ocean pollution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a></div>
<p>A recent survey of global ocean health concluded that all parts of the ocean have been impacted by human development and that 41 percent has been fouled with human polluted runoff, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, and other abuses. <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">Pollution</a> is not easy to fix, because pollution sources are so dispersed, and are built into the economic systems we depend on.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) mapped the impacts of stressors such as climate change, pollution, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_species" title="Exotic species">exotic species</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" title="Over-exploitation">over-exploitation</a> of resources on the oceans. The report shows at least 75 percent of the world's key fishing grounds may be affected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases_and_toxins">Diseases and toxins</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fish_diseases_and_parasites" title="Fish diseases and parasites">Fish diseases and parasites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmful_algal_blooms" title="Harmful algal blooms">Harmful algal blooms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_in_fish" title="Mercury in fish">Mercury in fish</a></div>
<p>Large predator fish contain significant amounts of mercury, a neurotoxin which can affect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fetal_development" title="Fetal development">fetal development</a>, memory, mental focus, and produce tremors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irrigation">Irrigation</span></h3>

<p>Lakes are dependent on the inflow of water from its <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>. In some areas, aggressive irrigation has caused this inflow to decrease significantly, causing water depletion and a shrinking of the lake. The most notable example is the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, formerly among the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Largest_lakes" title="Largest lakes">largest lakes</a> in the world, now only a tenth of its former surface area.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remediation">Remediation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_management">Fisheries management</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a> draws on <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries science</a> to enable sustainable exploitation. Modern fisheries management is often defined as mandatory rules based on concrete objectives and a mix of management techniques, enforced by a <a href="/wiki/Monitoring_control_and_surveillance" title="Monitoring control and surveillance">monitoring control and surveillance</a> system.</p>
<ul>
<li>Ideas and rules: Economist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Romer" title="Paul Romer">Paul Romer</a> believes sustainable growth is possible providing the right ideas (technology) are combined with the right rules, rather than simply hectoring fishers. There has been no lack of innovative ideas about how to harvest fish. He characterizes failures as primarily failures to apply appropriate rules.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Fishing subsidies: Government subsidies influence many of the world fisheries. Operating cost subsidies allow European and Asian <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleets</a> to fish in distant waters, such as West Africa. Many experts reject fishing subsidies and advocate restructuring incentives globally to help struggling fisheries recover.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Economics: Another focus of conservationists is on curtailing detrimental human activities by improving fisheries' market structure with techniques such as salable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFQ" title="IFQ">fishing quotas</a>, like those set up by the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Atlantic_Fisheries_Organization" title="Northwest Atlantic Fisheries Organization">Northwest Atlantic Fisheries Organization</a>, or laws such as those listed below.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Payment_for_ecosystem_services" title="Payment for ecosystem services">Payment for Ecosystem Services</a>: Environmental Economist, Essam Y Mohammed, argues that by creating direct economic incentives, whereby people are able to receive payment for the services their property provides, will help to establish sustainable fisheries around the world as well as inspire conservation where it otherwise would not.</li>
<li>Sustainable fisheries certification: A promising direction is the independent certification programs for sustainable fisheries conducted by organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Stewardship_Council" title="Marine Stewardship Council">Marine Stewardship Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friend_of_the_Sea" title="Friend of the Sea">Friend of the Sea</a>. These programs work at raising consumer awareness and insight into the nature of their seafood purchases.</li>
<li>Ecosystem based fisheries: See next section</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_based_fisheries">Ecosystem based fisheries</span></h3>

<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_ecology" title="Marine ecology">marine ecologist</a> Chris Frid, the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a> points to <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">marine pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> as the causes of recent, unprecedented declines in fish populations. Frid counters that <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> has also altered the way the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> works. "Everybody would like to see the rebuilding of fish stocks and this can only be achieved if we understand all of the influences, human and natural, on fish dynamics.” He adds: “fish communities can be altered in a number of ways, for example they can decrease if particular-sized individuals of a species are targeted, as this affects <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries#Predator-prey_equations" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">predator and prey dynamics</a>. Fishing, however, is not the sole cause of changes to marine life—<a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">pollution</a> is another example....No one factor operates in isolation and components of the ecosystem respond differently to each individual factor."</p>

This can be contrasted with the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_Approach" title="Ecosystem Approach">ecosystem-based approach</a>. Ecosystem-based fishery concepts have been implemented in some regions. In a 2007 effort to "stimulate much needed discussion" and "clarify the essential components" of ecosystem-based fisheries science, a group of scientists offered the following <b>ten commandments for ecosystem-based fisheries scientists</b></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_protected_areas">Marine protected areas</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_protected_areas" title="Marine protected areas">Marine protected areas</a></div>
<p>Strategies and techniques for marine conservation tend to combine theoretical disciplines, such as population biology, with practical conservation strategies, such as setting up protected areas, as with <a href="/wiki/Marine_protected_area" title="Marine protected area">Marine Protected Areas</a> (MPAs) or <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Marine_Conservation_Area" title="Voluntary Marine Conservation Area">Voluntary Marine Conservation Areas</a>. Each nation defines MPAs independently, but they commonly involve increased protection for the area from fishing and other threats.</p>
<p>Marine life is not evenly distributed in the oceans. Most of the really valuable ecosystems are in relatively shallow coastal waters, above or near the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, where the sunlit waters are often nutrient rich from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_runoff" title="Land runoff">land runoff</a> or <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwellings</a> at the continental edge, allowing photosynthesis, which energizes the lowest trophic levels. In the 1970s, for reasons more to do with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_drilling" title="Oil drilling">oil drilling</a> than with fishing, the U.S. extended its jurisdiction, then 12 miles from the coast, to 200 miles. This made huge shelf areas part of its territory. Other nations followed, extending national control to what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> (EEZ). This move has had many implications for fisheries conservation, since it means that most of the most productive maritime ecosystems are now under national jurisdictions, opening possibilities for protecting these ecosystems by passing appropriate laws.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a> characterises marine protected areas as "a conservation tool of revolutionary importance that is being incorporated into the fisheries mainstream." <a href="/wiki/The_Pew_Charitable_Trusts" title="The Pew Charitable Trusts">The Pew Charitable Trusts</a> have funded various initiatives aimed at encouraging the development of MPAs and other ocean conservation measures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_farming">Fish farming</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salmon_farming_issues" title="Salmon farming issues">Salmon farming issues</a></div>
<p>There exists concerns that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_farm" title="Fish farm">farmed fish</a> cannot produce necessary yields efficiently. For example, farmed salmon eat three pounds of wild fish to produce one pound of salmon.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_and_treaties">Laws and treaties</span></h3>
<p>International laws and treaties related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">marine conservation</a> include the 1966 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_Fishing_and_Conservation_of_Living_Resources_of_the_High_Seas" title="Convention on Fishing and Conservation of Living Resources of the High Seas">Convention on Fishing and Conservation of Living Resources of the High Seas</a>. <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> laws related to marine conservation include the 1972 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Mammal_Protection_Act_of_1972" title="Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972">Marine Mammal Protection Act</a>, as well as the 1972 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Dumping_Act" title="Ocean Dumping Act">Marine Protection, Research and Sanctuaries Act</a> which established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._National_Marine_Sanctuary" title="U.S. National Marine Sanctuary">National Marine Sanctuaries</a> program. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnuson-Stevens_Fishery_Conservation_and_Management_Act" title="Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act">Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awareness_campaigns">Awareness campaigns</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_seafood_advisory_lists_and_certification" title="Sustainable seafood advisory lists and certification">Sustainable seafood advisory lists and certification</a></div>

<p>Various organizations promote sustainable fishing strategies, educate the public and stakeholders, and lobby for conservation law and policy. <p>The United Nations <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> include, as goal #7: target 2, the intention to "reduce biodiversity loss, achieving, by 2010, a significant reduction in the rate of loss", including improving fisheries management to reduce depletion of fish stocks.</p>
<p>Some organizations certify fishing industry players for sustainable or good practices, such as the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Stewardship_Council" title="Marine Stewardship Council">Marine Stewardship Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friend_of_the_Sea" title="Friend of the Sea">Friend of the Sea</a>.</p>
<p>Other organizations offer advice to members of the public who eat with an eye to sustainability. According to the marine conservation biologist <a href="/wiki/Callum_Roberts" title="Callum Roberts">Callum Roberts</a>, four criteria apply when choosing seafood:</p>
<blockquote>
<ul>
<li>Is the species in trouble in the wild where the animals were caught?</li>
<li>Does fishing for the species damage ocean habitats?</li>
<li>Is there a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> taken with the target species?</li>
<li>Does the fishery have a problem with discards—generally, undersized animals caught and thrown away because their market value is low?</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_issues">Data issues</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_quality">Data quality</span></h3>
<p>One of the major impediments to the rational control of marine resources is inadequate data. According to fisheries scientist Milo Adkison (2007), the primary limitation in fisheries management decisions is poor data. Fisheries management decisions are often based on population models, but the models need quality data to be accurate. Scientists and fishery managers would be better served with simpler models and improved data.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unreported_fishing">Unreported fishing</span></h3>
<p>Estimates of illegal catch losses range between $10 billion and $23 billion annually, representing between 11 and 26 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shifting_baselines">Shifting baselines</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shifting_baseline" title="Shifting baseline">Shifting baselines</a> is a term which describes the way significant changes to a system are measured against previous baselines, which themselves may represent significant changes from the original state of the system. The term was first used by the <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries scientist</a> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a> in his paper "Anecdotes and the shifting baseline syndrome of fisheries". Pauly developed the term in reference to <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a> where fisheries scientists sometimes fail to identify the correct "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/baseline" title="wiktionary:baseline">baseline</a>" population size (e.g. how abundant a fish species population was <i>before</i> human exploitation) and thus work with a <b>shifted baseline</b>. He describes the way that radically depleted fisheries were evaluated by experts who used the state of the fishery at the start of their careers as the baseline, rather than the fishery in its untouched state. Areas that swarmed with a particular species hundreds of years ago, may have experienced long term decline, but it is the level of decades previously that is considered the appropriate reference point for current populations. In this way large declines in ecosystems or species over long periods of time were, and are, masked. There is a loss of <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> of change that occurs when each <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generation</a> redefines what is "natural".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Looting_the_seas"><i>Looting the seas</i></span></h2>
<p><i>Looting the seas</i> is the name given by the <a href="/wiki/International_Consortium_of_Investigative_Journalists" title="International Consortium of Investigative Journalists">International Consortium of Investigative Journalists</a> to a series of journalistic investigations into areas directly affecting the sustainability of fisheries. So far they have investigated three areas involving fraud, negligence and overfishing:</p>
<ul>
<li>The black market in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a></li>
<li>Subsidies propping up the Spanish fishing industry</li>
<li>Overfishing of the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_jack_mackerel" title="Chilean jack mackerel">southern jack mackerel</a></li>
</ul>
