<p><b>Flywheel energy storage</b> (<b>FES</b>) works by accelerating a rotor (<a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a>) to a very high speed and maintaining the energy in the system as <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">rotational energy</a>. When energy is extracted from the system, the flywheel's rotational speed is reduced as a consequence of the principle of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a>; adding energy to the system correspondingly results in an increase in the speed of the flywheel.</p>
<p>Most FES systems use electricity to accelerate and decelerate the flywheel, but devices that directly use <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> are being developed. Since FES can be used to absorb or release electrical energy, such devices may sometimes be incorrectly and confusingly described as either mechanical or <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> batteries.</p>
<p>Advanced FES systems have rotors made of high strength carbon-fiber composites, suspended by <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">magnetic bearings</a>, and spinning at speeds from 20,000 to over 50,000 rpm in a vacuum enclosure. Such flywheels can come up to speed in a matter of minutes – reaching their energy capacity much more quickly than some other forms of storage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_components">Main components</span></h2>

<p>A typical system consists of a rotor suspended by <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a> inside a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_chamber" title="Vacuum chamber">vacuum chamber</a> to reduce friction, connected to a combination <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generator</a>.</p>
<p>First generation flywheel energy storage systems use a large <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> flywheel rotating on mechanical bearings. Newer systems use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon-fiber" title="Carbon-fiber">carbon-fiber</a> composite <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">rotors</a> that have a higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> than steel and are an order of magnitude less heavy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">Magnetic bearings</a> are sometimes used instead of <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">mechanical bearings</a>, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>.</p>
<p>Other components are <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hub" title="wikt:hub">hub</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shaft" title="wikt:shaft">shaft</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_future_use_of_superconducting_bearings">Possible future use of superconducting bearings</span></h3>

<p>The expense of refrigeration led to the early dismissal of low-temperature superconductors for use in magnetic bearings. However, <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity" title="High-temperature superconductivity">high-temperature superconductor</a> (HTSC) bearings may be economical and could possibly extend the time energy could be stored economically. Hybrid bearing systems are most likely to see use first. High-temperature superconductor bearings have historically had problems providing the lifting forces necessary for the larger designs, but can easily provide a stabilizing force. Therefore, in hybrid bearings, permanent magnets support the load and high-temperature superconductors are used to stabilize it. The reason superconductors can work well stabilizing the load is because they are perfect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnet" title="Diamagnet">diamagnets</a>. If the rotor tries to drift off center, a restoring force due to <a href="/wiki/Flux_pinning" title="Flux pinning">flux pinning</a> restores it. This is known as the magnetic stiffness of the bearing. Rotational axis vibration can occur due to low stiffness and damping, which are inherent problems of superconducting magnets, preventing the use of completely superconducting magnetic bearings for flywheel applications.</p>
<p>Since flux pinning is an important factor for providing the stabilizing and lifting force, the HTSC can be made much more easily for FES than for other uses. HTSC powders can be formed into arbitrary shapes so long as flux pinning is strong. An ongoing challenge that has to be overcome before superconductors can provide the full lifting force for an FES system is finding a way to suppress the decrease of levitation force and the gradual fall of rotor during operation caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flux_creep" title="Flux creep">flux creep</a> of the superconducting material.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Flywheel#Physics" title="Flywheel">Flywheel § Physics</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span></h3>
<p>Compared with other ways to store electricity, FES systems have long lifetimes (lasting decades with little or no maintenance; full-cycle lifetimes quoted for flywheels range from in excess of 10, up to 10, cycles of use), high <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> (100–130 W·h/kg, or 360–500 kJ/kg), and large maximum power output. The <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a> (<i>ratio of energy out per energy in</i>) of flywheels, also known as round-trip efficiency, can be as high as 90%. Typical capacities range from 3 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kWh</a> to 133 kWh. Rapid charging of a system occurs in less than 15 minutes. The high specific energies often cited with flywheels can be a little misleading as commercial systems built have much lower specific energy, for example 11 W·h/kg, or 40 kJ/kg.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Material_properties">Material properties</span></h4>
<p>For energy storage purposes, materials with high strength, and low density are desirable. For this reason, composite materials are frequently being used in advanced flywheels. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_strength" title="Specific strength">strength-to-density ratio</a> of a material can be expressed in Wh/kg (or Nm/kg); values greater than 400 Wh/kg can be achieved by certain composite materials.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotor_materials">Rotor materials</span></h4>
<p>Several modern flywheel rotors are made from composite materials. Examples include the carbon fiber composite flywheel from Beacon Power Corporation and the <i>PowerThru</i> flywheel from Phillips Service Industries. Alternatively, Calnetix utilizes aerospace grade high-performance steel in their flywheel construction.</p>
<p>For these rotors, the relationship between material properties, geometry and energy density can be expressed by using a weighed-average approach.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tensile_strength_and_failure_modes">Tensile strength and failure modes</span></h3>
<p>One of the primary limits to flywheel design is the tensile strength of the material used for the rotor. Generally speaking, the stronger the disc, the faster it may be spun, and the more energy the system can store.</p>
<p>When the tensile strength of a composite flywheel's outer binding cover is exceeded, the binding cover will fracture, followed by the wheel shattering as the outer wheel compression is lost around the entire circumference, releasing all of its stored energy at once; this is commonly referred to as "flywheel explosion" since wheel fragments can reach kinetic energy comparable to that of a bullet. Composite materials that are wound and glued in layers tend to disintegrate quickly, first into small-diameter filaments that entangle and slow each other, and then into red-hot powder, instead of large chunks of high-velocity shrapnel as can occur with a cast metal flywheel.</p>
<p>For a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cast_metal" title="Cast metal">cast metal</a> flywheel, the failure limit is the binding strength of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_boundaries" title="Grain boundaries">grain boundaries</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycrystalline" title="Polycrystalline">polycrystalline</a> molded metal. Aluminum in particular suffers from <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a> and can develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfracture" title="Microfracture">microfractures</a> due to repeated low-energy stretching. Angular forces may cause portions of a metal flywheel to bend outward and begin dragging on the outer containment vessel, or to separate completely and bounce randomly around the interior. The rest of the flywheel is now severely unbalanced, which may lead to rapid bearing failure from vibration, and sudden shock fracturing of large segments of the flywheel.</p>
<p>Traditional flywheel systems require strong containment vessels as a safety precaution, which increases the total mass of the device. The energy release from failure can be dampened with a gelatinous or encapsulated liquid inner housing lining, which will boil and absorb the energy of destruction. Still, many customers of large-scale flywheel energy-storage systems prefer to have them embedded in the ground to halt any material that might escape the containment vessel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_storage_efficiency">Energy storage efficiency</span></h3>
<p>Flywheel energy storage systems using mechanical bearings can lose 20% to 50% of their energy in two hours. Much of the friction responsible for this energy loss results from the flywheel changing orientation due to the rotation of the earth (an effect similar to that shown by a <a href="/wiki/Foucault_pendulum" title="Foucault pendulum">Foucault pendulum</a>). This change in orientation is resisted by the gyroscopic forces exerted by the flywheel's angular momentum, thus exerting a force against the mechanical bearings. This force increases friction. This can be avoided by aligning the flywheel's axis of rotation parallel to that of the earth's axis of rotation.</p>
<p>Conversely, flywheels with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearings" title="Magnetic bearings">magnetic bearings</a> and high vacuum can maintain 97% <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_efficiency" title="Mechanical efficiency">mechanical efficiency</a>, and 85% round trip efficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_angular_momentum_in_vehicles">Effects of angular momentum in vehicles</span></h3>
<p>When used in vehicles, flywheels also act as <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopes</a>, since their <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> is typically of a similar order of magnitude as the forces acting on the moving vehicle. This property may be detrimental to the vehicle's handling characteristics while turning or driving on rough ground; driving onto the side of a sloped embankment may cause wheels to partially lift off the ground as the flywheel opposes sideways tilting forces. On the other hand, this property could be utilized to keep the car balanced so as to keep it from rolling over during sharp turns.</p>
<p>When a flywheel is used entirely for its effects on the attitude of a vehicle, rather than for energy storage, it is called a <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">reaction wheel</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Control_moment_gyroscope" title="Control moment gyroscope">control moment gyroscope</a>.</p>
<p>The resistance of angular tilting can be almost completely removed by mounting the flywheel within an appropriately applied set of <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">gimbals</a>, allowing the flywheel to retain its original orientation without affecting the vehicle (see <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope#Properties" title="Gyroscope"><i>Properties</i> of a gyroscope</a>). This doesn't avoid the complication of <a href="/wiki/Gimbal_lock" title="Gimbal lock">gimbal lock</a>, and so a compromise between the number of gimbals and the angular freedom is needed.</p>
<p>The center axle of the flywheel acts as a single gimbal, and if aligned vertically, allows for the 360 degrees of yaw in a horizontal plane. However, for instance driving up-hill requires a second pitch gimbal, and driving on the side of a sloped embankment requires a third roll gimbal.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-motion_gimbals">Full-motion gimbals</span></h4>
<p>Although the flywheel itself may be of a flat ring shape, a free-movement gimbal mounting inside a vehicle requires a spherical volume for the flywheel to freely rotate within. Left to its own, a spinning flywheel in a vehicle would slowly precess following the Earth's rotation, and precess further yet in vehicles that travel long distances over the Earth's curved spherical surface.</p>
<p>A full-motion gimbal has additional problems of how to communicate power into and out of the flywheel, since the flywheel could potentially flip completely over once a day, precessing as the Earth rotates. Full free rotation would require <a href="/wiki/Slip_ring" title="Slip ring">slip rings</a> around each gimbal axis for power conductors, further adding to the design complexity.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited-motion_gimbals">Limited-motion gimbals</span></h4>
<p>To reduce space usage, the gimbal system may be of a limited-movement design, using shock absorbers to cushion sudden rapid motions within a certain number of degrees of out-of-plane angular rotation, and then gradually forcing the flywheel to adopt the vehicle's current orientation. This reduces the gimbal movement space around a ring-shaped flywheel from a full sphere, to a short thickened cylinder, encompassing for example ± 30 degrees of pitch and ± 30 degrees of roll in all directions around the flywheel.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Counterbalancing_of_angular_momentum">Counterbalancing of angular momentum</span></h4>
<p>An alternative solution to the problem is to have two joined flywheels spinning synchronously in opposite directions. They would have a total angular momentum of zero and no gyroscopic effect. A problem with this solution is that when the difference between the momentum of each flywheel is anything other than zero the housing of the two flywheels would exhibit torque. Both wheels must be maintained at the same speed to keep the angular velocity at zero. Strictly speaking, the two flywheels would exert a huge <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torqueing</a> moment at the central point, trying to bend the axle. However, if the axle were sufficiently strong, no gyroscopic forces would have a net effect on the sealed container, so no torque would be noticed.</p>
<p>To further balance the forces and spread out strain, a single large flywheel can be balanced by two half-size flywheels on each side, or the flywheels can be reduced in size to be a series of alternating layers spinning in opposite directions. However this increases housing and bearing complexity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Automotive">Automotive</span></h4>
<p>In the 1950s, flywheel-powered buses, known as <a href="/wiki/Gyrobus" title="Gyrobus">gyrobuses</a>, were used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yverdon" title="Yverdon">Yverdon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> (<a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>) and there is ongoing research to make flywheel systems that are smaller, lighter, cheaper and have a greater capacity. It is hoped that flywheel systems can replace conventional chemical batteries for mobile applications, such as for electric vehicles. Proposed flywheel systems would eliminate many of the disadvantages of existing battery power systems, such as low capacity, long charge times, heavy weight and short usable lifetimes. Flywheels may have been used in the experimental <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Patriot" title="Chrysler Patriot">Chrysler Patriot</a>, though that has been disputed.</p>
<p>Flywheels have also been proposed for use in <a href="/wiki/Continuously_variable_transmission" title="Continuously variable transmission">continuously variable transmissions</a>. Punch Powertrain is currently working on such a device.</p>
<p>During the 1990s, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rosen_Motors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rosen Motors (page does not exist)">Rosen Motors</a> developed a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Series_hybrid" title="Series hybrid">series hybrid</a> automotive powertrain using a 55,000 rpm flywheel to provide bursts of acceleration which the small gas turbine engine could not provide. The flywheel also stored energy through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking">regenerative braking</a>. The flywheel was composed of a <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> hub with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> cylinder and was <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">gimbal</a>-mounted to minimize adverse gyroscopic effects on vehicle handling. The prototype vehicle was successfully road tested in 1997 but was never mass-produced.</p>
<p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Volvo_Cars" title="Volvo Cars">Volvo</a> announced a flywheel system fitted to the rear axle of its S60 sedan. Braking action spins the flywheel at up to 60,000 rpm and stops the front-mounted engine. Flywheel energy is applied via a special transmission to partially or completely power the vehicle. The 20-centimetre (7.9 in), 6-kilogram (13 lb) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> flywheel spins in a vacuum to eliminate friction. When partnered with a four-cylinder engine, it offers up to a 25 percent reduction in fuel consumption versus a comparably performing turbo six-cylinder, providing an 80 hp boost and allowing it to reach 100 kilometres per hour (62 mph) in 5.5 seconds. The company did not announce specific plans to include the technology in its product line.</p>
<p>In July 2014 <a href="/wiki/GKN" title="GKN">GKN</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/Williams_Grand_Prix_Engineering" title="Williams Grand Prix Engineering">Williams</a> Hybrid Power (WHP) division and intends to supply 500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> <i>Gyrodrive</i> electric flywheel systems to urban bus operators over the next two years As the former developer name implies, these were originally designed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formula_one" title="Formula one">Formula one</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_racing" title="Motor racing">motor racing</a> applications. In September 2014, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Bus_Company" title="Oxford Bus Company">Oxford Bus Company</a> announced that it is introducing 14 <i>Gyrodrive hybrid</i> buses by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dennis" title="Alexander Dennis">Alexander Dennis</a> on its Brookes Bus operation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_vehicles">Rail vehicles</span></h4>
<p>Flywheel systems have been used experimentally in small <a href="/wiki/Electric_locomotive" title="Electric locomotive">electric locomotives</a> for shunting or <a href="/wiki/Switcher" title="Switcher">switching</a>, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Sentinel_Waggon_Works" title="Sentinel Waggon Works">Sentinel-Oerlikon Gyro Locomotive</a>. Larger electric locomotives, e.g. <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_70_(electric)" title="British Rail Class 70 (electric)">British Rail Class 70</a>, have sometimes been fitted with flywheel boosters to carry them over gaps in the <a href="/wiki/Third_rail" title="Third rail">third rail</a>. Advanced flywheels, such as the 133 kWh pack of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>, can take a train from a standing start up to cruising speed.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parry_People_Mover" title="Parry People Mover">Parry People Mover</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">railcar</a> which is powered by a flywheel. It was trialled on Sundays for 12 months on the <a href="/wiki/Stourbridge_Town_Branch_Line" title="Stourbridge Town Branch Line">Stourbridge Town Branch Line</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> during 2006 and 2007 and was intended to be introduced as a full service by the train operator <a href="/wiki/London_Midland" title="London Midland">London Midland</a> in December 2008 once two units had been ordered. In January 2010, both units are in operation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_electrification">Rail electrification</span></h4>
<p>FES can be used at the lineside of electrified railways to help regulate the line voltage thus improving the acceleration of unmodified electric trains and the amount of energy recovered back to the line during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" title="Regenerative braking">regenerative braking</a>, thus lowering energy bills. Trials have taken place in London, New York, Lyon and Tokyo, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_MTA" title="New York MTA">New York MTA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Rail_Road" title="Long Island Rail Road">Long Island Rail Road</a> is now investing $5.2m in a pilot project on LIRR's <a href="/wiki/West_Hempstead_Branch" title="West Hempstead Branch">West Hempstead Branch</a> line. These trials and systems store kinetic energy in rotors consisting of a carbon-glass composite cylinder packed with neodymium-iron-boron powder that forms a permanent magnet. These spin at up to 37800rev/min, and each 100 kW unit can store 11 MJ of re-usable energy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uninterruptible_power_supplies">Uninterruptible power supplies</span></h3>
<p>Flywheel power storage systems in production as of 2001 have storage capacities comparable to batteries and faster discharge rates. They are mainly used to provide load leveling for large battery systems, such as an <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">uninterruptible power supply</a> for data centers as they save a considerable amount of space compared to battery systems.</p>
<p>Flywheel maintenance in general runs about one-half the cost of traditional battery UPS systems. The only maintenance is a basic annual preventive maintenance routine and replacing the bearings every five to ten years, which takes about four hours. Newer flywheel systems completely levitate the spinning mass using maintenance-free <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">magnetic bearings</a>, thus eliminating mechanical bearing maintenance and failures.</p>
<p>Costs of a fully installed flywheel UPS (including power conditioning) are (in 2009) about $330 per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatt</a> (for 15 seconds full-load capacity).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_Laboratories">Test Laboratories</span></h3>
<p>A long-standing niche market for flywheel power systems are facilities where circuit-breakers and similar devices are tested: even a small household circuit-breaker may be rated to interrupt a current of 10,000 or more amperes, and larger units may have <a href="/wiki/Breaking_capacity" title="Breaking capacity">interrupting ratings</a> of 100,000 or 1,000,000 amperes. The enormous transient loads produced by deliberately forcing such devices to demonstrate their ability to interrupt simulated short circuits would have unacceptable effects on the local grid if these tests were done directly from building power. Typically such a laboratory will have several large motor-generator sets, which can be spun up to speed over some minutes; then the motor is disconnected before a circuit breaker is tested.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_laboratories">Physics laboratories</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tokamak" title="Tokamak">Tokamak</a> fusion experiments need very high currents for brief intervals (mainly to power large electromagnets for a few seconds).</p>
<ul>
<li>JET (the <a href="/wiki/Joint_European_Torus" title="Joint European Torus">Joint European Torus</a>) has two 775 ton flywheels (installed in 1981) that spin up to 225 rpm. Each flywheel stores 3.75 GJ and can deliver at up to 400MW.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/ASDEX_Upgrade" title="ASDEX Upgrade">ASDEX</a> has 3 flywheel generators.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DIII-D_(fusion_reactor)" title="DIII-D (fusion reactor)">DIII-D</a> tokamak at <a href="/wiki/General_Atomics" title="General Atomics">General Atomics</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Large_Torus" title="Princeton Large Torus">Princeton Large Torus</a> (PLT) at the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Plasma_Physics_Laboratory" title="Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory">Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Also the non-tokamak: <a href="/wiki/Nimrod_(synchrotron)" title="Nimrod (synchrotron)">Nimrod synchrotron</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_Appleton_Laboratory" title="Rutherford Appleton Laboratory">Rutherford Appleton Laboratory</a> had two 30 ton flywheels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_launching_systems">Aircraft launching systems</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_class_aircraft_carrier" title="Ford class aircraft carrier"><i>Gerald R. Ford</i>-class aircraft carrier</a> will use flywheels to accumulate energy from the ship's power supply, for rapid release into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EMALS" title="EMALS">electromagnetic aircraft launch system</a>. The shipboard power system cannot on its own supply the high power transients necessary to launch aircraft. Each of four rotors will store 121 MJ at 6400 rpm. They can store 122 MJ in 45 secs and release it in 2–3 seconds. The flywheel energy densities are 28 kJ/kg; including the stators and cases this comes down to 18.1 kJ/kg, excluding the torque frame.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_G2_flywheel_for_spacecraft_energy_storage">NASA G2 flywheel for spacecraft energy storage</span></h3>
<p>This was a design funded by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s Glenn Research Center and intended for component testing in a laboratory environment. It used a carbon fiber rim with a titanium hub designed to spin at 60,000 rpm, mounted on magnetic bearings. Weight was limited to 250 pounds. Storage was 525 W-hr (1.89 MJ) and could be charged or discharged at 1 kW. The working model shown in the photograph at the top of the page ran at 41,000 rpm on September 2, 2004.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amusement_rides">Amusement rides</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incredible_Hulk_Coaster" title="Incredible Hulk Coaster">Incredible Hulk roller coaster</a> at <a href="/wiki/Islands_of_Adventure" title="Islands of Adventure">Universal's Islands of Adventure</a> features a rapidly accelerating uphill launch as opposed to the typical gravity drop. This is achieved through powerful <a href="/wiki/Traction_motor" title="Traction motor">traction motors</a> that throw the car up the track. To achieve the brief very high current required to accelerate a full coaster train to full speed uphill, the park utilizes several motor generator sets with large flywheels. Without these stored energy units, the park would have to invest in a new substation or risk <a href="/wiki/Brownout_(electricity)" title="Brownout (electricity)">browning-out</a> the local energy grid every time the ride launches.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulse_power_for_railguns">Pulse power for railguns</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compulsator" title="Compulsator">Compulsators</a> (low-inductance alternators) can provide the pulsed power for <a href="/wiki/Railgun" title="Railgun">railguns</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_sports">Motor sports</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy_recovery_system" title="Kinetic energy recovery system">Kinetic energy recovery system</a></div>

<p>Using a <a href="/wiki/Continuously_variable_transmission" title="Continuously variable transmission">continuously variable transmission</a> (CVT), energy is recovered from the drive train during braking and stored in a flywheel. This stored energy is then used during acceleration by altering the ratio of the CVT. In motor sports applications this energy is used to improve acceleration rather than reduce carbon dioxide emissions – although the same technology can be applied to road cars to improve fuel efficiency.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Automobile_Club_de_l%27Ouest" title="Automobile Club de l'Ouest">Automobile Club de l'Ouest</a>, the organizer behind the annual <a href="/wiki/24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="24 Hours of Le Mans">24 Hours of Le Mans</a> event and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Mans_Series" title="Le Mans Series">Le Mans Series</a>, is currently "studying specific rules for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Mans_prototype" title="Le Mans prototype">LMP1</a> which will be equipped with a kinetic energy recovery system."</p>
<p>Williams Hybrid Power, a subsidiary of Williams F1 Racing team, have supplied Porsche and Audi with flywheel based hybrid system for Porsche's 911 GT3 R Hybrid and Audi's R18 e-Tron Quattro. Audi's victory in 2012 24 Hours of Le Mans is the first for a hybrid(diesel-electric) vehicle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_energy_storage">Grid energy storage</span></h3>
<p><span id="Frequency_regulation"></span></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Flywheel_storage_power_system" title="Flywheel storage power system">Flywheel storage power system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">Grid energy storage</a></div>
<p>Lower carbon emissions, faster response times and ability to buy power at off-peak hours are among some advantages of using flywheels instead of traditional sources of energy for peaking power plants.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Beacon_Power" title="Beacon Power">Beacon Power</a> opened a 5 MWh (20 MW over 15 mins) flywheel energy storage plant in <a href="/wiki/Stephentown,_New_York" title="Stephentown, New York">Stephentown, New York</a> in 2011 using 200 flywheels and a similar 20 MW system at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazle_Township,_Pennsylvania" title="Hazle Township, Pennsylvania">Hazle Township, Pennsylvania</a> in 2014.</p>
<p>A 2 MW (for 15 min) <a href="/wiki/Minto_flywheel_facility" title="Minto flywheel facility">flywheel storage facility in Minto</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario,_Canada" title="Ontario, Canada">Ontario, Canada</a> opened in 2014. The flywheel system (developed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NRStor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NRStor (page does not exist)">NRStor</a>) uses 10 spinning steel flywheels on magnetic bearings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbines">Wind turbines</span></h3>
<p>Flywheels may be used to store energy generated by wind turbines during off-peak periods or during high wind speeds.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Beacon_Power" title="Beacon Power">Beacon Power</a> began testing of their Smart Energy 25 (Gen 4) flywheel energy storage system at a wind farm in <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi,_California" title="Tehachapi, California">Tehachapi, California</a>. The system is part of a wind power/flywheel demonstration project being carried out for the California Energy Commission.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toys">Toys</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friction_motor" title="Friction motor">Friction motors</a> used to power many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toy_car" title="Toy car">toy cars</a>, trucks, trains, action toys and such, are simple flywheel motors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toggle_action_presses">Toggle action presses</span></h3>
<p>In industry, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Toggle_action_press&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Toggle action press (page does not exist)">toggle action presses</a> are still popular. The usual arrangement involves a very strong <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshaft</a> and a heavy duty connecting rod which drives the press. Large and heavy flywheels are driven by electric motors but the flywheels only turn the crankshaft when clutches are activated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_electric_batteries">Comparison to electric batteries</span></h2>
<p>Flywheels are not as adversely affected by temperature changes, can operate at a much wider temperature range, and are not subject to many of the common failures of chemical <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable batteries</a>. They are also less potentially damaging to the environment, being largely made of <a href="/wiki/Inert" title="Inert">inert</a> or benign materials. Another advantage of flywheels is that by a simple measurement of the rotation speed it is possible to know the exact amount of energy stored.</p>
<p>Unlike most batteries which only operate for a finite period (for example roughly 36 months in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_ion_polymer_batteries" title="Lithium ion polymer batteries">lithium ion polymer batteries</a>), a flywheel potentially has an indefinite working lifespan. Flywheels built as part of <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_engines" title="Steam engines">steam engines</a> have been continuously working for more than two hundred years. Working examples of ancient flywheels used mainly in milling and pottery can be found in many locations in Africa, Asia, and Europe.</p>
<p>Most modern flywheels are typically a sealed device that needs minimal maintenance throughout its service life. Magnetic bearing flywheels in a vacuum enclosure, such as the NASA model depicted above, do not need any bearing maintenance and are therefore superior to batteries both in terms of total lifetime and energy storage capacity. Flywheel systems with mechanical bearings will have a limited lifespan due to wear.</p>
<p>The arrangement of batteries can be designed to a wide variety of configurations, whereas a flywheel at a minimum must occupy a square surface area. Where space is a constraint for the application of energy storage (e.g. under trains in tunnels) the flywheel may not be a valid application.</p>
