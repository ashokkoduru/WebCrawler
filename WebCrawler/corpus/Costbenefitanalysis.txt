<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Cost–benefit analysis</b> (<b>CBA</b>), sometimes called <b>benefit–cost analysis</b> (<b>BCA</b>), is a systematic approach to estimating the strengths and weaknesses of alternatives (for example in transactions, activities, functional business requirements); it is used to determine options that provide the best approach to achieve benefits while preserving savings. The CBA is also defined as a systematic process for calculating and comparing benefits and <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">costs</a> of a decision, policy (with particular regard to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_policy" title="Government policy">government policy</a>) or (in general) project.</p>
<p>Broadly, CBA has two main purposes:</p>
<ol>
<li>To determine if an investment/decision is sound (justification/feasibility) – verifying whether its benefits outweigh the costs, and by how much;</li>
<li>To provide a basis for comparing projects – which involves comparing the total expected cost of each option against its total expected benefits.</li>
</ol>
<p>CBA is related to, but distinct from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness" title="Cost-effectiveness">cost-effectiveness</a> analysis. In CBA, benefits and costs are expressed in monetary terms, and are adjusted for the <a href="/wiki/Time_value_of_money" title="Time value of money">time value of money</a>, so that all flows of benefits and flows of project costs over time (which tend to occur at different points in time) are expressed on a common basis in terms of their <a href="/wiki/Net_present_value" title="Net present value">net present value</a>.</p>
<p>Closely related, but slightly different, formal techniques include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness" title="Cost-effectiveness">cost-effectiveness</a> analysis, <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93utility_analysis" title="Cost–utility analysis">cost–utility analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Risk%E2%80%93benefit_ratio" title="Risk–benefit ratio">risk–benefit analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_impact_analysis" title="Economic impact analysis">economic impact analysis</a>, fiscal impact analysis, and <a href="/wiki/Social_return_on_investment" title="Social return on investment">social return on investment</a> (SROI) analysis.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span></h2>
<p>Cost–benefit analysis is often used by organizations to appraise the desirability of a given policy. It is an analysis of the expected balance of benefits and costs, including an account of foregone alternatives and the <i><a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">status quo</a></i>. CBA helps predict whether the benefits of a policy outweigh its costs, and by how much relative to other alternatives, so that one can rank alternate policies in terms of the cost–benefit ratio. Generally, accurate cost–benefit analysis identifies choices that increase <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a> from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a> perspective. Assuming an accurate CBA, changing the status quo by implementing the alternative with the lowest cost–benefit ratio can improve <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">Pareto efficiency</a>. While CBA can offer a well-educated estimate of the best alternative – perfect appraisal of all present and future costs and benefits is difficult –, perfection in terms of economic efficiency and social welfare are not guaranteed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span></h2>
<p>The following is a list of steps that comprise a generic cost–benefit analysis.</p>
<ol>
<li>List alternative projects/programs.</li>
<li>List <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>.</li>
<li>Select measurement(s) and measure all cost/benefit elements.</li>
<li>Predict outcome of cost and benefits over relevant time period.</li>
<li>Convert all costs and benefits into a common currency.</li>
<li>Apply <a href="/wiki/Annual_effective_discount_rate" title="Annual effective discount rate">discount rate</a>.</li>
<li>Calculate <a href="/wiki/Net_present_value" title="Net present value">net present value</a> of project options.</li>
<li>Perform <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_analysis" title="Sensitivity analysis">sensitivity analysis</a>.</li>
<li>Adopt recommended choice.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span></h2>
<p>CBA attempts to measure the positive or negative consequences of a project, which may include:</p>
<ol>
<li>Effects on users or participants</li>
<li>Effects on non-users or non-participants</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">Externality</a> effects</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Option_value" title="Option value">Option value</a> or other social benefits.</li>
</ol>
<p>A similar breakdown is employed in environmental analysis of <a href="/wiki/Total_economic_value" title="Total economic value">total economic value</a>. Both costs and benefits can be diverse. Financial costs tend to be most thoroughly represented in cost-benefit analyses due to relatively abundant market data. The net benefits of a project may incorporate cost savings or public <a href="/wiki/Willingness_to_pay" title="Willingness to pay">willingness to pay</a> compensation (implying the public has no legal right to the benefits of the policy) or <a href="/wiki/Willingness_to_accept" title="Willingness to accept">willingness to accept</a> compensation (implying the public has a right to the benefits of the policy) for the welfare change resulting from the policy. The guiding principle of evaluating benefits is to list all (categories of) parties affected by an intervention and add the (positive or negative) value, usually monetary, that they ascribe to its effect on their welfare.</p>
<p>The actual compensation an individual would require to have their welfare unchanged by a policy is inexact at best. Surveys (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stated_preference&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stated preference (page does not exist)">stated preference</a> techniques) or market behavior (<a href="/wiki/Revealed_preference" title="Revealed preference">revealed preference</a> techniques) are often used to estimate the compensation associated with a policy; however, survey respondents often have strong incentives to misreport their true preferences and market behavior does not provide any information about important non-market welfare impacts.</p>
<p>One controversy is valuing a human life, e.g. when assessing road safety measures or life-saving medicines. However, this can sometimes be avoided by using the related technique of cost-utility analysis, in which benefits are expressed in non-monetary units such as <a href="/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year" title="Quality-adjusted life year">quality-adjusted life years</a>. For example, road safety can be measured in terms of <i>cost per life saved</i>, without formally placing a financial value on the life. However, such non-monetary metrics have limited usefulness for evaluating policies with substantially different outcomes. Additionally, many other benefits may accrue from the policy, and metrics such as 'cost per life saved' may lead to a substantially different ranking of alternatives than traditional cost–benefit analysis.</p>
<p>Another controversy is valuing the environment, which in the 21st century is typically assessed by valuing <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> to humans, such as air and <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. Monetary values may also be assigned to other intangible effects such as business reputation, market penetration, or long-term enterprise strategy alignment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_and_discounting">Time and discounting</span></h2>
<p>CBA usually tries to put all relevant costs and benefits on a common temporal footing using <a href="/wiki/Time_value_of_money" title="Time value of money">time value of money</a> calculations. This is often done by converting the future expected streams of costs and benefits into a <a href="/wiki/Present_value" title="Present value">present value</a> amount using a discount rate. Empirical studies and a technical framework suggest that in reality, people do discount the future like this.</p>
<p>The choice of discount rate is subjective. A smaller rate values future generations equally with the current generation. Larger rates (e.g. a market rate of return) reflects humans' attraction to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_inconsistency" title="Time inconsistency">time inconsistency</a>—valuing money that they receive today more than money they get in the future. The choice makes a large difference in assessing interventions with long-term effects. One issue is the <a href="/wiki/Equity_premium_puzzle" title="Equity premium puzzle">equity premium puzzle</a>, in which long-term returns on equities may be rather higher than they should be. If so then arguably market rates of return should not be used to determine a discount rate, as doing so would have the effect of undervaluing the distant future (e.g. climate change).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_and_uncertainty">Risk and uncertainty</span></h2>
<p>Risk associated with project outcomes is usually handled using probability theory. This can be factored into the discount rate (to have uncertainty increasing over time), but is usually considered separately. Particular consideration is often given to <a href="/wiki/Risk_aversion" title="Risk aversion">risk aversion</a>—the preference for avoiding loss over achieving gain. <a href="/wiki/Expected_return" title="Expected return">Expected return</a> calculations do not account for the detrimental effect of uncertainty.</p>
<p>Uncertainty in CBA parameters (as opposed to risk of project failure etc.) can be evaluated using a sensitivity analysis, which shows how results respond to parameter changes. Alternatively a more formal risk analysis can be undertaken using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_simulation" title="Monte Carlo simulation">Monte Carlo simulations</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>

<p>The concept of CBA dates back to an 1848 article by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Dupuit" title="Jules Dupuit">Jules Dupuit</a> and was formalized in subsequent works by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Marshall" title="Alfred Marshall">Alfred Marshall</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Corps of Engineers</a> initiated the use of CBA in the US, after the Federal Navigation Act of 1936 effectively required cost–benefit analysis for proposed federal waterway infrastructure. The <a href="/wiki/Flood_Control_Act_of_1939" title="Flood Control Act of 1939">Flood Control Act of 1939</a> was instrumental in establishing CBA as federal policy; it demanded that "the benefits to whomever they accrue [be] in excess of the estimated costs."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_policy">Public policy</span></h3>
<p>The application for broader public policy started from the work of <a href="/wiki/Otto_Eckstein" title="Otto Eckstein">Otto Eckstein</a>, who in 1958 laid out a welfare economics foundation for CBA and its application for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_resource" title="Water resource">water resource</a> development. Over the 1960s, CBA was applied in the US for water quality, recreation travel, and land conservation. During this period, the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Option_value" title="Option value">option value</a> was developed to represent the non-tangible value of preserving resources such as national parks.</p>
<p>CBA was later expanded to address both intangible and tangible benefits of public policies relating to mental illness, substance abuse, college education, and chemical waste policies. In the US, the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a> of 1969 first required the application of CBA for regulatory programs, and since then, other governments have enacted similar rules. Government guidebooks for the application of CBA to public policies include the Canadian guide for regulatory analysis, Australian guide for regulation and finance, US guide for health care programs, and US guide for emergency management programs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_investment">Transportation investment</span></h3>
<p>CBA application for transport investment started in the UK, with the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> project in 1960. It was later applied on many projects including <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Victoria_line" title="Victoria line">Victoria line</a>. Later, the <a href="/wiki/New_Approach_to_Appraisal" title="New Approach to Appraisal">New Approach to Appraisal</a> (NATA) was introduced by the then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport,_Environment_and_the_Regions" title="Department for Transport, Environment and the Regions">Department for Transport, Environment and the Regions</a>. This presented cost–benefit results and detailed <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">environmental impact assessments</a> in a balanced way. NATA was first applied to national road schemes in the 1998 Roads Review but subsequently rolled out to all transport modes. As of 2011 it was a cornerstone of transport appraisal in the UK and is maintained and developed by the Department for Transport.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a>'s 'Developing Harmonised European Approaches for Transport Costing and Project Assessment' (HEATCO) project, part of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixth_Framework_Programme" title="Sixth Framework Programme">Sixth Framework Programme</a>, reviewed transport appraisal guidance across EU member states and found that significant differences exist between countries. HEATCO's aim was to develop guidelines to harmonise transport appraisal practice across the EU.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transport_Canada" title="Transport Canada">Transport Canada</a> promoted the use of CBA for major transport investments with the 1994 issuance of its Guidebook.</p>
<p>In the US, both federal and state transport departments commonly apply CBA, using a variety of available software tools including HERS, BCA.Net, StatBenCost, Cal-BC, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TREDIS" title="TREDIS">TREDIS</a>. Guides are available from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">Federal Highway Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Department_of_Transportation" title="Minnesota Department of Transportation">Minnesota Department of Transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Transportation" title="California Department of Transportation">California Department of Transportation</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltrans" title="Caltrans">Caltrans</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Research_Board" title="Transportation Research Board">Transportation Research Board</a> Transportation Economics Committee.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CBA_and_Regulation_under_various_US_Administrations">CBA and Regulation under various US Administrations</span></h3>
<p>The increased usage of CBA in the US regulatory process is often associated with President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>'s administration. Though the use of CBA in US policy making dating back many decades, Reagan's Executive Order 12291 mandated the use of CBA in the regulatory process. Reagan campaigned on a deregulation platform, and once he took office in 1981 quickly issued this EO, which vested the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Information_and_Regulatory_Affairs" title="Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs">Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs</a> (OIRA) with the authority to review agency regulations and required federal agencies to produce regulatory impact analyses when the annual impact could be estimated over $100M. Shortly thereafter, in the 1980s, academic and institutional critiques of CBA started to emerge. The three main criticisms were:</p>
<ol>
<li>That CBA could be used for political goals. Debates on the merits of cost and benefit comparisons can be used to sidestep political or philosophical goals, rules and regulations.</li>
<li>That CBA is inherently anti-regulatory, therefore not a neutral analysis tool. This is an ethical argument: that the monetization of policy impacts is an inappropriate tool for assessing things such as mortality risks and distributional impacts.</li>
<li>That the length of time necessary to complete CBA can create significant delays, which can impede policy regulations.</li>
</ol>
<p>These criticisms continued through the 1990s under the Clinton administration, who furthered the anti-regulatory environment through his Executive Order 12866. EO 12866 changed some of Reagan's language, requiring benefits to justify, rather than exceed costs, and added "reduction of discrimination or bias" as one of the benefits to be analyzed. Criticisms of aspects of CBA, including uncertainly valuations, discounting future values, and the calculation of risk, were used to argue that CBA should play no part in the regulatory process. The use of CBA in the regulatory process continues today under the Obama administration, though the debate over its practical and objective value continues. Some analysts oppose the use of CBA in policy making, while those in favor of its use favor improvements to the analysis and calculations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accuracy">Accuracy</span></h2>
<p>The value of a cost–benefit analysis depends on the accuracy of the individual cost and benefit estimates. Comparative studies indicate that such estimates are often flawed, preventing improvements in <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">Pareto</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaldor-Hicks_efficiency" title="Kaldor-Hicks efficiency">Kaldor-Hicks efficiency</a>. Causes of these inaccuracies include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Overreliance on data from past projects (often differing markedly in function or size and the skill levels of the team members)</li>
<li>Use of subjective impressions in assessment</li>
<li>Inappropriate use of heuristics to derive money cost of the intangible elements</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">Confirmation bias</a> among project supporters (looking for reasons to proceed).</li>
</ul>
<p>Interest groups may attempt to include or exclude significant costs from an analysis to influence the outcome.</p>
<p>In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Pinto" title="Ford Pinto">Ford Pinto</a> (where, because of design flaws, the Pinto was liable to burst into flames in a rear-impact collision), the company's decision was not to issue a recall. Ford's cost–benefit analysis had estimated that based on the number of cars in use and the probable accident rate, deaths due to the design flaw would cost it about $49.5 million to settle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrongful_death" title="Wrongful death">wrongful death</a> lawsuits versus recall costs of $137.5 million. Ford overlooked (or considered insignificant) the costs of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_PR" title="Black PR">negative publicity</a> that would result, which forced a recall <i>and</i> damaged sales.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Health_economics" title="Health economics">health economics</a>, some analysts think cost–benefit analysis can be an inadequate measure because willingness-to-pay methods of determining the value of human life can be influenced by income level. They support use of variants such as <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93utility_analysis" title="Cost–utility analysis">cost–utility analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year" title="Quality-adjusted life year">quality-adjusted life year</a> to analyze the effects of health policies.</p>
<p>For some environmental effects cost-benefit analysis can be substituted with <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">cost-effectiveness analysis</a>. This is especially true when there is only one type of physical outcome that is sought, such as the reduction of energy use by increasing energy efficiency. Using cost-effectiveness analysis is less laborious and time-consuming as it does not involve the monetization of outcomes, which can be difficult in some cases.</p>
<p>In environmental and occupational health regulation, it has been argued that if modern cost–benefit analyses had been applied prospectively to decisions such as whether to mandate the removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaded_gasoline" title="Leaded gasoline">lead</a> from gasoline, block the construction of two proposed dams just above and below the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>, and regulate workers' exposure to <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>, these measures would not have been implemented even though they are considered to be highly successful in retrospect. The <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> has been cited in retrospective studies as a case where benefits exceeded costs, but the knowledge of the benefits (attributable largely to the benefits of reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_emissions" title="Particulate emissions">particulate pollution</a>) was not available until many years later.</p>
