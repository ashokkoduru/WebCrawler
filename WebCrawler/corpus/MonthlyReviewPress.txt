<p>The <i><b>Monthly Review</b></i>, established in 1949, is an independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a> <a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">magazine</a> published monthly in New York City. As of 2015 the publication remains the longest continuously published socialist magazine in the United States. The journal has an <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> of 0.460.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span></h3>
<p>Following the failure of the independent <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1948" title="United States presidential election, 1948">1948 Presidential campaign</a> of <a href="/wiki/Henry_A._Wallace" title="Henry A. Wallace">Henry A. Wallace</a>, two former supporters of the Wallace effort met at the farm in <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> where one of them was living. The two men were literary scholar and <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">Christian socialist</a> <a href="/wiki/F._O._Matthiessen" title="F. O. Matthiessen">F.O. "Matty" Matthiessen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economist</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sweezy" title="Paul Sweezy">Paul Sweezy</a>, who were former colleagues at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. Matthiessen came into an inheritance after his father died in an automobile accident in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and had no pressing need for the money. Matthiessen made the offer to Sweezy to underwrite such a magazine to the sum of $5,000 per year for three years. Matthiessen funds made the launch of <i>Monthly Review</i> possible, although the amount of the seed money was reduced to $4,000 per year in the second and third years by the executors of Matthiessen's estate following his suicide in 1950.</p>
<p>Although Matthiessen was the financial angel of the new publication, from the outset the editorial task was handled by Sweezy and his co-thinker, the left wing popular writer <a href="/wiki/Leo_Huberman" title="Leo Huberman">Leo Huberman</a>. The author of an array of books and pamphlets during the 1930s and early 1940s, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>-educated Huberman worked full-time on <i>Monthly Review</i> from its establishment until his death of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> in 1968.</p>
<p>Sweezy and Huberman were complementary figures guiding the publication, with Sweezy's theoretical bent and writing ability put to use for a majority of the editorial content, while Huberman took charge of the business and administrative aspects of the enterprise. Sweezy remained at home in New Hampshire, traveling down to the New York City once a month to read <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a>, where Huberman conducted the day-to-day operations of the magazine along with his wife, Gerty Huberman, and family friend Sybil Huntington May.</p>
<p>Briefly joining Sweezy and Huberman as a third founding editor of <i>Monthly Review</i> — although not listed as such on the publication's <a href="/wiki/Masthead_(publishing)" title="Masthead (publishing)">masthead</a> — was German émigré <a href="/wiki/Otto_Nathan" title="Otto Nathan">Otto Nathan</a> (1893-1987). Although his time of editorial association with <i>MR</i> was short, Nathan was instrumental in obtaining what would become a seminal essay for the magazine, a lead piece for the debut May 1949 issue by <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> entitled "<a href="/wiki/Why_Socialism%3F" title="Why Socialism?">Why Socialism?</a>"</p>
<p>Another key contributor during the first 15 years of <i>MR</i> was economist <a href="/wiki/Paul_A._Baran" title="Paul A. Baran">Paul Baran</a>, frequently considered as the third member of an editorial troika including Sweezy and Huberman. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenure" title="Tenure">tenured</a> professor at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, Baran was one of a very few self-identified Marxists to teach economics at American universities during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period. Baran worked closely with Sweezy on a book regarded as a landmark in Marxist theory entitled <i>Monopoly Capital,</i> although he died of a heart attack prior to the work's first publication in 1966.</p>
<p><i>Monthly Review</i> launched in 1949 with a circulation of just 450 copies, most of whom were personal acquaintances of either Huberman or Sweezy. The magazine's ideology and readership closely paralleled that of the independent Marxist weekly newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/National_Guardian" title="National Guardian">The National Guardian</a></i>, established in 1948. Despite a conservative political climate in the United States, the magazine quickly reached a critical mass of subscribers, with its paid circulation rising to 2,500 in 1950 and to 6,000 in 1954.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="McCarthy_period">McCarthy period</span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/McCarthyism" title="McCarthyism">McCarthyism</a> of the early 1950s, editors Paul Sweezy and Leo Huberman were targeted for "subversive activities." Sweezy's case, tried by <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Attorney_General" title="New Hampshire Attorney General">New Hampshire Attorney General</a>, went all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> and became a seminal case on <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a> when the Court ruled in his favor.</p>
<p>In 1953 the magazine added veteran radical <a href="/wiki/Scott_Nearing" title="Scott Nearing">Scott Nearing</a> to the magazine's ranks. From that date and for nearly 20 years Nearing authored a monthly column descriptively entitled "World Events". During the Truman and Eisenhower years, a number of left wing intellectuals found a space for their work in <i>MR,</i> including a number that would gain in stature in the ensuing liberalized decade, such as pacifist activist <a href="/wiki/Staughton_Lynd" title="Staughton Lynd">Staughton Lynd</a> (1952), historian <a href="/wiki/William_Appleman_Williams" title="William Appleman Williams">William Appleman Williams</a> (1952), and sociologist <a href="/wiki/C._Wright_Mills" title="C. Wright Mills">C. Wright Mills</a> (1958).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Left_era_and_after">New Left era and after</span></h3>
<p>From the middle years of the 1960s, radical political theory saw a resurgence in association with the emergence of a <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a> in Europe and North America. <i>Monthly Review</i> grew in stature in tandem with this resurgence. While remaining an intellectual journal not oriented towards acquiring a mass readership, circulation of the publication nonetheless grew throughout this era, approaching 9,100 in 1970 before peaking at 11,500 in 1977.</p>
<p>While <i>MR</i> remained essentially a publication with roots in the so-called "Old Left", it was not unfriendly to the young radical movement which grew in conjunction with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_movement" title="Civil Rights movement">Civil Rights movement</a> and the opposition to <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. Among those associated with the 1960s New Left published by the <i>Monthly Review</i> were <a href="/wiki/C._Wright_Mills" title="C. Wright Mills">C. Wright Mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Marcuse" title="Herbert Marcuse">Herbert Marcuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todd_Gitlin" title="Todd Gitlin">Todd Gitlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Oglesby" title="Carl Oglesby">Carl Oglesby</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Horowitz" title="David Horowitz">David Horowitz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>Monthly Review</i> editorial staff was joined in May 1969 by radical economist <a href="/wiki/Harry_Magdoff" title="Harry Magdoff">Harry Magdoff</a>, replacing Leo Huberman, who had died in 1968. Magdoff, a reader of the publication from its first issue in 1949, bolstered the already well-developed "<a href="/wiki/Third-Worldism" title="Third-Worldism">Third-Worldist</a>" orientation of the publication, based upon revolutionary events in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, China, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. Certain <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoist</a> influence made itself felt in the content of the publication in this period.</p>
<p><i>Monthly Review</i> became steadily more critical of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the 1960s and 1970s, with editor Paul Sweezy objecting to the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia</a> in 1968 and the suppression of the <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Polish trade union "Solidarity"</a> through <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_in_Poland" title="Martial law in Poland">martial law</a> in 1981. In the latter case, Sweezy declared the incident had proved beyond doubt that</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...the Communist regimes of the Soviet bloc have become the expression and the guardians of a new rigidified hierarchical structure which has nothing in common with the kind of socialist society Marxists have always regarded as the goal of modern working class movements."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite an apparent decline of the American Left in the 1980s, <i>MR'</i>s circulation hovered in the 8,000 range throughout the decade.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_today">Publication today</span></h3>
<p>Since 2006 <a href="/wiki/John_Bellamy_Foster" title="John Bellamy Foster">John Bellamy Foster</a> has been the sole editor, together with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Yates_(economist)" title="Michael Yates (economist)">Michael D. Yates</a>, as associate editor, assistant editors, and an editorial committee.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_orientation">Political orientation</span></h2>

<p>From its first issue, <i>Monthly Review</i> attacked the premise that <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> was capable of infinite growth through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keynesianism" title="Keynesianism">Keynesian</a> <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomic</a> fine-tuning. Instead, the magazine's editors and leading writers have remained true to the traditional Marxist perspective that capitalist economies contain internal contradictions which will ultimately lead to their collapse and reconstitution on a new socialist basis. Topics of editorial concern have included <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, unequal distribution of incomes and wealth, <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> in relations between economically developed and less developed nations, and inefficiencies in production and distribution seen as endemic to the capitalist system.</p>
<p>Although not averse to discussion of esoteric matters of socialist theory, <i>MR</i> was generally characterized by an aversion to doctrinaire citations of Marxist canon in favor of the analysis of real-world economic and historical trends. Readability was emphasized and the use of academic jargon discouraged.</p>
<p>Editors Huberman and Sweezy argued as early as 1952 that massive and expanding military spending was an integral part of the process of capitalist stabilization, driving corporate profits, bolstering levels of employment, and absorbing surplus production. The illusion of an external military threat was required to sustain this system of priorities in government spending, they argued; consequently, effort was made by the editors to challenge the dominant Cold War paradigm of "Democracy versus Communism" in the material published in the magazine.</p>
<p>In its editorial line <i>Monthly Review</i> offered critical support of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> during its early years although over time the magazine became increasingly critical of Soviet dedication to <a href="/wiki/Socialism_in_One_Country" title="Socialism in One Country">Socialism in One Country</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peaceful_coexistence" title="Peaceful coexistence">peaceful coexistence</a>, seeing that country as playing a more or less conservative role in a world marked by national revolutionary movements. After the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split" title="Sino-Soviet split">Sino-Soviet split</a> of the 1960s, Sweezy and Huberman soon came to see the People's Republic of China as the actual center of the world revolutionary movement.</p>
<p><i>Monthly Review</i> remained true to an independent orientation throughout its history and never aligned with any specific <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutionary</a> movement or political organization. Many of its articles have been written by academics, journalists, and freelance public intellectuals, including <a href="/wiki/Tariq_Ali" title="Tariq Ali">Tariq Ali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Allende" title="Isabel Allende">Isabel Allende</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samir_Amin" title="Samir Amin">Samir Amin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julian_Bond" title="Julian Bond">Julian Bond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Buck" title="Marilyn Buck">Marilyn Buck</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._D._H._Cole" title="G. D. H. Cole">G. D. H. Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernardine_Dohrn" title="Bernardine Dohrn">Bernardine Dohrn</a>, <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">W. E. B. Du Bois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Ehrenreich" title="Barbara Ehrenreich">Barbara Ehrenreich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andre_Gunder_Frank" title="Andre Gunder Frank">Andre Gunder Frank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Galeano" title="Eduardo Galeano">Eduardo Galeano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Hansberry" title="Lorraine Hansberry">Lorraine Hansberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Herman" title="Edward S. Herman">Edward Herman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">Eric Hobsbawm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Klare" title="Michael Klare">Michael Klare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saul_Landau" title="Saul Landau">Saul Landau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Parenti" title="Michael Parenti">Michael Parenti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._McChesney" title="Robert W. McChesney">Robert W. McChesney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Miliband" title="Ralph Miliband">Ralph Miliband</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marge_Piercy" title="Marge Piercy">Marge Piercy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frances_Fox_Piven" title="Frances Fox Piven">Frances Fox Piven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Rich" title="Adrienne Rich">Adrienne Rich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Singer_(journalist)" title="Daniel Singer (journalist)">Daniel Singer</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._P._Thompson" title="E. P. Thompson">E. P. Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Wallerstein" title="Immanuel Wallerstein">Immanuel Wallerstein</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Williams" title="Raymond Williams">Raymond Williams</a>.</p>
<p>In 2004, <i>Monthly Review</i> editor <a href="/wiki/John_Bellamy_Foster" title="John Bellamy Foster">John Bellamy Foster</a> told the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The <i>Monthly Review</i>... was and is Marxist, but did not hew to the party line or get into sectarian struggles."</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-English_editions">Non-English editions</span></h2>
<p>In addition to the U.S.-based magazine, there are seven sister editions of <i>Monthly Review</i>. They are published in Greece; Turkey; Spain; South Korea; as well as separate English, Hindi, and Bengali editions in India.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monthly_Review_Press">Monthly Review Press</span></h2>
<p>Monthly Review Press, an allied endeavor, was launched in 1951 in response to the inability of the maverick left-wing journalist <a href="/wiki/I._F._Stone" title="I. F. Stone">I. F. Stone</a> to otherwise find a publisher for his book <i>The Hidden History of the Korean War</i>. Stone's work, which argued that the still ongoing <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> was not a case of simple Communist military aggression but was rather the product of political isolation, South Korean military buildup, and border provocations, became the first title offered by the MR Press in 1952.</p>
<p>Titles published by the press in its formative years include: <i>The Empire of Oil</i> by <a href="/wiki/Harvey_O%27Connor" title="Harvey O'Connor">Harvey O'Connor</a> (1955), <i>The Political Economy of Growth</i> by Paul Baran (1957), <i>The United States, Cuba, and Castro</i> by historian <a href="/wiki/William_Appleman_Williams" title="William Appleman Williams">William Appleman Williams</a> (1963), <i>Fanshen</i> by <a href="/wiki/William_H._Hinton" title="William H. Hinton">William Hinton</a> (1966), <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_Capital" title="Monopoly Capital">Monopoly Capital</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Paul_A._Baran" title="Paul A. Baran">Paul A. Baran</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_M._Sweezy" title="Paul M. Sweezy">Paul M. Sweezy</a> (1966), the English translation of <i><a href="/wiki/Open_Veins_of_Latin_America" title="Open Veins of Latin America">Open Veins of Latin America</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Galeano" title="Eduardo Galeano">Eduardo Galeano</a> (1973), and <i>Unequal Development</i> (1976) and <i>Eurocentrism</i> (1989) by <a href="/wiki/Samir_Amin" title="Samir Amin">Samir Amin</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harry_Braverman" title="Harry Braverman">Harry Braverman</a> (author of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_and_Monopoly_Capital" title="Labor and Monopoly Capital">Labor and Monopoly Capital</a></i>) became director of Monthly Review Press in 1967. The present director of the Press is <a href="/wiki/Michael_Yates_(economist)" title="Michael Yates (economist)">Michael D. Yates</a> (author of <i>Naming the System</i>).</p>
<p>In later years Monthly Review Press has published such titles as: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marx%27s_Ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marx's Ecology (page does not exist)">Marx's Ecology</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Bellamy_Foster" title="John Bellamy Foster">John Bellamy Foster</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Discourse_on_Colonialism" title="Discourse on Colonialism">Discourse on Colonialism</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Aim%C3%A9_C%C3%A9saire" title="Aimé Césaire">Aimé Césaire</a>; <i>The Great Financial Crisis</i> by Fred Magdoff and <a href="/wiki/John_Bellamy_Foster" title="John Bellamy Foster">John Bellamy Foster</a>; and <i>Biology under the Influence</i> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lewontin" title="Richard Lewontin">Richard Lewontin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Levins" title="Richard Levins">Richard Levins</a>. MR Press is also the U.S. publisher of <i><a href="/wiki/Socialist_Register" title="Socialist Register">The Socialist Register</a></i>, <i>a long-running annual series of topical essays written by radical academics and activists.</i></p>

<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">Journal Citation Reports</a></i>, the journal has a 2014 <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> of 0.460, ranking it 107th out of 161 journals in the category "Political Science".</p>
