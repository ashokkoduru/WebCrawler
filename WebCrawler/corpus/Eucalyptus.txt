<p><i><b>Eucalyptus</b></i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> L'Heritier 1789 is a diverse <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> and shrubs (including a distinct group with a multiple-stem <a href="/wiki/Mallee_(habit)" title="Mallee (habit)">mallee</a> growth habit) in the <a href="/wiki/Myrtus" title="Myrtus">myrtle</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a>. Members of the genus dominate the tree flora of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and include the tallest known flowering plant on Earth. There are more than 700 species of eucalyptus and most are native to Australia; a very small number are found in adjacent areas of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. One species, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_deglupta" title="Eucalyptus deglupta">Eucalyptus deglupta</a>,</i> ranges as far north as the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. Of the 15 species found outside Australia, just nine are exclusively non-Australian. Species of eucalyptus are cultivated widely in the tropical and temperate world, including the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. However, the range over which many eucalypts can be planted in the temperate zone is constrained by their limited cold tolerance.</p>
<p><i>Eucalyptus</i> is one of three similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a> that are commonly referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Eucalypt" title="Eucalypt">eucalypts</a>", the others being <i><a href="/wiki/Corymbia" title="Corymbia">Corymbia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Angophora" title="Angophora">Angophora</a></i>. Many species, though by no means all, are known as <b>gum trees</b> because they exude copious <a href="/wiki/Kino_(gum)" title="Kino (gum)">kino</a> from any break in the <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_haemastoma" title="Eucalyptus haemastoma">scribbly gum</a>). The generic name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> words ευ (<i>eu</i>) "well" and καλύπτω (<i>kalýpto</i>) "to cover", referring to the <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(botany)" title="Operculum (botany)">operculum</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">calyx</a> that initially conceals the <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a>.</p>
<p>Some eucalyptus species have attracted attention from horticulturists, <a href="/wiki/Development_economics" title="Development economics">global development</a> researchers, and environmentalists because of desirable traits such as being fast-growing sources of wood, producing oil that can be used for cleaning and as a natural <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticide</a>, or an ability to be used to drain <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and thereby reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>. <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_oil" title="Eucalyptus oil">Eucalyptus oil</a> finds many uses like in aromatherapy, as a cure for joint pains. Eucalyptus trees show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allelopathic" title="Allelopathic">allelopathic</a> effects; they release compounds which inhibit other plant species from growing nearby. Outside their natural ranges, eucalypts are both lauded for their beneficial economic impact on poor populations and criticised for being "water-guzzling" <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">aliens</a>, leading to controversy over their total impact.</p>
<p>On warm days, eucalyptus forests are sometimes shrouded in a <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>-like mist of vaporised <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> (<a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoids</a>); the Australian <a href="/wiki/Blue_Mountains_(New_South_Wales)" title="Blue Mountains (New South Wales)">Blue Mountains</a> take their name from the haze.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>





<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_habit">Size and habit</span></h3>
<p>A mature eucalyptus may take the form of a low shrub or a very large tree. The <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> can be divided into three main <a href="/wiki/Habit_(biology)" title="Habit (biology)">habits</a> and four size categories.</p>
<p>As a generalisation "<a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> trees" are single-stemmed and have a <a href="/wiki/Crown_(botany)" title="Crown (botany)">crown</a> forming a minor proportion of the whole tree height. "<a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">Woodland</a> trees" are single-stemmed, although they may branch at a short distance above ground level.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Mallee_(habit)" title="Mallee (habit)">Mallees</a>" are multistemmed from ground level, usually less than 10 m (33 ft) in height, often with the crown predominantly at the ends of the branchlets and individual plants may combine to form either an open or closed formation. Many mallee trees may be so low-growing as to be considered a <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a>.</p>
<p>Two other tree forms are notable in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> and described using the native names "mallet" and "marlock". The "<a href="/wiki/Mallet_(habit)" title="Mallet (habit)">mallet</a>" is a small to medium-sized tree that does not produce <a href="/wiki/Lignotuber" title="Lignotuber">lignotubers</a> and has a relatively long trunk, a steeply branching habit and often a conspicuously dense <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terminal_crown&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terminal crown (page does not exist)">terminal crown</a>. This is the normal habit of mature healthy specimens of <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_occidentalis" title="Eucalyptus occidentalis">Eucalyptus occidentalis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_astringens" title="Eucalyptus astringens">E. astringens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_spathulata" title="Eucalyptus spathulata">E. spathulata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_gardneri" title="Eucalyptus gardneri">E. gardneri</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_dielsii" title="Eucalyptus dielsii">E. dielsii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_forrestiana" title="Eucalyptus forrestiana">E. forrestiana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_salubris" title="Eucalyptus salubris">E. salubris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_clivicola" title="Eucalyptus clivicola">E. clivicola</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_ornata" title="Eucalyptus ornata">E. ornata</a></i>. The smooth <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> of mallets often has a satiny sheen and may be white, cream, grey, green, or copper.</p>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Marlock" title="Marlock">marlock</a> has been variously used; in <i>Forest Trees of Australia</i>, it is defined as a small tree without <a href="/wiki/Lignotuber" title="Lignotuber">lignotubers</a>, but with a shorter, lower-branching trunk than a mallet. They usually grow in more or less pure stands. Clearly recognisable examples are stands of <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_platypus" title="Eucalyptus platypus">E. platypus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_vesiculosa" title="Eucalyptus vesiculosa">E. vesiculosa</a></i>, and the unrelated <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_stoatei" title="Eucalyptus stoatei">E. stoatei</a></i>.</p>
<p>The term "morrell" is somewhat obscure in origin and appears to apply to trees of the western Australian wheatbelt and <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">goldfields</a> which have a long, straight trunk, completely rough-barked. It is now used mainly for <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_longicornis" title="Eucalyptus longicornis">E. longicornis</a></i> (red morrell) and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_melanoxylon" title="Eucalyptus melanoxylon">E. melanoxylon</a></i> (black morrell).</p>
<p>Tree sizes follow the convention of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Small: to 10 m (33 ft) in height</li>
<li>Medium-sized: 10–30 m (33–98 ft)</li>
<li>Tall: 30–60 m (98–197 ft)</li>
<li>Very tall: over 60 m (200 ft)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaves">Leaves</span></h3>
<p>Nearly all eucalyptus are <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a>, but some tropical species lose their leaves at the end of the dry season. As in other members of the <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">myrtle</a> family, eucalyptus leaves are covered with oil glands. The copious oils produced are an important feature of the genus. Although mature eucalyptus trees may be towering and fully leafed, their shade is characteristically patchy because the leaves usually hang downwards.</p>


<p>The leaves on a mature eucalyptus plant are commonly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanceolate" title="Lanceolate">lanceolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Characteristics_of_the_petiole" title="Leaf">petiolate</a>, apparently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternate_leaf" title="Alternate leaf">alternate</a> and waxy or glossy green. In contrast, the leaves of seedlings are often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternate_leaf" title="Alternate leaf">opposite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Characteristics_of_the_petiole" title="Leaf">sessile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Surface_of_the_leaf" title="Leaf">glaucous</a>, but many exceptions to this pattern exist. Many species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_melanophloia" title="Eucalyptus melanophloia">E. melanophloia</a></i> and <i>E. setosa</i> retain the juvenile leaf form even when the plant is reproductively mature. Some species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_macrocarpa" title="Eucalyptus macrocarpa">E. macrocarpa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_rhodantha" title="Eucalyptus rhodantha">E. rhodantha</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus._crucis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eucalyptus. crucis (page does not exist)">E. crucis</a></i>, are sought-after ornamentals due to this lifelong juvenile leaf form. A few species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_petraea" title="Eucalyptus petraea">E. petraea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_dundasii" title="Eucalyptus dundasii">E. dundasii</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=E._lansdowneana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="E. lansdowneana (page does not exist)">E. lansdowneana</a></i>, have shiny green leaves throughout their life cycle. <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_caesia" title="Eucalyptus caesia">E. caesia</a></i> exhibits the opposite pattern of leaf development to most eucalyptus, with shiny green leaves in the seedling stage and dull, glaucous leaves in mature crowns. The contrast between juvenile and adult leaf phases is valuable in field identification.</p>
<p>Four leaf phases are recognised in the development of a eucalyptus plant: the ‘seedling’, ‘juvenile’, ‘intermediate’, and ‘adult’ phases. However, no definite transitional point occurs between the phases. The intermediate phase, when the largest leaves are often formed, links the juvenile and adult phases.</p>
<p>In all except a few species, the leaves form in pairs on opposite sides of a square stem, consecutive pairs being at right angles to each other (decussate). In some narrow-leaved species, for example <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_oleosa" title="Eucalyptus oleosa">E. oleosa</a></i>, the seedling leaves after the second leaf pair are often clustered in a detectable <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">spiral</a> arrangement about a five-sided stem. After the spiral phase, which may last from several to many nodes, the arrangement reverts to decussate by the absorption of some of the leaf-bearing faces of the stem. In those species with opposite adult foliage the leaf pairs, which have been formed opposite at the stem apex, become separated at their bases by unequal elongation of the stem to produce the apparently alternate adult leaves.</p>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flowers">Flowers</span></h3>
<p>The most readily recognisable characteristics of eucalyptus species are the distinctive flowers and fruit (capsules or "gumnuts"). Flowers have numerous fluffy <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> which may be white, cream, yellow, pink, or red; in bud, the stamens are enclosed in a cap known as an <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(botany)" title="Operculum (botany)">operculum</a> which is composed of the fused sepals or petals, or both. Thus, flowers have no petals, but instead decorate themselves with the many showy stamens. As the stamens expand, the operculum is forced off, splitting away from the cup-like base of the flower; this is one of the features that unites the genus. The name <i>Eucalyptus</i>, from the Greek words <i>eu-</i>, well, and <i>kaluptos</i>, cover, meaning "well-covered", describes the operculum. The woody fruits or capsules are roughly cone-shaped and have valves at the end which open to release the seeds, which are waxy, rod-shaped, about 1 mm in length, and yellow-brown in colour. Most species do not flower until adult foliage starts to appear; <i>E. cinerea</i> and <i>E. perriniana</i> are notable exceptions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bark">Bark</span></h3>



<p>The appearance of eucalyptus bark varies with the age of the plant, the manner of bark shed, the length of the bark fibres, the degree of furrowing, the thickness, the hardness, and the colour. All mature eucalypts put on an annual layer of bark, which contributes to the increasing diameter of the stems. In some species, the outermost layer dies and is annually deciduous, either in long strips (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_sheathiana" title="Eucalyptus sheathiana">E. sheathiana</a></i>) or in variably sized flakes (<i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_diversicolor" title="Eucalyptus diversicolor">E. diversicolor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cosmophylla" title="Eucalyptus cosmophylla">E. cosmophylla</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cladocalyx" title="Eucalyptus cladocalyx">E. cladocalyx</a></i>). These are the gums or smooth-barked species. The gum bark may be dull, shiny, or satiny (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_ornata" title="Eucalyptus ornata">E. ornata</a></i>) or matte (<i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cosmophylla" title="Eucalyptus cosmophylla">E. cosmophylla</a></i>). In many species, the dead bark is retained. Its outermost layer gradually fragments with weathering and sheds without altering the essentially rough-barked nature of the trunks or stems — for example <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_marginata" title="Eucalyptus marginata">E. marginata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_jacksonii" title="Eucalyptus jacksonii">E. jacksonii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_obliqua" title="Eucalyptus obliqua">E. obliqua</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_porosa" title="Eucalyptus porosa">E. porosa</a></i>.</p>
<p>Many species are ‘half-barks’ or ‘blackbutts’ in which the dead bark is retained in the lower half of the trunks or stems — for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_brachycalyx" title="Eucalyptus brachycalyx">E. brachycalyx</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_ochrophloia" title="Eucalyptus ochrophloia">E. ochrophloia</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_occidentalis" title="Eucalyptus occidentalis">E. occidentalis</a></i> — or only in a thick, black accumulation at the base, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_clelandii" title="Eucalyptus clelandii">E. clelandii</a></i>. In some species in this category, for example <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_youngiana" title="Eucalyptus youngiana">E. youngiana</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_viminalis" title="Eucalyptus viminalis">E. viminalis</a></i>, the rough basal bark is very ribbony at the top, where it gives way to the smooth upper stems. The smooth upper bark of the half-barks and that of the completely smooth-barked trees and mallees can produce remarkable colour and interest, for example <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_deglupta" title="Eucalyptus deglupta">E. deglupta</a></i>.</p>
<p>Different commonly recognised types of bark include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stringybark" title="Stringybark">Stringybark</a> — consists of long fibres and can be pulled off in long pieces. It is usually thick with a spongy texture.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ironbark" title="Ironbark">Ironbark</a> — is hard, rough, and deeply furrowed. It is impregnated with dried <a href="/wiki/Kino_(gum)" title="Kino (gum)">kino</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sap_(plant)" title="Sap (plant)">sap</a> exuded by the tree) which gives a dark red or even black colour.</li>
<li>Tessellated — bark is broken up into many distinct flakes. They are <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">corkish</a> and can flake off.</li>
<li>Box — has short fibres. Some also show tessellation.</li>
<li>Ribbon — has the bark coming off in long, thin pieces, but is still loosely attached in some places. They can be long ribbons, firmer strips, or twisted curls.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span></h2>
<p>The oldest definitive <i>Eucalyptus</i> fossils are surprisingly from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, where eucalypts are no longer endemic, though have been introduced from Australia. The fossils are from the early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> (51.9 Mya), and were found in the Laguna del Hunco deposit in Chubut province in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. This shows that the genus had a <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwanan</a> distribution. Fossil leaves also occur in the Miocene of New Zealand, where the genus is not native today, but again have been introduced from Australia.</p>
<p>Despite the prominence of <i>Eucalyptus</i> in modern Australia, estimated to contribute some 75% of the modern vegetation, the fossil record is very scarce throughout much of the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>, and suggests that this rise to dominance is a geologically more recent phenomenon. The oldest reliably dated macrofossil of <i>Eucalyptus</i> is a 21-million-year-old tree-stump encased in basalt in the upper Lachlan Valley in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>. Other fossils have been found, but many are either unreliably dated or else unreliably identified.</p>
<p>It is useful to consider where <i>Eucalyptus</i> fossils have not been found. Extensive research has gone into the fossil floras of the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a> of South-Eastern Australia, and has failed to uncover a single <i>Eucalyptus</i> specimen. Although the evidence is sparse, the best hypothesis is that in the mid-Tertiary, the contintental margins of Australia only supported more mesic noneucalypt vegetation, and that eucalypts probably contributed to the drier vegetation of the arid continental interior. With the progressive drying out of the continent since the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>, eucalypts were displaced to the continental margins, and much of the mesic and rainforest vegetation that was once there was eliminated entirely.</p>
<p>The current superdominance of <i>Eucalyptus</i> in Australia may be an artefact of human influence on its ecology. In more recent sediments, numerous findings of a dramatic increase in the abundance of <i>Eucalyptus</i> pollen are associated with increased charcoal levels. Though this occurs at different rates throughout Australia, it is compelling evidence for a relationship between the artificial increase of fire frequency with the arrival of Aboriginals and increased prevalence of this exceptionally fire-tolerant genus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species_and_hybridism">Species and hybridism</span></h2>
<p>Over 700 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <i>Eucalyptus</i> are known; refer to the <b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Eucalyptus_species" title="List of Eucalyptus species">List of <i>Eucalyptus</i> species</a></b> for a comprehensive list of species. Some have <a href="/wiki/Divergent_evolution" title="Divergent evolution">diverged</a> from the mainstream of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> to the extent that they are quite isolated <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetically</a> and are able to be recognised by only a few relatively invariant characteristics. Most, however, may be regarded as belonging to large or small groups of related species, which are often in geographical contact with each other and between which <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> exchange still occurs. In these situations, many species appear to grade into one another, and intermediate forms are common. In other words, some species are relatively fixed genetically, as expressed in their <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, while others have not diverged completely from their nearest relatives.</p>
<p>Hybrid individuals have not always been recognised as such on first collection and some have been named as new species, such as <i>E. chrysantha</i> (<i>E. preissiana</i> × <i>E. sepulcralis</i>) and <i>E.</i> "rivalis" (<i>E. marginata</i> × <i>E. megacarpa</i>). Hybrid combinations are not particularly common in the field, but some other published species frequently seen in Australia have been suggested to be hybrid combinations. For example, <i>E. erythrandra</i> is believed to be <i>E. angulosa</i> × <i>E. teraptera</i> and due to its wide distribution is often referred to in texts.</p>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Renantherin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Renantherin (page does not exist)">Renantherin</a>, a phenolic compound present in the leaves of some eucalyptus species, allows <a href="/wiki/Chemotaxonomy" title="Chemotaxonomy">chemotaxonomic</a> discrimanation in the sections <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Renantheroideae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Renantheroideae (page does not exist)">renantheroideae</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Renantherae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Renantherae (page does not exist)">renantherae</a> and the ratio of the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leucoanthocyanin" title="Leucoanthocyanin">leucoanthocyanins</a> varies considerably in certain species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_genera">Related genera</span></h2>
<p>A small genus of similar trees, <i><a href="/wiki/Angophora" title="Angophora">Angophora</a></i>, has also been known since the 18th century. In 1995 new evidence, largely genetic, indicated that some prominent eucalyptus species were actually more closely related to <i>Angophora</i> than to the other eucalypts; they were split off into the new <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Corymbia" title="Corymbia">Corymbia</a></i>. Although separate, the three groups are allied and it remains acceptable to refer to the members of all three genera, <i>Angophora</i>, <i>Corymbia</i> and <i>Eucalyptus</i>, as "eucalypts".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tall_timber">Tall timber</span></h2>
<p>Several eucalypt species are among the tallest trees in the world. <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">Eucalyptus regnans</a></i>, the Australian 'mountain ash', is the tallest of all flowering plants (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperms</a>); today, the tallest measured specimen named <a href="/wiki/Centurion_(tree)" title="Centurion (tree)">Centurion</a> is 99.6 m (327 ft) tall. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Douglas-fir" title="Coast Douglas-fir">Coast Douglas-fir</a> is about the same height; only <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">coast redwood</a> is taller, and they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a>). Six other eucalypt species exceed 80 metres in height: <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_obliqua" title="Eucalyptus obliqua">Eucalyptus obliqua</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_delegatensis" title="Eucalyptus delegatensis">Eucalyptus delegatensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_diversicolor" title="Eucalyptus diversicolor">Eucalyptus diversicolor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_nitens" title="Eucalyptus nitens">Eucalyptus nitens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">Eucalyptus globulus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_viminalis" title="Eucalyptus viminalis">Eucalyptus viminalis</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frost_intolerance">Frost intolerance</span></h2>
<p>Most eucalypts are not tolerant of severe cold. Whilst in a wide range of climates mild frost is experienced by eucalypts, they in general only tolerate light frosts down to −5 °C (23 °F); the hardiest are the snow gums, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_pauciflora" title="Eucalyptus pauciflora">Eucalyptus pauciflora</a></i>, which is capable of withstanding cold and frost down to about −20 °C (−4 °F). Two subspecies, <i>E. pauciflora subsp. niphophila</i> and <i>E. pauciflora subsp. debeuzevillei</i> in particular are even hardier and can tolerate even quite severe winters. Several other species, especially from the high plateau and mountains of central <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_coccifera" title="Eucalyptus coccifera">Eucalyptus coccifera</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_subcrenulata" title="Eucalyptus subcrenulata">Eucalyptus subcrenulata</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_gunnii" title="Eucalyptus gunnii">Eucalyptus gunnii</a></i>, have also produced extreme cold-hardy forms and it is seed procured from these <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetically</a> hardy strains that are planted for ornament in colder parts of the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_relationships">Animal relationships</span></h2>


<p>An <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oil</a> extracted from eucalyptus leaves contains compounds that are powerful natural disinfectants and can be toxic in large quantities. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupial_herbivore" title="Marsupial herbivore">marsupial herbivores</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">koalas</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Phalangeriformes" title="Phalangeriformes">possums</a>, are relatively tolerant of it. The close correlation of these oils with other more potent toxins called formylated <a href="/wiki/Phloroglucinol" title="Phloroglucinol">phloroglucinol</a> compounds (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Euglobal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Euglobal (page does not exist)">euglobals</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Macrocarpal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Macrocarpal (page does not exist)">macrocarpals</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sideroxylonal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sideroxylonal (page does not exist)">sideroxylonals</a>) allows koalas and other <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a> species to make food choices based on the smell of the leaves. For koalas, these compounds are the most important factor in leaf choice.</p>
<p>Eucalyptus flowers produce a great abundance of <a href="/wiki/Nectar" title="Nectar">nectar</a>, providing food for many <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a> including <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, birds, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiroptera" title="Chiroptera">bats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phalangeriformes" title="Phalangeriformes">possums</a>. Although eucalyptus trees are seemingly well-defended from herbivores by the oils and phenolic compounds, they have insect pests. These include the eucalyptus <a href="/wiki/Longhorn_beetle" title="Longhorn beetle">longhorn borer</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Phoracantha_semipunctata" title="Phoracantha semipunctata">Phoracantha semipunctata</a></i> and the aphid-like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psyllid" title="Psyllid">psyllids</a> known as "<a href="/wiki/Bell_miner" title="Bell miner">bell lerps</a>", both of which have become established as pests throughout the world wherever eucalypts are cultivated.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusocial" title="Eusocial">eusocial</a> beetle <i><a href="/wiki/Austroplatypus_incompertus" title="Austroplatypus incompertus">Austroplatypus incompertus</a></i> makes and defends its galleries exclusively inside <i>Eucalyptus</i> plants.</p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lepidoptera_that_feed_on_Eucalyptus" title="List of Lepidoptera that feed on Eucalyptus">List of Lepidoptera that feed on <i>Eucalyptus</i></a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_to_fire">Adaptation to fire</span></h2>


<p>Eucalypts originated between 35 and 50 million years ago, not long after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia-New_Guinea" title="Australia-New Guinea">Australia-New Guinea</a> separated from <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>, their rise coinciding with an increase in fossil charcoal deposits (suggesting that fire was a factor even then), but they remained a minor component of the Tertiary rainforest until about 20 million years ago, when the gradual drying of the continent and depletion of soil nutrients led to the development of a more open forest type, predominantly <i><a href="/wiki/Casuarina" title="Casuarina">Casuarina</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i> species.</p>
<p>The aridification of Australia during the mid-tertiary period (25-40 million years ago), combined with the annual penetration of tropical convection storms, and associated lightning, deep into the continental interior stimulated the gradual evolution, diversification and geographic expansion of the flammable biota. The absence of great rivers or mountain chains meant that there were no geographic barriers to check the spread of fires. From the monsoonal 'cradle', fire-promoting species expanded into higher rainfall environments, where lightning was less frequent, gradually displacing the Gondwanan rainforest from all but the most fire-sheltered habitats.</p>
<p>The two valuable timber trees, alpine ash <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_delegatensis" title="Eucalyptus delegatensis">E. delegatensis</a></i> and Australian mountain ash <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">E. regnans</a></i>, are killed by fire and only regenerate from seed. The same 2003 bushfire that had little impact on forests around <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a> resulted in thousands of hectares of dead ash forests. However, a small amount of ash survived and put out new ash trees as well. There has been some debate as to whether to leave the stands or attempt to harvest the mostly undamaged timber, which is increasingly recognised as a damaging practice.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span></h2>
<p>The two most common hazards of eucalyptus species to people are fire and falling branches.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_hazard">Fire hazard</span></h3>

<p>Eucalyptus oil is highly flammable (ignited trees have been known to explode); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushfire" title="Bushfire">bushfires</a> can travel easily through the oil-rich air of the tree crowns. Eucalypts obtain long-term fire survivability from their ability to regenerate from <a href="/wiki/Epicormic_shoot" title="Epicormic shoot">epicormic buds</a> situated deep within their thick bark, or from <a href="/wiki/Lignotuber" title="Lignotuber">lignotubers</a>, or by producing <a href="/wiki/Serotiny" title="Serotiny">serotinous</a> fruits.</p>
<p>In seasonally dry climates oaks are often fire-resistant, particularly in open grasslands, as a grass fire is insufficient to ignite the scattered trees. In contrast a eucalyptus forest tends to promote fire because of the volatile and highly combustible oils produced by the leaves, as well as the production of large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Plant_litter" title="Plant litter">litter</a> which is high in phenolics, preventing its breakdown by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> and thus accumulates as large amounts of dry, combustible fuel. Consequently, dense eucalypt plantings may be subject to catastrophic firestorms. In fact, almost thirty years before the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_firestorm_of_1991" title="Oakland firestorm of 1991">Oakland firestorm of 1991</a>, a study of eucalyptus in the area warned that the litter beneath the trees builds up very rapidly and should be regularly monitored and removed. It has been estimated that 70% of the energy released through the combustion of vegetation in the Oakland fire was due to eucalyptus. In a <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> study, it was found that the fuel load (in tons per acre) of non-native eucalyptus woods is almost three times as great as native oak woodland.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Falling_branches"><span id="Unexpected_branch_drops"></span>Falling branches</span></h3>

<p>Some species of gum trees drop branches unexpectedly. In Australia, <a href="/wiki/Parks_Victoria" title="Parks Victoria">Parks Victoria</a> warns campers not to camp under <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_camaldulensis" title="Eucalyptus camaldulensis">river red gums</a>. Some councils in Australia such as <a href="/wiki/Gosnells,_Western_Australia" title="Gosnells, Western Australia">Gosnells, Western Australia</a>, have removed eucalypts after reports of damage from dropped branches, even in the face of lengthy, well publicised protests to protect particular trees. A former Australian National Botanic Gardens director and consulting arborist, Robert Boden, has been quoted referring to "summer branch drop". Dropping of branches is recognised in Australia literature through the fictional death of Judy in <a href="/wiki/Seven_Little_Australians" title="Seven Little Australians">Seven Little Australians</a>. Although all large trees can drop branches, the weight of eucalyptus wood is high because of its density and high resin content.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span></h2>

<p><i>Eucalyptus</i> was introduced from <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> to the rest of the world following the <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">Cook</a> expedition in 1770. Collected by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Sir Joseph Banks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanist</a> on the expedition, it was subsequently introduced to many parts of the world, notably <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. On the order of 250 species are under cultivation in California. In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and also <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <i>eucalypts</i> have been planted in <a href="/wiki/Pulpwood" title="Pulpwood">pulpwood</a> <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>. <i>Eucalyptus</i> are the basis for several industries, such as sawmilling, pulp, charcoal and others. Several species have become <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> and are causing major problems for local ecosystems, mainly due to the absence of wildlife corridors and rotations management. Eucalypts have many uses which have made them <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economically</a> important trees, and have become a <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crop</a> in poor areas such as <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>, Africa and the <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peruvian</a> <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, despite concerns that the trees are <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> in some countries like <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. Best-known are perhaps the varieties <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_diversicolor" title="Eucalyptus diversicolor">karri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_melliodora" title="Eucalyptus melliodora">yellow box</a>. Due to their fast growth, the foremost benefit of these trees is their wood. They can be chopped off at the root and grow back again. They provide many desirable characteristics for use as <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornament</a>, timber, firewood and <a href="/wiki/Pulpwood" title="Pulpwood">pulpwood</a>. It is also used in a number of industries, from fence posts and charcoal to <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> extraction for <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>. Fast growth also makes eucalypts suitable as <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">windbreaks</a> and to reduce <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>.</p>
<p>Eucalypts draw a tremendous amount of water from the soil through the process of <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>. They have been planted (or re-planted) in some places to lower the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> and reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salination" title="Soil salination">soil salination</a>. Eucalypts have also been used as a way of reducing <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> by draining the soil in Algeria, Lebanon, Sicily, elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, in Caucasus (Western <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>), and California. Drainage removes <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> which provide a habitat for <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, but can also destroy ecologically productive areas. This drainage is not limited to the soil surface, because the eucalyptus roots are up to 2.5 m (8.2 ft) in length and can, depending on the location, even reach the <a href="/wiki/Phreatic_zone" title="Phreatic zone">phreatic zone</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulpwood">Pulpwood</span></h3>
<p>Eucalyptus is the most common short fibre source for <a href="/wiki/Pulpwood" title="Pulpwood">pulpwood</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">pulp</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">Eucalyptus globulus</a></i> (in temperate climates) and the hybrid of <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_urophylla" title="Eucalyptus urophylla">Eucalyptus urophylla</a></i> x <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_grandis" title="Eucalyptus grandis">Eucalyptus grandis</a></i> (in tropical climates) are the most used varieties in <a href="/wiki/Papermaking" title="Papermaking">papermaking</a>. The fibre length of Eucalyptus is relatively short and uniform with low coarseness compared with other hardwoods commonly used as pulpwood. The fibres are slender, yet relatively thick walled. This gives uniform paper formation and high <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a> that are important for all types of <a href="/wiki/Fine_paper" title="Fine paper">fine papers</a>. The low coarseness is important for high quality <a href="/wiki/Coated_paper" title="Coated paper">coated papers</a>. Eucalyptus is suitable for many <a href="/wiki/Tissue_paper" title="Tissue paper">tissue papers</a> as the short and slender fibres gives a high number of fibres per gram and low coarseness contributes to softness.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eucalyptus_oil">Eucalyptus oil</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_oil" title="Eucalyptus oil">Eucalyptus oil</a> is readily <a href="/wiki/Steam_distillation" title="Steam distillation">steam distilled</a> from the leaves and can be used for cleaning and as an industrial solvent, as an antiseptic, for deodorising, and in very small quantities in <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> supplements, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweets" title="Sweets">sweets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cough_drops" title="Cough drops">cough drops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">toothpaste</a> and decongestants. It has insect repellent properties (Jahn 1991 a, b; 1992), and is an active ingredient in some commercial mosquito repellents (Fradin &amp; Day 2002). <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">Eucalyptus globulus</a></i> is the principal source of eucalyptus oil worldwide.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honey">Honey</span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nectar_(plant)" title="Nectar (plant)">nectar</a> of some eucalypts produces high-quality <a href="/wiki/Monofloral_honey" title="Monofloral honey">monofloral honey</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_instruments">Musical instruments</span></h3>
<p>Eucalypt wood is also commonly used to make <a href="/wiki/Didgeridoo" title="Didgeridoo">didgeridoos</a>, a traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginals" title="Australian Aboriginals">Australian Aboriginal</a> <a href="/wiki/Wind_instrument" title="Wind instrument">wind instrument</a>. The trunk of the tree is hollowed out by termites, and then cut down if the bore is of the correct size and shape.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyes">Dyes</span></h3>
<p>All parts of <i>Eucalyptus</i> may be used to make <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a> that are substantive on <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> fibres (such as <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>), simply by processing the plant part with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. Colours to be achieved range from yellow and orange through green, tan, chocolate and deep rust red. The material remaining after processing can be safely used as <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser">fertiliser</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prospecting">Prospecting</span></h3>
<p>Eucalyptus trees in the Australian outback draw up gold from tens of meters underground through their root system and deposit it as particles in their leaves and branches. A Maia detector for x-ray elemental imaging at the Australian Synchrotron clearly showed deposits of gold and other metals in the structure of Eucalyptus leaves from the Kalgoorlie region of Western Australia that would have been untraceable using other methods. The microscopic leaf-bound "nuggets" are not worth collecting themselves, but may provide an environmentally benign way of locating subsurface mineral deposits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eucalyptus_as_plantation_species">Eucalyptus as plantation species</span></h2>
<p>In the 20th century, scientists around the world experimented with eucalyptus species. They hoped to grow them in the tropics, but most experimental results failed until breakthroughs in the 1960s-1980s in species selection, silviculture, and breeding programs "unlocked" the potential of eucalypts in the tropics. Prior to then, as Brett Bennett noted in a 2010 article, eucalypts were something of the "<a href="/wiki/El_Dorado" title="El Dorado">El Dorado</a>" of forestry. Today, eucalyptus is the most widely planted type of tree in plantations around the world, in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> (mainly in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>), <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galicia,_Spain" title="Galicia, Spain">Galicia</a>, and many more.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span></h3>
<p><b>California</b>. In the 1850s, <i>Eucalyptus</i> trees were introduced to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> by Australians during the <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>. Much of California has a similar climate to parts of Australia. By the early 1900s, thousands of acres of eucalypts were planted with the encouragement of the state government. It was hoped that they would provide a renewable source of timber for construction, furniture making and <a href="/wiki/Railroad_tie" title="Railroad tie">railroad ties</a>. It was soon found that for the latter purpose eucalyptus was particularly unsuitable, as the ties made from eucalyptus had a tendency to twist while drying, and the dried ties were so tough that it was nearly impossible to hammer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_spike" title="Rail spike">rail spikes</a> into them.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>They went on to note that the promise of eucalyptus in California was based on the old virgin forests of Australia. This was a mistake as the young trees being harvested in California could not compare in quality to the centuries-old eucalyptus timber of Australia. It reacted differently to harvest. The older trees didn't split or warp as the infant California crop did. There was a vast difference between the two, and this would doom the California eucalyptus industry.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>One way in which the eucalyptus, mainly the blue gum <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">E. globulus</a></i>, proved valuable in California was in providing windbreaks for highways, orange groves, and other farms in the mostly treeless <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">central part of the state</a>. They are also admired as shade and ornamental trees in many cities and gardens.</p>
<p><i>Eucalyptus</i> plantations in California have been criticised because they compete with native plants and do not support native animals. Fire is also a problem. The 1991 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland_Hills_firestorm" title="Oakland Hills firestorm">Oakland Hills firestorm</a> which destroyed almost 3,000 homes and killed 25 people was partly fuelled by large numbers of eucalypts close to the houses.</p>
<p>In some parts of California, eucalypt plantations are being removed and native trees and plants restored. Individuals have also illegally destroyed some trees and are suspected of introducing insect pests from Australia which attack the trees.</p>
<p>Eucalyptus trees also grow in parts of the Pacific Northwest: <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span></h3>
<p><b>Uruguay</b>. <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Lussich" title="Antonio Lussich">Antonio Lussich</a> introduced <i>Eucalyptus</i> into <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> in approximately 1896, throughout what is now <a href="/wiki/Maldonado_Department" title="Maldonado Department">Maldonado Department</a>, and it has spread all over the south-eastern and eastern coast. There had been no trees in the area because it consisted of dry sand dunes and stones. (Lussich also introduced many other trees, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a>, but they have not expanded so massively.)</p>
<p>Uruguayan forestry crops using eucalyptus species have been promoted since 1989, when the new National Forestry Law established that 20% of the national territory would be dedicated to forestry. As the main landscape of Uruguay is grassland (140,000 km, 87% of the national territory), most of the forestry plantations would be established in prairie regions. The planting of <i>Eucalyptus</i> sp. has been criticised because of concerns that soil would be degraded by nutrient depletion and other biological changes. During the last ten years, in the northwestern regions of Uruguay the <i>Eucalyptus</i> sp. plantations have reached annual forestation rates of 300%. That zone has a potential forested area of 1,000,000 hectares, approximately 29% of the national territory dedicated to forestry, of which approximately 800,000 hectares are currently forested by monoculture of <i>Eucalyptus</i> spp. It is expected that the radical and durable substitution of vegetation cover leads to changes in the quantity and quality of soil organic matter. Such changes may also influence soil fertility and soil physical and chemical properties. The soil quality effects associated with <i>Eucalyptus</i> sp. plantations could have negative effects on soil chemistry; for example: soil acidification, iron leaching, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allelopathic" title="Allelopathic">allelopathic</a> activities and a high C:N ratio of litter. Additionally, as most scientific understanding of land cover change effects is related to ecosystems where forests were replaced by grasslands or crops, or grassland was replaced by crops, the environmental effects of the current Uruguayan land cover changes are not well understood. The first scientific publication on soil studies in western zone tree plantations (focused on pulp production) appeared in 2004 and described soil acidification and soil carbon changes, similar to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Podzolisation" title="Podzolisation">podzolisation</a> process, and destruction of clay (illite-like minerals), which is the main reservoir of potassium in the soil. Although these studies were carried out in an important zone for forest cultivation, they cannot define the current situation in the rest of the land area under eucalyptus cultivation. Moreover, recently Jackson and Jobbagy have proposed another adverse environmental impact that may result from <i>Eucalyptus</i> culture on prairie soils: stream acidification.</p>
<p>The eucalyptus species most planted are <i>E. grandis</i>, <i>E. globulus</i> and <i>E. dunnii</i>; they are used mainly for pulp mills. Approximately 80,000 ha of <i>E. grandis</i> situated in the departments of Rivera, Tacuarembó and Paysandú is primarily earmarked for the solid wood market, although a portion of it is used for sawlogs and plywood. The current area under commercial forest plantation is 6% of the total. The main uses of the wood produced are elemental chlorine free pulp mill production (for <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sawlog" title="Sawlog">sawlogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">bioenergy</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoelectric" title="Thermoelectric">thermoelectric</a> generation). Most of the products obtained from <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pulp_mill" title="Pulp mill">pulp mills</a>, as well as plywood and <a href="/wiki/Trunk_(botany)" title="Trunk (botany)">logs</a>, are exported. This has raised the income of this sector with respect to traditional products from other sectors. Uruguayan forestry plantations have rates of growth of 30 cubic metres per hectare per year and commercial harvesting occurs after 9 years.</p>
<p><b>Brazil</b>. <i>Eucalyptus</i> was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in 1910, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> substitution and the <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> industry. It has thrived in the local environment, and today there are around 7 million hectares planted. The wood is highly valued by the charcoal and pulp and paper industries. The short rotation allows a larger wood production and supplies wood for several other activities, helping to preserve the native forests from logging. When well managed, the plantation soils can sustain endless replanting. <i>Eucalyptus</i> plantings are also used as <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">wind breaks</a>. Brazil's plantations have world-record rates of growth, typically over 40 cubic metres per hectare per year, and commercial harvesting occurs after years 5. Due to continual development and governmental funding, year-on-year growth is consistently being improved. <i>Eucalyptus</i> can produce up to 100 cubic metres per hectare per year. Brazil has become the top exporter and producer of <i>Eucalyptus</i> round wood and pulp, and has played an important role in developing the Australian market through the country's committed research in this area. The local iron producers in Brazil rely heavily on sustainably grown <i>Eucalyptus</i> for <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>; this has greatly pushed up the price of charcoal in recent years. The plantations are generally owned and operated for national and international industry by timber asset companies such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomson Forestry (page does not exist)">Thomson Forestry</a>, Greenwood Management or cellulose producers such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aracruz_Cellulose&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aracruz Cellulose (page does not exist)">Aracruz Cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stora_Enso" title="Stora Enso">Stora Enso</a>.</p>
<p>Overall, South America is expected to produce 55% of the world's <i>Eucalyptus</i> round-wood by 2010. Many environmental NGOs have criticised the use of exotic tree species for forestry in Latin America.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span></h3>
<p><b>Ethiopia</b>. <i>Eucalyptus</i> was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> in either 1894 or 1895, either by Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Menelik_II_of_Ethiopia" title="Menelik II of Ethiopia">Menelik II</a>'s French advisor Mondon-Vidailhet or by the Englishman Captain O'Brian. Menelik II endorsed its planting around his new capital city of <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a> because of the massive <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> around the city for <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">firewood</a>. According to Richard R.K. Pankhurst, "The great advantage of the eucalypts was that they were fast growing, required little attention and when cut down grew up again from the roots; it could be harvested every ten years. The tree proved successful from the onset". Plantations of eucalypts spread from the capital to other growing urban centres such as <a href="/wiki/Debre_Marqos" title="Debre Marqos">Debre Marqos</a>. Pankhurst reports that the most common species found in Addis Ababa in the mid-1960s was <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">E. globulus</a></i>, although he also found <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_melliodora" title="Eucalyptus melliodora">E. melliodora</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_rostrata_(disambiguation)" title="Eucalyptus rostrata (disambiguation)">E. rostrata</a></i> in significant numbers. David Buxton, writing of central Ethiopia in the mid-1940s, observed that eucalyptus trees "have become an integral -- and a pleasing -- element in the <a href="/wiki/Shewa" title="Shewa">Shoan</a> landscape and has largely displaced the slow-growing native 'cedar' <i><a href="/wiki/Juniperus_procera" title="Juniperus procera">Juniperus procera</a></i>)."</p>
<p>It was commonly believed that the thirst of the <i>Eucalyptus</i> "tended to dry up rivers and wells", creating such opposition to the species that in 1913 a proclamation was issued ordering a partial destruction of all standing trees, and their replacement with <a href="/wiki/Morus_(plant)" title="Morus (plant)">mulberry</a> trees. Pankhurst reports, "The proclamation however remained a dead letter; there is no evidence of eucalypts being uprooted, still less of mulberry trees being planted." Eucalypts remain a defining feature of Addis Ababa.</p>
<p><b>Madagascar</b>. Much of Madagascar's original native forest has been replaced with <i>Eucalyptus</i>, threatening biodiversity by isolating remaining natural areas such as <a href="/wiki/Andasibe-Mantadia_National_Park" title="Andasibe-Mantadia National Park">Andasibe-Mantadia National Park</a>.</p>
<p><b>South Africa</b>. Numerous <i>Eucalyptus</i> species have been introduced into <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, mainly for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">firewood</a> but also for ornamental purposes. They are popular with <a href="/wiki/Beekeeper" title="Beekeeper">beekeepers</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> they provide. However, in South Africa they are considered invasive, with their water-sucking capabilities threatening water supplies. They also release a chemical into the surrounding soil which kills native competitors.</p>
<p><i>Eucalyptus</i> <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedlings</a> are usually unable to compete with the indigenous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grasses</a>, but after a fire when the grass cover has been removed, a seed-bed may be created. The following <i>Eucalyptus</i> species have been able to become naturalised in South Africa: <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_camaldulensis" title="Eucalyptus camaldulensis">E. camaldulensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cladocalyx" title="Eucalyptus cladocalyx">E. cladocalyx</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_diversicolor" title="Eucalyptus diversicolor">E. diversicolor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_grandis" title="Eucalyptus grandis">E. grandis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_lehmannii" title="Eucalyptus lehmannii">E. lehmannii</a></i>.</p>
<p><b>Zimbabwe</b>. As in South Africa, many <i>Eucalyptus</i> species have been introduced into <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, mainly for timber and firewood, and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_robusta" title="Eucalyptus robusta">E. robusta</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_tereticornis" title="Eucalyptus tereticornis">E. tereticornis</a></i> have been recorded as having become naturalised there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span></h3>
<p>In continental <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and the North of Spain (especially in the provinces of Cantabria, Vizcaya, Asturias, and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>) farmland has been replaced with eucalypt plantations.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the eucalyptus only arrived at the turn of the 19th century and large scale plantations were started at the beginning of the 20th century with the aim of drying up swampy ground to defeat malaria. This, their rapid growth in the Italian climate and excellent function as windbreaks, has made them a common sight in the south of the country, including the islands of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>. They are also valued for the characteristic smelling and tasting <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> that is produced from them. The variety of eucalyptus most commonly found in Italy is <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_camaldulensis" title="Eucalyptus camaldulensis">E. camaldulensis</a></i>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, eucalyptus is widely found, especially in southern Greece and <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>. It is cultivated and used for various purposes, including as an ingredient in pharmaceutical products (e.g., creams, elixirs and sprays) and for leather production. It was imported in 1862 by botanist Theodoros Georgios Orphanides. The principal species is <i>Eucalyptus globulus</i>.</p>
<p>Eucalyptus has been grown in Ireland since trials in the 1930s and now grows wild in South Western Ireland in the mild climate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span></h3>

<p>Eucalyptus seeds of the species <i>E. globulus</i> were imported into <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> in the 1860s, but did not acclimatise well. Later, <i>E. camaldulensis</i> was introduced more successfully and it is still a very common tree in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. The use of eucalyptus trees to drain swampy land was a common practice in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The German Templer colony of <a href="/wiki/Sarona_(colony)" title="Sarona (colony)">Sarona</a> had begun planting eucalyptus for this purpose by 1874, though it is not known where the seeds came from. Many Zionist colonies also adopted the practice in the following years under the guidance of the <a href="/wiki/Mikveh_Israel" title="Mikveh Israel">Mikveh Israel Agricultural School</a>.</p>
<p>In India, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICFRE#Research_Institutes_under_the_Council" title="ICFRE">Institute of Forest Genetics and Tree Breeding, Coimbatore</a> started a eucalyptus breeding program in the 1990s. The organisation released four varieties of conventionally bred, high yielding and genetically improved clones for commercial and research interests in 2010.</p>
<p>Eucalyptus trees were introduced to <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> in the late 19th century by <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a> planters, for wind protection, shade and fuel. Forestry replanting of eucalyptus began in the 1930s in deforested mountain areas, and currently there are about 10 species present in the island. They account for 20% of major reforestation plantings. They provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_sleeper" title="Railway sleeper">railway sleepers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">utility poles</a>, sawn <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuelwood" title="Fuelwood">fuelwood</a>, but are controversial because of their adverse effect on biodiversity, hydrology and soil fertility. They are associated with another invasive species, the <a href="/wiki/Gall_wasp" title="Gall wasp">eucalyptus gall wasp</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Leptocybe" title="Leptocybe">Leptocybe</a> invasa</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_Islands">Pacific Islands</span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a></b> Some 90 species of eucalyptus have been introduced to the islands, where they have displaced some native species due to their higher maximum height, fast growth and lower water needs. Particularly noticeable is the rainbow eucalyptus (<i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_deglupta" title="Eucalyptus deglupta">Eucalyptus deglupta</a></i>), native to Indonesia and the Philippines, whose bark falls off to reveal a trunk that can be green, red, orange, yellow, pink and purple.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonnative_eucalyptus_and_biodiversity">Nonnative eucalyptus and biodiversity</span></h2>
<p>Due to similar favourable climatic conditions, <i>Eucalyptus</i> plantations have often replaced <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> woodlands, for example in California, Spain and Portugal. The resulting monocultures have raised concerns about loss of biological diversity, through loss of <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a> that <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and birds feed on, absence of hollows that in oak trees provide shelter and nesting sites for birds and small mammals and for bee colonies, as well as lack of downed trees in managed plantations. A study of the relationship between birds and eucalyptus in the San Francisco Bay Area found that bird diversity was similar in native forest vs. eucalyptus forest but the species were different. One way in which the avifauna changes is that cavity nesting birds including woodpeckers, owls, chickadees, wood ducks, etc. are depauperate in eucalyptus groves because the decay-resistant wood of these trees prevents cavity formation by decay or excavation. Also those bird species that glean insects from foliage, such as warblers and vireos, have population declines when eucalyptus replace oak forest. Birds that do well in eucalyptus groves in California like tall vertical habitat like herons and egrets (possibly because redwood trees are less available), or have longer bills, which may play a role in preventing their nostrils from being clogged by eucalyptus resin/pitch. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Reyes_Bird_Observatory" title="Point Reyes Bird Observatory">Point Reyes Bird Observatory</a> observes that sometimes short-billed birds like the <a href="/wiki/Ruby-crowned_kinglet" title="Ruby-crowned kinglet">ruby-crowned kinglet</a> are found dead beneath eucalyptus trees with their nostrils clogged with pitch.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarch_(butterfly)" title="Monarch (butterfly)">Monarch</a> butterflies use eucalyptus in California for over-wintering, but in some locations have a preference for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monterey_pine" title="Monterey pine">Monterey pines</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Although eucalypts must have been seen by the very early European explorers and collectors, no botanical collections of them are known to have been made until 1770 when <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Solander" title="Daniel Solander">Daniel Solander</a> arrived at <a href="/wiki/Botany_Bay" title="Botany Bay">Botany Bay</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_James_Cook" title="Captain James Cook">Captain James Cook</a>. There they collected specimens of <i>E. gummifera</i> and later, near the <a href="/wiki/Endeavour_River" title="Endeavour River">Endeavour River</a> in northern <i>Queensland</i>, <i>E. platyphylla</i>; neither of these species was named as such at the time.</p>
<p>In 1777, on Cook's third expedition, David Nelson collected a eucalypt on <a href="/wiki/Bruny_Island" title="Bruny Island">Bruny Island</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>. This specimen was taken to the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, and was named <i>Eucalyptus obliqua</i> by the French botanist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Louis_L%27H%C3%A9ritier_de_Brutelle" title="Charles Louis L'Héritier de Brutelle">L'Héritier</a>, who was working in London at the time. He coined the generic name from the Greek roots <i>eu</i> and <i>calyptos</i>, meaning "well" and "covered" in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(botany)" title="Operculum (botany)">operculum</a> of the flower bud which protects the developing flower parts as the flower develops and is shed by the pressure of the emerging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stamens" title="Stamens">stamens</a> at flowering. It was most likely an accident that L'Heritier chose a feature common to all eucalypts.</p>
<p>The name <i>obliqua</i> was derived from the Latin <i>obliquus</i>, meaning "oblique", which is the <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanical</a> term describing a <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaf</a> base where the two sides of the leaf blade are of unequal length and do not meet the petiole at the same place.</p>
<p><i>E. obliqua</i> was published in 1788-89, which coincided with the first official European settlement of Australia. Between then and the turn of the 19th century, several more species of <i>Eucalyptus</i> were named and published. Most of these were by the English botanist <a href="/wiki/James_Edward_Smith" title="James Edward Smith">James Edward Smith</a> and most were, as might be expected, trees of the <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> region. These include the economically valuable <i>E. pilularis</i>, <i>E. saligna</i> and <i>E. tereticornis</i>.</p>
<p>The first endemic Western Australian <i>Eucalyptus</i> to be collected and subsequently named was the Yate (<i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cornuta" title="Eucalyptus cornuta">E. cornuta</a></i>) by the French botanist <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Labillardi%C3%A8re" title="Jacques Labillardière">Jacques Labillardière</a>, who collected in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Esperance,_Western_Australia" title="Esperance, Western Australia">Esperance</a> area in 1792.</p>
<p>Several Australian botanists were active during the 19th century, particularly <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Mueller" title="Ferdinand von Mueller">Ferdinand von Mueller</a>, whose work on <i>eucalypts</i> contributed greatly to the first comprehensive account of the genus in <a href="/wiki/George_Bentham" title="George Bentham">George Bentham</a>'s <i>Flora Australiensis</i> in 1867. The account is the most important early systematic treatment of the genus. Bentham divided it into five series whose distinctions were based on characteristics of the stamens, particularly the anthers (Mueller, 1879–84), work elaborated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry_Maiden" title="Joseph Henry Maiden">Joseph Henry Maiden</a> (1903–33) and still further by William Faris Blakely (1934). The anther system became too complex to be workable and more recent systematic work has concentrated on the characteristics of buds, fruits, leaves and bark.</p>
