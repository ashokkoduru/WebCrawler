For beverages containing <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverage</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Alcohol_(disambiguation)" title="Alcohol (disambiguation)">Alcohol (disambiguation)</a>.</div>


<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, an <b>alcohol</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> (–<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a>) is bound to a <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom. The term alcohol originally referred to the primary alcohol <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (ethyl alcohol), the predominant alcohol in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>.</p>
<p>The suffix <i>-ol</i> appears in the <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">IUPAC chemical name</a> of all substances where the hydroxyl group is the functional group with the highest priority; in substances where a higher priority group is present the prefix <i>hydroxy-</i> will appear in the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) name. The suffix <i>-ol</i> in non-systematic names (such as <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">paracetamol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>) also typically indicates that the substance includes a hydroxyl functional group and, so, can be termed an alcohol. But many substances, particularly sugars (examples <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>) contain hydroxyl functional groups without using the suffix. An important class of alcohols, of which <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and ethanol are the simplest members is the <a href="/wiki/Aliphatic_compound" title="Aliphatic compound">saturated straight chain</a> alcohols, the general formula for which is C<sub>n</sub>H<sub>2n+1</sub>OH.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>The word <i>alcohol</i> appears in English as a term for a very fine powder in the sixteenth century. It was borrowed from French, which took it from medical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Latin" title="Middle Latin">Latin</a>.</p>
<p>Ultimately the word is from the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A7%D9%84%D9%83%D8%AD%D9%84" title="wikt:الكحل">كحل</a></span> (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Arabic transliteration">al-kuḥl</span>,</i> "<a href="/wiki/Kohl_(cosmetics)" title="Kohl (cosmetics)">kohl</a>, a powder used as an eyeliner"). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-" title="Al-">Al-</a> is the Arabic <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">definitive article</a>, equivalent to <i>the</i> in English; <i>alcohol</i> was originally used for the very fine powder produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a> of the natural mineral <a href="/wiki/Stibnite" title="Stibnite">stibnite</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">Sb</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a><sub>3</sub> (hence the essence or "spirit" of the substance), which was used as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a>, eyeliner, and cosmetic (see <a href="/wiki/Kohl_(cosmetics)" title="Kohl (cosmetics)">kohl (cosmetics)</a>) According to this theory, the meaning of alkuhul would have been first extended to distilled substances in general, and then narrowed to ethanol.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Traheron" title="Bartholomew Traheron">Bartholomew Traheron</a>, in his 1543 translation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Vigo" title="John of Vigo">John of Vigo</a>, introduces the word as a term used by "barbarous" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moorish" title="Moorish">Moorish</a>) authors for "fine powder." Vigo wrote: <i>the barbarous auctours use alcohol, or (as I fynde it sometymes wryten) alcofoll, for moost fine poudre.</i></p>
<p>The 1657 <i><a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books/about/Lexicon_Chymicum.html?id=d645AAAAcAAJ" rel="nofollow">Lexicon Chymicum</a></i>, by William Johnson glosses the word as <i>antimonium sive stibium.</i> By extension, the word came to refer to any fluid obtained by distillation, including "alcohol of wine," the distilled essence of wine. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libavius" title="Libavius">Libavius</a> in <i>Alchymia</i> (1594) refers to <i>vini alcohol vel vinum alcalisatum</i>. Johnson (1657) glosses <i>alcohol vini</i> as <i>quando omnis superfluitas vini a vino separatur, ita ut accensum ardeat donec totum consumatur, nihilque fæcum aut phlegmatis in fundo remaneat.</i> The word's meaning became restricted to "spirit of wine" (the chemical known today as <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>) in the 18th century and was extended to the class of substances so-called as "alcohols" in modern chemistry after 1850.</p>
<p>The term <i>ethanol</i> was invented 1838, modeled on the German word äthyl (Liebig), which is in turn based on Greek <i>aither</i> <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> and <i>hyle</i> "stuff."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematic_names">Systematic names</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chemical_nomenclature" title="Chemical nomenclature">IUPAC nomenclature</a> is used in scientific publications and where precise identification of the substance is important, especially in cases where the relative complexity of the molecule does not make such a systematic name unwieldy. In the IUPAC system, in naming simple alcohols, the name of the alkane chain loses the terminal "e" and adds "ol", <i>e.g.</i>, as in "methanol" and "ethanol". When necessary, the position of the hydroxyl group is indicated by a number between the alkane name and the "ol": <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propan-1-ol" title="Propan-1-ol">propan-1-ol</a> for CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH, <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">propan-2-ol</a> for CH<sub>3</sub>CH(OH)CH<sub>3</sub>. If a higher priority group is present (such as an <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>), then the prefix "hydroxy" is used, e.g., as in 1-hydroxy-2-propanone (CH<sub>3</sub>C(O)CH<sub>2</sub>OH).</p>

<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_names">Common names</span></h3>
<p>In other less formal contexts, an alcohol is often called with the name of the corresponding alkyl group followed by the word "alcohol", e.g., <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl</a> alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_group" title="Ethyl group">ethyl</a> alcohol. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propyl" title="Propyl">Propyl</a> alcohol may be <a href="/wiki/Propanol" title="Propanol"><i>n</i>-propyl alcohol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">isopropyl alcohol</a>, depending on whether the hydroxyl group is bonded to the end or middle carbon on the straight <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> chain. As described under systematic naming, if another group on the molecule takes priority, the alcohol moiety is often indicated using the "hydroxy-" prefix.</p>
<p><span id="secondary"></span><span id="tertiary"></span> Alcohols are then classified into primary, secondary (<i>sec-</i>, <i>s-</i>), and tertiary (<i>tert-</i>, <i>t-</i>), based upon the number of carbon atoms connected to the carbon atom that bears the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>. (The respective numeric shorthands 1°, 2°, and 3° are also sometimes used in informal settings.) The primary alcohols have general formulas RCH<sub>2</sub>OH. The simplest primary alcohol is methanol (CH<sub>3</sub>OH), for which R=H, and the next is ethanol, for which R=CH<sub>3</sub>, the <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl group</a>. Secondary alcohols are those of the form RR'CHOH, the simplest of which is 2-propanol (R=R'=CH<sub>3</sub>). For the tertiary alcohols the general form is RR'R"COH. The simplest example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tert-butanol" title="Tert-butanol">tert-butanol</a> (2-methylpropan-2-ol), for which each of R, R', and R" is CH<sub>3</sub>. In these shorthands, R, R', and R" represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substituents" title="Substituents">substituents</a>, alkyl or other attached, generally organic groups.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkyl_chain_variations_in_alcohols">Alkyl chain variations in alcohols</span></h4>
<p>Short-chain alcohols have alkyl chains of 1–3 carbons. Medium-chain alcohols have alkyl chains of 4–7 carbons. Long-chain alcohols (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_alcohols" title="Fatty alcohols">fatty alcohols</a>) have alkyl chains of 8–21 carbons, and very long-chain alcohols have alkyl chains of 22 carbons or longer.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_alcohols">Simple alcohols</span></h4>
<p>"Simple alcohols" appears to be a completely undefined term. However, simple alcohols are often referred to by common names derived by adding the word "alcohol" to the name of the appropriate alkyl group. For instance, a chain consisting of one carbon (a methyl group, CH<sub>3</sub>) with an OH group attached to the carbon is called "methyl alcohol" while a chain of two carbons (an ethyl group, CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>3</sub>) with an OH group connected to the CH<sub>2</sub> is called "ethyl alcohol." For more complex alcohols, the IUPAC nomenclature must be used.</p>
<p>Simple alcohols, in particular ethanol and methanol, possess <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denaturing</a> and inert rendering properties, leading to their use as anti-microbial agents in medicine, pharmacy, and industry.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_alcohols">Higher alcohols</span></h4>
<p>Encyclopædia Britannica states, "The higher alcohols—those containing 4 to 10 carbon atoms—are somewhat viscous, or oily, and they have heavier fruity odours. Some of the highly branched alcohols and many alcohols containing more than 12 carbon atoms are solids at room temperature."</p>
<p>Like ethanol, <a href="/wiki/N-Butanol" title="N-Butanol">butanol</a> can be produced by fermentation processes. Saccharomyces yeast are known to produce these higher alcohols at temperatures above 75 °F (24 °C). The bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_acetobutylicum" title="Clostridium acetobutylicum">Clostridium acetobutylicum</a></i> can feed on <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> to produce butanol on an industrial scale.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>

<p>Alcohol has a long history of several uses worldwide. It is found in alcoholic beverages sold to adults, as fuel, and also has many scientific, medical, and industrial uses. The term <b>alcohol-free</b> is often used to describe a product that does not contain alcohol.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcoholic_beverages">Alcoholic beverages</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">Alcoholic beverages</a>, typically containing 3–40% <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> by volume, have been produced and consumed by humans since pre-historic times. Other alcohols such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-methyl-2-butanol" title="2-methyl-2-butanol">2-methyl-2-butanol</a> (found in <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Hydroxybutyric_acid" title="Gamma-Hydroxybutyric acid">γ-hydroxybutyric acid</a> (GHB) are also consumed by humans for their psychoactive effects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antifreeze">Antifreeze</span></h3>
<p>A 50% <a href="/wiki/Volume_fraction" title="Volume fraction">v/v</a> (by volume) solution of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> in water is commonly used as an <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiseptics">Antiseptics</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol can be used as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> to disinfect the skin before injections are given, often along with <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. Ethanol-based <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soaps</a> are becoming common in restaurants and are convenient because they do not require drying due to the volatility of the compound. Alcohol based gels have become common as <a href="/wiki/Hand_sanitizer" title="Hand sanitizer">hand sanitizers</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuels">Fuels</span></h3>
<p>Some alcohols, mainly <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, can be used as an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_fuel" title="Alcohol fuel">alcohol fuel</a>. Fuel performance can be increased in <a href="/wiki/Forced_induction" title="Forced induction">forced induction</a> <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> by injecting alcohol into the air intake after the <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supercharger" title="Supercharger">supercharger</a> has pressurized the air. This cools the pressurized air, providing a denser air charge, which allows for more fuel, and therefore more power.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservative">Preservative</span></h3>
<p>Alcohol is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservative</a> for <a href="/wiki/Biological_specimen" title="Biological specimen">specimens</a> in the fields of science and medicine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvents">Solvents</span></h3>
<p>Hydroxyl groups (-OH), found in alcohols, are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a> and therefore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> (water loving) but their carbon chain portion is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-polar" title="Non-polar">non-polar</a> which make them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a>. The molecule increasingly becomes overall more nonpolar and therefore less soluble in the polar water as the carbon chain becomes longer. Methanol has the shortest carbon chain of all alcohols (one carbon atom) followed by ethanol (two carbon atoms.)</p>
<p>Alcohols have applications in industry and science as reagents or <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>. Because of its relatively low toxicity compared with other alcohols and ability to dissolve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-polar" title="Non-polar">non-polar</a> substances, ethanol can be used as a solvent in medical drugs, <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfumes</a>, and vegetable essences such as <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, alcohols serve as versatile intermediates.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h2>


<p>Ethanol is thought to cause harm partly as a result of direct damage to DNA caused by its metabolites.</p>

<p>Ethanol's toxicity is largely caused by its primary metabolite, <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> (systematically ethanal) and secondary metabolite, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>. Many primary alcohols are metabolized into aldehydes then to carboxylic acids whose toxicities are similar to acetaldehyde and acetic acid. Metabolite toxicity is reduced in rats fed <a href="/wiki/Acetylcysteine" title="Acetylcysteine"><i>N</i>-acetylcysteine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">thiamine</a>.</p>
<p>Tertiary alcohols cannot be metabolized into aldehydes and as a result they cause no hangover or toxicity through this mechanism.</p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Alcohol#Systematic_names" title="Alcohol">secondary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol#Systematic_names" title="Alcohol">tertiary alcohols</a> are less poisonous than ethanol, because the liver is unable to metabolize them into toxic by-products. This makes them more suitable for pharmaceutical use as the chronic harms are lower. <a href="/wiki/Ethchlorvynol" title="Ethchlorvynol">Ethchlorvynol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tert-Amyl_alcohol" title="Tert-Amyl alcohol"><i>tert</i>-amyl alcohol</a> are tertiary alcohols which have seen both medicinal and recreational use.</p>
<p>Other alcohols are substantially more poisonous than ethanol, partly because they take much longer to be metabolized and partly because their metabolism produces substances that are even more toxic. Methanol (wood alcohol), for instance, is oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and then to the poisonous <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> in the liver by <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">alcohol dehydrogenase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Formaldehyde dehydrogenase">formaldehyde dehydrogenase</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, respectively; accumulation of formic acid can lead to blindness or death. Likewise, poisoning due to other alcohols such as <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diethylene_glycol" title="Diethylene glycol">diethylene glycol</a> are due to their metabolites, which are also produced by alcohol dehydrogenase.</p>
<p>Methanol itself, while poisonous (<a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub>50</sub></a> 5628 mg/kg, oral, rat), has a much weaker <a href="/wiki/Sedative" title="Sedative">sedative</a> effect than ethanol.</p>
<p>Isopropyl alcohol is oxidized to form acetone by <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">alcohol dehydrogenase</a> in the liver, but has occasionally been abused by <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholics</a>, leading to a range of adverse health effects.</p>
<p>Although the mechanism is unclear, a meta-analysis of 572 studies have shown increased cancer risk from alcohol.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span></h3>
<p>An effective treatment to prevent toxicity after methanol or ethylene glycol ingestion is to administer ethanol. Alcohol dehydrogenase has a higher affinity for ethanol, thus preventing methanol from binding and acting as a <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a>. Any remaining methanol will then have time to be excreted through the kidneys.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_chemical_properties">Physical and chemical properties</span></h2>
<p>Alcohols have an odor that is often described as "biting" and as "hanging" in the nasal passages. Ethanol has a slightly sweeter (or more fruit-like) odor than the other alcohols.</p>
<p>In general, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> makes the alcohol molecule <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_molecule" title="Polar molecule">polar</a>. Those groups can form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> to one another and to other compounds (except in <a href="/wiki/2,2,4,4-Tetramethyl-3-t-butyl-pentane-3-ol" title="2,2,4,4-Tetramethyl-3-t-butyl-pentane-3-ol">certain large molecules</a> where the hydroxyl is protected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steric_hindrance" title="Steric hindrance">steric hindrance</a> of adjacent groups). This hydrogen bonding means that alcohols can be used as <a href="/wiki/Protic_solvent" title="Protic solvent">protic solvents</a>. Two opposing solubility trends in alcohols are: the tendency of the polar OH to promote solubility in water, and the tendency of the carbon chain to resist it. Thus, methanol, ethanol, and propanol are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> in water because the hydroxyl group wins out over the short carbon chain. <a href="/wiki/N-Butanol" title="N-Butanol">Butanol</a>, with a four-carbon chain, is moderately soluble because of a balance between the two trends. Alcohols of five or more carbons such as <a href="/wiki/Amyl_alcohol" title="Amyl alcohol">pentanol</a> and higher are effectively insoluble in water because of the hydrocarbon chain's dominance. All simple alcohols are miscible in organic solvents.</p>
<p>Because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>, alcohols tend to have higher boiling points than comparable <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a>. The boiling point of the alcohol ethanol is 78.29 °C, compared to 69 °C for the hydrocarbon <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> (a common constituent of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>), and 34.6 °C for <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>.</p>
<p>Alcohols, like water, can show either acidic or basic properties at the -OH group. With a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pK<sub>a</sub></a> of around 16-19, they are, in general, slightly weaker <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>, but they are still able to react with strong bases such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a> or reactive metals such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salts</a> that result are called <b><a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a></b>, with the general formula <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">R</a>O <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">M</a>.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, the oxygen atom has <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pairs</a> of nonbonded electrons that render it weakly <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> in the presence of strong acids such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. For example, with methanol:</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methanol_acid_base.gif" title="Acidity &amp; basicity of methanol"><img alt="Acidity &amp; basicity of methanol" data-file-height="161" data-file-width="636" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Methanol_acid_base.gif/500px-Methanol_acid_base.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/Methanol_acid_base.gif 1.5x" width="500"/></a></p>
<p>Alcohols can also undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a>, or they can be dehydrated to <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. They can react to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ester_compound" title="Ester compound">ester compounds</a>, and they can (if activated first) undergo <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">nucleophilic substitution</a> reactions. The lone pairs of electrons on the oxygen of the hydroxyl group also makes alcohols <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophiles</a>. For more details, see the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol#Reactions" title="Alcohol">reactions of alcohols</a> section below.</p>
<p>As one moves from primary to secondary to tertiary alcohols with the same backbone, the hydrogen bond strength, the boiling point, and the acidity typically decrease.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_in_nature">Occurrence in nature</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ethanol#Natural_occurrence" title="Ethanol">Ethanol occurs naturally</a> as a byproduct of the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic process</a> of yeast. As such, ethanol will be present in any yeast habitat. Ethanol can commonly be found in overripe fruit.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methanol#Occurrence" title="Methanol">Methanol is produced naturally</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of many varieties of bacteria, and is commonly present in small amounts in the environment.</p>
<p>Alcohols have been found outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> in low densities in star and planetary-system-forming regions of space.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ziegler_and_oxo_processes">Ziegler and oxo processes</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ziegler_process" title="Ziegler process">Ziegler process</a>, linear alcohols are produced from ethylene and <a href="/wiki/Triethylaluminium" title="Triethylaluminium">triethylaluminium</a> followed by oxidation and hydrolysis. An idealized synthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-octanol" title="1-octanol">1-octanol</a> is shown:</p>

<p>The process generates a range of alcohols that are separated by <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>.</p>
<p>Many higher alcohols are produced by <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a> of alkenes followed by hydrogenation. When applied to a terminal alkene, as is common, one typically obtains a linear alcohol:</p>

<p>Such processes give <a href="/wiki/Fatty_alcohol" title="Fatty alcohol">fatty alcohols</a>, which are useful for detergents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydration_reactions">Hydration reactions</span></h3>
<p>Low molecular weight alcohols of industrial importance are produced by the addition of water to alkenes. Ethanol, isopropanol, 2-butanol, and tert-butanol are produced by this general method. Two implementations are employed, the direct and indirect methods. The direct method avoids the formation of stable intermediates, typically using acid catalysts. In the indirect method, the alkene is converted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate_ester" title="Sulfate ester">sulfate ester</a>, which is subsequently hydrolyzed. The direct <a href="/wiki/Hydration_reaction" title="Hydration reaction">hydration</a> using <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ethanol#Ethylene_hydration" title="Ethanol">ethylene hydration</a>) or other alkenes from <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> of fractions of distilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.</p>
<p>Hydration is also used industrially to produce the diol <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_oxide" title="Ethylene oxide">ethylene oxide</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_routes">Biological routes</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> using <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> produced from sugar from the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, in the presence of yeast and temperature of less than 37 °C to produce ethanol. For instance, such a process might proceed by the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Invertase" title="Invertase">invertase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, then the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Zymase" title="Zymase">zymase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (and carbon dioxide).</p>
<p>Several of the benign bacteria in the intestine use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> as a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_metabolism" title="Anaerobic metabolism">anaerobic metabolism</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> reaction produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> as a waste product, just like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>. Thus, human bodies contain some quantity of alcohol endogenously produced by these bacteria. In rare cases, this can be sufficient to cause "<a href="/wiki/Auto-brewery_syndrome" title="Auto-brewery syndrome">auto-brewery syndrome</a>" in which intoxicating quantities of alcohol are produced.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Substitution">Substitution</span></h4>
<p>Primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> react with aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">KOH</a> mainly to primary alcohols in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_aliphatic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic aliphatic substitution">nucleophilic aliphatic substitution</a>. (Secondary and especially tertiary alkyl halides will give the elimination (alkene) product instead). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagent" title="Grignard reagent">Grignard reagents</a> react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> groups to secondary and tertiary alcohols. Related reactions are the <a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier reaction</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nozaki-Hiyama_reaction" title="Nozaki-Hiyama reaction">Nozaki-Hiyama reaction</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction">Reduction</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldehydes" title="Aldehydes">Aldehydes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> are <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_borohydride" title="Sodium borohydride">sodium borohydride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">lithium aluminium hydride</a> (after an acidic workup). Another reduction by aluminiumisopropylates is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley_reduction" title="Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction">Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Noyori_asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation">Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation</a> is the asymmetric reduction of β-keto-esters.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysis">Hydrolysis</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">Alkenes</a> engage in an acid catalysed <a href="/wiki/Hydration_reaction" title="Hydration reaction">hydration reaction</a> using concentrated sulfuric acid as a catalyst that gives usually secondary or tertiary alcohols. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroboration-oxidation" title="Hydroboration-oxidation">hydroboration-oxidation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxymercuration-reduction" title="Oxymercuration-reduction">oxymercuration-reduction</a> of alkenes are more reliable in organic synthesis. Alkenes react with NBS and water in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halohydrin_formation_reaction" title="Halohydrin formation reaction">halohydrin formation reaction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">Amines</a> can be converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diazonium_salt" title="Diazonium salt">diazonium salts</a>, which are then hydrolyzed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deprotonation">Deprotonation</span></h3>
<p>Alcohols can behave as weak acids, undergoing <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a>. The deprotonation reaction to produce an <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxide</a> salt is performed either with a strong base such as sodium hydride or <a href="/wiki/N-Butyllithium" title="N-Butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> or with sodium or potassium metal.</p>



<p>Water is similar in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pK<sub>a</sub></a> to many alcohols, so with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> there is an <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> set-up, which usually lies to the left:</p>

<p>It should be noted, however, that the bases used to deprotonate alcohols are strong themselves. The bases used and the alkoxides created are both highly moisture-sensitive chemical reagents.</p>
<p>The acidity of alcohols is also affected by the overall stability of the alkoxide ion. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron-withdrawing_group" title="Electron-withdrawing group">Electron-withdrawing groups</a> attached to the carbon containing the hydroxyl group will serve to stabilize the alkoxide when formed, thus resulting in greater acidity. On the other hand, the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron-donating_group" title="Electron-donating group">electron-donating group</a> will result in a less stable alkoxide ion formed. This will result in a scenario whereby the unstable alkoxide ion formed will tend to accept a proton to reform the original alcohol.</p>
<p>With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> alkoxides give rise to <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Williamson_ether_synthesis" title="Williamson ether synthesis">Williamson ether synthesis</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleophilic_substitution">Nucleophilic substitution</span></h3>
<p>The OH group is not a good <a href="/wiki/Leaving_group" title="Leaving group">leaving group</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">nucleophilic substitution</a> reactions, so neutral alcohols do not react in such reactions. However, if the oxygen is first protonated to give R−OH<sub>2</sub>, the leaving group (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>) is much more stable, and the nucleophilic substitution can take place. For instance, tertiary alcohols react with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> to produce tertiary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> is replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> atom by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unimolecular_nucleophilic_substitution" title="Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution">unimolecular nucleophilic substitution</a>. If primary or secondary alcohols are to be reacted with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, an activator such as <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a> is needed. In alternative fashion, the conversion may be performed directly using <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_chloride" title="Thionyl chloride">thionyl chloride</a>.</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif" title="Some simple conversions of alcohols to alkyl chlorides"><img alt="Some simple conversions of alcohols to alkyl chlorides" data-file-height="209" data-file-width="1419" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/700px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/1050px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/1400px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif 2x" width="700"/></a></p>
<p>Alcohols may, likewise, be converted to alkyl bromides using <a href="/wiki/Hydrobromic_acid" title="Hydrobromic acid">hydrobromic acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_tribromide" title="Phosphorus tribromide">phosphorus tribromide</a>, for example:</p>

<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barton-McCombie_deoxygenation" title="Barton-McCombie deoxygenation">Barton-McCombie deoxygenation</a> an alcohol is deoxygenated to an <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotin" title="Organotin">tributyltin hydride</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoborane" title="Organoborane">trimethylborane</a>-water complex in a <a href="/wiki/Radical_substitution" title="Radical substitution">radical substitution</a> reaction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dehydration">Dehydration</span></h3>
<p>Alcohols are themselves nucleophilic, so R−OH<sub>2</sub> can react with ROH to produce <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a> and water in a <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydration reaction</a>, although this reaction is rarely used except in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>.</p>
<p>More useful is the E1 <a href="/wiki/Elimination_reaction" title="Elimination reaction">elimination reaction</a> of alcohols to produce <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. The reaction, in general, obeys <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaitsev%27s_Rule" title="Zaitsev's Rule">Zaitsev's Rule</a>, which states that the most stable (usually the most substituted) alkene is formed. Tertiary alcohols eliminate easily at just above room temperature, but primary alcohols require a higher temperature.</p>

<p>A more controlled elimination reaction is the <a href="/wiki/Chugaev_elimination" title="Chugaev elimination">Chugaev elimination</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodomethane" title="Iodomethane">iodomethane</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Esterification">Esterification</span></h3>
<p>To form an <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> from an alcohol and a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> the reaction, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer_esterification" title="Fischer esterification">Fischer esterification</a>, is usually performed at <a href="/wiki/Reflux" title="Reflux">reflux</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> of concentrated sulfuric acid:</p>

<p>In order to drive the equilibrium to the right and produce a good <a href="/wiki/Yield_(chemistry)" title="Yield (chemistry)">yield</a> of ester, water is usually removed, either by an excess of H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> or by using a <a href="/wiki/Dean-Stark_apparatus" title="Dean-Stark apparatus">Dean-Stark apparatus</a>. Esters may also be prepared by reaction of the alcohol with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_chloride" title="Acid chloride">acid chloride</a> in the presence of a base such as <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>.</p>
<p>Other types of ester are prepared in a similar manner –  for example, <a href="/wiki/Tosyl" title="Tosyl">tosyl</a> (tosylate) esters are made by reaction of the alcohol with p-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toluenesulfonyl" title="Toluenesulfonyl">toluenesulfonyl</a> chloride in pyridine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_oxidation" title="Alcohol oxidation">Alcohol oxidation</a></div>
<p>Primary alcohols (R-CH<sub>2</sub>-OH) can be oxidized either to <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> (R-CHO) or to <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> (R-CO<sub>2</sub>H), while the oxidation of secondary alcohols (RRCH-OH) normally terminates at the <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a> (RRC=O) stage. Tertiary alcohols (RRRC-OH) are resistant to oxidation.</p>
<p>The direct <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_of_primary_alcohols_to_carboxylic_acids" title="Oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids">oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids</a> normally proceeds via the corresponding aldehyde, which is transformed via an <i>aldehyde hydrate</i> (R-CH(OH)<sub>2</sub>) by reaction with water before it can be further oxidized to the carboxylic acid.</p>
<div class="center">

</div>
<p>Reagents useful for the transformation of primary alcohols to aldehydes are normally also suitable for the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_of_secondary_alcohols_to_ketones" title="Oxidation of secondary alcohols to ketones">oxidation of secondary alcohols to ketones</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Collins_reagent" title="Collins reagent">Collins reagent</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dess-Martin_periodinane" title="Dess-Martin periodinane">Dess-Martin periodinane</a>. The direct oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids can be carried out using <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jones_reagent" title="Jones reagent">Jones reagent</a>.</p>
