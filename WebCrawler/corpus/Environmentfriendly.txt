<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Environmentally friendly</b> or <b>environment-friendly</b>, (also referred to as <b>eco-friendly</b>, <b>nature-friendly</b>, and <b>green</b>) are marketing and sustainability terms referring to <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">laws</a>, guidelines and policies that inflict reduced, minimal, or no harm upon <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. Companies use these ambiguous terms to promote goods and services, sometimes with additional, more specific certifications, such as <a href="/wiki/Ecolabel" title="Ecolabel">ecolabels</a>. Their overuse can be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> has developed ISO 14020 and ISO 14024 to establish principles and procedures for environmental labels and declarations that certifiers and eco-labellers should follow. In particular, these standards relate to the avoidance of financial <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflicts of interest</a>, the use of sound <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific methods</a> and accepted test procedures, and <a href="/wiki/Openness" title="Openness">openness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(behavior)" title="Transparency (behavior)">transparency</a> in the setting of standards.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variants">Regional variants</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span></h3>


<p>Products located in members of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> can use the EU's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-label" title="Eco-label">Eco-label</a> pending the EU's approval. <a href="/wiki/Eco-Management_and_Audit_Scheme" title="Eco-Management and Audit Scheme">EMAS</a> is another EU label that signifies whether an organization management is green as opposed to the product. <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> also uses the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angel_(certification)" title="Blue Angel (certification)">Blue Angel</a>, based on Germany's standard.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_marketing" title="Environmental marketing">environmental marketing</a> claims require caution. Ambiguous titles such as <i>environmentally friendly</i> can be confusing without a specific definition; some regulators are providing guidance. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> has deemed some ecolabels misleading in determining whether a product is truly "green".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, one label is that of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Choice_Program" title="Environmental Choice Program">Environmental Choice Program</a>. Created in 1988, only products approved by the program are allowed to display the label.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span></h3>
<p>The Energy Rating Label is a Type III label that provides information on "energy service per unit of energy consumption". It was first created in 1986, but negotiations led to a redesign in 2000.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span></h3>

<p>The environmentally friendly trends are marketed with a different color association, using the color blue for clean air and clean water, as opposed to green in western cultures. Japanese and Korean built hybrid vehicles use the color blue instead of green all throughout the vehicle, and use the word "blue" indiscriminately.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> is a program with a primary goal of decreasing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions. Energy Star has different sections for different nations or areas, including the United States, the European Union  and Australia. The program, which was founded in the United States, also exists in Canada, Japan, New Zealand, and Taiwan.</p>
