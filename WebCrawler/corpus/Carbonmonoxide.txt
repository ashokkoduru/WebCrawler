<p><b>Carbon monoxide</b> (<b>CO</b>) is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas that is slightly less dense than air. It is toxic to <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobic</a> animals (including humans) when encountered in concentrations above about 35 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">ppm</a>, although it is also produced in normal animal metabolism in low quantities, and is thought to have some normal biological functions. In the atmosphere, it is spatially variable and short lived, having a role in the formation of ground-level <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.</p>
<p>Carbon monoxide consists of one <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom and one <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom, connected by a <a href="/wiki/Triple_bond" title="Triple bond">triple bond</a> that consists of two <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> as well as one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipolar_bond" title="Dipolar bond">dative</a> covalent bond. It is the simplest <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon" title="Oxocarbon">oxocarbon</a> and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> anion, the <a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">nitrosonium</a> cation and molecular <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a> the carbon monoxide <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">carbonyl</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (384–322 BC) first recorded that burning coals produced toxic fumes. An ancient method of execution was to shut the criminal in a bathing room with smoldering coals. What was not known was the mechanism of death. Greek physician <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> (129–199 AD) speculated that there was a change in the composition of the air that caused harm when inhaled. In 1776, the French chemist de Lassone produced CO by heating <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>, but mistakenly concluded that the gaseous product was <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, as it burned with a blue flame. The gas was identified as a compound containing <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> by the Scottish chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Cumberland_Cruikshank" title="William Cumberland Cruikshank">William Cumberland Cruikshank</a> in 1800. Its toxic properties on dogs were thoroughly investigated by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bernard" title="Claude Bernard">Claude Bernard</a> around 1846.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a gas mixture including carbon monoxide was used to keep <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a> running in parts of the world where <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a> were scarce. External (with a few exceptions) charcoal or <a href="/wiki/Wood_gas_generator" title="Wood gas generator">wood gas generators</a> were fitted, and the mixture of atmospheric nitrogen, carbon monoxide, and small amounts of other gases produced by <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> was piped to a gas mixer. The gas mixture produced by this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Wood_gas" title="Wood gas">wood gas</a>. Carbon monoxide was also used on a large scale during <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a> at some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extermination_camps_in_the_Holocaust" title="Extermination camps in the Holocaust">Nazi German extermination camps</a>, the most notable by <a href="/wiki/Gas_van" title="Gas van">gas vans</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelmno_extermination_camp" title="Chelmno extermination camp">Chelmno</a>, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_T4" title="Action T4">Action T4</a> "<a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a>" program.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span></h2>
<p>Carbon monoxide is produced from the partial oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-containing compounds; it forms when there is not enough oxygen to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), such as when operating a <a href="/wiki/Stove" title="Stove">stove</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> in an enclosed space. In the presence of oxygen, including atmospheric concentrations, carbon monoxide burns with a blue flame, producing carbon dioxide. <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">Coal gas</a>, which was widely used before the 1960s for domestic lighting, cooking, and heating, had carbon monoxide as a significant fuel constituent. Some processes in modern technology, such as <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">iron smelting</a>, still produce carbon monoxide as a byproduct.</p>
<p>Worldwide, the largest source of carbon monoxide is natural in origin, due to <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemical</a> reactions in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> that generate about 5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10 kilograms per year. Other natural sources of CO include volcanoes, forest fires, and other forms of combustion.</p>
<p>In biology, carbon monoxide is naturally produced by the action of <a href="/wiki/Heme_oxygenase" title="Heme oxygenase">heme oxygenase</a> 1 and 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> breakdown. This process produces a certain amount of <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">carboxyhemoglobin</a> in normal persons, even if they do not breathe any carbon monoxide. Following the first report that carbon monoxide is a normal <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> in 1993, as well as one of three gases that naturally modulate <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory responses</a> in the body (the other two being <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>), carbon monoxide has received a great deal of clinical attention as a biological regulator. In many tissues, all three gases are known to act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatories" title="Anti-inflammatories">anti-inflammatories</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilators" title="Vasodilators">vasodilators</a>, and promoters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neovascular" title="Neovascular">neovascular</a> growth. Clinical trials of small amounts of carbon monoxide as a drug are ongoing. Too much carbon monoxide causes <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">carbon monoxide poisoning</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_properties">Molecular properties</span></h2>
<p>Carbon monoxide has a <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass" title="Molar mass">molar mass</a> of 28.0, which makes it slightly lighter than air, whose average molar mass is 28.8. According to the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>, CO is therefore less dense than air.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bond</a> length between the carbon atom and the oxygen atom is 112.8 <a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">pm</a>. This bond length is consistent with a triple bond, as in molecular <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N<sub>2</sub>), which has a similar bond length and nearly the same <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a>. Carbon–oxygen double bonds are significantly longer, 120.8 pm in <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, for example. The boiling point (82 K) and melting point (68 K) are very similar to those of N<sub>2</sub> (77 K and 63 K, respectively). The bond dissociation energy of 1072 kJ/mol is stronger than that of N<sub>2</sub> (942 kJ/mol) and represents the strongest chemical bond known.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_state" title="Electronic state">electronic state</a> of carbon monoxide is a <a href="/wiki/Singlet_state" title="Singlet state">singlet state</a> since there are no unpaired electrons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding_and_dipole_moment">Bonding and dipole moment</span></h3>
<p>Carbon and oxygen together have a total of 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_shell" title="Valence shell">valence shell</a>. Following the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> for both carbon and oxygen, the two atoms form a <a href="/wiki/Triple_bond" title="Triple bond">triple bond</a>, with six shared electrons in three bonding molecular orbitals, rather than the usual double bond found in organic carbonyl compounds. Since four of the shared electrons come from the oxygen atom and only two from carbon, one bonding orbital is occupied by two electrons from oxygen, forming a dative or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dipolar_bond" title="Dipolar bond">dipolar bond</a>. This causes a C ← O <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polarization</a> of the molecule, with a small negative charge on carbon and a small positive charge on oxygen. The other two bonding orbitals are each occupied by one electron from carbon and one from oxygen, forming (polar) covalent bonds with a reverse C → O polarization, since oxygen is more <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegative</a> than carbon. In the free carbon monoxide, a net negative charge δ- remains at the carbon end and the molecule has a small <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">dipole moment</a> of 0.122 <a href="/wiki/Debye" title="Debye">D</a>.</p>
<p>The molecule is therefore asymmetric: oxygen has more electron density than carbon, and is also slightly positively charged compared to carbon being negative. By contrast, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> dinitrogen molecule has no dipole moment.</p>
<p>If carbon monoxide acts as a <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">ligand</a>, the polarity of the dipole may reverse with a net negative charge on the oxygen end, depending on the structure of the <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complex</a>. See also the section <i><a href="#Coordination_chemistry">"Coordination chemistry"</a></i> below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bond_polarity_and_oxidation_state">Bond polarity and oxidation state</span></h3>
<p>Theoretical and experimental studies show that, despite the greater electronegativity of oxygen, the dipole moment points from the more-negative carbon end to the more-positive oxygen end. The three bonds are in fact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_covalent_bond" title="Polar covalent bond">polar covalent bonds</a> that are strongly polarized. The calculated polarization toward the oxygen atom is 71% for the <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">σ-bond</a> and 77% for both <a href="/wiki/Pi_bond" title="Pi bond">π-bonds</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of carbon in carbon monoxide is +2 in each of these structures. It is calculated by counting all the bonding electrons as belonging to the more electronegative oxygen. Only the two non-bonding electrons on carbon are assigned to carbon. In this count, carbon then has only two valence electrons in the molecule compared to four in the free atom.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_and_physiological_properties">Biological and physiological properties</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">Carbon monoxide poisoning</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">Carbon monoxide poisoning</a> is the most common type of fatal air poisoning in many countries. Carbon monoxide is colorless, odorless, and tasteless, but highly toxic. It combines with <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">carboxyhemoglobin</a>, which usurps the space in hemoglobin that normally carries oxygen, but is ineffective for delivering oxygen to bodily tissues. Concentrations as low as 667 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_notation" title="Parts per notation">ppm</a> may cause up to 50% of the body's hemoglobin to convert to carboxyhemoglobin. A level of 50% carboxyhemoglobin may result in seizure, coma, and fatality. In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> limits long-term workplace exposure levels above 50 ppm. Within short time scales, carbon monoxide absorption is cumulative, since the half-life is about 5 hours in fresh air.</p>
<p>The most common symptoms of carbon monoxide poisoning may resemble other types of poisonings and infections, including symptoms such as <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dizziness" title="Dizziness">dizziness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">fatigue</a>, and a feeling of weakness. Affected families often believe they are victims of food poisoning. Infants may be irritable and feed poorly. Neurological signs include confusion, disorientation, visual disturbance, <a href="/wiki/Syncope_(medicine)" title="Syncope (medicine)">syncope</a> (fainting), and seizures.</p>
<p>Some descriptions of carbon monoxide poisoning include <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retinal</a> hemorrhages, and an abnormal cherry-red blood hue. In most clinical diagnoses these signs are seldom noticed. One difficulty with the usefulness of this cherry-red effect is that it corrects, or masks, what would otherwise be an unhealthy appearance, since the chief effect of removing deoxygenated hemoglobin is to make an asphyxiated person appear more normal, or a dead person appear more lifelike, similar to the effect of red colorants in embalming fluid. The "false" or unphysiologic red-coloring effect in anoxic CO-poisoned tissue is related to the meat-coloring commercial use of carbon monoxide, discussed below.</p>
<p>Carbon monoxide also binds to other molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">myoglobin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrial</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytochrome_oxidase" title="Cytochrome oxidase">cytochrome oxidase</a>. Exposures to carbon monoxide may cause significant damage to the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>, especially to the <a href="/wiki/Globus_pallidus" title="Globus pallidus">globus pallidus</a>, often with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequelae" title="Sequelae">long-term chronic pathological conditions</a>. Carbon monoxide may have severe adverse effects on the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> of a pregnant woman.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Normal_human_physiology">Normal human physiology</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is produced naturally by the human body as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signaling_molecule" title="Signaling molecule">signaling molecule</a>. Thus, carbon monoxide may have a physiological role in the body, such as a <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">blood vessel relaxant</a>. Because of carbon monoxide's role in the body, abnormalities in its metabolism have been linked to a variety of diseases, including neurodegenerations, hypertension, heart failure, and pathological inflammation. Relative to inflammation, carbon monoxide has been shown to inhibit the movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leukocyte" title="Leukocyte">leukocytes</a> to inflamed tissues, stimulate leukocyte <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a> of bacteria, and reduce the production of pro-inflammatory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytokines" title="Cytokines">cytokines</a> by leukocytes. In animal model studies, furthermore, carbon monoxide reduced the severity of experimentally induced bacterial <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a>, pancreatitis, hepatic ischemia/reperfusion injury, colitis, osteoarthritis, lung injury, lung transplantation rejection, and neuropathic pain while promoting skin wound healing. These actions are similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Specialized_pro-resolving_mediators" title="Specialized pro-resolving mediators">Specialized pro-resolving mediators</a> which act to dampen, reverse, and repair the tissue damage due to diverse inflammation responses. Indeed, carbon monoxide can act additively with one of these mediators (<a href="/wiki/Resolvin" title="Resolvin">Resolvin</a> D1) to limit inflammatory responses. The studies implicate carbon monoxide as a physiological contributor to limiting inflammation and suggest that its delivery by inhalation or carbon monoxide-forming drugs may be therapeutically useful for controlling pathological inflammatory responses.</p>
<p>CO functions as an endogenous signaling molecule, modulates functions of the cardiovascular system, inhibits blood platelet aggregation and adhesion, suppresses, reverses, and repairs the damage caused by inflammatory responses. It may play a role as potential therapeutic agent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbiology">Microbiology</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is a nutrient for <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogenic</a> archaea, a building-block for <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl coenzyme A</a>. This is the theme for the emerging field of <a href="/wiki/Bioorganometallic_chemistry" title="Bioorganometallic chemistry">bioorganometallic chemistry</a>. Extremophile micro-organisms can, thus, metabolise carbon monoxide in such locations as the thermal vents of volcanoes.</p>
<p>In bacteria, carbon monoxide is produced via the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of carbon dioxide by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase" title="Carbon monoxide dehydrogenase">carbon monoxide dehydrogenase</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>-<a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Ni</a>-<a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a>-containing protein.</p>
<p>CooA is a carbon monoxide sensor protein. The scope of its biological role is still unknown; it may be part of a signaling pathway in bacteria and archaea. Its occurrence in mammals is not established.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_presence">Atmospheric presence</span></h3>


<p>Carbon monoxide is present in small amounts in the atmosphere, chiefly as a product of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a> but also from natural and man-made fires (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">forest</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushfire" title="Bushfire">bushfires</a>, burning of <a href="/wiki/Crop_residue" title="Crop residue">crop residues</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> fire-cleaning). The burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> also contributes to carbon monoxide production. Carbon monoxide occurs dissolved in molten volcanic rock at high <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressures</a> in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. Because natural sources of carbon monoxide are so variable from year to year, it is extremely difficult to accurately measure natural emissions of the gas.</p>
<p>Carbon monoxide is a short-lived <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> and also has an indirect <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> effect by elevating concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">tropospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> through chemical reactions with other atmospheric constituents (e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a>, OH) that would otherwise destroy them. Through natural processes in the atmosphere, it is eventually oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Carbon monoxide is both short-lived in the atmosphere (on average about two months) and spatially variable in concentration.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">atmosphere of Venus</a> carbon monoxide occurs as a result of the photodissociation of carbon dioxide by electromagnetic radiation of wavelengths shorter than 169 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>.</p>
<p>Due to its long lifetime in the mid-troposphere, carbon monoxide is also used as tracer of transport for pollutant plumes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_pollution">Urban pollution</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is a temporary atmospheric pollutant in some urban areas, chiefly from the exhaust of internal combustion engines (including vehicles, portable and back-up generators, lawn mowers, power washers, etc.), but also from incomplete combustion of various other fuels (including wood, coal, charcoal, oil, paraffin, propane, natural gas, and trash).</p>
<p>Large CO pollution events can be observed from space over cities.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_ground-level_ozone_formation">Role in ground-level ozone formation</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">Tropospheric ozone</a></div>

NO<sub>2</sub> gives O(P) via photolysis, thereby forming O<sub>3</sub> following reaction with O<sub>2</sub>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_pollution">Indoor pollution</span></h3>
<p>In closed environments, the concentration of carbon monoxide can easily rise to lethal levels. On average, 170 people in the United States die every year from carbon monoxide produced by non-automotive consumer products. However, according to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Department_of_Health" title="Florida Department of Health">Florida Department of Health</a>, "every year more than 500 Americans die from accidental exposure to carbon monoxide and thousands more across the U.S. require emergency medical care for non-fatal carbon monoxide poisoning" These products include malfunctioning fuel-burning appliances such as furnaces, ranges, water heaters, and <a href="/wiki/Gas_heater" title="Gas heater">gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_heater" title="Kerosene heater">kerosene</a> room heaters; engine-powered equipment such as portable generators; fireplaces; and charcoal that is burned in homes and other enclosed areas. The American Association of Poison Control Centers (AAPCC) reported 15,769 cases of carbon monoxide poisoning resulting in 39 deaths in 2007. In 2005, the CPSC reported 94 generator-related carbon monoxide poisoning deaths. Forty-seven of these deaths were known to have occurred during power outages due to severe weather, including <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>. Still others die from carbon monoxide produced by non-consumer products, such as cars left running in attached garages. The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> estimates that several thousand people go to hospital emergency rooms every year to be treated for carbon monoxide poisoning.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presence_in_blood">Presence in blood</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is absorbed through breathing and enters the blood stream through gas exchange in the lungs. It is also produced in hemoglobin metabolism and enters the blood from the tissues, and thus is present in all normal tissues, even if not inhaled.</p>
<p>Normal circulating levels in the blood are 0% to 3%, and are higher in smokers. Carbon monoxide levels cannot be assessed through a physical exam. Laboratory testing requires a blood sample (arterial or venous) and laboratory analysis on a CO-Oximeter. Additionally, a noninvasive carboxyhemoglobin (SpCO) test method from Pulse CO-Oximetry exists and has been validated compared to invasive methods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrophysics">Astrophysics</span></h3>
<p>Outside of Earth, carbon monoxide is the second-most common molecule in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a>. Because of its asymmetry, the carbon monoxide molecule produces far brighter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines" title="Spectral lines">spectral lines</a> than the hydrogen molecule, making CO much easier to detect. Interstellar CO was first detected with <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a> in 1970. It is now the most commonly used tracer of molecular gas in general in the interstellar medium of galaxies, as molecular hydrogen can only be detected using ultraviolet light, which requires <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_telescopes" title="Space telescopes">space telescopes</a>. Carbon monoxide observations provide much of the information about the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular clouds</a> in which most <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">stars form</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Beta_Pictoris" title="Beta Pictoris">Beta Pictoris</a>, the second brightest <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Pictor" title="Pictor">Pictor</a>, shows an <a href="/wiki/Infrared_excess" title="Infrared excess">excess of infrared emission</a> compared to normal stars of its type, which is caused by large quantities of dust and gas (including carbon monoxide) near the star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<p>Many methods have been developed for carbon monoxide's production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_production">Industrial production</span></h3>
<p>A major industrial source of CO is <a href="/wiki/Producer_gas" title="Producer gas">producer gas</a>, a mixture containing mostly carbon monoxide and nitrogen, formed by combustion of carbon in air at high temperature when there is an excess of carbon. In an oven, air is passed through a bed of <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>. The initially produced CO<sub>2</sub> equilibrates with the remaining hot carbon to give CO. The reaction of CO<sub>2</sub> with carbon to give CO is described as the <a href="/wiki/Boudouard_reaction" title="Boudouard reaction">Boudouard reaction</a>. Above 800 °C, CO is the predominant product:</p>

<p>Another source is "<a href="/wiki/Water_gas" title="Water gas">water gas</a>", a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and carbon monoxide produced via the endothermic reaction of <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> and carbon:</p>

<p>Other similar "<a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">synthesis gases</a>" can be obtained from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and other fuels.</p>
<p>Carbon monoxide is also a byproduct of the reduction of metal <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> with carbon, shown in a simplified form as follows:</p>

<p>Carbon monoxide is also produced by the direct oxidation of carbon in a limited supply of oxygen or air.</p>

<p>Since CO is a gas, the reduction process can be driven by heating, exploiting the positive (favorable) <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> of reaction. The <a href="/wiki/Ellingham_diagram" title="Ellingham diagram">Ellingham diagram</a> shows that CO formation is favored over CO<sub>2</sub> in high temperatures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_preparation">Laboratory preparation</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is conveniently produced in the laboratory by the <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>, for example with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. Another method is heating an intimate mixture of powdered <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> metal and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, which releases CO and leaves behind <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_chemistry">Coordination chemistry</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyl</a></div>



<p>Most metals form <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a> containing covalently attached carbon monoxide. Only metals in lower oxidation states will complex with carbon monoxide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligands" title="Ligands">ligands</a>. This is because there must be sufficient electron density to facilitate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back-donation" title="Back-donation">back-donation</a> from the metal d<sub>xz</sub>-orbital, to the π*<a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">molecular orbital</a> from CO. The lone pair on the carbon atom in CO, also donates electron density to the d<sub>x²−y²</sub> on the metal to form a <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">sigma bond</a>. This electron donation is also exhibited with the <a href="/wiki/Cis_effect" title="Cis effect">cis effect</a>, or the labilization of CO ligands in the cis position. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl" title="Nickel carbonyl">Nickel carbonyl</a>, for example, forms by the direct combination of carbon monoxide and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> metal:</p>

<p>For this reason, nickel in any tubing or part must not come into prolonged contact with carbon monoxide. Nickel carbonyl decomposes readily back to Ni and CO upon contact with hot surfaces, and this method is used for the industrial purification of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a>.</p>
<p>In nickel carbonyl and other carbonyls, the electron pair on the carbon interacts with the metal; the carbon monoxide donates the electron pair to the metal. In these situations, carbon monoxide is called the <b>carbonyl</b> <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>. One of the most important metal carbonyls is <a href="/wiki/Iron_pentacarbonyl" title="Iron pentacarbonyl">iron pentacarbonyl</a>, Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub>:</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IronPentacarbonylStructure.png" title="Structure of iron pentacarbonyl."><img alt="Structure of iron pentacarbonyl." data-file-height="730" data-file-width="903" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/IronPentacarbonylStructure.png/150px-IronPentacarbonylStructure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/IronPentacarbonylStructure.png/225px-IronPentacarbonylStructure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/IronPentacarbonylStructure.png/300px-IronPentacarbonylStructure.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png" title="Iron pentacarbonyl."><img alt="Iron pentacarbonyl." data-file-height="1112" data-file-width="1011" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png/120px-Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png/180px-Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png/240px-Iron-pentacarbonyl-3D-balls.png 2x" width="120"/></a></p>
<p>Many metal-CO complexes are prepared by decarbonylation of organic solvents, not from CO. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Iridium(III)_chloride" title="Iridium(III) chloride">iridium trichloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triphenylphosphine" title="Triphenylphosphine">triphenylphosphine</a> react in boiling <a href="/wiki/2-Methoxyethanol" title="2-Methoxyethanol">2-methoxyethanol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dimethylformamide" title="Dimethylformamide">DMF</a> to afford <a href="/wiki/Vaska%27s_complex" title="Vaska's complex">IrCl(CO)(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></a>.</p>
<p>Metal carbonyls in coordination chemistry are usually studied using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy_of_metal_carbonyls" title="Infrared spectroscopy of metal carbonyls">infrared spectroscopy</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_and_main_group_chemistry">Organic and main group chemistry</span></h2>
<p>In the presence of strong acids and water, carbon monoxide reacts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a> to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acids" title="Carboxylic acids">carboxylic acids</a> in a process known as the Koch–Haaf reaction. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gattermann%E2%80%93Koch_reaction" title="Gattermann–Koch reaction">Gattermann–Koch reaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">arenes</a> are converted to <a href="/wiki/Benzaldehyde" title="Benzaldehyde">benzaldehyde</a> derivatives in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_chloride" title="Aluminium chloride">AlCl<sub>3</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">HCl</a>. Organolithium compounds (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl_lithium" title="Butyl lithium">butyl lithium</a>) react with carbon monoxide, but these reactions have little scientific use.</p>
<p>Although CO reacts with <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanions</a>, it is relatively nonreactive toward organic compounds without the intervention of metal catalysts.</p>
<p>With main group reagents, CO undergoes several noteworthy reactions. <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">Chlorination</a> of CO is the industrial route to the important compound <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>. With <a href="/wiki/Borane" title="Borane">borane</a> CO forms an adduct, H<sub>3</sub>BCO, which is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acylium" title="Acylium">acylium</a> cation [H<sub>3</sub>CCO]. CO reacts with <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> to give products resulting from C-C coupling such as <a href="/wiki/Acetylenediol" title="Acetylenediol">sodium acetylenediolate</a> 2<span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br/></span></span>·<span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>

<p>The compounds <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanehexone" title="Cyclohexanehexone">cyclohexanehexone</a> or triquinoyl (C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentanepentone" title="Cyclopentanepentone">cyclopentanepentone</a> or leuconic acid (C<sub>5</sub>O<sub>5</sub>), which so far have been obtained only in trace amounts, can be regarded as polymers of carbon monoxide.</p>
<p>At pressures of over 5 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">gigapascals</a>, carbon monoxide converts into a <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonyl" title="Polycarbonyl">solid polymer of carbon and oxygen</a>. This is metastable at atmospheric pressure but is a powerful explosive.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_industry">Chemical industry</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is an <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gas</a> that has many applications in bulk chemicals manufacturing. Large quantities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldehydes" title="Aldehydes">aldehydes</a> are produced by the <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a> reaction of <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, carbon monoxide, and H<sub>2</sub>. Hydroformylation is coupled to the <a href="/wiki/Shell_higher_olefin_process" title="Shell higher olefin process">Shell higher olefin process</a> to give precursors to <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">Phosgene</a>, useful for preparing isocyanates, polycarbonates, and polyurethanes, is produced by passing purified carbon monoxide and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas through a bed of porous <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>, which serves as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>. World production of this compound was estimated to be 2.74 million tonnes in 1989.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol</a> is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of carbon monoxide. In a related reaction, the hydrogenation of carbon monoxide is coupled to C-C bond formation, as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a> where carbon monoxide is hydrogenated to liquid hydrocarbon fuels. This technology allows <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or biomass to be converted to diesel.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Monsanto_process" title="Monsanto process">Monsanto process</a>, carbon monoxide and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> react in the presence of a homogeneous <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroiodic_acid" title="Hydroiodic acid">hydroiodic acid</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>. This process is responsible for most of the industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>.</p>
<p>An industrial scale use for pure carbon monoxide is purifying <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meat_coloring">Meat coloring</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide is used in <a href="/wiki/Modified_atmosphere" title="Modified atmosphere">modified atmosphere</a> packaging systems in the US, mainly with fresh meat products such as beef, pork, and fish to keep them looking fresh. The carbon monoxide combines with <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">myoglobin</a> to form carboxymyoglobin, a bright-cherry-red pigment. Carboxymyoglobin is more stable than the oxygenated form of myoglobin, oxymyoglobin, which can become oxidized to the brown pigment <a href="/wiki/Metmyoglobin" title="Metmyoglobin">metmyoglobin</a>. This stable red color can persist much longer than in normally packaged meat. Typical levels of carbon monoxide used in the facilities that use this process are between 0.4% to 0.5%.</p>
<p>The technology was first given "<a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>" (GRAS) status by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) in 2002 for use as a secondary packaging system, and does not require labeling. In 2004, the FDA approved CO as primary packaging method, declaring that CO does not mask spoilage odor. Despite this ruling, the process remains controversial for fears that it masks spoilage. In 2007, a bill was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> to label modified atmosphere carbon monoxide packaging as a color additive, but the bill died in subcommittee. The process is banned in many other countries, including Japan, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span></h3>
<p>In biology, carbon monoxide is naturally produced by the action of <a href="/wiki/Heme_oxygenase" title="Heme oxygenase">heme oxygenase</a> 1 and 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> breakdown. This process produces a certain amount of carboxyhemoglobin in normal persons, even if they do not breathe any carbon monoxide.</p>
<p>Following the first report that carbon monoxide is a normal neurotransmitter in 1993, as well as one of three gases that naturally modulate inflammatory responses in the body (the other two being <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>), carbon monoxide has received a great deal of clinical attention as a biological regulator. In many tissues, all three gases are known to act as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatories" title="Anti-inflammatories">anti-inflammatories</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilators" title="Vasodilators">vasodilators</a>, and encouragers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neovascular" title="Neovascular">neovascular</a> growth. However, the issues are complex, as neovascular growth is not always beneficial, since it plays a role in tumor growth, and also the damage from <i>wet</i> <a href="/wiki/Macular_degeneration" title="Macular degeneration">macular degeneration</a>, a disease for which smoking (a major source of carbon monoxide in the blood, several times more than natural production) increases the risk from 4 to 6 times.</p>
<p>There is a theory that, in some nerve cell <a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">synapses</a>, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_memories" title="Long-term memories">long-term memories</a> are being laid down, the receiving cell makes carbon monoxide, which back-transmits to the transmitting cell, telling it to transmit more readily in future. Some such nerve cells have been shown to contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guanylate_cyclase" title="Guanylate cyclase">guanylate cyclase</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> that is activated by carbon monoxide.</p>
<p>Studies involving carbon monoxide have been conducted in many laboratories throughout the world for its anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective properties. These properties have potential to be used to prevent the development of a series of pathological conditions including ischemia reperfusion injury, transplant rejection, atherosclerosis, severe sepsis, severe malaria, or autoimmunity. Clinical tests involving humans have been performed, however the results have not yet been released.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lasers">Lasers</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide has also been used as a <a href="/wiki/Active_laser_medium" title="Active laser medium">lasing medium</a> in high-powered infrared <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span></h3>
<p>Carbon monoxide has been proposed for use as a fuel on Mars. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide/oxygen_engine" title="Carbon monoxide/oxygen engine">Carbon monoxide/oxygen engines</a> have been suggested for early surface transportation use as both carbon monoxide and oxygen can be straightforwardly produced from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconia" title="Zirconia">zirconia</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>, without using any <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">Martian water resources</a> to obtain hydrogen, which would be needed to make methane or any hydrogen-based fuel.</p>
