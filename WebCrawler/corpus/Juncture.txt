<p><b>Juncture</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, is the manner of moving (transition) or mode of relationship between two consecutive sounds. It is the relationship between two successive <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a> in speech. A juncture is, formally, a <a href="/wiki/Segment_(linguistics)" title="Segment (linguistics)">suprasegmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemic</a> cue, a means by which a listener can distinguish between two otherwise identical sequences of sounds that have different meanings.</p>
<p></p>

<p>Other less common typologies exist, such as the division (favoured by American <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">Structuralist linguists</a> in the middle twentieth century) into <b>plus</b>, <b>single bar</b>, <b>double bar</b>, and <b>double cross</b> junctures, denoted <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/+/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/|/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/||/</span>, and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/#/</span> respectively. These correspond to syllabification and differences in intonation, single bar being a level pitch before a break, double bar being an upturn in pitch and a break, and double cross being a downturn in pitch that usually comes at the end of an utterance.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_from_English">Examples from English</span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oronyms">Oronyms</span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recreational_linguistics" title="Recreational linguistics">recreational linguistics</a>, an <i>oronym</i> is a pair of phrases which are <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophonic</a>. When pronounced without a pause between words (internal open juncture), phrases which differ in meaning and spelling may share a similar pronunciation. Examples include "ice cream" <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aɪs+kriːm/</span> and "I scream" <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aɪ+skriːm/</span> (This is employed in the chant "I scream; you scream; we all scream for ice cream" that is familiar to many English-speaking children.) The label <i>oronym</i> was suggested by <a href="/wiki/Gyles_Brandreth" title="Gyles Brandreth">Gyles Brandreth</a> and first published in his book <i>The Joy of Lex</i> (1980).</p>
