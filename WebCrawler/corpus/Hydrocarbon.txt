<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, a <b>hydrocarbon</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> consisting entirely of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, and thus are <a href="/wiki/Group_14_hydride" title="Group 14 hydride">group 14 hydrides</a>. Hydrocarbons from which one hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> has been removed are <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a>, called <b>hydrocarbyls</b>. <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">Aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (arenes), <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">cycloalkanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkyne</a>-based compounds are different types of hydrocarbons.</p>
<p>The majority of hydrocarbons found on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> naturally occur in <a href="/wiki/Petroleum#Crude_oil" title="Petroleum">crude oil</a>, where decomposed organic matter provides an abundance of carbon and hydrogen which, when bonded, can <a href="/wiki/Catenation" title="Catenation">catenate</a> to form seemingly limitless chains.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_hydrocarbons">Types of hydrocarbons</span></h2>
<p>The classifications for hydrocarbons, defined by <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry</a> are as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>Saturated hydrocarbons are the simplest of the hydrocarbon species. They are composed entirely of single bonds and are saturated with hydrogen. The formula for <a href="/wiki/Open-chain_compound" title="Open-chain compound">acyclic</a> saturated hydrocarbons (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a>) is C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+2</sub>. The most general form of saturated hydrocarbons is C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+2(1-<i>r</i>)</sub>, where <i>r</i> is the number of rings. Those with exactly one ring are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycloalkanes" title="Cycloalkanes">cycloalkanes</a>. Saturated hydrocarbons are the basis of petroleum fuels and are found as either linear or branched species. Substitution reaction is their characteristics property (like chlorination reaction to form chloroform). Hydrocarbons with the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> but different <a href="/wiki/Structural_formula" title="Structural formula">structural formulae</a> are called <a href="/wiki/Structural_isomer" title="Structural isomer">structural isomers</a>. As given in the example of 3-methylhexane and its higher <a href="/wiki/Homology_(chemistry)" title="Homology (chemistry)">homologues</a>, branched hydrocarbons can be <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a>. Chiral saturated hydrocarbons constitute the side chains of biomolecules such as <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">tocopherol</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unsaturated_hydrocarbon" title="Unsaturated hydrocarbon">Unsaturated hydrocarbons</a> have one or more double or triple bonds between carbon atoms. Those with double bond are called <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. Those with one double bond have the formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i></sub> (assuming non-cyclic structures). Those containing triple bonds are called <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a>, with general formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>âˆ’2</sub>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">Aromatic hydrocarbons</a>, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arene" title="Arene">arenes</a>, are hydrocarbons that have at least one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_ring" title="Aromatic ring">aromatic ring</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>Hydrocarbons can be <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>), <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxes</a> or low melting <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin wax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_properties">General properties</span></h3>
<p>Because of differences in molecular structure, the empirical formula remains different between hydrocarbons; in linear, or "straight-run" alkanes, alkenes and alkynes, the amount of bonded hydrogen lessens in alkenes and alkynes due to the "self-bonding" or catenation of carbon preventing entire saturation of the hydrocarbon by the formation of double or triple bonds.</p>
<p>This inherent ability of hydrocarbons to bond to themselves is known as <a href="/wiki/Catenation" title="Catenation">catenation</a>, and allows hydrocarbons to form more complex molecules, such as <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane" title="Cyclohexane">cyclohexane</a>, and in rarer cases, arenes such as <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>. This ability comes from the fact that the bond character between carbon atoms is entirely non-polar, in that the distribution of electrons between the two elements is somewhat even due to the same electronegativity values of the elements (~0.30), and does not result in the formation of an electrophile.</p>
<p>Generally, with catenation comes the loss of the total amount of bonded hydrocarbons and an increase in the amount of energy required for bond cleavage due to strain exerted upon the molecule; in molecules such as cyclohexane, this is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ring_strain" title="Ring strain">ring strain</a>, and occurs due to the "destabilized" spatial electron configuration of the atom.</p>
<p>In simple chemistry, as per <a href="/wiki/Valence_bond_theory" title="Valence bond theory">valence bond theory</a>, the carbon atom must follow the "<i>4-hydrogen rule</i>", which states that the maximum number of atoms available to bond with carbon is equal to the number of electrons that are attracted into the outer shell of carbon. In terms of shells, carbon consists of an incomplete outer shell, which comprises 4 electrons, and thus has 4 electrons available for covalent or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative_bonding" title="Dative bonding">dative bonding</a>.</p>
<p>Hydrocarbons are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> like <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>.</p>
<p>Some hydrocarbons also are abundant in the solar system. Lakes of liquid methane and ethane have been found on <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, Saturn's largest moon, confirmed by the Cassini-Huygens Mission. Hydrocarbons are also abundant in nebulae forming <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon</a> (PAH) compounds.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span></h2>

<p>Hydrocarbons are a primary energy source for current civilizations. The predominant use of hydrocarbons is as a combustible <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> source. In their solid form, hydrocarbons take the form of asphalt (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a>).</p>
<p>Mixtures of volatile hydrocarbons are now used in preference to the <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellant</a> for <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">aerosol sprays</a>, due to chlorofluorocarbons' impact on the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>) are gaseous at ambient temperatures and cannot be readily liquefied by pressure alone. <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">Propane</a> (C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>8</sub>) is however easily liquefied, and exists in 'propane bottles' mostly as a liquid. <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">Butane</a> (C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>) is so easily liquefied that it provides a safe, volatile fuel for small pocket lighters. <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">Pentane</a> (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>12</sub>) is a clear liquid at room temperature, commonly used in chemistry and industry as a powerful nearly odorless <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> of waxes and high molecular weight organic compounds, including greases. <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">Hexane</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>14</sub>) is also a widely used non-polar, non-aromatic solvent, as well as a significant fraction of common <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. The C through C alkanes, alkenes and isomeric cycloalkanes are the top components of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_naphtha" title="Petroleum naphtha">naphtha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> and specialized industrial solvent mixtures. With the progressive addition of carbon units, the simple non-ring structured hydrocarbons have higher viscosities, lubricating indices, boiling points, solidification temperatures, and deeper color. At the opposite extreme from methane lie the heavy tars that remain as the <i>lowest fraction</i> in a crude oil refining retort. They are collected and widely utilized as roofing compounds, pavement composition, wood preservatives (the <a href="/wiki/Creosote" title="Creosote">creosote</a> series) and as extremely high viscosity shear-resisting liquids.</p>
<p>Hydrocarbon use is also prevalent in nature. Some eusocial arthropods, such as the Brazilian stingless bee <i><a href="/wiki/Schwarziana_quadripunctata" title="Schwarziana quadripunctata">Schwarziana quadripunctata</a></i>, use unique hydrocarbon "scents" in order to determine kin from non-kin. The chemical hydrocarbon composition varies between age, sex, nest location, and hierarchal position.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poisoning">Poisoning</span></h2>
<p>Hydrocarbon poisoning such as that of benzene and petroleum usually occurs accidentally by inhalation or ingestion of these cytotoxic chemical compounds. Intravenous or subcutaneous injection of petroleum compounds with intent of suicide or abuse is an extraordinary event that can result in local damage or systemic toxicity such as tissue necrosis, abscess formation, respiratory system failure and partial damage to the kidneys, the brain and the nervous system. Moaddab and Eskandarlou report a case of chest wall necrosis and empyema resulting from attempting suicide by injection of petroleum into the pleural cavity.</p>

The hydrocarbon is burnt and the heat is used to heat water, which is then circulated. A similar principle is used to create electric energy in power plants.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum">Petroleum</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">Petroleum</a></div>

<p>Extracted hydrocarbons in a liquid form are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> (literally "rock oil") or <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a>, whereas hydrocarbons in a gaseous form are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. Petroleum and natural gas are found in the Earth's subsurface with the tools of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geology" title="Petroleum geology">petroleum geology</a> and are a significant source of fuel and raw materials for the production of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemicals</a>.</p>
<p>The extraction of liquid hydrocarbon fuel from <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basins</a> is integral to modern <a href="/wiki/Energy_development" title="Energy development">energy development</a>. Hydrocarbons are <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mined</a> from <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>, and potentially extracted from sedimentary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">methane hydrates</a>. These reserves require distillation and upgrading to produce <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude" title="Synthetic crude">synthetic crude</a> and petroleum.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">Oil reserves</a> in sedimentary rocks are the source of hydrocarbons for the energy, <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> industries.</p>
<p>Economically important hydrocarbons include <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, petroleum and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, and its derivatives such as <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> and oils. Hydrocarbons â€“ along with <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> â€“ contribute to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">tropospheric ozone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioremediation">Bioremediation</span></h4>
<p>Bacteria in the gabbroic layer of the ocean's crust can degrade hydrocarbons; but the extreme environment makes research difficult. Other bacteria such as <i>Lutibacterium anuloederans</i> can also degrade hydrocarbons. Mycoremediation or breaking down of hydrocarbon by mycellium and mushroom is possible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>Many hydrocarbons are highly flammable, therefore, care should be taken to prevent injury. Benzene and many aromatic compounds are possible <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>, and proper safety equipment must be worn to prevent these harmful compounds from entering the body. If hydrocarbons undergo combustion in tight areas, toxic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> can form. Hydrocarbons should be kept away from fluorine compounds due to the high probability of forming toxic <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>.</p>
