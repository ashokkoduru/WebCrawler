<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">For a discussion on how dietary fats affect cardiovascular health, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diet_and_heart_disease" title="Diet and heart disease">Diet and heart disease</a>.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> and nutrition, <b>monounsaturated fatty acids</b> (abbreviated <b>MUFAs</b>, or more plainly <b>monounsaturated fats</b>) are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a> that have one <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bond</a> in the fatty acid chain with all of the remainder <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> being <a href="/wiki/Single_bond" title="Single bond">single-bonded</a>. By contrast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated" title="Polyunsaturated">polyunsaturated</a> fatty acids (PUFAs) have more than one double bond.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">Fatty acids</a> are long-chained <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> having an <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> group at one end and a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> group at the other end. Fatty acid <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> (thickness) and <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting temperature</a> increases with decreasing number of double bonds; therefore, monounsaturated fatty acids have a higher melting point than polyunsaturated fatty acids (more double bonds) and a lower melting point than <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated</a> fatty acids (no double bonds). Monounsaturated fatty acids are liquids at room temperature and semisolid or solid when refrigerated.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_description_of_monounsaturated_fatty_acids">Molecular description of monounsaturated fatty acids</span></h2>
<p>Common monounsaturated fatty acids are <a href="/wiki/Palmitoleic_acid" title="Palmitoleic acid">palmitoleic acid</a> (16:1 n−7), cis-vaccenic acid (18:1 n−7) and <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a> (18:1 n−9). Palmitoleic acid has 16 carbon atoms with the first double bond occurring 7 carbon atoms away from the methyl group (and 9 carbons from the carboxyl end). It can be lengthened to the 18-carbon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cis-vaccenic_acid" title="Cis-vaccenic acid">cis-vaccenic acid</a>. Oleic acid has 18 carbon atoms with the first double bond occurring 9 carbon atoms away from the carboxylic acid group. The illustrations below show a molecule of oleic acid in Lewis formula and as a <a href="/wiki/Space-filling_model" title="Space-filling model">space-filling model</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_health">Relation to health</span></h2>
<p>Polyunsaturated fats protect against cardiovascular disease by providing more <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">membrane</a> fluidity than monounsaturated fats, but they are more vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Lipid_peroxidation" title="Lipid peroxidation">lipid peroxidation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">rancidity</a>). On the other hand, some monounsaturated fatty acids (in the same way as saturated fats) may promote <a href="/wiki/Insulin_resistance" title="Insulin resistance">insulin resistance</a>, whereas polyunsaturated fatty acids may be protective against insulin resistance. Furthermore, the large scale KANWU study found that increasing monounsaturated fat and decreasing saturated fat intake could improve insulin sensitivity, but only when the overall fat intake of the diet was low. Studies have shown that substituting dietary monounsaturated fat for saturated fat is associated with increased daily physical activity and resting energy expenditure. More physical activity was associated with a higher-oleic acid diet than one of a <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic acid</a> diet. From the study, it is shown that more monounsaturated fats lead to less anger and irritability.</p>
<p>Foods containing monounsaturated fats reduce <a href="/wiki/Low-density_lipoprotein" title="Low-density lipoprotein">low-density lipoprotein (LDL)</a> cholesterol, while possibly increasing <a href="/wiki/High-density_lipoprotein" title="High-density lipoprotein">high-density lipoprotein (HDL)</a> cholesterol. However, their true ability to raise HDL is still in debate.</p>
<p>Levels of <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic</a> along with other monounsaturated fatty acids in red blood cell membranes were positively associated with <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> risk. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saturation_index&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saturation index (page does not exist)">saturation index</a> (SI) of the same membranes was inversely associated with breast cancer risk. Monounsaturated fats and low SI in erythrocyte membranes are predictors of postmenopausal breast cancer. Both of these variables depend on the activity of the enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta-9_desaturase" title="Delta-9 desaturase">delta-9 desaturase (Δ9-d)</a>.</p>
<p>In children, consumption of monounsaturated oils is associated with healthier serum lipid profiles.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Diet" title="Mediterranean Diet">Mediterranean Diet</a> is one heavily influenced by monounsaturated fats. People in Mediterranean countries consume more total fat than Northern European countries, but most of the fat is in the form of monounsaturated fatty acids from olive oil and omega-3 fatty acids from fish, vegetables, and certain meats like lamb, while consumption of saturated fat is minimal in comparison. The diet in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> is fairly high in total fat (40% of total calories, almost exclusively provided by olive oil) yet affords a remarkable protection from coronary heart disease (and probably colon cancer) , but unlikely from <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer#Lifestyle" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> due to a very high <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid#Health_effects" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a> content.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_sources">Natural sources</span></h2>
<p>Monounsaturated fats are found in natural foods such as red <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, whole milk products, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a> and high fat fruits such as olives and <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocados</a>. <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">Olive oil</a> is about 75% monounsaturated fat. The high oleic variety sunflower oil contains as much as 85% monounsaturated fat. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canola_oil" title="Canola oil">Canola oil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cashews" title="Cashews">Cashews</a> are both about 58% monounsaturated fat. <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">Tallow</a> (beef fat) is about 50% monounsaturated fat and <a href="/wiki/Lard" title="Lard">lard</a> is about 40% monounsaturated fat. Other sources include <a href="/wiki/Avocado_oil" title="Avocado oil">avocado oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macadamia_nut_oil" title="Macadamia nut oil">macadamia nut oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_oil" title="Grape seed oil">grapeseed oil</a>, groundnut oil (<a href="/wiki/Peanut_oil" title="Peanut oil">peanut oil</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sesame_oil" title="Sesame oil">sesame oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_oil" title="Corn oil">corn oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Popcorn" title="Popcorn">popcorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grain</a> <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oatmeal" title="Oatmeal">oatmeal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safflower_oil" title="Safflower oil">safflower oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almond_oil" title="Almond oil">almond oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_oil" title="Sunflower oil">sunflower oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp_oil" title="Hemp oil">hemp oil</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tea-oil_Camellia" title="Tea-oil Camellia">tea-oil Camellia</a>.</p>
