<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>The <b>unit of analysis</b> is the major <a href="/wiki/Entity" title="Entity">entity</a> that is being <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Analysis" title="wikt:Analysis">analyzed</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">study</a>. It is the 'what' or 'who' that is being studied. In <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>, typical units of analysis include individuals (most common), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_(sociology)" title="Group (sociology)">groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_organization" title="Social organization">social organizations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_artifact" title="Social artifact">social artifacts</a>.</p>
<p>The literature of <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> provides a good example of units of analysis. In "Man, the State and War", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_N._Waltz" title="Kenneth N. Waltz">Kenneth N. Waltz</a> creates a tripartite analysis with three different units of analysis: the man (individual), the state (a group), and the international system (the system in which groups interact).</p>
<p>This is not to be confused with the <b><a href="/wiki/Unit_of_observation" title="Unit of observation">unit of observation</a></b>, which is the unit described by one's data (neighborhoods using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Census" title="U.S. Census">U.S. Census</a>, individuals using surveys, etc.). For example, a study may have a unit of observation at the individual level but may have the unit of analysis at the neighborhood level, drawing conclusions on neighborhood characteristics from data collected from individuals.</p>
