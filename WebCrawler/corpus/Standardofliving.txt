<p><b>Standard of living</b> refers to the level of <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>, comfort, material goods and necessities available to a certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_class" title="Socioeconomic class">socioeconomic class</a> in a certain geographic area. The standard of living includes factors such as income, quality and availability of <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a>, class disparity, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_rate" title="Poverty rate">poverty rate</a>, quality and affordability of housing, hours of work required to purchase necessities, <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflation_rate" title="Inflation rate">inflation rate</a>, amount of leisure time every year, affordable (or free) access to quality healthcare, quality and availability of <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, life expectancy, incidence of disease, cost of goods and services, infrastructure, national economic growth, economic and political stability, political and religious freedom, environmental quality, climate and safety. The standard of living is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> ranked the top six countries for quality of living as: <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span></h2>
<p>Standard of living is generally measured by standards such as real (i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflation_(economics)" title="Inflation (economics)">inflation</a> adjusted) income per person and poverty rate. Other measures such as access and quality of <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">income growth inequality</a>, and educational standards are also used. Examples are access to certain goods (such as number of refrigerators per 1000 people), or measures of health such as life expectancy. It is the ease by which people living in a time or place are able to satisfy their needs and/or wants.</p>
<p>The main idea of a 'standard' may be contrasted with the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>, which takes into account not only the material standard of living, but also other more intangible aspects that make up human life, such as leisure, safety, cultural resources, social life, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_health" title="Physical health">physical health</a>, environmental quality issues, etc. More complex means of measuring well-being must be employed to make such judgements, and these are very often political, thus controversial. Even between two nations or societies that have similar material standards of living, quality of life factors may in fact make one of these places more attractive to a given individual or group.</p>
