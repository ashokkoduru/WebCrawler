<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">

<p><b>Aston Martin Lagonda Limited</b> is a British manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Luxury_vehicle" title="Luxury vehicle">luxury</a> <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">sports cars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_tourer" title="Grand tourer">grand tourers</a>. It was founded in 1913 by Lionel Martin and Robert Bamford. The firm became associated with luxury grand touring cars in the 1950s and 1960s, and with the fictional character <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> following his use of a <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">DB5</a> model in the 1964 film <i><a href="/wiki/Goldfinger_(film)" title="Goldfinger (film)">Goldfinger</a></i>. The car is regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Culture of the United Kingdom">British cultural icon</a>.</p>
<p>The company has had a chequered financial history, including bankruptcy in the 1970s, but has also enjoyed long periods of success and stability, including under the ownership of <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_(entrepreneur)" title="David Brown (entrepreneur)">David Brown</a>, from 1947 to 1972 and of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> from 1994 to 2007.</p>
<p>In March 2007, a consortium of investors, led by <a href="/wiki/David_Richards_(racing)" title="David Richards (racing)">David Richards</a>, purchased 92% of Aston Martin for £479 million, with Ford retaining a £40 million stake. David Richards became chairman of Aston Martin. In December 2012, the Italian private equity fund Investindustrial signed a deal to buy 37.5% of Aston Martin, investing £150 million as a capital increase.</p>
<p></p>

<p></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>


<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span></h3>
<p>Aston Martin was founded in 1913 by Lionel Martin and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bamford" title="Robert Bamford">Robert Bamford</a>. The two had joined forces as Bamford &amp; Martin the previous year to sell cars made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer_(car)" title="Singer (car)">Singer</a> from premises in Callow Street, London where they also serviced <a href="/wiki/GWK_(car)" title="GWK (car)">GWK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calthorpe_cars" title="Calthorpe cars">Calthorpe</a> vehicles. Martin raced specials at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aston_Hill" title="Aston Hill">Aston Hill</a> near <a href="/wiki/Aston_Clinton" title="Aston Clinton">Aston Clinton</a>, and the pair decided to make their own vehicles. The first car to be named <i>Aston Martin</i> was created by Martin by fitting a four-cylinder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coventry-Simplex" title="Coventry-Simplex">Coventry-Simplex</a> engine to the chassis of a 1908 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotta-Fraschini" title="Isotta-Fraschini">Isotta-Fraschini</a>.</p>
<p>They acquired premises at Henniker Mews in <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a> and produced their first car in March 1915. Production could not start because of the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and Martin joined the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> and Bamford the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Army_Service_Corps" title="Royal Army Service Corps">Royal Army Service Corps</a>. All machinery was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Sopwith_Aviation_Company" title="Sopwith Aviation Company">Sopwith Aviation Company</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inter_war_years">Inter war years</span></h3>
<p>After the war, the company was refounded at Abingdon Road, Kensington and a new car designed to carry the Aston-Martin name. Bamford left in 1920 and the company was revitalised with funding from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Count_Louis_Zborowski" title="Count Louis Zborowski">Count Louis Zborowski</a>. In 1922, Bamford &amp; Martin produced cars to compete in the <a href="/wiki/French_Grand_Prix" title="French Grand Prix">French Grand Prix</a>, which went on to set world speed and endurance records at <a href="/wiki/Brooklands" title="Brooklands">Brooklands</a>. Three works Team Cars with 16-valve <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Bla_5mr.jpg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="File:Bla 5mr.jpg (page does not exist)">twin cam engines</a> were built for racing and record breaking: chassis number 1914, later developed as the Green Pea; chassis number 1915, the <a href="/wiki/Razor_Blade" title="Razor Blade">Razor Blade</a> record car; and chassis number 1916, later developed as the <a href="/wiki/Halford_Special" title="Halford Special">Halford Special</a>.</p>
<p>Approximately 55 cars were built for sale in two configurations, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Side_valve.jpg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="File:Side valve.jpg (page does not exist)">long chassis</a> and short chassis. The company went bankrupt in 1924 and was bought by Lady Charnwood, who put her son John Benson on the board. The company failed again in 1925 and the factory closed in 1926, with Lionel Martin leaving.</p>
<p>Later that year, Bill Renwick, <a href="/wiki/A_C_Bertelli" title="A C Bertelli">Augustus (Bert) Bertelli</a> and investors which included Lady Charnwood took control of the company. They renamed it Aston Martin Motors and moved it to the former Whitehead Aircraft Limited works in <a href="/wiki/Feltham" title="Feltham">Feltham</a>. Renwick and Bertelli had been in partnership some years and had developed an overhead-cam four-cylinder engine using Renwick's patented combustion chamber design, which they had tested in an <a href="/wiki/Enfield-Allday" title="Enfield-Allday">Enfield-Allday</a> chassis. The only "Renwick and Bertelli" motor car made, it was known as "Buzzbox" and still survives.</p>
<p>The pair had planned to sell their engine to motor manufacturers, but having heard that the Aston Martin was no longer in production realised they could capitalise on its reputation to jump start the production of a completely new car.</p>
<p>Between 1926 and 1937 Bertelli was both technical director and designer of all new Aston Martins, since known as "Bertelli cars". They included the 1½-litre "T-type", "International", "Le Mans", "MKII" and its racing derivative, the "Ulster", and the 2-litre 15/98 and its racing derivative, the "Speed Model". Most were open two-seater sports cars bodied by Bert Bertelli's brother <a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Bertelli_coachwork" title="commons:Category:Bertelli coachwork">Enrico (Harry)</a>, with a small number of long-chassis four-seater tourers, dropheads and saloons also produced.</p>
<p>Bertelli was a competent driver keen to race his cars, one of few owner/manufacturer/drivers. The "LM" team cars were very successful in national and international motor racing including at Le Mans and the Mille Miglia.</p>
<p>Financial problems reappeared in 1932. The company was rescued for a year by L. Prideaux Brune before passing it on to Sir Arthur Sutherland. In 1936, Aston Martin decided to concentrate on road cars, producing just 700 until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> halted work. Production shifted to aircraft components during the war.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Brown_era">David Brown era</span></h3>

<p>In 1947, tractor manufacturer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Brown_Limited" title="David Brown Limited">David Brown Limited</a> bought the company under the leadership of managing director <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_(entrepreneur)" title="David Brown (entrepreneur)">Sir David Brown</a>—its "post-war saviour". The company also acquired <a href="/wiki/Lagonda" title="Lagonda">Lagonda</a> that year for its 2.6-litre <a href="/wiki/W._O._Bentley" title="W. O. Bentley">W. O. Bentley</a>-designed engine. Both companies shared resources and workshops, leading to the classic "DB" series of cars. In 1950, the company announced the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2" title="Aston Martin DB2">DB2</a>, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2/4" title="Aston Martin DB2/4">DB2/4</a> in 1953, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2/4" title="Aston Martin DB2/4">DB2/4 MkII</a> in 1955, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB_Mark_III" title="Aston Martin DB Mark III">DB Mark III</a> in 1957 and the Italian-styled 3.7 L <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB4" title="Aston Martin DB4">DB4</a> in 1958.</p>
<p>While these models helped Aston Martin establish a good racing pedigree, the DB4 stood out and yielded the famous <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">DB5</a> in 1963. The company stayed true to its emerging "<a href="/wiki/Grand_tourer" title="Grand tourer">grand touring</a>" style with the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB6" title="Aston Martin DB6">DB6</a> (1965–70), and <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DBS" title="Aston Martin DBS">DBS</a> (1967–1972).</p>
<p>The six-cylinder engines of these cars from 1954 up to 1965 were designed by <a href="/wiki/Tadek_Marek" title="Tadek Marek">Tadek Marek</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s.E2.80.94changing_ownership">1970s—changing ownership</span></h3>
<p>The Aston Martin company was often financially troubled. In 1972, the firm was sold to Company Developments, a <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>-based consortium chaired by <a href="/wiki/William_Wilson_(businessman)" title="William Wilson (businessman)">William Wilson</a>, MBE.</p>
<p>The company was resold in 1975 by its receiver following a further bankruptcy to North American businessmen Peter Sprague and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Minden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Minden (page does not exist)">George Minden</a> for £1.05 million. A successful turn-around strategy led to the recruitment of 360 new employees and, by 1977, a trading profit of £750,000. The new owners pushed the company into modernising its line, producing the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Vantage_(1977)" title="Aston Martin V8 Vantage (1977)">V8 Vantage</a> in 1977, the convertible <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8" title="Aston Martin V8">Volante</a> in 1978, and the one-off <a href="/wiki/William_Towns" title="William Towns">William Towns</a>-styled <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Bulldog" title="Aston Martin Bulldog">Bulldog</a> in 1980. Towns also styled the futuristic new <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Lagonda" title="Aston Martin Lagonda">Lagonda</a> saloon, based on the V8 model.</p>
<p>In 1980 Aston-Martin sought to buy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MG_(car)" title="MG (car)">MG</a>, planning to design a new model and offering their take on an updated 1981 model <a href="/wiki/MG_MGB" title="MG MGB">MGB</a>. The acquisition never developed, as the company was badly hit by the economic contraction of the early 1980s. Worldwide sales shrank to three cars per week, prompting chairman Alan Curtis, Sprague, and Minden to consider shutting down production to concentrate on service and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive_restoration" title="Automotive restoration">restoration</a>. At this point Curtis attended the 1980 Pace sponsored <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Moss" title="Stirling Moss">Stirling Moss</a> benefit day at <a href="/wiki/Brands_Hatch" title="Brands Hatch">Brands Hatch</a>, and met fellow <a href="/wiki/Farnham" title="Farnham">Farnham</a> resident <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gauntlett" title="Victor Gauntlett">Victor Gauntlett</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s.E2.80.94Victor_Gauntlett">1980s—Victor Gauntlett</span></h3>
<p>Gauntlett bought a 12.5% stake in Aston Martin for £500,000 via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pace_Petroleum" title="Pace Petroleum">Pace Petroleum</a> in 1980, with Tim Hearley of CH Industrials taking a similar share. Pace and CHI took over as joint 50/50 owners at the beginning of 1981, with Gauntlett as executive chairman. Gauntlett also led the sales team, and after some development and publicity when it became the world's fastest 4-seater production car, was able to sell the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Lagonda" title="Aston Martin Lagonda">Aston Martin Lagonda</a> in Oman, Kuwait, and Qatar.</p>
<p>In 1982, Aston Martin was granted a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Warrant_of_Appointment_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Warrant of Appointment (United Kingdom)">Royal Warrant of Appointment</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>. The company holds the warrant to this day.</p>
<p>Understanding that it would take some time to develop new Aston Martin products, they created an engineering service subsidiary to develop automotive products for other companies. It was decided to use the name of the coachbuilding company <a href="/wiki/Tickford" title="Tickford">Tickford</a> which Aston Martin had owned since 1955, the name being already associated with quality cars in the public perception. Products included a Tickford <a href="/wiki/Austin_Metro" title="Austin Metro">Austin Metro</a>, a Tickford <a href="/wiki/Ford_Capri" title="Ford Capri">Ford Capri</a> and even Tickford train interiors, particularly on the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XJS" title="Jaguar XJS">Jaguar XJS</a>. Pace continued sponsoring racing events, and now sponsored all Aston Martin Owners Club events, taking a Tickford-engined Nimrod Group C car owned by AMOC President <a href="/wiki/Viscount_Downe" title="Viscount Downe">Viscount Downe</a>, which came third in the Manufacturers Championship in both 1982 and 1983. It also finished seventh in the <a href="/wiki/1982_24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="1982 24 Hours of Le Mans">1982 24 Hours of Le Mans</a> race. However, sales of production cars were now at an all-time low of 30 cars produced in 1982.</p>
<p>As trading became tighter in the petroleum market, and Aston Martin was requiring more time and money, Gauntlett agreed to sell Hays/Pace to the Kuwait Investment Office in September 1983. As Aston Martin required greater investment, he also agreed to sell his share holding to American importer and Greek shipping tycoon <a href="/wiki/Peter_Livanos" title="Peter Livanos">Peter Livanos</a>, who invested via his joint venture company with Nick and John Papanicolaou, ALL Inc. Gauntlett remained chairman of the AML company 55% owned by ALL, with Tickford a 50/50 venture between ALL and CHI. The uneasy relationship was ended when ALL exercised options to buy a larger share in AML; CHI's residual shares were exchanged for CHI's complete ownership of Tickford, which retained development of existing Aston Martin projects. In 1984, Titan the main shipping company of the Papanicolaou's was in trouble, so Livanos's father George bought out the Papanicolaou's shares in ALL, while Gauntlett again became a shareholder with a 25% holding in AML. The deal valued Aston Martin/AML at £2 million, the year it built its 10,000th car.</p>
<p>Although as a result Aston Martin had to make 60 members of the workforce redundant, Gauntlett bought a stake in Italian styling house <a href="/wiki/Zagato" title="Zagato">Zagato</a>, and resurrected its collaboration with Aston Martin.</p>

<p>In 1986, Gauntlett negotiated the return of fictional British secret agent <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> to Aston Martin. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubby_Broccoli" title="Cubby Broccoli">Cubby Broccoli</a> had chosen to recast the character using actor <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Dalton" title="Timothy Dalton">Timothy Dalton</a>, in an attempt to re-root the Bond-brand back to a more <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a>-like feel. Gauntlett supplied his personal pre-production <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Vantage_(1977)" title="Aston Martin V8 Vantage (1977)">Vantage</a> for use in the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Living_Daylights" title="The Living Daylights">The Living Daylights</a></i>, and sold a Volante to Broccoli for use at his home in America. Gauntlett turned down the role of a <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> colonel in the film, however: "I would have loved to have done it but really could not afford the time."</p>
<p>The company needed funds to survive in the long term. In May 1987, Gauntlett and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Michael_of_Kent" title="Prince Michael of Kent">Prince Michael of Kent</a> were staying at the home of Contessa Maggi, the wife of the founder of the original <a href="/wiki/Mille_Miglia" title="Mille Miglia">Mille Miglia</a>, while watching the revival event. Another house guest was <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hayes" title="Walter Hayes">Walter Hayes</a>, vice-President of <a href="/wiki/Ford_of_Europe" title="Ford of Europe">Ford of Europe</a>. Despite problems over the previous acquisition of <a href="/wiki/AC_Cars" title="AC Cars">AC Cars</a>, Hayes saw the potential of the brand and the discussion resulted in <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> taking a share holding in September 1987. In 1988, having produced some 5,000 cars in 20 years, a revived economy and successful sales of limited edition <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Vantage" title="Aston Martin Vantage">Vantage</a>, and 52 <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Zagato" title="Aston Martin V8 Zagato">Volante Zagato</a> coupes at £86,000 each; the company finally retired the ancient V8 and introduced the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Virage" title="Aston Martin Virage">Virage</a> range—the first new Aston launched in 20 years.</p>
<p>Although Gauntlett was contractually to stay as chairman for two years, his racing interests took Aston back into sports car racing in 1989 with limited European success. However, with engine rule changes for the 1990 season and the launch of the new <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Volante" title="Aston Martin Volante">Aston Martin Volante</a> model, Ford provided the limited supply of <a href="/wiki/Cosworth" title="Cosworth">Cosworth</a> engines to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaguar_cars" title="Jaguar cars">Jaguar cars</a> racing team. As the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB7" title="Aston Martin DB7">"small Aston" DB7</a> would require a large engineering input, Ford agreed to take full control of Aston Martin, and Gauntlett handed over the company chairmanship to Hayes in 1991. In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Virage#Vantage" title="Aston Martin Virage">Vantage</a> version was announced, and the following year the company renewed the DB range by announcing the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB7" title="Aston Martin DB7">DB7</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ford_era">Ford era</span></h3>
<p>Ford placed Aston in the <a href="/wiki/Premier_Automotive_Group" title="Premier Automotive Group">Premier Automotive Group</a>, invested in new manufacturing and ramped up production. In 1994, Ford opened a new factory at Banbury Road in <a href="/wiki/Bloxham" title="Bloxham">Bloxham</a>. In 1995, the company produced a record 700 vehicles. Until the Ford era, cars had been produced by hand coachbuilding craft methods, such as the <a href="/wiki/English_wheel" title="English wheel">English wheel</a>. In 1998 the 2,000th DB7 was built, and in 2002 the 6,000th, exceeding production of all previous DB models. The DB7 range was boosted by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB7" title="Aston Martin DB7">V12 Vantage</a> models in 1999, and in 2001 the company introduced the V12-engined <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Vanquish" title="Aston Martin Vanquish">Aston Martin Vanquish</a>.</p>
<p>At the North American International Auto Show in Detroit, Michigan in 2003, Aston Martin introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Vantage_(2005)" title="Aston Martin V8 Vantage (2005)">AMV8 Vantage</a> concept car. Expected to have few changes before its introduction in 2005, the Vantage brought back the classic V8 engine to allow the company to compete in a larger market. 2003 also saw the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Gaydon" title="Gaydon">Gaydon</a> factory, the first purpose-built factory in Aston Martin's history. Also introduced in 2003 was the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB9" title="Aston Martin DB9">DB9</a> coupé, which replaced the ten-year-old <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB7" title="Aston Martin DB7">DB7</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">convertible</a> version of the DB9, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DB9_Volante" title="DB9 Volante">DB9 Volante</a>, was introduced at the 2004 Detroit Auto Show.</p>
<p>In October 2004, the company set up the dedicated 12,500 square metres (135,000 sq ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Engine_Plant" title="Aston Martin Engine Plant">AMEP</a> engine production plant within the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Germany" title="Ford Germany">Ford Germany</a> <a href="/wiki/Niehl,_Cologne" title="Niehl, Cologne">Niehl, Cologne</a> plant. With capacity to produce up to 5,000 engines a year by 100 specially trained personnel, like traditional Aston Martin engine production from Newport Pagnell, assembly of each unit is entrusted to a single technician from a pool of 30, with V8 and V12 variants assembled in under 20 hours. By bringing engine production back to within the company, the promise was that Aston Martin would be able to produce small runs of higher performance variants engines. This expanded engine capacity allowed in 2006, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Vantage_(2005)" title="Aston Martin V8 Vantage (2005)">V8 Vantage</a> sports car to enter production at the Gaydon factory, joining the DB9 and DB9 Volante.</p>
<p>In December 2003 Aston Martin announced it would return to motor racing in 2005. A new division was created, called <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Racing" title="Aston Martin Racing">Aston Martin Racing</a>, which became responsible, together with <a href="/wiki/Prodrive" title="Prodrive">Prodrive</a>, for the design, development, and management of the DBR9 program. The DBR9 competes in the GT class in <a href="/wiki/Sports_car_racing" title="Sports car racing">sports car races</a>, including the world-famous <a href="/wiki/24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="24 Hours of Le Mans">24 Hours of Le Mans</a>.</p>
<p>In 2006, an internal audit led Ford to consider divesting itself of parts of its <a href="/wiki/Premier_Automotive_Group" title="Premier Automotive Group">Premier Automotive Group</a>. After suggestions of selling <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Cars" title="Jaguar Cars">Jaguar Cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Volvo_Cars" title="Volvo Cars">Volvo Cars</a> were weighed, Ford announced in August 2006 it had engaged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UBS_AG" title="UBS AG">UBS AG</a> to sell all or part of Aston Martin at auction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007.E2.80.94the_Richards_era">2007—the Richards era</span></h3>
<p>On 12 March 2007, a consortium led by <a href="/wiki/Prodrive" title="Prodrive">Prodrive</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/David_Richards_(racing)" title="David Richards (racing)">David Richards</a> purchased Aston Martin for £475m (US$848m). The group included American investment banker John Singers and two <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwaiti</a> companies, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Investment_Dar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Investment Dar (page does not exist)">Investment Dar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adeem_Investment" title="Adeem Investment">Adeem Investment</a>; Prodrive had no financial involvement in the deal. Ford kept a stake in the company valued at £40m (US$70m).</p>
<p>To demonstrate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_V8_Vantage_(2005)" title="Aston Martin V8 Vantage (2005)">V8 Vantage</a>'s durability across hazardous terrain and promote the car in China, the first east-west crossing of the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Highway_Network#First_car_crossing" title="Asian Highway Network">Asian Highway</a> was undertaken between June and August 2007. A pair of Britons drove 12,089 km (7,512 miles) from Tokyo to <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> before joining the European motorway network for another 3,259 km (2,025 miles) to London. The promotion was so successful the company opened dealerships in Shanghai and Beijing within three months.</p>
<p>On 19 July 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Newport_Pagnell" title="Newport Pagnell">Newport Pagnell</a> plant rolled out the last of nearly 13,000 cars made there since 1955, a Vanquish S. The Tickford Street facility was converted to Aston Martin's service and restoration department. UK production is now concentrated at Gaydon on the former RAF V-bomber airfield. In March 2008 the company announced a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Magna_Steyr" title="Magna Steyr">Magna Steyr</a> to outsource manufacture of over 2,000 cars annually to Graz, Austria, reassuringly stating: "The continuing growth and success of the company is based upon Gaydon as the focal point and heart of the business, with the design and engineering of all Aston Martin products continuing to be carried out there."</p>
<p>More dealers in Europe and the new pair in China brought the total to 120 in 28 countries.</p>
<p>On 1 September 2008, Aston Martin announced the revival of the <a href="/wiki/Lagonda" title="Lagonda">Lagonda</a> marque, proposing a concept to be shown in 2009 to coincide with the brand's 100th anniversary. The first production cars are slated for 2012.</p>
<p>In December 2008, Aston Martin announced it would cut its workforce from 1,850 to 1,250.</p>
<p>The first four-door <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Rapide" title="Aston Martin Rapide">Aston Martin Rapide</a> sports cars rolled out of the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Steyr" title="Magna Steyr">Magna Steyr</a> factory in <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>, Austria in 2010. The contract manufacturer provides dedicated facilities to ensure compliance with the exacting standards of Aston Martin and other marques, including <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a>. Ulrich Bez has publicly speculated about outsourcing all of Aston Martin's operations with the exception of marketing. In September 2011 it was announced Rapide production would be returned to Gaydon in the second half of 2012, restoring all manufacture there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012.E2.80.94Investindustrial_stakeholding_and_new_chief_executive_officer">2012—Investindustrial stakeholding and new chief executive officer</span></h3>
<p>In late 2012, Investment Dar reviewed its stake, with <a href="/wiki/Mahindra_%26_Mahindra" title="Mahindra &amp; Mahindra">Mahindra &amp; Mahindra</a> emerging as a potential bidder for as much as half of Aston Martin. Instead, Italian private equity fund <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Investindustrial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Investindustrial (page does not exist)">Investindustrial</a> signed a deal on 6 December 2012 to buy 37.5% of Aston Martin, investing £150 million as a capital increase. This was confirmed by Aston Martin in a press release on 7 December 2012. In April 2013 it was reported that Dr Ulrich Bez would be leaving his role as chief executive officer to take up a more ambassadorial position widely seen as the first move by the new shareholders in reviewing the leadership and strategy of the company. On 2 September 2014, Aston Martin announced they had appointed the <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> executive <a href="/wiki/Andy_Palmer" title="Andy Palmer">Andy Palmer</a> as their new CEO with Ulrich Bez retaining a position as Non-Executive Chairman. As sales had been declining, from 2015 Aston Martin sought new customers (particularly wealthy female buyers) with cars like Lagonda and DBX while releasing concepts cars like the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Vulcan" title="Aston Martin Vulcan">Vulcan</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Mercedes-AMG">Relationship with Mercedes-AMG</span></h3>
<p>In 2013 Aston Martin signed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a> to supply the next generation Aston Martin cars with new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercedes_AMG" title="Mercedes AMG">Mercedes-AMG</a> engines. Daimler AG now owns 5% of Aston Martin. Mercedes-AMG will also supply Aston Martin with electrical systems. This technical partnership will support Aston Martin’s launch of a new generation of models that will incorporate new technology and V8s. The first model to sport Mercedes technology is the DB11, announced at the 2016 Geneva Motor, sporting Mercedes electronics for the entertainment, navigation and other systems.</p>
