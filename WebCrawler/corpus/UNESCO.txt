<p>The <b>United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization</b> (<b>UNESCO</b>) (In French: Organisation des Nations unies pour l'éducation, la science et la culture) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_specialized_agencies_of_the_United_Nations" title="List of specialized agencies of the United Nations">specialized agency</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN) based in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. Its declared purpose is to contribute to peace and security by promoting international collaboration through educational, scientific, and cultural reforms in order to increase universal respect for justice, the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> along with fundamental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_(political)" title="Freedom (political)">freedom</a> proclaimed in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a>. It is the heir of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>' <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_on_Intellectual_Cooperation" title="International Committee on Intellectual Cooperation">International Committee on Intellectual Cooperation</a>.</p>
<p>UNESCO has <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_UNESCO" title="Member states of UNESCO">195 member states</a> and nine associate members. Most of its field offices are "cluster" offices covering three or more countries; national and regional offices also exist.</p>
<p>UNESCO pursues its objectives through five major programs: education, <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social</a>/<a href="/wiki/Human_science" title="Human science">human sciences</a>, culture and communication/information. Projects sponsored by UNESCO include <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a>, technical, and teacher-training programmes, international science programmes, the promotion of independent media and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a>, regional and cultural history projects, the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_diversity" title="Cultural diversity">cultural diversity</a>, translations of world literature, international cooperation agreements to secure the world <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_heritage" title="Natural heritage">natural heritage</a> (<a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a>) and to preserve human rights, and attempts to bridge the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_digital_divide" title="Global digital divide">worldwide digital divide</a>. It is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Group" title="United Nations Development Group">United Nations Development Group</a>.</p>
<p>UNESCO's aim is "to contribute to the building of peace, the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">eradication of poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> and intercultural dialogue through education, the sciences, culture, communication and information". Other priorities of the organization include attaining quality <a href="/wiki/Education_For_All" title="Education For All">Education For All</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lifelong_learning" title="Lifelong learning">lifelong learning</a>, addressing emerging social and ethical challenges, fostering <a href="/wiki/Cultural_diversity" title="Cultural diversity">cultural diversity</a>, a culture of peace and building inclusive knowledge societies through information and communication.</p>
<p>The broad goals and concrete objectives of the international community – as set out in the internationally agreed development goals, including the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs) – underpin all UNESCO's strategies and activities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>UNESCO and its mandate for international co-operation can be traced back to the League of Nations resolution on 21 September 1921, to elect a Commission to study feasibility. On 18 December 1925, the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Education" title="International Bureau of Education">International Bureau of Education</a> (IBE) began work as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental" title="Non-governmental">non-governmental</a> organization in the service of international educational development. However, the work of these predecessor organizations was largely interrupted by the onset of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>After the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Charter" title="Atlantic Charter">Atlantic Charter</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_United_Nations" title="Declaration of the United Nations">Declaration of the United Nations</a>, the Conference of Allied Ministers of Education (CAME) began meetings in London which continued between 16 November 1942 to 5 December 1945. On 30 October 1943, the necessity for an international organization was expressed in the Moscow Declaration, agreed upon by China, the United Kingdom, the United States and the USSR. This was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton_Oaks_Conference" title="Dumbarton Oaks Conference">Dumbarton Oaks Conference</a> proposals of 9 October 1944. Upon the proposal of CAME and in accordance with the recommendations of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_International_Organization" title="United Nations Conference on International Organization">United Nations Conference on International Organization</a> (UNCIO), held in San Francisco in April–June 1945, a United Nations Conference for the establishment of an educational and cultural organization (ECO/CONF) was convened in London 1–16 November 1945 with 44 governments represented. A prominent figure in the initiative for UNESCO was <a href="/wiki/Rab_Butler" title="Rab Butler">Rab Butler</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Education" title="Minister of Education">Minister of Education</a> for the United Kingdom. At the ECO/CONF, the Constitution of UNESCO was introduced and signed by 37 countries, and a Preparatory Commission was established. The Preparatory Commission operated between 16 November 1945, and 4 November 1946—the date when UNESCO's Constitution came into force with the deposit of the twentieth ratification by a member state.</p>
<p>The first General Conference took place from 19 November to 10 December 1946, and elected Dr. <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">Julian Huxley</a> to the post of Director-General. The Constitution was amended in November 1954 when the General Conference resolved that members of the Executive Board would be representatives of the governments of the States of which they are nationals and would not, as before, act in their personal capacity. This change in governance distinguished UNESCO from its predecessor, the CICI, in terms of how member states would work together in the organization's fields of competence. As member states worked together over time to realize UNESCO's mandate, political and historical factors have shaped the organization's operations in particular during the Cold War, the decolonization process, and the dissolution of the USSR.</p>
<p>Among the major achievements of the organization is its work against racism, for example through influential <a href="/wiki/The_Race_Question" title="The Race Question">statements on race</a> starting with a declaration of anthropologists (among them was <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>) and other scientists in 1950 and concluding with the 1978 <a href="/wiki/The_Race_Question#Legacy_and_other_UNESCO_statements" title="The Race Question">Declaration on Race and Racial Prejudice</a>. In 1956, the Republic of South Africa withdrew from UNESCO claiming that some of the organization's publications amounted to "interference" in the country's "racial problems." South Africa rejoined the organization in 1994 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a>.</p>
<p>UNESCO's early work in the field of education included the pilot project on fundamental education in the Marbial Valley, Haiti, started in 1947. This project was followed by expert missions to other countries, including, for example, a mission to Afghanistan in 1949. In 1948, UNESCO recommended that Member States should make free primary education compulsory and universal. In 1990 the World Conference on Education for All, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jomtien" title="Jomtien">Jomtien</a>, Thailand, launched a global movement to provide basic education for all children, youths and adults. Ten years later, the 2000 <a href="/wiki/World_Education_Forum" title="World Education Forum">World Education Forum</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, Senegal, led member governments to commit to achieving basic education for all by 2015.</p>
<p>UNESCO's early activities in the field of culture included, for example, the Nubia Campaign, launched in 1960. The purpose of the campaign was to move the <a href="/wiki/Great_and_Small_Temples_of_Abu_Simbel" title="Great and Small Temples of Abu Simbel">Great Temple of Abu Simbel</a> to keep it from being swamped by the Nile after construction of the <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">Aswan Dam</a>. During the 20-year campaign, 22 monuments and architectural complexes were relocated. This was the first and largest in a series of campaigns including <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a> (Pakistan), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fes" title="Fes">Fes</a> (Morocco), <a href="/wiki/Kathmandu" title="Kathmandu">Kathmandu</a> (Nepal), <a href="/wiki/Borobudur" title="Borobudur">Borobudur</a> (Indonesia) and the <a href="/wiki/Acropolis" title="Acropolis">Acropolis</a> (Greece). The organization's work on heritage led to the adoption, in 1972, of the Convention concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage. The <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a> was established in 1976 and the first sites inscribed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_List" title="World Heritage List">World Heritage List</a> in 1978. Since then important legal instruments on cultural heritage and diversity have been adopted by UNESCO member states in 2003 (Convention for the Safeguarding of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intangible_Cultural_Heritage" title="Intangible Cultural Heritage">Intangible Cultural Heritage</a>) and 2005 (<a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Protection_and_Promotion_of_the_Diversity_of_Cultural_Expressions" title="Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions">Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions</a>).</p>
<p>An intergovernmental meeting of UNESCO in Paris in December 1951 led to the creation of the European Council for Nuclear Research, which was responsible for establishing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Organization_for_Nuclear_Research" title="European Organization for Nuclear Research">European Organization for Nuclear Research</a> (CERN) in 1954.</p>
<p>Arid Zone programming, 1948–1966, is another example of an early major UNESCO project in the field of natural sciences. In 1968, UNESCO organized the first intergovernmental conference aimed at reconciling the environment and development, a problem which continues to be addressed in the field of sustainable development. The main outcome of the 1968 conference was the creation of UNESCO's <a href="/wiki/Man_and_the_Biosphere_Programme" title="Man and the Biosphere Programme">Man and the Biosphere Programme</a>.</p>
<p>In the field of communication, the free flow of information has been a priority for UNESCO from its beginnings. In the years immediately following World War II, efforts were concentrated on reconstruction and on the identification of needs for means of mass communication around the world. UNESCO started organizing training and education for journalists in the 1950s. In response to calls for a "<a href="/wiki/New_World_Information_and_Communication_Order" title="New World Information and Communication Order">New World Information and Communication Order</a>" in the late 1970s, UNESCO established the International Commission for the Study of Communication Problems, which produced the 1980 <a href="/wiki/MacBride_report" title="MacBride report">MacBride report</a> (named after the Chair of the Commission, the Nobel Peace Prize laureate <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_MacBride" title="Seán MacBride">Seán MacBride</a>). Following the MacBride report, UNESCO introduced the Information Society for All programme and Toward Knowledge Societies programme in the lead up to the World Summit on the Information Society in 2003 (<a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>) and 2005 (<a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>).</p>
<p>In 2011, Palestine became a UNESCO member following a vote in which 107 member states supported and 14 opposed. Laws passed in the United States in 1990 and 1994 mean that it cannot contribute financially to any UN organisation that accepts Palestine as a full member. As a result, it withdrew its funding which accounted for about 22% of UNESCO's budget. Israel also reacted to Palestine's admittance to UNESCO by freezing Israel payments to the UNESCO and imposing sanctions to the <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_National_Authority" title="Palestinian National Authority">Palestinian Authority</a>, claiming that Palestine's admittance would be detrimental "to potential peace talks". Two years after they stopped paying their dues to UNESCO, US and Israel lost UNESCO voting rights in 2013.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span></h2>
<p>UNESCO and its specialized institutions issue a number of magazines.</p>
<p><i>The UNESCO Courier</i> magazine states its mission to "promote UNESCO’s ideals, maintain a platform for the dialogue between cultures and provide a forum for international debate." Since March 2006 it is available online, with limited printed issues. Its articles express the opinions of the authors which are not necessarily the opinions of UNESCO. As of 2016, the latest issue posted was October–December 2011.</p>
<p>In 1950 UNESCO initiated the quarterly review <i>Impact of Science on Society</i> (also known as <i>Impact</i>) to discuss the influence of science on society., UNESCO also publish museum international quarterly from the year 1948.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_World_Information_and_Communication_order">New World Information and Communication order</span></h3>
<p>UNESCO has been the centre of controversy in the past, particularly in its relationships with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. During the 1970s and 1980s, UNESCO's support for a "<a href="/wiki/New_World_Information_and_Communication_Order" title="New World Information and Communication Order">New World Information and Communication Order</a>" and its <a href="/wiki/MacBride_report" title="MacBride report">MacBride report</a> calling for democratization of the media and more egalitarian access to information was condemned in these countries as attempts to curb <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a>. UNESCO was perceived by some as a platform for communists and Third World dictators to attack the West, a stark contrast to accusations made by the USSR in the late 1940s and early 1950s. In 1984, the United States withheld its contributions and withdrew from the organization in protest, followed by the United Kingdom in 1985. Singapore took the opportunity to withdraw also at the end of 1985, citing rising membership fees. Following a change of government in 1997, the UK rejoined. The United States rejoined in 2003, followed by Singapore on 8 October 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> was admitted to UNESCO in 1949, one year after its creation. Israel has maintained its membership since 1949. In 2010, Israel designated the <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_the_Patriarchs" title="Cave of the Patriarchs">Cave of the Patriarchs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebron" title="Hebron">Hebron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rachel%27s_Tomb" title="Rachel's Tomb">Rachel's Tomb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a> as <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Heritage_Sites_of_Israel" title="List of National Heritage Sites of Israel">National Heritage Sites</a> and announced restoration work, prompting criticism from the United States and protests from Palestinians. In October 2010, UNESCO's Executive Board voted to declare the sites as "al-Haram al-Ibrahimi/Tomb of the Patriarchs" and "Bilal bin Rabah Mosque/Rachel's Tomb" and stated that they were "an integral part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupied_Palestinian_Territories" title="Occupied Palestinian Territories">occupied Palestinian Territories</a>" and any unilateral Israeli action was a violation of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. UNESCO described the sites as significant to "people of the Muslim, Christian and Jewish traditions", and accused Israel of highlighting only the Jewish character of the sites. Israel in turn accused UNESCO of "detach[ing] the Nation of Israel from its heritage", and accused it of being politically motivated. The <a href="/wiki/Shmuel_Rabinovitch" title="Shmuel Rabinovitch">Rabbi of the Western Wall</a> claimed that Rachel's tomb had not previously been declared a holy Muslim site. Israel partially suspended ties with UNESCO. Israeli Deputy Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Danny_Ayalon" title="Danny Ayalon">Danny Ayalon</a> declared that the resolution was a "part of Palestinian escalation". <a href="/wiki/Zevulun_Orlev" title="Zevulun Orlev">Zevulun Orlev</a>, chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Knesset" title="Knesset">Knesset</a> Education and Culture Committee, referred to the resolutions as an attempt to undermine the mission of UNESCO as a scientific and cultural organization that promotes cooperation throughout the world.</p>
<p>On 28 June 2011, UNESCO's World Heritage Committee, at <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>'s insistence, censured Israel's decision to demolish and rebuild the <a href="/wiki/Mughrabi-Bridge" title="Mughrabi-Bridge">Mughrabi Gate</a> Bridge in Jerusalem for safety reasons. Israel stated that Jordan had signed an agreement with Israel stipulating that the existing bridge must be dismantled for safety reasons; Jordan disputed the agreement, saying that it was only signed under U.S. pressure. Israel was also unable to address the UNESCO committee over objections from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.</p>
<p>In January 2014, days before it was scheduled to open, UNESCO Director-General, <a href="/wiki/Irina_Bokova" title="Irina Bokova">Irina Bokova</a>, "indefinitely postponed" and effectively cancelled an exhibit created by the <a href="/wiki/Simon_Wiesenthal_Center" title="Simon Wiesenthal Center">Simon Wiesenthal Center</a> entitled, "The People, The Book, The Land: The 3,500-year relationship between the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_and_Judaism_in_the_Land_of_Israel" title="History of the Jews and Judaism in the Land of Israel">Jewish people and the Land of Israel</a>." The event was scheduled to run from 21 January through 30 January in Paris. Bokova cancelled the event after representatives of Arab states at UNESCO argued that its display would "harm the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab-Israeli_peace_process" title="Arab-Israeli peace process">peace process</a>". The author of the exhibition, Professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Wistrich" title="Robert Wistrich">Robert Wistrich</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_University" title="Hebrew University">Hebrew University</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidal_Sassoon_International_Center_for_the_Study_of_Anti-Semitism" title="Vidal Sassoon International Center for the Study of Anti-Semitism">Vidal Sassoon International Center for the Study of Anti-Semitism</a>, called the cancellation an "appalling act," and characterized Bokova's decision as "an arbitrary act of total cynicism and, really, contempt for the Jewish people and its history." UNESCO amended the decision to cancel the exhibit within the year, and it quickly achieved popularity and was viewed as a great success.</p>
<p>On 13 October 2016, UNESCO passed a resolution on East Jerusalem that condemned Israel for "aggressions" by Israeli police and soldiers and "illegal measures" against the freedom of worship and Muslims' access to their holy sites, while also recognizing Israel as the occupying power. Palestinian leaders welcomed the decision. While the text acknowledged the "importance of the Old City of Jerusalem and its walls for the three monotheistic religions", it referred to the sacred hilltop compound in Jerusalem's Old City only by its Muslim name "Al-Haram al-Sharif", Arabic for Noble Sanctuary. In response, Israel denounced the UNESCO resolution for its omission of the words "Temple Mount" stating that it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_denial" title="Temple denial">denies Jewish ties to the key holy site</a>. After receiving criticism from numerous Israeli politicians and diplomats, including <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Netanyahu" title="Benjamin Netanyahu">Benjamin Netanyahu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayelet_Shaked" title="Ayelet Shaked">Ayelet Shaked</a>, Israel froze all ties with the organization. The resolution was condemned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ban_ki-Moon" title="Ban ki-Moon">Ban ki-Moon</a> and the Director-General of UNESCO, Irina Bokova, who said that Judaism, Islam and Christianity have clear historical connections to Jerusalem and "to deny, conceal or erase any of the Jewish, Christian or Muslim traditions undermines the integrity of the site. Al-Aqsa Mosque is also Temple Mount, whose Western Wall is the holiest place in Judaism." It was also rejected by the Czech Parliament which said the resolution reflects a "hateful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Israel" title="Anti-Israel">anti-Israel</a> sentiment", and hundreds of Italian Jews demonstrated in Rome over Italy abstention. On October 26, UNESCO approved a reviewed version of the resolution, which also criticized Israel for its continuous "refusal to let the body's experts access Jerusalem's holy sites to determine their conservation status." Despite containing some softening of language following Israeli protests over a previous version, Israel continued to denounce the text. The resolution refers to the site Jews and Christians refer to as the Temple Mount only by its Arab name — a significant semantic decision also adopted by UNESCO's executive board last week, triggering condemnation from Israel and its allies. U.S. Ambassador Crystal Nix Hines stated: "This item should have been defeated. These politicized and one-sided resolutions are damaging the credibility of UNESCO." In November 2016, the City Council of <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_California" title="Beverly Hills, California">Beverly Hills, California</a> passed a resolution to condemn the UNESCO resolution.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palestinian_Authority">Palestinian Authority</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palestinian_Youth_Magazine_controversy">Palestinian Youth Magazine controversy</span></h4>
<p>In February 2011, an article was published in a Palestinian youth magazine in which a teenage girl described one of her four role-models as <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>. In December 2011, UNESCO, which partly funded the magazine, condemned the material and subsequently withdrew support.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_University_of_Gaza_controversy">Islamic University of Gaza controversy</span></h4>
<p>In 2012, UNESCO decided to establish a chair at the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_University_of_Gaza" title="Islamic University of Gaza">Islamic University of Gaza</a> in the field of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">space sciences</a>, fueling much controversy and criticism. <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Israel)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Israel)">foreign ministry</a> criticized the move and stated that the university supports <a href="/wiki/Hamas" title="Hamas">Hamas</a> (which Israel and other countries designate as a terrorist organization) and houses bomb laboratories for Hamas.</p>
<p>The university has been linked to Hamas in the past. However, the university head, <a href="/wiki/Kamalain_Shaath" title="Kamalain Shaath">Kamalain Shaath</a>, defended UNESCO, stating that "the Islamic University is a purely academic university that is interested only in education and its development". Israeli ambassador to UNESCO Nimrod Barkan planned to submit a letter of protest with information about the university's ties to Hamas, especially angry that this was the first Palestinian university that UNESCO chose to cooperate with. The Jewish organization <a href="/wiki/B%27nai_B%27rith" title="B'nai B'rith">B'nai B'rith</a> criticized the move as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wikileaks">Wikileaks</span></h3>
<p>On 16 and 17 February 2012 UNESCO held a conference entitled, "The Media World after <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> and <a href="/wiki/News_of_the_World" title="News of the World">News of the World</a>." Despite all six panels being focused on WikiLeaks, no member of WikiLeaks staff were invited to speak. After receiving a complaint from WikiLeaks spokesman <a href="/wiki/Kristinn_Hrafnsson" title="Kristinn Hrafnsson">Kristinn Hrafnsson</a>, UNESCO invited him to attend, but did not offer a place on any panels. The offer also came only a week before the conference, which was held in Paris, France. Many of the speakers featured, including <a href="/wiki/David_Leigh_(journalist)" title="David Leigh (journalist)">David Leigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heather_Brooke" title="Heather Brooke">Heather Brooke</a>, had spoken out openly against WikiLeaks and its founder <a href="/wiki/Julian_Assange" title="Julian Assange">Julian Assange</a> in the past. WikiLeaks released a press statement on 15 February 2012 denouncing UNESCO which stated, "UNESCO has made itself an international human rights joke. To use "freedom of expression" to censor WikiLeaks from a conference about WikiLeaks is an Orwellian absurdity beyond words."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Che_Guevara">Che Guevara</span></h3>
<p>In 2013, UNESCO announced that the collection "The Life and Works of Ernesto <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a>" became part of the <a href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Programme" title="Memory of the World Programme">Memory of the World Register</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ileana_Ros-Lehtinen" title="Ileana Ros-Lehtinen">Ileana Ros-Lehtinen</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">US congresswoman</a>, condemned this decision, saying that the organization acts against its own ideals:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This decision is more than an insult to the families of those Cubans who were lined up and <a href="/wiki/Summary_execution" title="Summary execution">summarily executed</a> by Che and his merciless cronies it also serves as a direct contradiction to the UNESCO ideals of encouraging peace and universal respect for human rights.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/UN_Watch" title="UN Watch">UN Watch</a>, also condemned this selection by UNESCO.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Listing_Nanjing_Massacre_documents">Listing Nanjing Massacre documents</span></h3>
<p>In 2015, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> threatened to halt funding for UNESCO over the organization's decision to include documents relating to the 1937 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanjing_massacre" title="Nanjing massacre">Nanjing massacre</a> in the latest listing for its "Memory of the World" program. In October 2016, Japanese Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Fumio_Kishida" title="Fumio Kishida">Fumio Kishida</a> confirmed that Japan's 2016 annual funding of ¥4.4 billion had been suspended although denied any direct link with the Nanjing document controversy.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_Processing_Tools.5B124.5D">Information Processing Tools</span></h3>
<p>UNESCO develops, maintains and disseminates, free-of-charge, two interrelated software packages for database management (CDS/ISIS) and data mining/statistical analysis (IDAMS).</p>
<ul>
<li>CDS/ISIS - a generalised Information Storage and Retrieval system. The Windows version may run on a single computer or in a local area network. The JavaISIS client/server components allow remote database management over the Internet and are available for Windows, Linux and Macintosh. Furthermore, GenISIS allows the user to produce HTML Web forms for CDS/ISIS database searching. The ISIS_DLL provides an API for developing CDS/ISIS based applications.</li>
<li>OpenIDAMS – a software package for processing and analysing numerical data developed, maintained and disseminated by UNESCO. The original package was proprietary but UNESCO has initiated a project to provide it as open source.</li>
<li>IDIS - a tool for direct data exchange between CDS/ISIS and IDAMS.</li>
</ul>
