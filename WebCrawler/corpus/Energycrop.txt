<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">

<p>An <b>energy crop</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> grown as a low-cost and low-maintenance <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> used to make <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioethanol" title="Bioethanol">bioethanol</a>, or combusted for its energy content to generate electricity or heat. Energy <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> are generally categorized as woody or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> plants; many of the latter are grasses (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graminaceae" title="Graminaceae">Graminaceae</a></i>).</p>
<p>Commercial energy crops are typically densely planted, high-yielding crop species, processed to bio-fuel and burnt to generate power. Woody crops such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salix" title="Salix">willow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a> are widely utilised, as well as temperate grasses such as <i><a href="/wiki/Miscanthus" title="Miscanthus">Miscanthus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pennisetum_purpureum" title="Pennisetum purpureum">Pennisetum purpureum</a></i> (both known as elephant grass). If carbohydrate content is desired for the production of <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, whole-crops such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudan_grass" title="Sudan grass">Sudan grass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melilotus_albus" title="Melilotus albus">white sweet clover</a> and many others, can be made into silage and then converted into biogas.</p>
<p>Through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_modification" title="Genetic modification">genetic modification</a> and application of <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> plants can be manipulated to create greater yields, reduce associated costs and require less water. However, high energy yield can be realized with existing cultivars.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_energy_crops">Types of energy crops</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_state">By state</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_biomass">Solid biomass</span></h4>

<p>(Note: The terms biofuel, biomass, and so on, are often used interchangeably.)</p>
<p>Energy generated by burning plants grown for the purpose, often after the dry matter is <a href="/wiki/Pelletizing" title="Pelletizing">pelletized</a>. Energy crops are used for firing power plants, either alone or co-fired with other fuels. Alternatively they may be used for heat or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_heat_and_power" title="Combined heat and power">combined heat and power</a> (CHP) production.</p>
<p>To cover the increasing requirements of woody biomass, <a href="/wiki/Short_rotation_coppice" title="Short rotation coppice">short rotation coppice</a> (SRC) were applied to agricultural sites. Within this cropping systems fast growing tree species like <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplars</a> are planted in growing cycles of three to five years. The cultivation of this cultures is dependent on wet soil conditions and could be an alternative for moist field sieds. However, an influence on local water conditions could not be excluded. This indicates that an establishment should exclude the vicinity to vulnarable <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> ecosystems.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_biomass_.28methane.29">Gas biomass (methane)</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digesters" title="Anaerobic digesters">Anaerobic digesters</a> or biogas plants can be directly supplemented with energy crops once they have been ensiled into <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a>. The fastest growing sector of German biofarming has been in the area of "Renewable Energy Crops" on nearly 500,000 ha (1,200,000 acres) of land (2006). Energy crops can also be grown to boost gas yields where feedstocks have a low energy content, such as manures and spoiled grain. It is estimated that the energy yield presently of bioenergy crops converted via silage to methane is about 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour" title="Watt-hour">GWh</a>/<a href="/wiki/Square_kilometre" title="Square kilometre">km</a> (1.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10 <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTU</a>/<a href="/wiki/Square_mile" title="Square mile">sq mi</a>). Small mixed cropping enterprises with animals can use a portion of their acreage to grow and convert energy crops and sustain the entire farms energy requirements with about 1/5 the acreage. In Europe and especially Germany, however, this rapid growth has occurred only with substantial government support, as in the German bonus system for <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. Similar developments of integrating crop farming and bioenergy production via silage-methane have been almost entirely overlooked in N. America, where political and structural issues and a huge continued push to centralize energy production has overshadowed positive developments.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_biomass">Liquid biomass</span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</span></h5>


<p>European production of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> from energy crops has grown steadily in the last decade, principally focused on <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a> used for oil and energy. Production of oil/biodiesel from rape covers more than 12,000 km² in Germany alone, and has doubled in the past 15 years. Typical yield of oil as pure biodiesel may be is 100,000 L/km (68,000 US gal/sq mi; 57,000 imp gal/sq mi) or more, making biodiesel crops economically attractive, provided sustainable <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotations</a> exist that are nutrient-balanced and preventative of the spread of disease such as <a href="/wiki/Clubroot" title="Clubroot">clubroot</a>. Biodiesel yield of soybeans is significantly lower than that of rape.</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioethanol">Bioethanol</span></h5>
<p>Energy crops for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biobutanol" title="Biobutanol">biobutanol</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a>. Two leading non-food crops for the production of <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic bioethanol</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switchgrass" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_miscanthus" title="Giant miscanthus">giant miscanthus</a>. There has been a preoccupation with cellulosic bioethanol in America as the agricultural structure supporting biomethane is absent in many regions, with no credits or bonus system in place. Consequently, a lot of private money and investor hopes are being pinned on marketable and patentable innovations in enzyme hydrolysis and the like.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioethanol" title="Bioethanol">Bioethanol</a> also refers to the technology of using principally corn (maize seed) to make ethanol directly through fermentation, a process that under certain field and process conditions can consume as much energy as is the energy value of the ethanol it produces, therefore being non-sustainable. New developments in converting grain stillage (referred to as distillers grain stillage or DGS) into biogas energy looks promising as a means to improve the poor energy ratio of this type of bioethanol process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_dedication">By dedication</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" title="Food vs fuel">Food vs fuel</a></div>
<p>Dedicated energy crops are non-<a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> energy crops as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_miscanthus" title="Giant miscanthus">giant miscanthus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switchgrass" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jatropha" title="Jatropha">jatropha</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>. Dedicated energy crops are promising cellulose sources that can be <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainably produced</a> in many regions of the United States.</p>
<p>Additionally, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproduct" title="Byproduct">Byproducts</a> (<a href="/wiki/Green_waste" title="Green waste">green waste</a>) of food and non-food energy crops can be used to produce various biofuels.</p>
