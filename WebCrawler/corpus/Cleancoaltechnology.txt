<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about technological innovations related to clean coal. For clean coal as a general concept, see <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal" title="Clean coal">clean coal</a>.</div>
<p><b>Clean coal technology</b> is a collection of technologies being developed to mitigate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_coal" title="Environmental impact of coal">environmental impact of coal energy generation</a>. When coal is used as a fuel source, the gaseous emissions generated by the thermal decomposition of the coal include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), mercury, and other chemical byproducts that vary depending on the type of the coal being used. These emissions have been established to have a negative impact on the environment and human health, contributing to acid rain, lung cancer and cardiovascular disease. As a result, clean coal technologies are being developed to remove or reduce pollutant emissions to the atmosphere. Some of the techniques that would be used to accomplish this include chemically washing <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and impurities from the coal, <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">IGCC</a>), improved technology for treating <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">flue</a> gases to remove pollutants to increasingly stringent levels and at higher efficiency, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> technologies to capture the carbon dioxide from the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> and dewatering lower rank coals (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_coal" title="Brown coal">brown coals</a>) to improve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calorific_value" title="Calorific value">calorific value</a>, and thus the efficiency of the conversion into <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. Figures from the United States Environmental Protection Agency show that these technologies have made today’s coal-based generating fleet 77 percent cleaner on the basis of regulated emissions per unit of energy produced.</p>
<p>Clean coal technology usually addresses atmospheric problems resulting from burning coal. Historically, the primary focus was on SO<sub>2</sub> and NO<sub>x</sub>, the most important gases in causation of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, and particulates which cause visible air pollution and <a href="/wiki/Coal#Health_effects" title="Coal">deleterious effects on human health</a>. Concerns exist regarding the economic viability of these technologies and the timeframe of delivery, potentially high hidden economic costs in terms of social and environmental damage, and the costs and viability of disposing of removed carbon and other toxic matter.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span></h2>
<p>Several different technological methods are available for the purpose of carbon capture as demanded by the clean coal concept:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pre-combustion capture - This involves <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> of a feedstock (such as coal) to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a>, which may be <a href="/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction" title="Water-gas shift reaction">shifted</a> to produce a H<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rich_burn" title="Rich burn">rich gas mixture</a>, from which the CO<sub>2</sub> can be efficiently captured and separated, transported, and ultimately sequestered, This technology is usually associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle" title="Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle">Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle</a> process configurations.</li>
<li>Post-combustion capture - This refers to capture of CO<sub>2</sub> from exhaust gases of combustion processes.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_combustion" title="Oxy-fuel combustion">Oxy-fuel combustion</a> - Fossil fuels such as coal are burned in a mixture of recirculated <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> and oxygen, rather than in air, which largely eliminates nitrogen from the flue gas enabling efficient, low-cost CO<sub>2</sub> capture.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper County IGCC Project</a>, a 582 <a href="/wiki/Watt#Megawatt" title="Watt">MW</a> <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a>-based power plant, will use pre-combustion capture of CO<sub>2</sub> to capture 65% of the CO<sub>2</sub> the plant produces, which will be utilized/geologically sequestered in <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> operations.</p>
<p>The Saskatchewan Government's <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Dam_Power_Station" title="Boundary Dam Power Station">Boundary Dam Integrated Carbon Capture and Sequestration Demonstration Project</a> will use post-combustion, amine-based scrubber technology to capture 90% of the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by Unit 3 of the power plant; this CO<sub>2</sub> will be pipelined to and utilized for enhanced oil recovery in the Weyburn oil fields.</p>

<p>An early example of a coal-based plant using (oxy-fuel) carbon-capture technology is Swedish company <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe power station</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Spremberg" title="Spremberg">Spremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, built by German firm <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, which went on-line in September 2008. The facility captures CO<sub>2</sub> and acid rain producing pollutants, separates them, and compresses the CO<sub>2</sub> into a liquid. Plans are to inject the CO<sub>2</sub> into depleted natural gas fields or other geological formations. Vattenfall opines that this technology is considered not to be a final solution for CO<sub>2</sub> reduction in the atmosphere, but provides an achievable solution in the near term while more desirable alternative solutions to power generation can be made economically practical.</p>
<p>Other examples of oxy-combustion carbon capture are in progress. <a href="/wiki/Callide_Power_Station" title="Callide Power Station">Callide Power Station</a> has retrofitted a 30-MWth existing PC-fired power plant to operate in oxy-fuel mode; in Ciuden, Spain, Endesa has a newly built 30-MWth oxy-fuel plant using circulating fluidized bed combustion (CFBC) technology. Babcock-ThermoEnergy's Zero Emission Boiler System (ZEBS) is oxy-combustion-based; this system features near 100% carbon-capture and according to company information virtually no air-emissions.</p>
<p>Other carbon capture and storage technologies include those that <a href="/wiki/Dewatering" title="Dewatering">dewater</a> low-rank coals. Low-rank coals often contain a higher level of moisture content which contains a lower energy content per tonne. This causes a reduced burning efficiency and an increased emissions output. Reduction of moisture from the coal prior to combustion can reduce emissions by up to 50 percent.</p>
<p>The UK government's is working towards a clean energy future and supports clean coal projects across the country. In August 2010, UK-based company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B9_Coal" title="B9 Coal">B9 Coal</a> announced a clean coal project with 90% carbon capture to be put forward to DECC. This would help the UK raise its profile amongst green leaders across the world. This proposed project, <a href="/wiki/Underground_coal_gasification" title="Underground coal gasification">gasifies coal underground</a> and processes it to create pure streams of hydrogen and carbon dioxide. The hydrogen is then used as an emissions-free fuel to run an <a href="/wiki/AFC_Energy" title="AFC Energy">alkaline fuel cell</a> whilst the carbon dioxide is captured. This UK project could provide a world-leading template for clean coal with CCS globally.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstration_projects_in_the_United_States">Demonstration projects in the United States</span></h2>
<p>In the late 1980s and early 1990s, the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) began conducting a joint program with the industry and State agencies to demonstrate clean coal technologies large enough for commercial use. The program, called the Clean Coal Technology &amp; Clean Coal Power Initiative (CCPI), has had a number of successes that have reduced emissions and waste from coal-based electricity generation. The <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Technology_Laboratory" title="National Energy Technology Laboratory">National Energy Technology Laboratory</a> has administered three rounds of CCPI funding and the following projects were selected during each round:</p>
<ul>
<li>Round 1 CCPI Projects
<ul>
<li>Advanced Multi-Product Coal Utilization By-Product Processing Plant</li>
<li>Demonstration of Integrated Optimization Software at the Baldwin Energy Complex</li>
<li>Gilberton Coal-to-Clean Fuels and Power Co-Production Project</li>
<li>Increasing Power Plant Efficiency: Lignite Fuel Enhancement</li>
<li>TOXECON Retrofit for Mercury and Multi-Pollutant Control on Three 90-MW Coal-Fired Boilers</li>
<li>Western Greenbrier Co-Production Demonstration Project</li>
<li>Commercial Demonstration of the Airborne Process</li>
<li>Integration of Advanced Emission Controls to Produce Next-Generation Circulating Fluid Bed Coal Generating Unit</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Round 2 CCPI Projects
<ul>

<li>Demonstration of a Coal-Based Transport Gasifier</li>
<li>Mercury Species and Multi-Pollutant Control Project</li>
<li>Mesaba Energy Project</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Round 3 CCPI Projects
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/American_Electric_Power" title="American Electric Power">American Electric Power</a> Project</li>
<li>Antelope Valley Station Post-Combustion CO<sub>2</sub> Project</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_Energy_California" title="Hydrogen Energy California">Hydrogen Energy California</a> Project</li>

<li>Summit <a href="/wiki/Texas_Clean_Energy_Project" title="Texas Clean Energy Project">Texas Clean Energy Project</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These programs have helped to meet regulatory challenges by incorporating pollution control technologies into a portfolio of cost-effective regulatory compliance options for conventional and developmental coal-fired power plants. This portfolio has positioned the U.S. as a top exporter of clean coal technologies such as those used for SOx, NOx and mercury, and more recently for carbon capture, consistent with a goal of deploying advanced coal-based power systems in commercial service with improved efficiency and environmental performance to meet increasingly stringent environmental regulations and market demands, leading to widespread, global deployment which will contribute to significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. The DOE continues its programs and initiatives through regional sequestration partnerships, a carbon sequestration leadership forum and the Carbon Sequestration Core Program, a CCS research and development program.</p>
<p>According to a report by the assistant secretary for fossil energy at the U.S. Department of Energy, clean coal technology has paid measurable dividends. Technological innovation introduced through the CCT Program now provides consumers cost-effective, clean, coal-based energy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_coal_and_the_environment">Clean coal and the environment</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_coal" title="Environmental effects of coal">Environmental effects of coal</a></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, the burning of coal, a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>, is a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. (See the UN <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a>). As 25.5% of the world's electrical generation in 2004 was from coal-fired generation (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_energy_resources_and_consumption" title="World energy resources and consumption">World energy resources and consumption</a>), reaching the carbon dioxide reduction targets of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> will require modifications to how coal is utilized.</p>
<p>Coal, which is primarily used for the generation of electricity, is the second largest domestic contributor to carbon dioxide emissions in the USA. The public has become more concerned about global warming which has led to new legislation. The coal industry has responded by running advertising touting clean coal in an effort to counter negative perceptions and claiming more than $50 billion towards the development and deployment of "traditional" clean coal technologies over the past 30 years; and promising $500 million towards carbon capture and storage research and development.</p>
<p>Some in the coal industry and the U.S. Department of Energy refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_sequestration" title="Carbon capture and sequestration">carbon capture and sequestration</a> (CCS) as the latest in "clean coal" technologies. The "clean coal" terminology is generally not endorsed by professionals in CCS, and is actively opposed by environmental organizations that favor CCS. CCS is a means to capture carbon dioxide from any source, compress it to a dense liquid-like state, and inject and permanently store it underground. Currently, there are more than 80 carbon capture and sequestration projects underway in the United States. All components of CCS technology have been used for decades in conjunction with enhanced oil recovery and other applications; commercial-scale CCS is currently being tested in the U.S. and other countries. Proposed CCS sites are subjected to extensive investigation and monitoring to avoid potential hazards, which could include leakage of sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> to the atmosphere, induced geological instability, or contamination of aquifers used for drinking water supplies.</p>
<p>Supporters of clean coal use the Great Plains Synfuels plant to support the technical feasibility of carbon dioxide sequestration. Carbon dioxide from the coal gasification is shipped to Canada where it is injected into the ground to aid in oil recovery. Supporters admit that carbon sequestration is expensive.</p>
