<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about the evolutionary process. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Adaptation_(disambiguation)" title="Adaptation (disambiguation)">Adaptation (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">Adoption</a> or <a href="/wiki/Acclimatization" title="Acclimatization">Acclimatization</a>.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, an <b>adaptation</b>, also called an <b>adaptive trait</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_trait" title="Phenotypic trait">trait</a> with a current functional role in the life of an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> that is maintained and <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. Adaptation refers to both the current state of being adapted and to the dynamic evolutionary process that leads to the adaptation. Adaptations enhance the <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">fitness</a> and survival of individuals. Organisms face a succession of environmental challenges as they grow and develop and are equipped with an adaptive <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_plasticity" title="Phenotypic plasticity">plasticity</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> of traits develop in response to the imposed conditions. The <a href="/wiki/Ontogeny" title="Ontogeny">developmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Reaction_norm" title="Reaction norm">norm of reaction</a> for any given trait is essential to the correction of adaptation as it affords a kind of biological insurance or resilience to varying environments.</p>

<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>The significance of an adaptation can only be understood in relation to the total biology of the species.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">Julian Huxley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution:_The_Modern_Synthesis" title="Evolution: The Modern Synthesis">Evolution: The Modern Synthesis</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Adaptation is, first of all, a <i>process</i>, rather than a part of a body. An internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasite</a> (such as a <a href="/wiki/Liver_fluke" title="Liver fluke">liver fluke</a>) can illustrate the distinction: such a parasite may have a very simple bodily structure, but nevertheless the organism is highly adapted to its specific environment. From this we see that adaptation is not just a matter of visible traits: in such parasites critical adaptations take place in the <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycle</a>, which is often quite complex. However, as a practical term, adaptation is often used for the <i>product</i>: those features of a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> which result from the process. Many aspects of an animal or plant can be correctly called adaptations, though there are always some features whose function is in doubt. By using the term <i>adaptation</i> for the evolutionary <i>process</i>, and <i>adaptive trait</i> for the bodily part or function (the product), the two senses of the word may be distinguished.</p>
<p>Adaptation is one of the two main processes that explain the diverse species we see in biology, such as the different species of <a href="/wiki/Darwin%27s_finches" title="Darwin's finches">Darwin's finches</a>. The other is <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a> (species-splitting or <a href="/wiki/Cladogenesis" title="Cladogenesis">cladogenesis</a>), caused by <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">geographical isolation</a> or some other mechanism. A favorite example used today to study the interplay of adaptation and speciation is the evolution of <a href="/wiki/Cichlid" title="Cichlid">cichlid</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> in African lakes, where the question of reproductive isolation is much more complex.</p>
<p>Adaptation is not always a simple matter, where the ideal phenotype evolves for a given external environment. An organism must be viable at all stages of its development and at all stages of its evolution. This places <i>constraints</i> on the evolution of development, <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a> and structure of organisms. The main constraint, over which there has been much debate, is the requirement that each <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> and phenotypic change during evolution should be relatively small, because developmental systems are so complex and interlinked. However, it is not clear what "relatively small" should mean, for example <a href="/wiki/Polyploid" title="Polyploid">polyploidy</a> in plants is a reasonably common large genetic change. The origin of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a> is a more dramatic example.</p>
<p>All adaptations help organisms survive in their <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niches</a>. These adaptive traits may be structural, behavioral or <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiological</a>. Structural adaptations are physical features of an organism (shape, body covering, armament; and also the <a href="/wiki/Comparative_anatomy" title="Comparative anatomy">internal organization</a>). Behavioral adaptations are composed of inherited behavior chains and/or the ability to learn: behaviors may be inherited in detail (<a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instincts</a>), or a capacity for <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a> may be inherited (see <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychology" title="Neuropsychology">neuropsychology</a>). Examples: <a href="/wiki/Foraging" title="Foraging">searching for food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mating" title="Mating">mating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_communication" title="Animal communication">vocalizations</a>. Physiological adaptations permit the organism to perform special functions (for instance, making <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a>, secreting <a href="/wiki/Snail_slime" title="Snail slime">slime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phototropism" title="Phototropism">phototropism</a>); but also more general functions such as <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">growth and development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">temperature regulation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ionic</a> balance and other aspects of <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. Adaptation, then, affects all aspects of the life of an organism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h3>
<p>The following definitions are mainly due to <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_Dobzhansky" title="Theodosius Dobzhansky">Theodosius Dobzhansky</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptedness_and_fitness">Adaptedness and fitness</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fitness_(biology)" title="Fitness (biology)">Fitness (biology)</a></div>
<p>From the above definitions, it is clear that there is a relationship between adaptedness and <i>fitness</i> (a key <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> concept). Differences in fitness between <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotypes</a> predict the rate of evolution by natural selection. Natural selection changes the relative frequencies of alternative phenotypes, insofar as they are <a href="/wiki/Heritability" title="Heritability">heritable</a>. Although the two are connected, the one does not imply the other: a phenotype with high adaptedness may not have high fitness. Dobzhansky mentioned the example of the <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">Californian redwood</a>, which is highly adapted, but a <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relict</a> species in danger of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Sober" title="Elliott Sober">Elliott Sober</a> commented that adaptation was a retrospective concept since it implied something about the history of a trait, whereas fitness predicts a trait's future.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_history">Brief history</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">History of evolutionary thought</a></div>
<p>Adaptation is a fact of life that has been accepted by many of the great thinkers who have tackled the world of living organisms. It is their explanations of how adaptation arises that separates these thinkers. A few of the most significant ideas:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a> did not believe that adaptation required a final cause (~ purpose), but "came about naturally, since such things survived." <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, however, did believe in final causes.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Natural_theology" title="Natural theology">natural theology</a>, adaptation was interpreted as the work of a deity, even as evidence for the existence of God. <a href="/wiki/William_Paley" title="William Paley">William Paley</a> believed that organisms were perfectly adapted to the lives they lead, an argument that shadowed <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a>, who had argued that God had brought about "<a href="/wiki/Best_of_all_possible_worlds" title="Best of all possible worlds">the best of all possible worlds</a>." <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>'s Dr. Pangloss is a parody of this optimistic idea, and <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> also argued against design. The <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_theology#The_Bridgewater_Treatises" title="Natural theology">Bridgewater Treatises</a></i> are a product of natural theology, though some of the authors managed to present their work in a fairly neutral manner. The series was lampooned by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Knox" title="Robert Knox">Robert Knox</a>, who held quasi-evolutionary views, as the <i>Bilgewater Treatises</i>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> broke with the tradition by emphasising the flaws and limitations which occurred in the animal and plant worlds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lamarckism" title="Lamarckism">Lamarckism</a> is a proto-evolutionary hypothesis of the <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_of_acquired_characteristics" title="Inheritance of acquired characteristics">inheritance of acquired characteristics</a>, whose main purpose is to explain adaptations by natural means. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lamarck" title="Jean-Baptiste Lamarck">Jean-Baptiste Lamarck</a> proposed a tendency for organisms to become more complex, moving up a ladder of progress, plus "the influence of circumstances," usually expressed as <i>use and disuse</i>. His evolutionary ideas, and those of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Geoffroy_Saint-Hilaire" title="Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire">Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire</a>, fail because they cannot be reconciled with heredity. This was known even before <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Mendel" title="Gregor Mendel">Gregor Mendel</a> by medical men interested in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> races (<a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Wells" title="William Charles Wells">William Charles Wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Lawrence,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir William Lawrence, 1st Baronet">William Lawrence</a>), and especially by <a href="/wiki/August_Weismann" title="August Weismann">August Weismann</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Many other students of natural history, such as <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon">Buffon</a>, accepted adaptation, and some also accepted evolution, without voicing their opinions as to the mechanism. This illustrates the real merit of Darwin and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">Alfred Russel Wallace</a>, and secondary figures such as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Walter_Bates" title="Henry Walter Bates">Henry Walter Bates</a>, for putting forward a mechanism whose significance had only been glimpsed previously. A century later, experimental field studies and breeding experiments by people such as <a href="/wiki/E._B._Ford" title="E. B. Ford">E. B. Ford</a> and Dobzhansky produced evidence that natural selection was not only the 'engine' behind adaptation, but was a much stronger force than had previously been thought.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_adaptations">Types of adaptations</span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Adaptation is the heart and soul of evolution.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Niles_Eldredge" title="Niles Eldredge">Niles Eldredge</a>, Reinventing Darwin: The Great Debate at the High Table of Evolutionary Theory</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_habitat">Changes in habitat</span></h3>
<p>Before Charles Darwin, adaptation was seen as a fixed relationship between an organism and its habitat. It was not appreciated that as the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> changed, so did the habitat; and as the habitat changed, so did the <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>. Also, habitats are subject to changes in their biota: for example, <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasions</a> of species from other areas. The relative numbers of species in a given habitat are always changing. Change is the rule, though much depends on the speed and degree of the change.</p>
<p>When the habitat changes, three main things may happen to a resident population: habitat tracking, genetic change or extinction. In fact, all three things may occur in sequence. Of these three effects, only genetic change brings about adaptation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_tracking">Habitat tracking</span></h4>
<p>When a habitat changes, the most common thing to happen is that the resident population moves to another locale which suits it; this is the typical response of flying <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> or oceanic organisms, who have wide (though not unlimited) opportunity for movement. This common response is called <i>habitat tracking</i>. It is one explanation put forward for the periods of apparent stasis in the <a href="/wiki/Fossil#Fossil_record" title="Fossil">fossil record</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Punctuated_equilibrium" title="Punctuated equilibrium">punctuated equilibrium</a> theory).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_change">Genetic change</span></h4>
<p>Genetic change is what occurs in a population when natural selection acts on the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_variability" title="Genetic variability">genetic variability</a> of the population; moreover, some <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> may create genetic variation that will lead to differing characteristics of offspring and hence abet adaptation. The first pathways of enzyme-based metabolism may have been parts of <a href="/wiki/Purine_metabolism" title="Purine metabolism">purine nucleotide metabolism</a>, with previous metabolic pathways being part of the ancient <a href="/wiki/RNA_world" title="RNA world">RNA world</a>. By this means, the population adapts genetically to its circumstances. Genetic changes may result in visible structures, or may adjust physiological activity in a way that suits the habitat.</p>
<p>It is now clear that habitats and biota do frequently change. Therefore, it follows that the process of adaptation is never finally complete. Over time, it may happen that the environment changes little, and the species comes to fit its surroundings better and better. On the other hand, it may happen that changes in the environment occur relatively rapidly, and then the species becomes less and less well adapted. Seen like this, adaptation is a genetic <i>tracking process</i>, which goes on all the time to some extent, but especially when the population cannot or does not move to another, less hostile area. Also, to a greater or lesser extent, the process affects every species in a particular <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leigh_Van_Valen" title="Leigh Van Valen">Leigh Van Valen</a> thought that even in a stable environment, competing species had to constantly adapt to maintain their relative standing. This became known as the <a href="/wiki/Red_Queen_hypothesis" title="Red Queen hypothesis">Red Queen hypothesis</a>. One of the manifestations of the Red Queen dynamics can be seen in host-parasite interaction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intimate_relationships:_co-adaptations">Intimate relationships: co-adaptations</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Co-adaptation" title="Co-adaptation">Co-adaptation</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Coevolution" title="Coevolution">coevolution</a>, where the existence of one species is tightly over bound up with the life of another species, new or 'improved' adaptations which occur in one species are often followed by the appearance and spread of corresponding features in the other species. There are many examples of this; the idea emphasises that the life and death of living things is intimately connected, not just with the physical environment, but with the life of other species. These relationships are intrinsically dynamic, and may continue on a trajectory for millions of years, as has the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> and insects (<a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">Pollinator</a> constancy: these <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a> selectively visit flowers from only one species, as can be seen by the colour of the pollen in their baskets:</p>

<p>The gut contents, <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> structures, and mouthpart morphologies of fossilized <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a> suggest that they acted as early pollinators. The association between beetles and <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">angiosperms</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">Early Cretaceous</a> period led to parallel radiations of angiosperms and insects into the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a>. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mimicry">Mimicry</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">Mimicry</a></div>

<p>Bates' work on Amazonian <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> led him to develop the first scientific account of <a href="/wiki/Mimicry" title="Mimicry">mimicry</a>, especially the kind of mimicry which bears his name: <a href="/wiki/Batesian_mimicry" title="Batesian mimicry">Batesian mimicry</a>. This is the mimicry by a palatable species of an unpalatable or noxious species. A common example seen in temperate gardens is the <a href="/wiki/Hoverfly" title="Hoverfly">hoverfly</a>, many of which—though bearing no sting—mimic the warning colouration of <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">hymenoptera</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>). Such mimicry does not need to be perfect to improve the survival of the palatable species.</p>
<p>Bates, Wallace and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_M%C3%BCller" title="Fritz Müller">Fritz Müller</a> believed that Batesian and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCllerian_mimicry" title="Müllerian mimicry">Müllerian mimicry</a> provided evidence for the action of natural selection, a view which is now standard amongst biologists. All aspects of this situation can be, and have been, the subject of research. Field and experimental work on these ideas continues to this day; the topic connects strongly to speciation, genetics and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_developmental_biology" title="Evolutionary developmental biology">development</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_basic_machinery:_internal_adaptations">The basic machinery: internal adaptations</span></h3>
<p>There are some important adaptations to do with the overall coordination of the systems in the body. Such adaptations may have significant consequences. Examples, in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, would be temperature regulation, or improvements in <a href="/wiki/Brain#Functions" title="Brain">brain function</a>, or an effective <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>. An example in plants would be the development of the reproductive system in flowering plants. Such adaptations may make the <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> (<a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic</a> group) more viable in a wide range of habitats. The acquisition of such major adaptations has often served as the spark for <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_radiation" title="Adaptive radiation">adaptive radiation</a>, and huge success over long periods of time for a whole group of animals or plants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compromise_and_conflict_between_adaptations">Compromise and conflict between adaptations</span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>It is a profound truth that Nature does not know best; that genetical evolution... is a story of waste, makeshift, compromise and blunder.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Peter_Medawar" title="Peter Medawar">Peter Medawar</a>, The Future of Man</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>All adaptations have a downside: <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> legs are great for running on grass, but they can't scratch their backs; <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>' hair helps temperature, but offers a niche for <a href="/wiki/Parasitism#Types" title="Parasitism">ectoparasites</a>; the only flying <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a> do is under water. Adaptations serving different functions may be mutually destructive. Compromise and makeshift occur widely, not perfection. Selection pressures pull in different directions, and the adaptation that results is some kind of compromise.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Since the phenotype as a whole is the target of selection, it is impossible to improve simultaneously all aspects of the phenotype to the same degree.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mayr" title="Ernst Mayr">Ernst Mayr</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Growth_of_Biological_Thought" title="The Growth of Biological Thought">The Growth of Biological Thought: Diversity, Evolution, and Inheritance</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Consider the antlers of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_elk" title="Irish elk">Irish elk</a>, (often supposed to be far too large; in <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> antler size has an <a href="/wiki/Allometry" title="Allometry">allometric</a> relationship to body size). Obviously, antlers serve positively for defence against <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a>, and to score victories in the annual <a href="/wiki/Rut_(mammalian_reproduction)" title="Rut (mammalian reproduction)">rut</a>. But they are costly in terms of resource. Their size during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> presumably depended on the relative gain and loss of reproductive capacity in the population of elks during that time. Another example: <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a> to avoid detection is destroyed when vivid colors are displayed at mating time. Here the risk to life is counterbalanced by the necessity for reproduction.</p>
<p>Stream-dwelling salamanders, such as <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_salamander" title="Caucasian salamander">Caucasian salamander</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gold-striped_salamander" title="Gold-striped salamander">Gold-striped salamander</a> have very slender, long bodies, perfectly adapted to life at the banks of fast small rivers and mountain <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">brooks</a>. Elongated body protects their <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> from being washed out by current. However, elongated body increases risk of desiccation and decreases dispersal ability of the salamanders; it also negatively affects their <a href="/wiki/Fecundity" title="Fecundity">fecundity</a>. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Fire_salamander" title="Fire salamander">fire salamander</a>, less perfectly adapted to the mountain brook habitats, is in general more successful, have a higher fecundity and broader geographic range.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peafowl" title="Peafowl">peacock</a>'s ornamental train (grown anew in time for each mating season) is a famous adaptation. It must reduce his maneuverability and flight, and is hugely conspicuous; also, its growth costs food resources. Darwin's explanation of its advantage was in terms of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a>: "This depends on the advantage which certain individuals have over other individuals of the same sex and species, in exclusive relation to reproduction." The kind of sexual selection represented by the peacock is called 'mate choice,' with an implication that the process selects the more fit over the less fit, and so has survival value. The recognition of sexual selection was for a long time in abeyance, but has been rehabilitated. In practice, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_peafowl" title="Indian peafowl">Indian peafowl</a> (<i>Pavo cristatus</i>) is a successful species, with a large natural range in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, so the overall outcome of their mating system is quite viable.</p>
<p>The conflict between the size of the human <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">foetal</a> brain at birth, (which cannot be larger than about 400 cm, else it will not get through the mother's <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a>) and the size needed for an adult brain (about 1400 cm), means the brain of a newborn child is quite immature. The most vital things in human life (locomotion, speech) just have to wait while the brain grows and matures. That is the result of the birth compromise. Much of the problem comes from our upright <a href="/wiki/Bipedalism" title="Bipedalism">bipedal</a> stance, without which our pelvis could be shaped more suitably for birth. <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a> had a similar problem.</p>
<p>As another example, the long neck of a <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffe</a> is a burden and a blessing. The neck of a giraffe can be up to 2 m (6 ft 7 in) in length. This neck can be used for inter-species competition or for foraging on tall trees where shorter <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> cannot reach. However, as previously stated, there is always a trade-off. This long neck is heavy and it adds to the body mass of a giraffe, so the giraffe needs an abundance of nutrition to provide for this costly adaptation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shifts_in_function">Shifts in function</span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Adaptation and function are two aspects of one problem.</i></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Julian Huxley, Evolution: The Modern Synthesis</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-adaptations">Pre-adaptations</span></h3>
<p>This occurs when a species or population has characteristics which (by chance) are suited for conditions which have not yet arisen. For example, the polyploid <a href="/wiki/Spartina" title="Spartina">cordgrass</a> <i>Spartina townsendii</i> is better adapted than either of its parent species to their own habitat of saline marsh and mud-flats. <a href="/wiki/Leghorn_chicken" title="Leghorn chicken">White Leghorn</a> <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a> are markedly more resistant to <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">vitamin B<sub>1</sub></a> deficiency than other breeds. On a plentiful diet there is no difference, but on a restricted diet this preadaptation could be decisive.</p>
<p>Pre-adaptation may occur because a natural population carries a huge quantity of genetic variability. In <a href="/wiki/Ploidy#Diploid" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>, this is a consequence of the system of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a>, where mutant alleles get partially shielded, for example, by the selective advantage of <a href="/wiki/Zygosity#Heterozygous" title="Zygosity">heterozygotes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microorganisms</a>, with their huge populations, also carry a great deal of genetic variability.</p>
<p>The first experimental evidence of the pre-adaptive nature of genetic variants in microorganisms was provided by <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Luria" title="Salvador Luria">Salvador Luria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Delbr%C3%BCck" title="Max Delbrück">Max Delbrück</a> who developed <a href="/wiki/Luria%E2%80%93Delbr%C3%BCck_experiment" title="Luria–Delbrück experiment">Fluctuation Test</a>, a method to show the random fluctuation of pre-existing genetic changes that conferred resistance to <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophage</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterium</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-option_of_existing_traits:_exaptation">Co-option of existing traits: exaptation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exaptation" title="Exaptation">Exaptation</a></div>
<p>The classic example is the <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_mammalian_auditory_ossicles" title="Evolution of mammalian auditory ossicles">ear ossicles of mammals</a>, which we know from <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryological</a> studies originated in the upper and lower <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">jaws</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hyoid_bone" title="Hyoid bone">hyoid bone</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Synapsid" title="Synapsid">synapsid</a> ancestors, and further back still were part of the <a href="/wiki/Branchial_arch" title="Branchial arch">gill arches</a> of early fish. We owe this esoteric knowledge to the comparative anatomists, who, a century ago, were at the cutting edge of evolutionary studies. The word <i>exaptation</i> was coined to cover these shifts in function, which are surprisingly common in evolutionary history. The origin of wings from <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> that were originally used for temperature regulation is a more recent discovery (see <a href="/wiki/Feathered_dinosaur#List_of_non-avian_dinosaur_species_preserved_with_evidence_of_feathers" title="Feathered dinosaur">feathered dinosaurs</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_issues">Related issues</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-adaptive_traits">Non-adaptive traits</span></h3>
<p>Some traits do not appear to be adaptive, that is, they appear to have a neutral or even deleterious effect on fitness in the current environment. Because genes have <a href="/wiki/Pleiotropy" title="Pleiotropy">pleiotropic</a> effects, not all traits may be functional (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Spandrel_(biology)" title="Spandrel (biology)">spandrels</a>). Alternatively, a trait may have been adaptive at some point in an organism's evolutionary history, but a change in habitats caused what used to be an adaptation to become unnecessary or even a hindrance (<a href="/wiki/Maladaptation" title="Maladaptation">maladaptations</a>). Such adaptations are termed <a href="/wiki/Vestigiality" title="Vestigiality">vestigial</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vestigial_organs">Vestigial organs</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vestigiality" title="Vestigiality">Vestigiality</a></div>
<p>Many organisms have vestigial organs, which are the remnants of fully functional structures in their ancestors. As a result of changes in lifestyle the organs became redundant, and are either not functional or reduced in functionality. With the loss of function goes the loss of positive selection, and the subsequent accumulation of deleterious mutations. Since any structure represents some kind of cost to the general economy of the body, an advantage may accrue from their elimination once they are not functional. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Wisdom_tooth" title="Wisdom tooth">wisdom teeth</a> in humans; the loss of pigment and functional <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eyes</a> in cave fauna; the loss of structure in <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_parasite" title="Intestinal parasite">endoparasites</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fitness_landscapes">Fitness landscapes</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fitness_landscape" title="Fitness landscape">Fitness landscape</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sewall_Wright" title="Sewall Wright">Sewall Wright</a> proposed that populations occupy <i>adaptive peaks</i> on a fitness landscape. In order to evolve to another, higher peak, a population would first have to pass through a valley of maladaptive intermediate stages. A given population might be "trapped" on a peak that is not optimally adapted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction">Extinction</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">Extinction</a></div>
<p>If a population cannot move or change sufficiently to preserve its long-term viability, then obviously, it will become extinct, at least in that locale. The species may or may not survive in other locales. Species extinction occurs when the death rate over the entire species exceeds the birth rate for a long enough period for the species to disappear. It was an observation of Van Valen that groups of species tend to have a characteristic and fairly regular rate of extinction.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coextinction">Coextinction</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coextinction" title="Coextinction">Coextinction</a></div>
<p>Just as we have co-adaptation, there is also coextinction. Coextinction refers to the loss of a species due to the extinction of another; for example, the extinction of <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasitic</a> insects following the loss of their hosts. Coextinction can also occur when a flowering plant loses its pollinator, or through the disruption of a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecologist</a> Lian Pin Koh and colleagues discuss coextinction, stating, "Species coextinction is a manifestation of the interconnectedness of organisms in complex ecosystems. . . . While coextinction may not be the most important cause of species extinctions, it is certainly an insidious one."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexibility.2C_acclimatization.2C_learning">Flexibility, acclimatization, learning</span></h3>

<p><i>Flexibility</i> deals with the relative capacity of an organism to maintain themselves in different habitats: their degree of <a href="/wiki/Generalist_and_specialist_species" title="Generalist and specialist species">specialization</a>. <i>Acclimatization</i> is a term used for automatic physiological adjustments during life; <i>learning</i> is the term used for improvement in behavioral performance during life. In biology these terms are preferred, not adaptation, for changes during life which improve the performance of individuals. These adjustments are not inherited genetically by the next generation.</p>
<p>Adaptation, on the other hand, occurs over many generations; it is a <a href="/wiki/Gradualism" title="Gradualism">gradual</a> process caused by natural selection which changes the genetic make-up of a population so the collective performs better in its niche.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexibility">Flexibility</span></h4>
<p>Populations differ in their <a href="/wiki/Phenotypic_plasticity" title="Phenotypic plasticity">phenotypic plasticity</a>, which is the ability of an organism with a given genotype to change its phenotype in response to changes in its habitat, or to move to a different habitat.</p>
<p>To a greater or lesser extent, all living things can adjust to circumstances. The degree of flexibility is inherited, and varies to some extent between individuals. A highly specialized animal or plant lives only in a well-defined habitat, eats a specific type of food, and cannot survive if its needs are not met. Many herbivores are like this; extreme examples are <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">koalas</a> which depend on <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Giant_panda" title="Giant panda">giant pandas</a> which require <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>. A generalist, on the other hand, eats a range of food, and can survive in many different conditions. Examples are humans, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and many carnivores. The <i>tendency</i> to behave in a specialized or exploratory manner is inherited—it is an adaptation.</p>
<p>Rather different is <i>developmental flexibility:</i> "An animal or plant is developmentally flexible if when it is raised in or transferred to new conditions, it changes in structure so that it is better fitted to survive in the new environment," writes <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biologist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Smith" title="John Maynard Smith">John Maynard Smith</a>. Once again, there are huge differences between species, and the <i>capacities</i> to be flexible are inherited.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acclimatization">Acclimatization</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acclimatization" title="Acclimatization">Acclimatization</a></div>
<p>If humans move to a higher altitude, respiration and physical exertion become a problem, but after spending time in high altitude conditions they <i>acclimatize</i> to the pressure by increasing production of <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">Red blood cells</a>. The <i>ability</i> to acclimatize is an adaptation, but not the acclimatization itself. Fecundity goes down, but deaths from some tropical diseases also goes down.</p>
<p>Over a longer period of time, some people will reproduce better at these high altitudes than others. They will contribute more heavily to later generations. Gradually the whole population becomes adapted to the new conditions. This we know takes place, because the performance of long-term communities at higher altitude is significantly better than the performance of new arrivals, even when the new arrivals have had time to make physiological adjustments.</p>
<p>Some kinds of acclimatization happen so rapidly that they are better called reflexes. The rapid colour changes in some <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chameleon" title="Chameleon">chameleons</a> are examples.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning">Learning</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">Learning</a></div>
<p>Social learning is supreme for humans, and is possible for quite a few mammals and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>: of course, that does not involve genetic transmission except to the extent that the capacities are inherited. Similarly, the <i>capacity to learn</i> is an inherited adaptation, but not what is learnt; the capacity for human speech is inherited, but not the details of language.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity_of_genome_DNAs">Diversity of genome DNAs</span></h3>
<p>A large diversity of genome <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNAs</a> in a species is the basis for species’ adaptation and for species’ differentiation. A great number of individuals are needed for carrying the great number of different genome DNAs. According to the Misrepair-accumulation aging theory, Misrepair mechanism is important in maintaining the sufficient number of individuals in a species. Misrepair is a way of repair for increasing the surviving chance of an organism when it has severe injuries. Without Misrepairs, no individual could survive to reproduction age. Thus Misrepair mechanism is an essential mechanism for the survival of a species and for maintaining the number of individuals. Although individuals die from aging, genome DNAs are being recopied and transmitted by individuals generation by generation. In addition, the DNA Misrepairs in germ cells contribute also to the diversity of genome DNAs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function_and_teleonomy">Function and teleonomy</span></h2>
<p>Adaptation raises some issues concerning how biologists use key terms such as <i>function</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Function_(biology)" title="Function (biology)">Function (biology)</a></div>
<p>To say something has a function is to say something about what it does for the organism, obviously. It also says something about its history: how it has come about. A <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> pumps blood: that is its function. It also emits sound, which is just an ancillary side-effect. That is not its function. The heart has a history (which may be well or poorly understood), and that history is about how natural selection formed and maintained the heart as a pump. Every aspect of an organism that has a function has a history. Now, an adaptation must have a functional history: therefore we expect it must have undergone selection caused by relative survival in its habitat. It would be quite wrong to use the word adaptation about a trait which arose as a by-product.</p>
<p>It is widely regarded as unprofessional for a biologist to say something like "A wing is for flying," although that is their normal function. A biologist would be conscious that sometime in the remote past feathers on a small dinosaur had the function of retaining heat, and that later many wings were not used for flying (e.g. penguins, <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostriches</a>). So, the biologist would rather say that the wings on a bird or an insect usually had the <i>function</i> of aiding flight. That would carry the connotation of being an adaptation with a history of evolution by natural selection.</p>
