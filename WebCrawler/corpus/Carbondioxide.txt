<p><b>Carbon dioxide</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <b>CO<sub>2</sub></b>) is a colorless and odorless <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> vital to <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> on Earth. This naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> is composed of a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalently</a> <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonded</a> to two <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Carbon dioxide exists in <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">trace gas</a> at a concentration of about 0.04 percent (400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>) by volume. Natural sources include <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geysers</a>, and it is freed from <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rocks</a> by <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolution</a> in water and acids. Because carbon dioxide is soluble in water, it occurs naturally in <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivers" title="Rivers">rivers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lakes" title="Lakes">lakes</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_caps" title="Ice caps">ice caps</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciers" title="Glaciers">glaciers</a> and also in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>. It is present in deposits of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide" title="Atmospheric carbon dioxide">Atmospheric carbon dioxide</a> is the primary source of carbon in life on Earth and its concentration in Earth's pre-industrial atmosphere since late in the <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> was regulated by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> organisms and geological phenomena. As part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> use <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> to <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesize</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> from carbon dioxide and water, with oxygen produced as a waste product.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) is produced by all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_organisms" title="Aerobic organisms">aerobic organisms</a> when they metabolize carbohydrate and lipids to produce energy by <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a>. It is returned to water via the <a href="/wiki/Fish_physiology#Respiration" title="Fish physiology">gills of fish</a> and to the air via the lungs of air-breathing land animals, including humans. Carbon dioxide is produced during the processes of <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decay</a> of organic materials and the <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> of sugars in <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">winemaking</a>. It is produced by combustion of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> such as coal, <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.</p>
<p>It is a versatile industrial material, used, for example, as an inert gas in welding and fire extinguishers, as a pressurizing gas in air guns and oil recovery, as a chemical feedstock and in liquid form as a solvent in decaffeination of coffee and <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_drying" title="Supercritical drying">supercritical drying</a>. It is added to drinking water and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonated_beverage" title="Carbonated beverage">carbonated beverages</a> including <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sparkling_wine" title="Sparkling wine">sparkling wine</a> to add <a href="/wiki/Effervescence" title="Effervescence">effervescence</a>. The frozen solid form of CO<sub>2</sub>, known as "<a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>" is used as a refrigerant and as an abrasive in <a href="/wiki/Dry-ice_blasting" title="Dry-ice blasting">dry-ice blasting</a>.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is a significant <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. Since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, anthropogenic emissions - including the burning of carbon-based <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> and land use changes (primarily <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>) - have rapidly increased its concentration in the atmosphere, leading to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. It is also a major cause of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> because it dissolves in water to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h2>

<p>Carbon dioxide was the first gas to be described as a discrete substance. In about 1640, the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont" title="Jan Baptist van Helmont">Jan Baptist van Helmont</a> observed that when he burned <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> in a closed vessel, the mass of the resulting <a href="/wiki/Ash_(analytical_chemistry)" title="Ash (analytical chemistry)">ash</a> was much less than that of the original charcoal. His interpretation was that the rest of the charcoal had been transmuted into an invisible substance he termed a "gas" or "wild spirit" (<i>spiritus sylvestre</i>).</p>
<p>The properties of carbon dioxide were studied more thoroughly in the 1750s by the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Black" title="Joseph Black">Joseph Black</a>. He found that <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>) could be heated or treated with <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> to yield a gas he called "fixed air." He observed that the fixed air was denser than air and supported neither flame nor animal life. Black also found that when bubbled through <a href="/wiki/Limewater" title="Limewater">limewater</a> (a saturated aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>), it would <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a> calcium carbonate. He used this phenomenon to illustrate that carbon dioxide is produced by animal respiration and microbial fermentation. In 1772, English chemist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> published a paper entitled <i>Impregnating Water with Fixed Air</i> in which he described a process of dripping <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (or <i>oil of vitriol</i> as Priestley knew it) on chalk in order to produce carbon dioxide, and forcing the gas to dissolve by agitating a bowl of water in contact with the gas.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide was first liquefied (at elevated pressures) in 1823 by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>. The earliest description of solid carbon dioxide was given by <a href="/wiki/Adrien-Jean-Pierre_Thilorier" title="Adrien-Jean-Pierre Thilorier">Adrien-Jean-Pierre Thilorier</a>, who in 1835 opened a pressurized container of liquid carbon dioxide, only to find that the cooling produced by the rapid evaporation of the liquid yielded a "snow" of solid CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_and_physical_properties">Chemical and physical properties</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital_diagram#Carbon_dioxide" title="Molecular orbital diagram">Molecular orbital diagram § Carbon dioxide</a></div>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Co2comp.png"><img alt="Co2comp.png" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="1507" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Co2comp.png/250px-Co2comp.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Co2comp.png/375px-Co2comp.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Co2comp.png/500px-Co2comp.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>

<p>The carbon dioxide molecule is linear and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrosymmetric" title="Centrosymmetric">centrosymmetric</a>. The two C=O bonds are equivalent and are short (116.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picometer" title="Picometer">pm</a>), consistent with double bonding. Since it is centrosymmetric, the molecule has no electrical <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a>. Consequently, only two vibrational bands are observed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IR_spectrum" title="IR spectrum">IR spectrum</a> – an antisymmetric stretching mode at 2349 cm and a <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels" title="Degenerate energy levels">degenerate</a> pair of bending modes at 667 cm. There is also a symmetric stretching mode at 1388 cm which is only observed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raman_spectrum" title="Raman spectrum">Raman spectrum</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions_of_CO2">Chemical reactions of CO<sub>2</sub></span></h3>

<p>CO<sub>2</sub> is a weak <a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophile</a>. Its reaction with basic water illustrates this property, in which case <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a>. Other nucleophiles react as well. <p>In <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex" title="Metal carbon dioxide complex">metal carbon dioxide complexes</a>, CO<sub>2</sub> serves as a ligand, which can facilitate the conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to other chemicals.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_data" title="Carbon dioxide data">Carbon dioxide data</a>.</div>


<p>Carbon dioxide is colorless. At low concentrations, the gas is odorless. At higher concentrations it has a sharp, acidic odor. At <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, the density of carbon dioxide is around 1.98 kg/m, about 1.67 times that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide has no liquid state at pressures below 5.1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmospheres</a> (520 kPa). At 1 atmosphere (near mean sea level pressure), the gas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(physics)" title="Deposition (physics)">deposits</a> directly to a solid at temperatures below −78.5 °C (−109.3 °F; 194.7 K) and the solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimes</a> directly to a gas above −78.5 °C. In its solid state, carbon dioxide is commonly called <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>.</p>
<p>Liquid carbon dioxide forms only at <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressures</a> above 5.1 atm; the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of carbon dioxide is about 518 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KPa" title="KPa">kPa</a> at −56.6 °C (see phase diagram at left). The <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a> is 7.38 MPa at 31.1 °C. Another form of solid carbon dioxide observed at high pressure is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> glass-like solid. This form of glass, called <i><a href="/wiki/Amorphous_carbonia" title="Amorphous carbonia">carbonia</a></i>, is produced by <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooling</a> heated CO<sub>2</sub> at extreme pressure (40–48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">GPa</a> or about 400,000 atmospheres) in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil" title="Diamond anvil">diamond anvil</a>. This discovery confirmed the theory that carbon dioxide could exist in a glass state similar to other members of its elemental family, like <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica glass</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Germanium_dioxide" title="Germanium dioxide">germanium dioxide</a>. Unlike silica and germania glasses, however, carbonia glass is not stable at normal pressures and reverts to gas when pressure is released.</p>
<p>At temperatures and pressures above the critical point, carbon dioxide behaves as a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>

<p>Carbon dioxide is used by the food industry, the oil industry, and the chemical industry. The compound has varied commercial uses but one of its greatest use as a chemical is in the production of carbonated beverages; it provides the sparkle in carbonated beverages such as soda water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursor_to_chemicals">Precursor to chemicals</span></h3>

<p>In the chemical industry, carbon dioxide is mainly consumed as an ingredient in the production of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, with a smaller fraction being used to produce <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and a range of other products. Metal <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a>, as well as some carboxylic acids derivatives (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sodium_salicylate" title="Sodium salicylate">sodium salicylate</a>) are prepared using CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>In addition to conventional processes using CO<sub>2</sub> for chemical production, electrochemical methods are also being explored at a research level. In particular, the use of renewable energy for production of fuels from CO<sub>2</sub> (such as methanol) is attractive as this could result in fuels that could be easily transported and used within conventional combustion technologies but have no net CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foods">Foods</span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additive</a> used as a propellant and acidity regulator in the food industry. It is approved for usage in the EU (listed as <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E number</a> E290), US and Australia and New Zealand (listed by its INS number 290).</p>
<p>A candy called <a href="/wiki/Pop_Rocks" title="Pop Rocks">Pop Rocks</a> is pressurized with carbon dioxide gas at about 4 x 10 Pa (40 bar, 580 psi). When placed in the mouth, it dissolves (just like other hard candy) and releases the gas bubbles with an audible pop.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">Leavening agents</a> cause dough to rise by producing carbon dioxide. <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">Baker's yeast</a> produces carbon dioxide by fermentation of sugars within the dough, while chemical leaveners such as <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">baking powder</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baking_soda" title="Baking soda">baking soda</a> release carbon dioxide when heated or if exposed to <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beverages">Beverages</span></h4>
<p>Carbon dioxide is used to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonated</a> <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda_water" title="Soda water">soda water</a>. Traditionally, the carbonation of beer and sparkling wine came about through natural fermentation, but many manufacturers carbonate these drinks with carbon dioxide recovered from the fermentation process. In the case of bottled and kegged beer, the most common method used is carbonation with recycled carbon dioxide. With the exception of British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Ale" title="Real Ale">Real Ale</a>, draught beer is usually transferred from kegs in a cold room or cellar to dispensing taps on the bar using pressurized carbon dioxide, sometimes mixed with nitrogen.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_making">Wine making</span></h4>
<p>Carbon dioxide in the form of <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> is often used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_making" title="Wine making">wine making</a> process to cool down clusters of <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapes</a> quickly after picking to help prevent spontaneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermentation</a> by wild <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">yeast</a>. The main advantage of using dry ice over regular water ice is that it cools the grapes without adding any additional water that may decrease the <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> concentration in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_must" title="Grape must">grape must</a>, and therefore also decrease the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> concentration in the finished wine.</p>
<p>Dry ice is also used during the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_soak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cold soak (page does not exist)">cold soak</a> phase of the wine making process to keep grapes cool. The carbon dioxide gas that results from the sublimation of the dry ice tends to settle to the bottom of tanks because it is denser than air. The settled carbon dioxide gas creates a hypoxic environment which helps to prevent bacteria from growing on the grapes until it is time to start the fermentation with the desired strain of yeast.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is also used to create a hypoxic environment for <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_maceration" title="Carbonic maceration">carbonic maceration</a>, the process used to produce <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais" title="Beaujolais">Beaujolais</a> wine.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is sometimes used to top up wine bottles or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storage_(wine)" title="Storage (wine)">storage</a> vessels such as barrels to prevent oxidation, though it has the problem that it can dissolve into the wine, making a previously still wine slightly fizzy. For this reason, other gases such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> are preferred for this process by professional wine makers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inert_gas">Inert gas</span></h3>
<p>It is one of the most commonly used compressed gases for pneumatic (pressurized gas) systems in portable pressure tools. Carbon dioxide is also used as an atmosphere for <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>, although in the welding arc, it reacts to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidize</a> most metals. Use in the automotive industry is common despite significant evidence that welds made in carbon dioxide are more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> than those made in more inert atmospheres, and that such weld joints deteriorate over time because of the formation of carbonic acid. It is used as a welding gas primarily because it is much less expensive than more inert gases such as <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. When used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIG_welding" title="MIG welding">MIG welding</a>, CO<sub>2</sub> use is sometimes referred to as MAG welding, for Metal Active Gas, as CO<sub>2</sub> can react at these high temperatures. It tends to produce a hotter puddle than truly inert atmospheres, improving the flow characteristics. Although, this may be due to atmospheric reactions occurring at the puddle site. This is usually the opposite of the desired effect when welding, as it tends to embrittle the site, but may not be a problem for general mild steel welding, where ultimate ductility is not a major concern.</p>
<p>It is used in many consumer products that require pressurized gas because it is inexpensive and nonflammable, and because it undergoes a phase transition from gas to liquid at room temperature at an attainable pressure of approximately 60 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a> (870 psi, 59 atm), allowing far more carbon dioxide to fit in a given container than otherwise would. Life jackets often contain canisters of pressured carbon dioxide for quick inflation. Aluminium capsules of CO<sub>2</sub> are also sold as supplies of compressed gas for <a href="/wiki/Air_gun" title="Air gun">airguns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paintball" title="Paintball">paintball</a> markers, inflating bicycle tires, and for making <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a>. Rapid vaporization of liquid carbon dioxide is used for blasting in coal mines. High concentrations of carbon dioxide can also be used to kill pests. Liquid carbon dioxide is used in <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_drying" title="Supercritical drying">supercritical drying</a> of some food products and technological materials, in the preparation of specimens for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscopy" title="Scanning electron microscopy">scanning electron microscopy</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">decaffeination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a> beans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_extinguisher">Fire extinguisher</span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide can be used to extinguish flames by flooding the environment around the flame with the gas. It does not itself react to extinguish the flame, but starves the flame of oxygen by displacing it. Some <a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher#Clean_agents_and_carbon_dioxide" title="Fire extinguisher">fire extinguishers</a>, especially those designed for electrical fires, contain liquid carbon dioxide under pressure. Carbon dioxide extinguishers work well on small flammable liquid and electrical fires, but not on ordinary combustible fires, because although it excludes oxygen, it does not cool the burning substances significantly and when the carbon dioxide disperses they are free to catch fire upon exposure to atmospheric oxygen. Their desirability in electrical fire stems from the fact that, unlike water or other chemical based methods, Carbon dioxide will not cause short circuits, leading to even more damage to equipment. Because it is a gas, it is also easy to dispense large amounts of the gas automatically in IT infrastructure rooms, where the fire itself might be hard to reach with more immediate methods because it is behind rack doors and inside of cases. Carbon dioxide has also been widely used as an extinguishing agent in fixed fire protection systems for local application of specific hazards and total flooding of a protected space. International Maritime Organization standards also recognize carbon dioxide systems for fire protection of ship holds and engine rooms. Carbon dioxide based fire protection systems have been linked to several deaths, because it can cause suffocation in sufficiently high concentrations. A review of CO<sub>2</sub> systems identified 51 incidents between 1975 and the date of the report, causing 72 deaths and 145 injuries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_CO2_as_solvent">Supercritical CO<sub>2</sub> as solvent</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">Supercritical carbon dioxide</a></div>
<p>Liquid carbon dioxide is a good <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophilic" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a> <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> and is used to remove <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>. Carbon dioxide has attracted attention in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> and other chemical processing industries as a less toxic alternative to more traditional solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">organochlorides</a>. It is used by some <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaners</a> for this reason (see <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a>). It is used in the preparation of some <a href="/wiki/Aerogel#Production" title="Aerogel">aerogels</a> because of the properties of supercritical carbon dioxide.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_and_biological_applications">Agricultural and biological applications</span></h3>
<p>Plants require carbon dioxide to conduct <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. The atmospheres of greenhouses may (if of large size, must) be enriched with additional CO<sub>2</sub> to sustain and increase the rate of plant growth. At very high concentrations (100 times atmospheric concentration, or greater), carbon dioxide can be toxic to animal life, so raising the concentration to 10,000 ppm (1%) or higher for several hours will eliminate pests such as <a href="/wiki/Whitefly" title="Whitefly">whiteflies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider_mite" title="Spider mite">spider mites</a> in a greenhouse.</p>
<p>In medicine, up to 5% carbon dioxide (130 times atmospheric concentration) is added to <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for stimulation of breathing after <a href="/wiki/Apnea" title="Apnea">apnea</a> and to stabilize the <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span>/CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span> balance in blood.</p>
<p>It has been proposed that carbon dioxide from power generation be bubbled into ponds to stimulate growth of algae that could then be converted into <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> fuel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_recovery">Oil recovery</span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide is used in <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> where it is injected into or adjacent to producing oil wells, usually under <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> conditions, when it becomes miscible with the oil. This approach can increase original oil recovery by reducing residual oil saturation by between 7 per cent to 23 per cent additional to <a href="/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum#Primary_recovery" title="Extraction of petroleum">primary extraction</a>. It acts as both a pressurizing agent and, when dissolved into the underground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, significantly reduces its viscosity, and changing surface chemistry enabling the oil to flow more rapidly through the reservoir to the removal well. In mature oil fields, extensive pipe networks are used to carry the carbon dioxide to the injection points.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio_transformation_into_fuel">Bio transformation into fuel</span></h3>
<p>Researchers have genetically modified a strain of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyanobacterium" title="Cyanobacterium">cyanobacterium</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Synechococcus" title="Synechococcus">Synechococcus elongatus</a></i> to produce the fuels <a href="/wiki/Isobutyraldehyde" title="Isobutyraldehyde">isobutyraldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isobutanol" title="Isobutanol">isobutanol</a> from CO<sub>2</sub> using photosynthesis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigerant">Refrigerant</span></h3>

<p>Liquid and solid carbon dioxide are important <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>, especially in the food industry, where they are employed during the transportation and storage of ice cream and other frozen foods. Solid carbon dioxide is called "dry ice" and is used for small shipments where refrigeration equipment is not practical. Solid carbon dioxide is always below −78.5 °C at regular atmospheric pressure, regardless of the air temperature.</p>
<p><span id="R744"></span> Liquid carbon dioxide (industry nomenclature R744 or R-744) was used as a refrigerant prior to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodifluoromethane" title="Dichlorodifluoromethane">R-12</a> and may enjoy a renaissance due to the fact that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R134a" title="R134a">R134a</a> contributes to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Its physical properties are highly favorable for cooling, refrigeration, and heating purposes, having a high volumetric cooling capacity. Due to the need to operate at pressures of up to 130 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a> (1880 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>), CO<sub>2</sub> systems require highly resistant components that have already been developed for mass production in many sectors. In automobile air conditioning, in more than 90% of all driving conditions for latitudes higher than 50°, R744 operates more efficiently than systems using R134a. Its environmental advantages (<a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">GWP</a> of 1, non-ozone depleting, non-toxic, non-flammable) could make it the future working fluid to replace current HFCs in cars, supermarkets, and heat pump water heaters, among others. <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> has fielded CO<sub>2</sub>-based beverage coolers and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> is interested in CO<sub>2</sub> refrigeration and heating technology.</p>
<p>The global automobile industry is expected to decide on the next-generation refrigerant in car air conditioning. CO<sub>2</sub> is one discussed option.(see <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning" title="Sustainable automotive air conditioning">Sustainable automotive air conditioning</a>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_bed_methane_recovery">Coal bed methane recovery</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_coal_bed_methane_recovery" title="Enhanced coal bed methane recovery">enhanced coal bed methane recovery</a>, carbon dioxide would be pumped into the coal seam to displace methane, as opposed to current methods which primarily rely on the removal of water (to reduce pressure) to make the coal seam release its trapped methane.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span></h3>

<p>Carbon dioxide is the <a href="/wiki/Active_laser_medium" title="Active laser medium">lasing medium</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_laser" title="Carbon dioxide laser">carbon dioxide laser</a>, which is one of the earliest type of lasers.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide can be used as a means of controlling the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of swimming pools, by continuously adding gas to the water, thus keeping the pH from rising. Among the advantages of this is the avoidance of handling (more hazardous) acids. Similarly, it is also used in the maintaining <a href="/wiki/Reef_aquarium" title="Reef aquarium">reef aquaria</a>, where it is commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_reactor" title="Calcium reactor">calcium reactors</a> to temporarily lower the pH of water being passed over <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> in order to allow the calcium carbonate to dissolve into the water more freely where it is used by some <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> to build their skeleton.</p>
<p>Used as the primary coolant in the British <a href="/wiki/Advanced_gas-cooled_reactor" title="Advanced gas-cooled reactor">advanced gas-cooled reactor</a> for nuclear power generation.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide induction is commonly used for the euthanasia of laboratory research animals. Methods to administer CO<sub>2</sub> include placing animals directly into a closed, prefilled chamber containing CO<sub>2</sub>, or exposure to a gradually increasing concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>. In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/American_Veterinary_Medical_Association" title="American Veterinary Medical Association">American Veterinary Medical Association</a> issued new guidelines for carbon dioxide induction, stating that a displacement rate of 10% to 30% of the <a href="/wiki/Gas_chamber" title="Gas chamber">gas chamber</a> volume per minute is optimal for the humane euthanization of small rodents.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is also used in several related <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_cleaning" title="Carbon dioxide cleaning">cleaning and surface preparation</a> techniques.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Earth.27s_atmosphere">In Earth's atmosphere</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">Carbon cycle</a></div>

<p>Carbon dioxide in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">trace gas</a>, currently (early 2016) having an average concentration of 402 parts per million by volume (or 611 parts per million by mass). Atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide fluctuate slightly with the seasons, falling during the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> spring and summer as plants consume the gas and rising during northern autumn and winter as plants go dormant or die and decay. Concentrations also vary on a regional basis, most strongly <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">near the ground</a> with much smaller variations aloft. In urban areas concentrations are generally higher and indoors they can reach 10 times background levels.</p>

<p>Combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> have caused the atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide to increase by about 43% since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">age of industrialization</a>. Most carbon dioxide from human activities is released from burning coal and other fossil fuels. Other human activities, including deforestation, biomass burning, and cement production also produce carbon dioxide. Volcanoes emit between 0.2 and 0.3 billion tons of carbon dioxide per year, while human activities emit about 29 billion tons.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, absorbing and emitting infrared radiation at its two infrared-active vibrational frequencies (see Structure and bonding above). This process causes carbon dioxide to warm the surface and lower atmosphere, while cooling the upper atmosphere. The increase in atmospheric concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>, and thus in the CO<sub>2</sub>-induced greenhouse effect, is the reason for the rise in average global temperature since the mid-20th century. Although carbon dioxide is the greenhouse gas primarily responsible for the rise, methane, nitrous oxide, ozone, and various other long-lived greenhouse gases also contribute. Carbon dioxide is of greatest concern because it exerts a larger overall warming influence than all of those other gases combined, and because it has a long atmospheric lifetime.</p>

<p>Not only do increasing carbon dioxide concentrations lead to increases in global surface temperature, but increasing global temperatures also cause increasing concentrations of carbon dioxide. This produces a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> for changes induced by other processes such as <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">orbital cycles</a>. Five hundred million years ago the carbon dioxide concentration was 20 times greater than today, decreasing to 4–5 times during the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period and then slowly declining with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azolla_Event" title="Azolla Event">a particularly swift reduction</a> occurring 49 million years ago.</p>
<p>Local concentrations of carbon dioxide can reach high values near strong sources, especially those that are isolated by surrounding terrain. At the Bossoleto hot spring near <a href="/wiki/Rapolano_Terme" title="Rapolano Terme">Rapolano Terme</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, situated in a bowl-shaped depression about 100 m (330 ft) in diameter, concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> rise to above 75% overnight, sufficient to kill insects and small animals. After sunrise the gas is dispersed by convection during the day. High concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> produced by disturbance of deep lake water saturated with CO<sub>2</sub> are thought to have caused 37 fatalities at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> in 1984 and 1700 casualties at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a>, Cameroon in 1986.</p>
<p>Human-made carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) continues to increase above levels not seen in hundreds of thousands of years. Currently, about half of the carbon dioxide released from the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">burning of fossil fuels</a> remains in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and is not absorbed by vegetation and the oceans.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_oceans">In the oceans</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">Carbon cycle</a></div>
<p>Carbon dioxide dissolves in the ocean to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> (CO<sub>3</sub>). There is about fifty times as much carbon dissolved in the oceans as exists in the atmosphere. The oceans act as an enormous <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>, and have taken up about a third of CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by human activity.</p>
<p>As the concentration of carbon dioxide increases in the atmosphere, the increased uptake of carbon dioxide into the oceans is causing a measurable decrease in the pH of the oceans, which is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>. This reduction in pH affects biological systems in the oceans, primarily oceanic <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcifying</a> organisms. These effects span the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> from <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> to <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a> and include organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">coccolithophores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacea" title="Crustacea">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">mollusks</a>. Under normal conditions, calcium carbonate is stable in surface waters since the carbonate ion is at <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturating</a> concentrations. However, as ocean pH falls, so does the concentration of this ion, and when carbonate becomes undersaturated, structures made of calcium carbonate are vulnerable to dissolution. Corals, coccolithophore algae, coralline algae, foraminifera, <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pteropod" title="Pteropod">pteropods</a> experience reduced calcification or enhanced dissolution when exposed to elevated <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br/>
2</span></span>.</p>
<p>Gas solubility decreases as the temperature of water increases (except when both pressure exceeds 300 bar and temperature exceeds 393 K, only found near deep geothermal vents) and therefore the rate of uptake from the atmosphere decreases as ocean temperatures rise.</p>
<p>Most of the CO<sub>2</sub> taken up by the ocean, which is about 30% of the total released into the atmosphere, forms carbonic acid in equilibrium with bicarbonate. Some of these chemical species are consumed by photosynthetic organisms that remove carbon from the cycle. Increased CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere has led to decreasing <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> of seawater, and there is concern that this may adversely affect organisms living in the water. In particular, with decreasing alkalinity, the availability of carbonates for forming shells decreases, although there's evidence of increased shell production by certain species under increased CO<sub>2</sub> content.</p>
<p>NOAA states in their May 2008 "State of the science fact sheet for <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>" that:<br/>
"The oceans have absorbed about 50% of the carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) released from the burning of fossil fuels, resulting in chemical reactions that lower ocean pH. This has caused an increase in hydrogen ion (acidity) of about 30% since the start of the industrial age through a process known as "ocean acidification." A growing number of studies have demonstrated adverse impacts on marine organisms, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The rate at which reef-building corals produce their skeletons decreases, while production of numerous varieties of jellyfish increases.</li>
<li>The ability of marine algae and free-swimming zooplankton to maintain protective shells is reduced.</li>
<li>The survival of larval marine species, including commercial fish and shellfish, is reduced."</li>
</ul>
<p>Also, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) writes in their Climate Change 2007: Synthesis Report:<br/>
"The uptake of anthropogenic carbon since 1750 has led to the ocean becoming more acidic with an average decrease in pH of 0.1 units. Increasing atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations lead to further acidification ... While the effects of observed ocean acidification on the marine biosphere are as yet undocumented, the progressive acidification of oceans is expected to have negative impacts on marine shell-forming organisms (e.g. corals) and their dependent species."</p>
<p>Some marine calcifying organisms (including coral reefs) have been singled out by major research agencies, including NOAA, OSPAR commission, NANOOS and the IPCC, because their most current research shows that ocean acidification should be expected to impact them negatively.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide is also introduced into the oceans through hydrothermal vents. The <i>Champagne</i> hydrothermal vent, found at the Northwest Eifuku volcano at <a href="/wiki/Marianas_Trench_Marine_National_Monument" title="Marianas Trench Marine National Monument">Marianas Trench Marine National Monument</a>, produces almost pure liquid carbon dioxide, one of only two known sites in the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide is an end product of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a> in organisms that obtain energy by breaking down sugars, fats and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as part of their <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. This includes all plants, algae and animals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic</a> fungi and bacteria. In <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, the carbon dioxide travels in the blood from the body's tissues to the skin (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>) or the gills (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>), from where it dissolves in the water, or to the lungs from where it is exhaled. During active photosynthesis, <a href="/wiki/Compensation_point" title="Compensation point">plants can absorb more carbon dioxide from the atmosphere than they release</a> in respiration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photosynthesis_and_carbon_fixation">Photosynthesis and carbon fixation</span></h3>


<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">Carbon fixation</a> is a biochemical process by which atmospheric carbon dioxide is incorporated by <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and (<a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>) into <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">energy-rich</a> organic <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, thus creating their own food by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. Photosynthesis uses carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> from which other <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> can be constructed, and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> is produced as a by-product.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase oxygenase</a>, commonly abbreviated to RuBisCO, is the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> involved in the first major step of carbon fixation, the production of two molecules of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-phosphoglycerate" title="3-phosphoglycerate">3-phosphoglycerate</a> from CO<sub>2</sub> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose_bisphosphate" title="Ribulose bisphosphate">ribulose bisphosphate</a>, as shown in the diagram at left.</p>
<p>RuBisCO is thought to be the single most abundant protein on Earth.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">Phototrophs</a> use the products of their photosynthesis as internal food sources and as raw material for the <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a> of more complex organic molecules, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. These are used for their own growth, and also as the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chains</a> and webs that feed other organisms, including animals such as ourselves. Some important phototrophs, the <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">coccolithophores</a> synthesise hard <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> scales. A globally significant species of coccolithophore is <i><a href="/wiki/Emiliania_huxleyi" title="Emiliania huxleyi">Emiliania huxleyi</a></i> whose <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> scales have formed the basis of many <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, where what was previously atmospheric carbon can remain fixed for geological timescales.</p>
<p>Plants can grow as much as 50 percent faster in concentrations of 1,000 ppm CO<sub>2</sub> when compared with ambient conditions, though this assumes no change in climate and no limitation on other nutrients. Elevated CO<sub>2</sub> levels cause increased growth reflected in the harvestable yield of crops, with wheat, rice and soybean all showing increases in yield of 12–14% under elevated CO<sub>2</sub> in FACE experiments.</p>
<p>Increased atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations result in fewer stomata developing on plants which leads to reduced water usage and increased <a href="/wiki/Water-use_efficiency" title="Water-use efficiency">water-use efficiency</a>. Studies using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-Air_Concentration_Enrichment" title="Free-Air Concentration Enrichment">FACE</a> have shown that CO<sub>2</sub> enrichment leads to decreased concentrations of micronutrients in crop plants. This may have knock-on effects on other parts of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> as herbivores will need to eat more food to gain the same amount of protein.</p>
<p>The concentration of secondary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolites" title="Metabolites">metabolites</a> such as phenylpropanoids and flavonoids can also be altered in plants exposed to high concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>Plants also emit CO<sub>2</sub> during respiration, and so the majority of plants and algae, which use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C3_photosynthesis" title="C3 photosynthesis">C3 photosynthesis</a>, are only net absorbers during the day. Though a growing forest will absorb many tons of CO<sub>2</sub> each year, a mature forest will produce as much CO<sub>2</sub> from respiration and decomposition of dead specimens (e.g., fallen branches) as is used in photosynthesis in growing plants. Contrary to the long-standing view that they are carbon neutral, mature forests can continue to accumulate carbon and remain valuable <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>, helping to maintain the carbon balance of Earth's atmosphere. Additionally, and crucially to life on earth, photosynthesis by phytoplankton consumes dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> in the upper ocean and thereby promotes the absorption of CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_poisoning" title="Carbon dioxide poisoning">Carbon dioxide poisoning</a></div>

<p>Carbon dioxide content in fresh air (averaged between sea-level and 10 kPa level, i.e., about 30 km (19 mi) altitude) varies between 0.036% (360 ppm) and 0.041% (410 ppm), depending on the location.</p>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> is an <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiant gas</a> and not classified as toxic or harmful in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals" title="Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals">Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals standards</a> of <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_Commission_for_Europe" title="United Nations Economic Commission for Europe">United Nations Economic Commission for Europe</a> by using the <a href="/wiki/OECD_Guidelines_for_the_Testing_of_Chemicals" title="OECD Guidelines for the Testing of Chemicals">OECD Guidelines for the Testing of Chemicals</a>. In concentrations up to 1% (10,000 ppm), it will make some people feel drowsy and give the lungs a stuffy feeling. Concentrations of 7% to 10% (70,000 to 100,000 ppm) may cause suffocation, even in the presence of sufficient oxygen, manifesting as dizziness, headache, visual and hearing dysfunction, and unconsciousness within a few minutes to an hour. The physiological effects of acute carbon dioxide exposure are grouped together under the term <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>, a subset of <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiation</a>.</p>
<p>Because it is heavier than air, in locations where the gas seeps from the ground (due to sub-surface volcanic or geothermal activity) in relatively high concentrations, without the dispersing effects of wind, it can collect in sheltered/pocketed locations below average ground level, causing animals located therein to be suffocated. Carrion feeders attracted to the carcasses are then also killed. Children have been killed in the same way near the city of <a href="/wiki/Goma" title="Goma">Goma</a> by CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from the nearby volcano <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Nyiragongo" title="Mt. Nyiragongo">Mt. Nyiragongo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a> term for this phenomenon is '<a href="/wiki/Mazuku" title="Mazuku">mazuku</a>'.</p>
<p>Adaptation to increased concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> occurs in humans, including <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_adaptation" title="Respiratory adaptation">modified breathing</a> and kidney bicarbonate production, in order to balance the effects of blood acidification (<a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>). Several studies suggested that 2.0 percent inspired concentrations could be used for closed air spaces (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a>) since the adaptation is physiological and reversible, as decrement in performance or in normal physical activity does not happen at this level of exposure for five days. Yet, other studies show a decrease in cognitive function even at much lower levels. Also, with ongoing respiratory acidosis, adaptation or <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">compensatory mechanisms will be unable to reverse such condition</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Below_1.25">Below 1%</span></h4>
<p>There are few studies of the health effects of long-term continuous CO<sub>2</sub> exposure on humans and animals at levels below 1% and there is potentially a significant risk to humans in the near future with rising atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> levels associated with <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Occupational CO<sub>2</sub> exposure limits have been set in the United States at 0.5% (5000 ppm) for an eight-hour period. At this CO<sub>2</sub> concentration, <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> crew experienced headaches, lethargy, mental slowness, emotional irritation, and sleep disruption. Studies in animals at 0.5% CO<sub>2</sub> have demonstrated kidney calcification and bone loss after eight weeks of exposure. A study of humans exposed in 2.5 hour sessions demonstrated significant effects on cognitive abilities at concentrations as low as 0.1% (1000ppm) CO<sub>2</sub> likely due to CO<sub>2</sub> induced increases in cerebral blood flow. Another study observed a decline in basic activity level and information usage at 1000 ppm, when compared to 500 ppm.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">Ventilation</span></h4>
<p>Poor ventilation is one of the main causes of excessive CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations in closed spaces. Carbon dioxide differential above outdoor concentrations at steady state conditions (when the occupancy and ventilation system operation are sufficiently long that CO<sub>2</sub> concentration has stabilized) are sometimes used to estimate ventilation rates per person. Higher CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations are associated with occupant health, comfort and performance degradation. <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">ASHRAE</a> Standard 62.1–2007 ventilation rates may result in indoor levels up to 2,100 ppm above ambient outdoor conditions. Thus if the outdoor concentration is 400 ppm, indoor concentrations may reach 2,500 ppm with ventilation rates that meet this industry consensus standard. Concentrations in poorly ventilated spaces can be found even higher than this (range of 3,000 or 4,000).</p>
<p>Miners, who are particularly vulnerable to gas exposure due to an insufficient ventilation, referred to mixtures of carbon dioxide and nitrogen as "<a href="/wiki/Blackdamp" title="Blackdamp">blackdamp</a>," "choke damp" or "stythe." Before more effective technologies were developed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miners" title="Miners">miners</a> would frequently monitor for dangerous levels of blackdamp and other gases in mine shafts by bringing a caged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_Canary" title="Domestic Canary">canary</a> with them as they worked. The canary is more sensitive to asphyxiant gases than humans, and as it became unconscious would stop singing and fall off its perch. The <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">Davy lamp</a> could also detect high levels of blackdamp (which sinks, and collects near the floor) by burning less brightly, while <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, another suffocating gas and explosion risk, would make the lamp burn more brightly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_physiology">Human physiology</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span></h4>
<p>The body produces approximately 2.3 pounds (1.0 kg) of carbon dioxide per day per person, containing 0.63 pounds (290 g) of carbon. <span id="partial_pressure"></span> In humans, this carbon dioxide is carried through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venous_system" title="Venous system">venous system</a> and is breathed out through the lungs, resulting in lower concentrations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>. The carbon dioxide content of the blood is often given as the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a>, which is the pressure which carbon dioxide would have had if it alone occupied the volume.</p>
<p>In humans, the carbon dioxide contents are as follows:</p>
<center>

</center>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_in_the_blood">Transport in the blood</span></h4>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> is carried in blood in three different ways. (The exact percentages vary depending whether it is arterial or venous blood).</p>
<ul>
<li>Most of it (about 70% to 80%) is converted to <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> ions <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span> by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> in the red blood cells, by the reaction CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → H + <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span>.</li>
<li>5% – 10% is dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a></li>
<li>5% – 10% is bound to <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> as <a href="/wiki/Carbamino" title="Carbamino">carbamino</a> compounds</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">Hemoglobin</a>, the main oxygen-carrying molecule in <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>, carries both oxygen and carbon dioxide. However, the CO<sub>2</sub> bound to hemoglobin does not bind to the same site as oxygen. Instead, it combines with the N-terminal groups on the four globin chains. However, because of <a href="/wiki/Allosteric_regulation" title="Allosteric regulation">allosteric</a> effects on the hemoglobin molecule, the binding of CO<sub>2</sub> decreases the amount of oxygen that is bound for a given partial pressure of oxygen. The decreased binding to carbon dioxide in the blood due to increased oxygen levels is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haldane_Effect" title="Haldane Effect">Haldane Effect</a>, and is important in the transport of carbon dioxide from the tissues to the lungs. Conversely, a rise in the partial pressure of CO<sub>2</sub> or a lower pH will cause offloading of oxygen from hemoglobin, which is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohr_Effect" title="Bohr Effect">Bohr Effect</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_of_respiration">Regulation of respiration</span></h4>

<p>Carbon dioxide is one of the mediators of local <a href="/wiki/Autoregulation" title="Autoregulation">autoregulation</a> of blood supply. If its concentration is high, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> expand to allow a greater blood flow to that tissue.</p>
<p>Bicarbonate ions are crucial for regulating blood pH. A person's breathing rate influences the level of CO<sub>2</sub> in their blood. Breathing that is too slow or shallow causes <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>, while breathing that is too rapid leads to <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a>, which can cause <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">respiratory alkalosis</a>.</p>
<p>Although the body requires oxygen for metabolism, low oxygen levels normally do not stimulate breathing. Rather, breathing is stimulated by higher carbon dioxide levels. As a result, breathing low-pressure air or a gas mixture with no oxygen at all (such as pure nitrogen) can lead to loss of consciousness without ever experiencing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_hunger" title="Air hunger">air hunger</a>. This is especially perilous for high-altitude fighter pilots. It is also why flight attendants instruct passengers, in case of loss of cabin pressure, to apply the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">oxygen mask</a> to themselves first before helping others; otherwise, one risks losing consciousness.</p>
<p>The respiratory centers try to maintain an arterial CO<sub>2</sub> pressure of 40 mm Hg. With intentional hyperventilation, the CO<sub>2</sub> content of arterial blood may be lowered to 10–20 mm Hg (the oxygen content of the blood is little affected), and the respiratory drive is diminished. This is why one can hold one's breath longer after hyperventilating than without hyperventilating. This carries the risk that unconsciousness may result before the need to breathe becomes overwhelming, which is why hyperventilation is particularly dangerous before free diving.</p>
